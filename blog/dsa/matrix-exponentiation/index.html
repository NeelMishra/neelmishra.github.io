    Matrix Exponentiation: Linear Recurrences in O(log n)

    June 2026 · 24 min read

    Everyone learns to compute the $n$-th Fibonacci number in $O(n)$: start from the bottom and add your way up. It feels optimal. How could you possibly find $F(n)$ without touching every term before it? Yet there is a technique that computes $F(n)$ in $O(\log n)$, and the very same idea handles any linear recurrence: Tribonacci, climbing stairs, tiling counts, constrained-string counts. That technique is matrix exponentiation, and once it clicks it feels like a cheat code.

    The Problem: Fibonacci Faster Than O(n)

    Recall the Fibonacci recurrence and its base cases:

    $$F(0) = 0, \quad F(1) = 1, \quad F(n) = F(n-1) + F(n-2).$$

    You already know three ways to evaluate it, with three very different costs:

    ApproachTimeWhy
    Plain recursion$O(2^n)$Each call spawns two, with no memory of past work.
    Memoized / top-down$O(n)$Each subproblem solved once.
    Bottom-up / iterative$O(n)$One pass, two rolling variables.

    For most people the story ends at $O(n)$. But $n$ in a competitive problem can be $10^{18}$, far too large to loop over even once. Matrix exponentiation breaks the $O(n)$ wall and lands at $O(\log n)$. The trick is to stop thinking of the recurrence as "add the last two numbers" and start thinking of it as "apply the same linear transformation, over and over."

    Linear Recurrences and Their Degree

    Matrix exponentiation works for linear recurrences: relations where the next term is a fixed weighted sum of a fixed number of previous terms. Fibonacci is the canonical example:

    $$F(n) = \underbrace{1}_{c_1}\cdot F(n-1) + \underbrace{1}_{c_2}\cdot F(n-2).$$

    Two things matter. First, it is linear: no squares, no products of terms, no $F(n-1)^2$ or $F(n-1)\cdot F(n-2)$. Each previous term appears to the first power with a constant coefficient. Second, the answer depends on exactly two earlier terms, so we call this a degree-2 recurrence. If the relation reached back three terms, like $T(n) = T(n-1) + T(n-2) + T(n-3)$, it would be degree 3. The degree $d$ is the single most important number here: it decides how big our matrix will be.

    The litmus test. If you can write $F(n) = c_1 F(n-1) + c_2 F(n-2) + \dots + c_d F(n-d)$ with the $c_i$ constant, the recurrence is linear of degree $d$ and matrix exponentiation applies. Anything nonlinear (a square, a product of two terms, a max, a conditional) breaks the method.

    The Core Idea: A Recurrence Is a Matrix

    Here is the move that unlocks everything. Instead of tracking a single number, track a state vector of the last $d$ terms. For Fibonacci, $d = 2$, so the state is a column of two values:

    $$v_n = \begin{bmatrix} F(n) \\ F(n-1) \end{bmatrix}.$$

    We want a single matrix $T$ that advances the state by one step, taking the window $\big[F(n-1),\,F(n-2)\big]$ to the next window $\big[F(n),\,F(n-1)\big]$:

    $$\begin{bmatrix} F(n) \\ F(n-1) \end{bmatrix} = T \cdot \begin{bmatrix} F(n-1) \\ F(n-2) \end{bmatrix}.$$

    What must $T$ be? Read the two output rows as equations. The top row has to produce $F(n)$, and we know $F(n) = 1\cdot F(n-1) + 1\cdot F(n-2)$. The bottom row has to produce $F(n-1)$, which is just $1\cdot F(n-1) + 0\cdot F(n-2)$, a copy of the first input. Stacking those coefficient rows gives the transformation matrix:

    $$T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.$$

    Let us sanity-check the multiplication once, by hand:

    $$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} F(n-1) \\ F(n-2) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\cdot F(n-1) + 1\cdot F(n-2) \\ 1\cdot F(n-1) + 0\cdot F(n-2) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} F(n) \\ F(n-1) \end{bmatrix}. \checkmark$$

    That top entry is literally the recurrence, and the bottom entry just shifts the old "current" value down into the "previous" slot. Multiplying by $T$ slides a two-wide window one step along the sequence. Step through it below.

    ▶ Multiplying by T Slides the Window

    Each step multiplies the current window $\big[F(n-1),F(n-2)\big]$ by $T=\left[\begin{smallmatrix}1&1\\1&0\end{smallmatrix}\right]$ to produce the next window. Watch the top row recompute $F(n)$ and the bottom row carry $F(n-1)$ down.

    Unrolling to a Matrix Power

    The single-step rule is $v_n = T\,v_{n-1}$. Apply it repeatedly and the matrix products stack up:

    $$v_n = T\,v_{n-1} = T^2\,v_{n-2} = T^3\,v_{n-3} = \dots = T^{\,n-1}\,v_1.$$

    We stop at $v_1$ because that is the smallest window we can write entirely from the base cases:

    $$v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} F(1) \\ F(0) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}.$$

    So the whole computation collapses into a single closed form:

    $$\begin{bmatrix} F(n) \\ F(n-1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}^{\,n-1} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}.$$

    Concretely: $F(3)$ needs $T^2 v_1$, $F(4)$ needs $T^3 v_1$, and in general $F(n)$ needs $T^{\,n-1} v_1$. The answer $F(n)$ is the top entry of the resulting vector. Because $v_1 = [1,\,0]^\top$, multiplying $T^{\,n-1}$ by it just selects the first column, so equivalently:

    $$F(n) = \big(T^{\,n-1}\big)_{0,0}.$$

    All the work is now in one place: raising a fixed $2\times 2$ matrix to the power $n-1$. If we compute that power naively by multiplying $T$ into an accumulator $n-1$ times, we are back to $O(n)$ and have gained nothing. The payoff comes from computing the power cleverly.

    Fast Matrix Power: Binary Exponentiation

    Raising anything to a power, a number or a matrix, can be done in $O(\log n)$ multiplications by binary exponentiation (also called exponentiation by squaring). The idea: instead of $n-1$ multiplications, repeatedly square the base and multiply the squares that line up with the 1-bits of the exponent.

    Why squaring helps: $T^8 = \big(T^4\big)^2 = \big((T^2)^2\big)^2$. Three squarings reach the 8th power instead of seven multiplications. Any exponent is a sum of powers of two (its binary digits), so

    $$T^{\,9} = T^{\,1001_2} = T^{\,8}\cdot T^{\,1}, \qquad T^{\,13} = T^{\,1101_2} = T^{\,8}\cdot T^{\,4}\cdot T^{\,1}.$$

    The standard implementation walks the exponent bit by bit from the least significant end. It keeps a running result (starting at the identity matrix $I$, the matrix analogue of the number 1) and a base that is squared every iteration. Whenever the current bit is 1, it folds the current base into result.

    ▶ Binary Exponentiation of T (computing $T^{9}$)

    To get $F(10)$ we need $T^{9}$, and $9 = 1001_2$. The base keeps squaring ($T^1,T^2,T^4,T^8,\dots$). Each bit equal to 1 multiplies that square into the result. Useful identity: $T^k=\left[\begin{smallmatrix}F(k{+}1)&F(k)\\F(k)&F(k{-}1)\end{smallmatrix}\right]$.

    Only $\lfloor \log_2 9 \rfloor + 1 = 4$ iterations, each doing a constant number of $2\times 2$ multiplications. That is the whole speedup: $\log n$ matrix multiplications instead of $n$.

    Putting It Together in C++

    Two helper routines do all the heavy lifting: a matrix multiply, and a fast matrix power built on it. Everything else is wiring. The code below works for a matrix of any size, which is exactly what we need when we generalize beyond Fibonacci. Most contest problems ask for the answer modulo a prime, so the multiply reduces as it goes.

    #include <bits/stdc++.h>
using namespace std;

const long long MOD = 1e9 + 7;            // typical problem modulus
typedef vector<vector<long long>> Matrix;

// Multiply two square matrices of equal size, modulo MOD.
Matrix multiply(const Matrix& A, const Matrix& B) {
    int n = A.size();
    Matrix C(n, vector<long long>(n, 0));
    for (int i = 0; i < n; ++i)
        for (int k = 0; k < n; ++k)
            if (A[i][k])                  // small speedup: skip zero entries
                for (int j = 0; j < n; ++j)
                    C[i][j] = (C[i][j] + A[i][k] * B[k][j]) % MOD;
    return C;
}

// The n x n identity matrix (the "1" of matrix multiplication).
Matrix identity(int n) {
    Matrix I(n, vector<long long>(n, 0));
    for (int i = 0; i < n; ++i) I[i][i] = 1;
    return I;
}

// base^exp via binary exponentiation: O(d^3 log exp).
Matrix matrixPower(Matrix base, long long exp) {
    int n = base.size();
    Matrix result = identity(n);
    while (exp > 0) {
        if (exp & 1) result = multiply(result, base);  // fold in this 1-bit
        base = multiply(base, base);                   // square the base
        exp >>= 1;                                      // next bit
    }
    return result;
}

// F(n) in O(log n).
long long fib(long long n) {
    if (n == 0) return 0;
    Matrix T = {{1, 1},
                {1, 0}};
    Matrix Tp = matrixPower(T, n - 1);    // T^(n-1)
    // [F(n); F(n-1)] = T^(n-1) * [F(1); F(0)] = T^(n-1) * [1; 0]
    // so F(n) = Tp[0][0]*1 + Tp[0][1]*0 = Tp[0][0]
    return Tp[0][0];
}

int main() {
    for (long long n : {0, 1, 2, 10, 50, 100})
        cout << "F(" << n << ") = " << fib(n) << "\n";
}

    A common alternative writes matrixPower recursively, computing half = matrixPower(base, exp/2), squaring it, and multiplying one extra base when the exponent is odd. That recursion and the iterative loop above compute the identical result; the loop simply avoids the call stack. Use whichever you find clearer.

    Complexity Analysis

    Let $d$ be the degree of the recurrence, so $T$ is a $d \times d$ matrix. Two costs combine:

    • One matrix multiply is the schoolbook triple loop: $O(d^3)$.
    • Binary exponentiation performs $O(\log n)$ multiplications (the exponent halves every iteration).

    Multiplying the two gives the headline result:

    $$\boxed{\,O\!\left(d^3 \log n\right)\,}$$

    For Fibonacci $d = 2$, so $d^3 = 8$ is a constant and the running time is a clean $O(\log n)$. That is the "magic": the term index $n$ only ever shows up inside a logarithm. The catch is the $d^3$ factor, which makes the method shine for small degree and huge $n$. If $n$ is small there is no reason to leave the simple $O(n)$ table; matrix exponentiation earns its keep precisely when $n$ is astronomically large.

    Generalizing to Any n-Degree Linear Recurrence

    Nothing above was special to Fibonacci. For a general degree-$d$ linear recurrence

    $$F(n) = c_1 F(n-1) + c_2 F(n-2) + \dots + c_d F(n-d),$$

    carry a length-$d$ state vector $v_n = \big[F(n),\,F(n-1),\,\dots,\,F(n-d+1)\big]^\top$ and build the $d \times d$ companion matrix. The first row holds the coefficients; the rest of the matrix is a shifted identity that pushes each value down one slot:

    $$T = \begin{bmatrix} c_1 & c_2 & c_3 & \cdots & c_{d-1} & c_d \\ 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & & & \ddots & & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.$$

    The top row reproduces the recurrence; every other row $i$ has a single 1 that copies $F(n-i)$ into the next slot. The structure is easy to read off as a diagram: one "brain" row of coefficients, then a sub-diagonal of carry-down 1s.

    The same unrolling applies. With initial vector $v_{d-1} = \big[F(d-1),\,\dots,\,F(0)\big]^\top$ built from the base cases,

    $$v_n = T^{\,n-d+1}\,v_{d-1}, \qquad F(n) = (v_n)_0.$$

    The C++ above already handles this: just build the right $T$ and initial vector, and read the top entry. Tribonacci ($d=3$, all coefficients 1) uses

    $$T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad v_n = \begin{bmatrix} T(n) \\ T(n-1) \\ T(n-2) \end{bmatrix}.$$
    Handling a constant term. An inhomogeneous recurrence like $F(n) = F(n-1) + F(n-2) + 5$ is not strictly linear because of the $+5$. Recover linearity by appending a constant "1" lane to the state vector: track $\big[F(n),\,F(n-1),\,1\big]^\top$ and add a row/column so the matrix carries the $+5$ and keeps the $1$ alive. The degree grows by one, the method is unchanged.

    When to Reach for Matrix Exponentiation

    • Linear recurrence, small degree. The coefficients are constant and $d$ is a handful, so $d^3$ stays tiny.
    • The index is enormous. $n$ up to $10^9$, $10^{18}$, or larger, where even one $O(n)$ pass is impossible.
    • Counting over steps. Many "number of ways to reach step / fill a board / build a valid string of length $n$" problems hide a linear recurrence; once you find it, the matrix is mechanical.

    Do not reach for it when $n$ is small (the plain DP table is simpler and just as fast in practice), or when the recurrence is nonlinear (squares, products, min/max), where no fixed matrix can encode the transition.
