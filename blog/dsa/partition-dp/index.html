    Partition DP: The Complete Guide

    June 2026 · 24 min read

    Some of the most feared dynamic programming problems share one idea: you are given a linear object (an array, a string, a chain of matrices, a row of balloons) and you must chop it into contiguous pieces so that the total cost of the pieces is optimal. That idea is partition DP, and once you see the shared skeleton, a dozen "hard" problems collapse into the same three lines of code.

    This series is built around two families that look different but think the same way:

    • Front (linear) partition. Walk left to right, and at each step decide where the current piece ends. State is one index: dp[i] = best way to partition the prefix ending at i. Palindrome Partitioning II, Word Break, Partition Array for Maximum Sum, and Split Array Largest Sum all live here.
    • Interval partition. The cost of merging a range depends on a split point inside it, and the two halves are themselves ranges. State is two indices: dp[i][j] = best way to resolve the segment $[i, j]$. Matrix Chain Multiplication, Burst Balloons, and Minimum Cost to Cut a Stick all live here.
    What this series covers. The Overview (this post) draws the map and the shared split-point intuition. The Front Partition series then derives the linear template and solves Palindrome Partitioning II end to end. The Interval Partition series builds Matrix Chain Multiplication, then the famously slippery Burst Balloons, then Minimum Cost to Cut a Stick. Every post ships C++ and at least one step-through animation.

    What Makes a Problem "Partition DP"?

    You are looking at a partition DP problem when all three of these are true:

    1. The input is a linear sequence, and any solution is described by where you place cut points between elements.
    2. The pieces are contiguous. You never reorder elements; you only group adjacent ones.
    3. The cost is additive over pieces, and the cost of a piece can be computed (or looked up) independently once its boundaries are fixed.

    If the pieces could be non-contiguous subsets, you are in subset / bitmask DP territory instead. If there are no pieces at all, just a running scalar choice, you probably want ordinary 1D DP. Partition DP is specifically about where the boundaries between contiguous groups go.

    The One Idea: Fix the Last (or Middle) Split

    Every partition DP recurrence is built by fixing one split and recursing on what remains. The two families differ only in which split you fix.

    Front partition fixes the last piece. To partition the prefix $[0, i]$, choose where the final piece starts, say at index $j+1$. Everything before it, the prefix $[0, j]$, is a smaller subproblem you already solved:

    $$dp[i] = \min_{0 \le j \le i} \Big(\, dp[j-1] + \text{cost}(j, i) \,\Big).$$

    Interval partition fixes a middle split. To resolve the segment $[i, j]$, pick the split point $k$ where the segment breaks into $[i, k]$ and $[k+1, j]$ (or in MCM-style problems, the matrix at which you multiply last). Both halves are smaller segments:

    $$dp[i][j] = \min_{i \le k < j} \Big(\, dp[i][k] + dp[k+1][j] + \text{merge}(i, k, j) \,\Big).$$

    That is the whole subject. The art is in three things: defining the state precisely, writing cost / merge correctly (especially its boundary indices), and ordering the computation so that every subproblem is ready before it is used.

    A Recognition Checklist

    When you meet a new problem, ask in order:

    • Is the answer a set of cuts on a line? If no, this is not partition DP.
    • Does each piece's cost depend only on its own boundaries? If yes, front partition is likely enough: dp[i] over prefixes.
    • Does the cost of combining two pieces depend on something between them (the dimension shared by two matrices, the balloons still standing on both sides, the original endpoints of a stick)? If yes, you need interval DP: dp[i][j] with a middle split.
    • Is there a fixed number of pieces $k$? Add a dimension: dp[i][p] = best partition of prefix i into p pieces.

    Three Pitfalls to Pre-empt

    • Off-by-one in the cost boundaries. In the front recurrence, dp[j-1] + cost(j, i), the piece is the closed range $[j, i]$ and the carried subproblem is the prefix ending at $j-1$. Mixing inclusive and exclusive indices is the single most common bug. Pick a convention and write it on paper before you code.
    • Wrong fill order for intervals. $dp[i][j]$ needs all strictly shorter segments first. Loop by increasing length, not by raw $i$ and $j$, or memoize top-down so the order takes care of itself.
    • Thinking "first split" when "last element" is the trick. Burst Balloons is the canonical lesson: deciding which balloon you pop first leaves a messy, dependent subproblem, while deciding which you pop last makes the two sides independent. Choosing the right thing to fix is the whole game.
