    QR and SVD Least-Squares Solvers in C++

    July 2026 · 45 min read

    The previous post solved least squares by assembling the $(d{+}1) \times (d{+}1)$ normal-equation matrix $A = X_b^\top X_b$ and applying Gaussian elimination to $A\beta = X_b^\top y$. That is the right approach for well-scaled, non-collinear features, but it pays a precision tax that grows quadratically with the condition of $X_b$. This post keeps the same public interface, the same fit / predict / evaluate contract, and the same fit on raw $X$ with an implicit intercept, and replaces the normal-equation solve with two solvers that operate directly on the augmented design matrix $X_b$: a Householder QR decomposition that handles full-column-rank designs, and a one-sided Jacobi SVD that additionally produces a minimum-norm solution for rank-deficient designs. All code uses only standard-library headers and vector<vector<double>>.

    Conditioning and the Normal Equations

    Every finite-precision computation has a condition number story. For least squares the relevant quantity is $\operatorname{cond}(X_b)$, the ratio of the largest to smallest singular value of the augmented design matrix. A condition number of $10^4$ means a $10^4$-fold amplification of input perturbations in the worst case. The problem is that the normal equations work with $A = X_b^\top X_b$, and forming this product squares the condition number:

    $$\operatorname{cond}(X_b^\top X_b) = \operatorname{cond}(X_b)^2.$$

    A moderately ill-conditioned $X_b$ with $\operatorname{cond}(X_b) = 10^4$ produces an $A$ with $\operatorname{cond}(A) = 10^8$. Double-precision arithmetic carries about $15{-}16$ decimal digits, so $10^8$ conditioning leaves only $7{-}8$ reliable digits in the solution. For many regression problems this is acceptable; for near-collinear or poorly scaled features it can wipe out the signal before the solver even runs. The two solvers introduced here avoid this squaring entirely: QR and SVD operate on $X_b$ itself, so they only inherit $\operatorname{cond}(X_b)$, not its square.

    The Augmented Design Matrix

    Both solvers augment the raw $n \times d$ feature matrix $X$ with a leading column of ones to produce the $n \times (d{+}1)$ augmented matrix $X_b$, exactly as the normal-equation formulation does conceptually. Throughout this post $p = d{+}1$ denotes the number of parameters (intercept plus $d$ coefficients). The augmented matrix must be tall: $n \ge p$ is required for any unique least-squares solution to exist.

    SymbolShapeMeaning
    $n$scalarnumber of training rows
    $d$scalarcaller-supplied feature columns

    Materializing $X_b$ as an explicit $n \times p$ matrix costs $O(np)$ memory. Both solvers here allocate that matrix internally, unlike the normal-equation approach in the previous post, which avoided it entirely. This is the trade: the stability gain of working directly on $X_b$ comes at the cost of one additional $O(np)$ allocation on top of the caller's own $X$.

    Householder QR

    The QR decomposition factors $X_b = QR$ where $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is orthogonal ($Q^\top Q = I$) and $R \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ is upper triangular. For a tall full-rank matrix, only the first $p$ rows of $R$ are nonzero, so the least-squares problem reduces to:

    $$X_b^\top X_b \beta = X_b^\top y \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad R^\top R \beta = R^\top Q^\top y \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad R_p \beta = (Q^\top y)_{0:p},$$

    where $R_p$ is the top $p \times p$ block of $R$ and $(Q^\top y)_{0:p}$ is the first $p$ entries of $Q^\top y$. Since $R_p$ is upper triangular and (for a full-rank design) nonsingular, the solution follows directly from back-substitution. No squaring of the condition number occurs because the orthogonal factor $Q$ preserves norms exactly.

    Householder Reflectors

    Householder triangularization applies a sequence of orthogonal reflections. At step $k$ the goal is to zero out all entries in column $k$ below the diagonal. The reflector is constructed as follows. Let $x = X_b[k:n, k]$ (the sub-column starting at the diagonal). Define

    $$\alpha = -\operatorname{sign}(x_0)\,\lVert x \rVert_2, \qquad v = x - \alpha e_1,$$

    where $e_1$ is the first standard basis vector of the same length as $x$. The sign convention for $\alpha$ is chosen to avoid catastrophic cancellation when $x_0$ is close to $\lVert x \rVert_2$. The Householder matrix is

    $$H = I - \frac{2vv^\top}{v^\top v}.$$

    Multiplying $x$ by $H$ maps it to $\alpha e_1$: the entry at position $(k,k)$ becomes $\alpha$ (nonzero by construction) and all entries below it become zero. Because $H$ is orthogonal, $\lVert Hx \rVert_2 = \lVert x \rVert_2$, so no amplification of errors occurs. In code the reflector is never formed explicitly: the action of $H$ on any vector $u$ is computed as $Hu = u - \tau v$ where $\tau = 2(v^\top u)/(v^\top v)$, reducing the work from $O(n^2)$ for a dense matrix multiplication to $O(n)$ per application.

    The algorithm applies the same reflector to all remaining columns of $X_b$ in column $k$ onward, and simultaneously to the running right-hand side $Q^\top y$ (initialized to $y$ and updated in place). After $p$ steps, $X_b$ has been overwritten with $R$ (upper triangular) and $Q^\top y$ holds the rotated right-hand side. Only the first $p$ entries of $Q^\top y$ are needed for the solve; the remaining $n{-}p$ entries are the norm of the residual but not the solution.

    Rank Checks Without Column Pivoting

    Householder QR without column pivoting does not rearrange columns, so a rank-deficient design may not produce a clean zero on the diagonal: rounding can place a small but nonzero value there instead. After all $p$ reflections, the implementation scans the diagonal entries $|R_{kk}|$ for $k = 0, \dots, p{-}1$ and counts those above a tolerance:

    $$\text{rankTol} = p \cdot \epsilon_{\text{mach}} \cdot 64 \cdot \max_k |R_{kk}|.$$

    If the count falls below $p$, the design is treated as rank-deficient and a std::runtime_error is thrown with a clear message directing the caller to the SVD solver. This is a deliberate design choice: QR without pivoting cannot reliably compute a minimum-norm solution for rank-deficient problems (the pivot column order matters for that), so it is better to fail explicitly and let the caller escalate to SVD.

    For full-rank designs the condition estimate is the ratio of the largest to smallest diagonal magnitude:

    $$\widehat{\operatorname{cond}}(R) = \frac{\max_k |R_{kk}|}{\min_k |R_{kk}|}.$$

    This is a rough approximation of the true condition number of $X_b$ (not exact because the diagonal entries of $R$ are not the singular values), but it is cheap to compute and captures large differences in column scaling.

    QR Regressor Implementation

    QRRegressor follows the same struct layout as OLSRegressor: a fitted flag, nFeatures, intercept, and a coef vector. Two extra diagnostic fields, rank and condEst, are populated after every successful fit and are useful for inspecting the numerical health of the design.

    struct QRRegressor {
    bool fitted = false;
    int nFeatures = 0;
    double intercept = 0.0;
    vector<double> coef;
    int rank = 0;       // p after a full-rank fit; may be less if pivoting were used
    double condEst = 0.0;  // max|R_kk| / min|R_kk|

    void fit(const Matrix& X, const vector<double>& y);
    double predict(const vector<double>& x) const;
    vector<double> predict(const Matrix& X) const;
};

    The core of fit builds the augmented matrix, applies $p$ Householder reflections, checks the rank, then back-substitutes:

    void fit(const Matrix& X, const vector<double>& y) {
    fitted = false;
    validateFitInput(X, y, "QRRegressor");
    int n = (int)X.size(), d = (int)X[0].size(), p = d + 1;

    // Build X_b: intercept column first, then caller's features.
    Matrix Xb(n, vector<double>(p));
    for (int i = 0; i < n; ++i) {
        Xb[i][0] = 1.0;
        for (int j = 0; j < d; ++j) Xb[i][j+1] = X[i][j];
    }
    vector<double> rhs = y;   // accumulates Q^T y in-place

    const double eps = numeric_limits<double>::epsilon();
    for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) {
        // Norm of sub-column Xb[k:n][k].
        double sigma2 = 0.0;
        for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) sigma2 += Xb[i][k] * Xb[i][k];
        if (!isfinite(sigma2))
            throw runtime_error(
                "QRRegressor::fit: column norm became non-finite; rescale the input");
        double sigma = sqrt(sigma2);

        if (sigma < eps * 64.0)
            throw runtime_error(
                "QRRegressor::fit: column " + to_string(k) +
                " is linearly dependent; design is rank-deficient. "
                "Use SVDRegressor for a min-norm solution.");

        // Reflector v: choose sign to avoid cancellation.
        double alpha = -copysign(sigma, Xb[k][k]);
        vector<double> v(n - k);
        for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) v[i-k] = Xb[i][k];
        v[0] -= alpha;  // v[0] = Xb[k][k] - alpha

        double vTv = 0.0;
        for (double vi : v) vTv += vi * vi;
        if (!isfinite(vTv))
            throw runtime_error(
                "QRRegressor::fit: reflector norm became non-finite; rescale the input");
        if (vTv < eps * eps) continue;

        // Apply H = I - 2vv^T/v^Tv to columns k..p-1 of Xb.
        for (int j = k; j < p; ++j) {
            double vTx = 0.0;
            for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) vTx += v[i-k] * Xb[i][j];
            double tau = 2.0 * vTx / vTv;
            for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) Xb[i][j] -= tau * v[i-k];
        }
        // Apply same reflector to accumulated Q^T y.
        double vTr = 0.0;
        for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) vTr += v[i-k] * rhs[i];
        double tau = 2.0 * vTr / vTv;
        for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) rhs[i] -= tau * v[i-k];
    }

    // Rank check via R diagonal.
    double maxAbs = 0.0;
    for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) maxAbs = max(maxAbs, fabs(Xb[k][k]));
    double rankTol = maxAbs * (double)p * eps * 64.0;
    rank = 0;
    for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) if (fabs(Xb[k][k]) > rankTol) ++rank;

    if (rank < p)
        throw runtime_error(
            "QRRegressor::fit: " + to_string(p - rank) +
            " R diagonal entr" + (p - rank == 1 ? "y" : "ies") +
            " below rank tolerance; design is numerically rank-deficient. "
            "Use SVDRegressor for a min-norm solution.");

    double minAbs = fabs(Xb[0][0]);
    for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) minAbs = min(minAbs, fabs(Xb[k][k]));
    condEst = (minAbs > 0.0) ? maxAbs / minAbs
                              : numeric_limits<double>::infinity();

    // Back-substitute R * beta = (Q^T y)[0:p].
    vector<double> beta(p, 0.0);
    for (int k = p-1; k >= 0; --k) {
        double s = rhs[k];
        for (int j = k+1; j < p; ++j) s -= Xb[k][j] * beta[j];
        beta[k] = s / Xb[k][k];
        if (!isfinite(beta[k]))
            throw runtime_error(
                "QRRegressor::fit: back-substitution produced a non-finite coefficient");
    }

    intercept = beta[0];
    coef.assign(beta.begin()+1, beta.end());
    nFeatures = d;
    fitted = true;
}

    One-Sided Jacobi SVD

    The singular value decomposition $X_b = USV^\top$ factors the $n \times p$ augmented matrix into an orthogonal $U$ (columns are the left singular vectors), a diagonal $S$ with nonnegative entries (singular values in some order), and an orthogonal $V$ (columns are the right singular vectors). The least-squares solution, including a minimum-norm solution for rank-deficient problems, is the pseudoinverse:

    $$\beta = V S^+ U^\top y = \sum_{k: \sigma_k > \text{tol}} \frac{u_k^\top y}{\sigma_k} v_k,$$

    where $S^+$ replaces each nonzero singular value with its reciprocal and leaves the zero (or near-zero) singular values at zero. This truncated form is the minimum-norm least-squares solution: among all $\beta$ that minimize $\lVert y - X_b\beta \rVert_2$, it selects the one with the smallest $\lVert \beta \rVert_2$. Singular values below a tolerance are omitted from the sum, and their corresponding directions in $\mathbb{R}^p$ (the null space of $X_b$) are not constrained by the data.

    The Jacobi Iteration on Columns of $X_b$

    One-sided Jacobi SVD never forms $X_b^\top X_b$. Instead it applies plane rotations directly to the columns of a working copy of $X_b$ and accumulates the same rotations in $V$, driving the columns of the working matrix toward orthogonality. Once converged, the column norms equal the singular values and the normalized columns are the left singular vectors $U$.

    At each iteration step the algorithm selects a pair of columns $(i, j)$ and computes the inner products of the Gram matrix fragment: $G_{ii} = \lVert a_i \rVert^2$, $G_{jj} = \lVert a_j \rVert^2$, and $G_{ij} = a_i^\top a_j$ where $a_i, a_j$ are columns $i$ and $j$ of the current working matrix. If $|G_{ij}| / \sqrt{G_{ii} G_{jj}}$ is already below a tight tolerance, the pair is skipped as already orthogonal. Otherwise the Jacobi rotation angle is

    $$\zeta = \frac{G_{jj} - G_{ii}}{2 G_{ij}}, \qquad t = \frac{\operatorname{sign}(\zeta)}{|\zeta| + \sqrt{1 + \zeta^2}}, \qquad c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+t^2}}, \quad s = tc,$$

    and the rotation is applied to both the working matrix and $V$:

    $$a_i \leftarrow c\,a_i - s\,a_j, \qquad a_j \leftarrow s\,a_i + c\,a_j, \qquad V_{:,i} \leftarrow c\,V_{:,i} - s\,V_{:,j}, \qquad V_{:,j} \leftarrow s\,V_{:,i} + c\,V_{:,j}.$$

    The algorithm sweeps over all $\binom{p}{2}$ column pairs, repeating until no pair triggers a rotation. Convergence is typically reached in a handful of sweeps for the small $p$ values common in regression problems. Each pair costs $O(n)$ to measure and rotate the working columns plus $O(p)$ to rotate $V$, so one full sweep costs $O(np^2 + p^3)$. In the tall-matrix regime $n \ge p$, this is $O(np^2)$, and $s$ sweeps cost $O(snp^2)$.

    Rank, Tolerance, and Min-Norm

    After convergence the singular values are extracted as $\sigma_k = \lVert a_k \rVert_2$ (the column norms of the working matrix), then sorted in descending order along with the corresponding columns of $V$. A rank tolerance is set as

    $$\text{tol} = \sigma_0 \cdot \max(n,p) \cdot \epsilon_{\text{mach}} \cdot 4,$$

    where $\sigma_0$ is the largest singular value. Singular values below this threshold are treated as numerically zero, and the effective rank is the count of values above it. The condition estimate is $\sigma_0 / \sigma_{r-1}$ where $r$ is the effective rank. For a full-rank design this equals the true condition number of $X_b$; for a rank-deficient design it measures the conditioning within the identifiable subspace.

    The regression coefficients are then

    $$\beta_j = \sum_{k=0}^{r-1} \frac{a_k^\top y}{\sigma_k^2} \cdot V_{jk},$$

    where $a_k^\top y / \sigma_k$ plays the role of $u_k^\top y$ (the normalized dot product) and the extra $1/\sigma_k$ factor comes from the pseudoinverse. The first element of $\beta$ is the intercept; the remaining $d$ elements are the feature coefficients.

    SVD Regressor Implementation

    struct SVDRegressor {
    bool fitted = false;
    int nFeatures = 0;
    double intercept = 0.0;
    vector<double> coef;
    int rank = 0;
    double condEst = 0.0;
    double singularTol = 0.0;  // 0 = auto from data scale

    void fit(const Matrix& X, const vector<double>& y) {
        fitted = false;
        validateFitInput(X, y, "SVDRegressor");
        if (!isfinite(singularTol) || singularTol < 0.0)
            throw invalid_argument(
                "SVDRegressor::fit: singularTol must be finite and non-negative");
        int n = (int)X.size(), d = (int)X[0].size(), p = d + 1;

        Matrix A(n, vector<double>(p));
        for (int i = 0; i < n; ++i) {
            A[i][0] = 1.0;
            for (int j = 0; j < d; ++j) A[i][j+1] = X[i][j];
        }
        Matrix V(p, vector<double>(p, 0.0));
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) V[k][k] = 1.0;  // V = I_p

        const double eps = numeric_limits<double>::epsilon();
        const int maxSweeps = 100;
        bool changed = true;
        int sweep = 0;
        for (; sweep < maxSweeps && changed; ++sweep) {
            changed = false;
            for (int i = 0; i < p-1; ++i) {
                for (int j = i+1; j < p; ++j) {
                    double Gii = 0.0, Gjj = 0.0, Gij = 0.0;
                    for (int row = 0; row < n; ++row) {
                        Gii += A[row][i] * A[row][i];
                        Gjj += A[row][j] * A[row][j];
                        Gij += A[row][i] * A[row][j];
                    }
                    if (!isfinite(Gii) || !isfinite(Gjj) || !isfinite(Gij))
                        throw runtime_error(
                            "SVDRegressor::fit: Jacobi inner product became non-finite; "
                            "rescale the input");
                    double denom = sqrt(Gii) * sqrt(Gjj);
                    if (denom == 0.0 || fabs(Gij) / denom < eps * 4.0) continue;

                    double zeta = (Gjj - Gii) / (2.0 * Gij);
                    double azeta = fabs(zeta), t;
                    if (azeta > 1.0) {
                        double inv = 1.0 / azeta;
                        t = copysign(inv / (1.0 + hypot(1.0, inv)), zeta);
                    } else {
                        t = copysign(1.0 / (azeta + hypot(1.0, azeta)), zeta);
                    }
                    if (t == 0.0) continue;
                    double c = 1.0 / sqrt(1.0 + t*t);
                    double s = t * c;

                    for (int row = 0; row < n; ++row) {
                        double ai = A[row][i], aj = A[row][j];
                        A[row][i] = c*ai - s*aj;
                        A[row][j] = s*ai + c*aj;
                    }
                    for (int row = 0; row < p; ++row) {
                        double vi = V[row][i], vj = V[row][j];
                        V[row][i] = c*vi - s*vj;
                        V[row][j] = s*vi + c*vj;
                    }
                    changed = true;
                }
            }
        }
        if (changed)
            throw runtime_error(
                "SVDRegressor::fit: Jacobi iteration did not converge in " +
                to_string(maxSweeps) + " sweeps");

        // Extract and sort singular values.
        vector<double> S(p);
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) {
            double s2 = 0.0;
            for (int row = 0; row < n; ++row) s2 += A[row][k] * A[row][k];
            if (!isfinite(s2))
                throw runtime_error(
                    "SVDRegressor::fit: singular-value norm became non-finite");
            S[k] = sqrt(s2);
        }
        vector<int> order(p);
        iota(order.begin(), order.end(), 0);
        sort(order.begin(), order.end(), [&](int a, int b){ return S[a] > S[b]; });

        vector<double> Ss(p);
        Matrix As(n, vector<double>(p));
        Matrix Vs(p, vector<double>(p));
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) {
            Ss[k] = S[order[k]];
            for (int row = 0; row < n; ++row) As[row][k] = A[row][order[k]];
            for (int row = 0; row < p; ++row) Vs[row][k] = V[row][order[k]];
        }

        double tol = singularTol > 0.0
                     ? singularTol
                     : Ss[0] * (double)max(n, p) * eps * 4.0;
        rank = 0;
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) if (Ss[k] > tol) ++rank;

        if (rank == 0)
            throw runtime_error(
                "SVDRegressor::fit: all singular values are below tolerance; "
                "check input for near-zero or identical features");

        condEst = Ss[0] / Ss[rank-1];

        // beta = sum_{k < rank} (A[:,k] . y / S_k^2) * V[:,k]
        vector<double> beta(p, 0.0);
        for (int k = 0; k < rank; ++k) {
            double coeff = 0.0;
            for (int row = 0; row < n; ++row) coeff += As[row][k] * y[row];
            coeff /= (Ss[k] * Ss[k]);
            for (int row = 0; row < p; ++row) beta[row] += coeff * Vs[row][k];
        }
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k)
            if (!isfinite(beta[k]))
                throw runtime_error(
                    "SVDRegressor::fit: pseudoinverse produced a non-finite coefficient");

        intercept = beta[0];
        coef.assign(beta.begin()+1, beta.end());
        nFeatures = d;
        fitted = true;
    }
    // predict overloads identical to QRRegressor
};

    Validation and Errors

    Both solvers share a validation helper that checks input shapes and finiteness before any computation begins, matching the convention from the OLS post:

    • Row count mismatch between X and y throws std::invalid_argument.
    • An empty training set, zero feature columns, ragged rows, or non-finite entries all throw std::invalid_argument with the row index named where relevant.
    • Fewer than $p = d{+}1$ rows throws std::invalid_argument before any reflector or rotation is computed.
    • QRRegressor throws std::runtime_error if any Householder sub-column norm is numerically zero (immediately rank-deficient) or if the post-factorization rank check detects near-linear dependence.
    • SVDRegressor rejects a negative or non-finite custom tolerance and throws std::runtime_error if Jacobi iteration misses its sweep cap or all singular values fall below tolerance.
    • Calling predict before fit throws std::logic_error; a feature-count mismatch or non-finite feature throws std::invalid_argument.

    Three-Scenario Comparison

    The complete program below runs both solvers against three designs on the same synthetic true model $y = 3 + 2x_1 - 1.5x_2 + \varepsilon$ (same seed and split as the OLS post). Scenario A is well-conditioned independent features. Scenario B has $x_2 \approx x_1$, introducing near-collinearity. Scenario C has $x_2 = 2x_1$ exactly, making the design rank-deficient. The output shows that QR and SVD agree with OLS on Scenario A, both flag the condition inflation in Scenario B, QR throws on Scenario C while SVD computes the minimum-norm solution.

    #include <algorithm>
#include <cmath>
#include <cstdio>
#include <limits>
#include <numeric>
#include <random>
#include <stdexcept>
#include <string>
#include <vector>
using namespace std;
using Matrix = vector<vector<double>>;

// ============================================================
// Input validation helper shared by both solvers.
// ============================================================
static void validateFitInput(const Matrix& X, const vector<double>& y,
                              const string& who) {
    if (X.size() != y.size())
        throw invalid_argument(who + "::fit: X has " + to_string(X.size()) +
                               " rows but y has " + to_string(y.size()) + " entries");
    int n = (int)X.size();
    if (n == 0) throw invalid_argument(who + "::fit: empty training set");
    int d = (int)X[0].size();
    if (d == 0) throw invalid_argument(who + "::fit: zero feature columns");
    for (int i = 0; i < n; ++i) {
        if ((int)X[i].size() != d)
            throw invalid_argument(who + "::fit: row " + to_string(i) +
                                   " has " + to_string(X[i].size()) +
                                   " columns, expected " + to_string(d));
        for (int j = 0; j < d; ++j)
            if (!isfinite(X[i][j]))
                throw invalid_argument(who + "::fit: non-finite feature in row " + to_string(i));
        if (!isfinite(y[i]))
            throw invalid_argument(who + "::fit: non-finite target at row " + to_string(i));
    }
    if (n < d + 1)
        throw invalid_argument(who + "::fit: need at least " + to_string(d + 1) +
                               " rows for " + to_string(d) + " features plus intercept, got " +
                               to_string(n));
}

// ============================================================
// Metric helpers.
// ============================================================
static void validateMetricInputs(const vector<double>& yTrue,
                                  const vector<double>& yPred,
                                  const string& name) {
    if (yTrue.size() != yPred.size())
        throw invalid_argument(name + ": size mismatch");
    if (yTrue.empty())
        throw invalid_argument(name + ": vectors must be non-empty");
    for (size_t i = 0; i < yTrue.size(); ++i)
        if (!isfinite(yTrue[i]) || !isfinite(yPred[i]))
            throw invalid_argument(name + ": non-finite value at index " + to_string(i));
}

static double mse(const vector<double>& yTrue, const vector<double>& yPred) {
    validateMetricInputs(yTrue, yPred, "mse");
    double s = 0.0;
    for (size_t i = 0; i < yTrue.size(); ++i) { double e = yTrue[i]-yPred[i]; s += e*e; }
    return s / yTrue.size();
}

static double rmse(const vector<double>& yTrue, const vector<double>& yPred) {
    return sqrt(mse(yTrue, yPred));
}

static double r2(const vector<double>& yTrue, const vector<double>& yPred) {
    validateMetricInputs(yTrue, yPred, "r2");
    double meanY = accumulate(yTrue.begin(), yTrue.end(), 0.0) / yTrue.size();
    double ssRes = 0.0, ssTot = 0.0;
    for (size_t i = 0; i < yTrue.size(); ++i) {
        ssRes += (yTrue[i]-yPred[i])*(yTrue[i]-yPred[i]);
        ssTot += (yTrue[i]-meanY)*(yTrue[i]-meanY);
    }
    if (ssTot == 0.0)
        throw invalid_argument("r2: target values are constant, so R^2 is undefined");
    return 1.0 - ssRes / ssTot;
}

// ============================================================
// QRRegressor: Householder triangularization on X_b directly.
// ============================================================
struct QRRegressor {
    bool   fitted   = false;
    int    nFeatures = 0;
    double intercept = 0.0;
    vector<double> coef;
    int    rank    = 0;
    double condEst = 0.0;

    void fit(const Matrix& X, const vector<double>& y) {
        fitted = false;
        validateFitInput(X, y, "QRRegressor");
        int n = (int)X.size(), d = (int)X[0].size(), p = d + 1;

        // Build augmented matrix X_b (intercept column first).
        Matrix Xb(n, vector<double>(p));
        for (int i = 0; i < n; ++i) {
            Xb[i][0] = 1.0;
            for (int j = 0; j < d; ++j) Xb[i][j+1] = X[i][j];
        }
        vector<double> rhs = y;  // accumulates Q^T y in-place

        const double eps = numeric_limits<double>::epsilon();

        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) {
            // Compute norm of sub-column Xb[k:n][k].
            double sigma2 = 0.0;
            for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) sigma2 += Xb[i][k] * Xb[i][k];
            if (!isfinite(sigma2))
                throw runtime_error(
                    "QRRegressor::fit: column norm became non-finite; rescale the input");
            double sigma = sqrt(sigma2);

            if (sigma < eps * 64.0)
                throw runtime_error(
                    "QRRegressor::fit: column " + to_string(k) +
                    " is linearly dependent; design is rank-deficient. "
                    "Use SVDRegressor for a min-norm solution.");

            // Householder reflector: choose sign to avoid cancellation.
            double alpha = -copysign(sigma, Xb[k][k]);
            vector<double> v(n - k);
            for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) v[i-k] = Xb[i][k];
            v[0] -= alpha;

            double vTv = 0.0;
            for (double vi : v) vTv += vi * vi;
            if (!isfinite(vTv))
                throw runtime_error(
                    "QRRegressor::fit: reflector norm became non-finite; rescale the input");
            if (vTv < eps * eps) continue;

            // Apply H = I - 2vv^T/v^Tv to columns k..p-1 of Xb.
            for (int j = k; j < p; ++j) {
                double vTx = 0.0;
                for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) vTx += v[i-k] * Xb[i][j];
                double tau = 2.0 * vTx / vTv;
                for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) Xb[i][j] -= tau * v[i-k];
            }
            // Apply same reflector to rhs (accumulates Q^T y).
            double vTr = 0.0;
            for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) vTr += v[i-k] * rhs[i];
            double tau = 2.0 * vTr / vTv;
            for (int i = k; i < n; ++i) rhs[i] -= tau * v[i-k];
        }

        // Rank check via R diagonal magnitudes.
        double maxAbs = 0.0;
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) maxAbs = max(maxAbs, fabs(Xb[k][k]));
        double rankTol = maxAbs * (double)p * eps * 64.0;
        rank = 0;
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) if (fabs(Xb[k][k]) > rankTol) ++rank;

        if (rank < p)
            throw runtime_error(
                "QRRegressor::fit: " + to_string(p - rank) +
                " R diagonal entr" + (p - rank == 1 ? "y" : "ies") +
                " below rank tolerance; design is numerically rank-deficient. "
                "Use SVDRegressor for a min-norm solution.");

        double minAbs = fabs(Xb[0][0]);
        for (int k = 1; k < p; ++k) minAbs = min(minAbs, fabs(Xb[k][k]));
        condEst = (minAbs > 0.0) ? maxAbs / minAbs
                                  : numeric_limits<double>::infinity();

        // Back-substitute R * beta = (Q^T y)[0:p].
        vector<double> beta(p, 0.0);
        for (int k = p-1; k >= 0; --k) {
            double s = rhs[k];
            for (int j = k+1; j < p; ++j) s -= Xb[k][j] * beta[j];
            beta[k] = s / Xb[k][k];
            if (!isfinite(beta[k]))
                throw runtime_error(
                    "QRRegressor::fit: back-substitution produced a non-finite coefficient");
        }

        intercept = beta[0];
        coef.assign(beta.begin()+1, beta.end());
        nFeatures = d;
        fitted = true;
    }

    double predict(const vector<double>& x) const {
        if (!fitted)
            throw logic_error("QRRegressor::predict: model is not fitted");
        if ((int)x.size() != nFeatures)
            throw invalid_argument("QRRegressor::predict: expected " +
                                   to_string(nFeatures) + " features, got " +
                                   to_string(x.size()));
        double out = intercept;
        for (int j = 0; j < nFeatures; ++j) {
            if (!isfinite(x[j]))
                throw invalid_argument(
                    "QRRegressor::predict: non-finite feature at index " +
                    to_string(j));
            out += coef[j] * x[j];
        }
        if (!isfinite(out))
            throw runtime_error("QRRegressor::predict: prediction became non-finite");
        return out;
    }

    vector<double> predict(const Matrix& X) const {
        vector<double> out; out.reserve(X.size());
        for (const auto& row : X) out.push_back(predict(row));
        return out;
    }
};

// ============================================================
// SVDRegressor: one-sided Jacobi SVD directly on X_b.
// ============================================================
struct SVDRegressor {
    bool   fitted      = false;
    int    nFeatures   = 0;
    double intercept   = 0.0;
    vector<double> coef;
    int    rank        = 0;
    double condEst     = 0.0;
    double singularTol = 0.0;  // 0 = auto from data scale

    void fit(const Matrix& X, const vector<double>& y) {
        fitted = false;
        validateFitInput(X, y, "SVDRegressor");
        if (!isfinite(singularTol) || singularTol < 0.0)
            throw invalid_argument(
                "SVDRegressor::fit: singularTol must be finite and non-negative");
        int n = (int)X.size(), d = (int)X[0].size(), p = d + 1;

        // Build working copy of X_b.
        Matrix A(n, vector<double>(p));
        for (int i = 0; i < n; ++i) {
            A[i][0] = 1.0;
            for (int j = 0; j < d; ++j) A[i][j+1] = X[i][j];
        }
        // V starts as identity.
        Matrix V(p, vector<double>(p, 0.0));
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) V[k][k] = 1.0;

        const double eps = numeric_limits<double>::epsilon();
        const int maxSweeps = 100;
        bool changed = true;
        int sweep = 0;
        for (; sweep < maxSweeps && changed; ++sweep) {
            changed = false;
            for (int i = 0; i < p-1; ++i) {
                for (int j = i+1; j < p; ++j) {
                    double Gii = 0.0, Gjj = 0.0, Gij = 0.0;
                    for (int row = 0; row < n; ++row) {
                        Gii += A[row][i] * A[row][i];
                        Gjj += A[row][j] * A[row][j];
                        Gij += A[row][i] * A[row][j];
                    }
                    if (!isfinite(Gii) || !isfinite(Gjj) || !isfinite(Gij))
                        throw runtime_error(
                            "SVDRegressor::fit: Jacobi inner product became non-finite; "
                            "rescale the input");
                    double denom = sqrt(Gii) * sqrt(Gjj);
                    if (denom == 0.0 || fabs(Gij) / denom < eps * 4.0) continue;

                    double zeta = (Gjj - Gii) / (2.0 * Gij);
                    double azeta = fabs(zeta), t;
                    if (azeta > 1.0) {
                        double inv = 1.0 / azeta;
                        t = copysign(inv / (1.0 + hypot(1.0, inv)), zeta);
                    } else {
                        t = copysign(1.0 / (azeta + hypot(1.0, azeta)), zeta);
                    }
                    if (t == 0.0) continue;
                    double c = 1.0 / sqrt(1.0 + t*t);
                    double s = t * c;

                    for (int row = 0; row < n; ++row) {
                        double ai = A[row][i], aj = A[row][j];
                        A[row][i] = c*ai - s*aj;
                        A[row][j] = s*ai + c*aj;
                    }
                    for (int row = 0; row < p; ++row) {
                        double vi = V[row][i], vj = V[row][j];
                        V[row][i] = c*vi - s*vj;
                        V[row][j] = s*vi + c*vj;
                    }
                    changed = true;
                }
            }
        }
        if (changed)
            throw runtime_error(
                "SVDRegressor::fit: Jacobi iteration did not converge in " +
                to_string(maxSweeps) + " sweeps");

        // Extract singular values (column norms of A after convergence).
        vector<double> S(p);
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) {
            double s2 = 0.0;
            for (int row = 0; row < n; ++row) s2 += A[row][k] * A[row][k];
            if (!isfinite(s2))
                throw runtime_error(
                    "SVDRegressor::fit: singular-value norm became non-finite");
            S[k] = sqrt(s2);
        }

        // Sort singular triplets in descending order.
        vector<int> order(p);
        iota(order.begin(), order.end(), 0);
        sort(order.begin(), order.end(), [&](int a, int b){ return S[a] > S[b]; });

        vector<double> Ss(p);
        Matrix As(n, vector<double>(p));
        Matrix Vs(p, vector<double>(p));
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) {
            Ss[k] = S[order[k]];
            for (int row = 0; row < n; ++row) As[row][k] = A[row][order[k]];
            for (int row = 0; row < p; ++row) Vs[row][k] = V[row][order[k]];
        }

        double tol = singularTol > 0.0
                     ? singularTol
                     : Ss[0] * (double)max(n, p) * eps * 4.0;
        rank = 0;
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k) if (Ss[k] > tol) ++rank;

        if (rank == 0)
            throw runtime_error(
                "SVDRegressor::fit: all singular values are below tolerance; "
                "check input for near-zero or identical features");

        condEst = Ss[0] / Ss[rank-1];

        // beta = sum_{k < rank} (As[:,k] . y / Ss[k]^2) * Vs[:,k]
        vector<double> beta(p, 0.0);
        for (int k = 0; k < rank; ++k) {
            double coeff = 0.0;
            for (int row = 0; row < n; ++row) coeff += As[row][k] * y[row];
            coeff /= (Ss[k] * Ss[k]);
            for (int row = 0; row < p; ++row) beta[row] += coeff * Vs[row][k];
        }
        for (int k = 0; k < p; ++k)
            if (!isfinite(beta[k]))
                throw runtime_error(
                    "SVDRegressor::fit: pseudoinverse produced a non-finite coefficient");

        intercept = beta[0];
        coef.assign(beta.begin()+1, beta.end());
        nFeatures = d;
        fitted = true;
    }

    double predict(const vector<double>& x) const {
        if (!fitted)
            throw logic_error("SVDRegressor::predict: model is not fitted");
        if ((int)x.size() != nFeatures)
            throw invalid_argument("SVDRegressor::predict: expected " +
                                   to_string(nFeatures) + " features, got " +
                                   to_string(x.size()));
        double out = intercept;
        for (int j = 0; j < nFeatures; ++j) {
            if (!isfinite(x[j]))
                throw invalid_argument(
                    "SVDRegressor::predict: non-finite feature at index " +
                    to_string(j));
            out += coef[j] * x[j];
        }
        if (!isfinite(out))
            throw runtime_error("SVDRegressor::predict: prediction became non-finite");
        return out;
    }

    vector<double> predict(const Matrix& X) const {
        vector<double> out; out.reserve(X.size());
        for (const auto& row : X) out.push_back(predict(row));
        return out;
    }
};

    When to Use Each Solver

    SituationRecommended solverReason
    Well-scaled features, no severe collinearity, modest $d$OLS (normal equations)Cheapest; $O(d^3)$ solve after $O(nd^2)$ assembly; no extra $O(np)$ allocation.
    Correlated or poorly scaled features, $\operatorname{cond}(X_b) > 10^4$QR (Householder)Works on $X_b$ directly, so condition is not squared. Same $O(np^2)$ cost.
    Rank-deficient design or unknown rankSVD (one-sided Jacobi)Provides minimum-norm solution and explicit rank determination via singular values.
    $n$ or $d$ is very large, or an existing fit needs incremental updatesGradient descent (Part 3)Replaces factorization with $O(Bp)$ batch updates. The teaching batch fit stores standardized data; its frozen-scaler online path uses $O(p)$ work per new sample after the initial fit.
    Production code, performance-criticalEigen or LAPACK backendTuned BLAS kernels with SIMD, cache blocking, and parallelism; see note below.
    Eigen and LAPACK in production. The implementations here are written to be readable and self-contained, not to match the performance of a tuned numerical library. Eigen's HouseholderQR and JacobiSVD (or LAPACK's dgeqrf and dgesdd) use tuned kernels, cache-blocking, and SIMD, achieving much higher throughput on large matrices. The implementations in this post expose the same QR, singular-value, rank-tolerance, and pseudoinverse concepts, while production routines may use different internal SVD algorithms and workspace strategies.

    StepQR timeSVD timeExtra memory

    Both solvers are $O(np^2)$ in the regime $n \gg p$, which matches the normal-equation path once the $O(nd^2)$ assembly step is included. The extra $O(np)$ allocation for $X_b$ is the main additional cost over the OLS approach, which avoided materializing $X_b$ at all.

    Limitations and Edge Cases

    • QR without pivoting and near-rank-deficiency. For near-zero but not exactly zero sub-columns, the Householder sigma check may not fire, and the rank check on $|R_{kk}|$ may also not fire if the small diagonal entry is above the tolerance. In this case the back-substitution will divide by a very small number and produce a coefficient vector with large, nearly-cancelling entries. Column-pivoting QR (not implemented here) reorders columns to bring the most informative directions first, guaranteeing that the largest available pivot is always used; this is the standard fix for near-rank-deficient designs in a QR context.
    • SVD sweep count. For $p$ up to about $20$ the one-sided Jacobi algorithm typically converges in $5{-}10$ sweeps. For larger $p$ the convergence can slow, though it is always guaranteed for exact arithmetic. The cap of 100 sweeps is generous for any regression problem with double-precision data.
    • Sign ambiguity in singular vectors. The sign of each column of $U$ and $V$ is arbitrary (flipping both leaves the decomposition valid). This does not affect the coefficients $\beta$ because the sign cancels in the pseudoinverse product, but it means the singular vector columns themselves should not be compared across runs without sign normalization.
    • Min-norm vs other regularized solutions. The SVD minimum-norm solution is not the same as a ridge-regularized solution, which also produces a unique answer for rank-deficient designs but shrinks all coefficients toward zero rather than minimizing their Euclidean norm. The regularized regression post covers ridge and how it modifies the singular-value spectrum.
    • Duplicate or constant feature columns. A column of all ones in the caller's $X$ duplicates the implicit intercept column; a constant feature column becomes linearly dependent on the intercept. Both cause rank deficiency. QR throws; SVD returns a min-norm fit but the resulting individual coefficients are not separately interpretable.
