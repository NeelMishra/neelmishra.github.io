FLOPS Calculation for Transformer Models

The foundational equation for estimating the compute required to train a dense transformer model is the 6ND approximation, introduced by Kaplan et al. (2020) and refined by Hoffmann et al. (2022). It states that the total floating-point operations for a single training run is approximately:

C ≈ 6 × N × D

where N is the number of model parameters and D is the number of training tokens. The factor of 6 arises from two passes through the network per token:

  • Forward pass: ~2N FLOPS per token. Each parameter participates in exactly one multiply-add operation during inference, yielding 2 FLOPS per parameter (one multiply, one add).
  • Backward pass: ~4N FLOPS per token. The gradient computation requires approximately 2× the forward pass: one pass to compute gradients with respect to activations, and another to compute gradients with respect to weights.

Thus: C_total = (2N + 4N) × D = 6ND.

Derivation detail: For a single transformer layer with hidden dimension d and an MLP with expansion ratio 4, the per-token FLOP count for the forward pass breaks down as: Self-attention Q/K/V projections = 3 × 2d² FLOPS, attention output projection = 2d², MLP up-projection = 2 × 4d² = 8d², down-projection = 8d². Total per layer forward = 6d² + 16d² = 24d² ≈ 2 × 12d² = 2N_layer. Summed across L layers, this confirms the ~2N approximation for the forward pass.

For Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models, the formula adjusts because only a fraction of parameters are activated per token. If the model has E total experts but routes to k per token, the effective parameter count becomes:

N_eff = N_attention + (k / E) × N_ffn

For example, Mixtral 8×7B activates 2 of 8 experts per token, giving N_eff ≈ 12.9B active parameters despite having ~46.7B total parameters. The training FLOPS becomes C ≈ 6 × N_eff × D.

Caution: The 6ND formula does not account for activation recomputation (gradient checkpointing), which can add 33–50% overhead to the forward FLOPS. When using full activation recomputation, use C ≈ 8ND as a more accurate estimate. Additionally, communication overhead in distributed training (AllReduce, pipeline bubble) is not captured by this formula.

GPU-Hours Estimation Methodology

Once you have the total FLOPS C, the next step is converting to GPU-hours. The key formula is:

GPU_hours = C / (GPU_FLOPS × MFU × 3600)

where GPU_FLOPS is the peak theoretical throughput of the accelerator and MFU (Model FLOPS Utilization) is the fraction of peak throughput actually achieved during training.

Realistic MFU Values

Achieving high MFU is one of the hardest engineering challenges in LLM training. Typical values observed in production:

  • Single-node (1–8 GPUs): 45–55% MFU with optimized FSDP or tensor parallelism.
  • Multi-node (64–512 GPUs): 35–45% MFU. Pipeline parallelism bubbles, gradient AllReduce latency, and load imbalance reduce efficiency.
  • Large-scale (1024+ GPUs): 30–40% MFU. Meta reported ~38% MFU for Llama 3 405B on 16,384 H100s. Google achieved ~46% MFU for PaLM 540B on TPU v4 pods.
Worked example: Training a 70B parameter model on 2T tokens using H100 GPUs at 40% MFU.
C = 6 × 70×10⁹ × 2×10¹² = 8.4×10²³ FLOPS.
GPU-hours = 8.4×10²³ / (989×10¹² × 0.40 × 3600) = 8.4×10²³ / 1.424×10¹⁸ ≈ 589,888 GPU-hours.
With 2,048 GPUs: wall-clock time ≈ 589,888 / 2,048 ≈ 288 hours ≈ 12 days.

Spot vs On-Demand Pricing Strategies

Spot/preemptible instances offer 60–90% discounts but introduce the risk of interruption. Effective LLM training on spot requires robust checkpointing infrastructure.

Spot Pricing Ranges

  • AWS Spot p5.48xlarge: ~$29–$49/hr (50–70% discount), but capacity is extremely scarce in most regions. Interruption frequency: 5–15% per hour in us-east-1.
  • GCP Preemptible a3-highgpu: ~$30–$40/hr (~60% discount), maximum 24-hour lifetime, then force-terminated.
  • Azure Low-Priority ND96: ~$29–$39/hr (~60% discount), evicted with 30-second notice when capacity is needed.

Checkpointing Strategy for Spot Training

The expected cost of a preemption event depends on checkpoint frequency and restart overhead:

E[wasted_compute] = (checkpoint_interval / 2) × num_gpus × gpu_cost_per_hour

For a 2,048-GPU cluster at $3/GPU/hr with 15-minute checkpoint intervals, each preemption wastes on average 7.5 minutes of compute = 2,048 × $3 × (7.5/60) = $768 per preemption event. With a 10% hourly preemption rate over a 12-day run, expect ~29 preemptions costing ~$22K in wasted compute — still far less than the ~$4M saved by using spot pricing.

Warning: Checkpointing a 70B model with optimizer states (Adam: 2× model size for m and v) requires writing ~560GB per checkpoint (model in BF16 = 140GB, optimizer in FP32 = 2×280GB). At 10 GB/s sustained write throughput to cloud storage, each checkpoint takes ~56 seconds. With asynchronous checkpointing, training can overlap checkpoint I/O, reducing the effective overhead to near zero — but you need sufficient CPU memory to buffer the snapshot.

Hybrid Strategy

The optimal approach for most organizations is a hybrid fleet: maintain a reserved/on-demand baseline (enough GPUs to make steady progress) and burst onto spot instances when available. Target 30–40% of your cluster as reserved for guaranteed progress, with the remainder as spot capacity.

Total Cost of Ownership Analysis

Compute is the dominant cost, but a complete training budget must account for several additional categories. The following SVG illustrates the typical cost breakdown for a large-scale LLM training run:

Component Deep-Dive

  • Compute (70%): GPU/TPU rental or amortized hardware cost. This is the single largest line item and scales linearly with model size, dataset size, and inversely with MFU.
  • Storage & I/O (10%): Training data storage (S3/GCS), checkpoint storage (each 70B checkpoint ≈ 560GB with optimizer states), and the throughput-optimized storage layer (e.g., AWS FSx for Lustre at ~$0.14/GB-month + $0.000025/request).
  • Networking (5%): Cross-node communication via InfiniBand/RoCE. On AWS, Elastic Fabric Adapter (EFA) is included with p5 instances, but data transfer across availability zones costs $0.01/GB — with AllReduce traffic of ~100TB over a training run, this adds up.
  • Engineering (10%): A team of 4–8 senior ML engineers × 3–6 months at $200K+ annualized fully-loaded cost. This includes infrastructure engineering, training recipe development, debugging loss spikes, and hyperparameter tuning.
  • Data curation (5%): Web scraping infrastructure, deduplication pipelines (MinHash/SimHash), quality filtering models, PII removal, and human annotation for alignment data.

Chinchilla Scaling Laws for Optimal Compute Allocation

The Chinchilla paper (Hoffmann et al., 2022) established that for a given compute budget C, there exists an optimal pairing of model size N and token count D that minimizes the training loss. The key findings:

The Core Relationship

The loss function is modeled as:

L(N, D) = E + A / Nα + B / Dβ

where E is the irreducible entropy of the data, and the fitted exponents are approximately α ≈ 0.34, β ≈ 0.28, A ≈ 406.4, B ≈ 410.7. The optimal allocation under a fixed compute budget C = 6ND yields:

N_opt ∝ C0.50,   D_opt ∝ C0.50

This implies model size and training tokens should scale roughly equally with compute budget, leading to the famous ~20 tokens per parameter rule:

D_opt ≈ 20 × N_opt

Practical impact: Chinchilla (70B, 1.4T tokens) matched the performance of Gopher (280B, 300B tokens) while using 4× fewer FLOPS at inference time. This showed that GPT-3 (175B, 300B tokens) was dramatically undertrained — Chinchilla-optimal training for 175B would require ~3.5T tokens.

Beyond Chinchilla: Over-Training

In practice, frontier labs intentionally over-train smaller models beyond the Chinchilla-optimal point because inference cost (proportional to N) dominates deployment cost. The Llama approach:

  • Llama 2 7B: Trained on 2T tokens (Chinchilla-optimal: ~140B tokens → 14× over-trained)
  • Llama 3 8B: Trained on 15T tokens (~190× over-trained relative to Chinchilla)
  • Llama 3 70B: Trained on 15T tokens (~10× over-trained)

The rationale is that an over-trained smaller model achieves comparable quality to a Chinchilla-optimal larger model, but costs far less to serve at scale. The additional training compute is a one-time cost, while inference savings compound with every query.

Scaling Law for Compute-Optimal Budgeting

Given a target model quality (measured by validation loss L_target), you can estimate the minimum compute required:

C_min ≈ 6 × (A / (L_target - E))1/α × 20 × (A / (L_target - E))1/α

This lets you work backwards from a desired capability level to a dollar budget, which is critical for organizational planning.

Detailed Cost Breakdown Components

A rigorous cost estimate must decompose every material line item. Below is a comprehensive framework used by ML infrastructure teams at frontier labs:

1. Compute Costs

  • Base GPU/TPU hours: cost_compute = GPU_hours × price_per_GPU_hour
  • Hyperparameter search: Budget 10–20% additional compute for learning rate sweeps, batch size tuning, and architecture ablations. Use μP (Maximal Update Parameterization) to transfer hyperparameters from small proxy models to reduce this overhead.
  • Failed runs / loss spikes: Budget 5–15% for runs that diverge or hit loss spikes requiring rollback. The Llama 3 405B training experienced multiple interventions requiring checkpoint rollback and hyperparameter adjustment.
  • Evaluation runs: Periodic eval on benchmarks (MMLU, HumanEval, etc.) — typically 1–3% of total compute.

2. Storage Costs

  • Training data: A 15T-token dataset stored as tokenized sequences at 2 bytes/token ≈ 30TB. On S3 Standard: ~$690/month.
  • Checkpoints: Saving every 1,000 steps for a 70B model: ~560GB per checkpoint × ~150 checkpoints = 84TB. On S3 Infrequent Access: ~$1,050/month. Keep the last 5–10 checkpoints in hot storage, archive the rest.
  • High-performance scratch: For data loading throughput, you need a parallel filesystem. AWS FSx for Lustre: 50TB at $0.14/GB-month ≈ $7,000/month with 1+ GB/s/TiB throughput.

3. Networking Costs

  • Intra-cluster: NVLink/NVSwitch within a node is free. Cross-node InfiniBand/EFA is typically bundled with GPU instances on major cloud providers.
  • Data egress: Downloading checkpoints, logs, and eval results. AWS charges $0.09/GB for egress, but first 100GB/month free. Budget ~$500–2,000/month.
  • Multi-region: If training spans regions for capacity reasons, cross-region transfer at $0.02/GB adds significant cost to AllReduce operations — avoid this architecture if possible.

4. Engineering & Overhead

  • ML engineers: 4–8 engineers × $300K–500K total comp (SF market) × 4–6 months = $600K–$2M.
  • Infrastructure/DevOps: 1–2 engineers for cluster management, monitoring (Prometheus/Grafana), job scheduling (SLURM/Kubernetes).
  • Experiment tracking: W&B Enterprise at ~$50/user/month, or self-hosted MLflow.
  • Opportunity cost: GPU time used for training could instead be used for inference revenue — factor this in for startups with limited GPU access.

Memory Requirements and Parallelism Strategy

The GPU count is also constrained by memory, not just throughput. For a 70B model in BF16 with Adam optimizer (FP32 master weights + momentum + variance):

  • Model parameters (BF16): 70B × 2 bytes = 140 GB
  • Optimizer states (FP32): 70B × 12 bytes = 840 GB (master weights + m + v)
  • Gradients (BF16): 70B × 2 bytes = 140 GB
  • Activation memory: Depends on batch size and sequence length. For seq_len=8192, micro_batch=1 per GPU: ~25 GB per layer × 80 layers ÷ pipeline stages
  • Total per-GPU with ZeRO-3 across 128 GPUs: ~(140+840+140)/128 + activations ≈ 8.75 GB + activations ≈ 35–50 GB — fits in H100 80GB

Key Takeaways for Cost Planning

  • Provider selection is the biggest lever: A 5× cost difference between hyperscaler on-demand and GPU cloud providers dwarfs most other optimizations.
  • MFU is the second biggest lever: Improving MFU from 30% to 45% reduces training time (and cost) by 33%. Invest in optimized kernels (FlashAttention, Triton), communication-computation overlap, and pipeline schedule optimization.
  • Over-training small models saves inference cost: Use Chinchilla as a starting point, then multiply token count by 5–20× for production-deployed models where inference cost dominates.
  • Budget for failure: No frontier training run succeeds on the first attempt. Plan for 1.5–2× the base compute budget to account for restarts, hyperparameter tuning, and data quality iterations.
  • Monitor cost per quality unit: Track $/perplexity-point or $/benchmark-score improvement rather than absolute cost to make informed scaling decisions.
