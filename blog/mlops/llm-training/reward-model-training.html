Training Reward Models for RLHF

MLOps Series LLM Training Infrastructure

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) depends on a reward model that faithfully captures human preferences. The reward model acts as a learned proxy for the expensive human evaluation signal, scoring every candidate response produced by the policy LLM. A poorly calibrated reward model leads to reward hacking—the policy exploits artifacts in the learned reward function rather than genuinely improving. This guide covers the full lifecycle of reward model training: collecting preference data, fitting a Bradley-Terry model, designing the architecture, training with pairwise ranking losses, mitigating reward hacking, and rigorous evaluation.

1  Preference Data Collection

The foundation of any reward model is a dataset of human preference comparisons. For a given prompt x, two candidate completions yw (chosen/winner) and yl (rejected/loser) are presented to a human annotator who selects the preferred response. The resulting dataset D = {(x(i), yw(i), yl(i))} forms the training signal for the reward model.

Human Labeler Pipelines

Production-scale preference labeling at organizations like Anthropic and OpenAI involves dedicated labeling teams with detailed rubrics. Annotators are typically recruited with domain expertise relevant to the task—coding, creative writing, factual QA—and undergo calibration rounds where they label pre-evaluated examples. A robust pipeline includes:

  • Prompt sampling: Stratified sampling from real user traffic to cover diverse intents, difficulty levels, and topic areas. Prompt diversity prevents the reward model from overfitting to narrow distributions.
  • Response generation: Multiple responses are sampled from the policy at varying temperatures (e.g., T ∈ {0.3, 0.7, 1.0}) using nucleus sampling (top-p). Higher temperatures produce more diverse pairs, exposing the reward model to a wider quality spectrum.
  • Annotation UI: Side-by-side comparison interface with clearly defined criteria (helpfulness, harmlessness, honesty). Annotators can select "A is better," "B is better," or "tie." Ties are typically discarded or down-weighted during training.
  • Quality control: Gold-standard questions (known correct rankings) are interspersed at ~10% rate. Annotators falling below 85% accuracy on gold questions trigger review. Periodic re-calibration sessions maintain consistency.

Comparison-Based Annotation: Pairwise Rankings

Pairwise comparisons are the dominant paradigm because they are cognitively simpler than absolute scoring. Humans are better at relative judgments ("A is better than B") than assigning numerical scores. For k candidate responses per prompt, we can form C(k,2) = k(k-1)/2 pairs. In practice, k=4 yields 6 pairs per prompt, balancing annotation cost with data efficiency.

Practical tip: Anthropic's preference data collection for Claude uses k=2 (single pair per prompt) at massive scale rather than k=4 with fewer prompts. The intuition: prompt diversity matters more than exhaustive pairwise coverage within a single prompt. Covering 100K distinct prompts with 1 pair each outperforms 25K prompts with 6 pairs each.

Likert Scale Ratings

Some pipelines augment pairwise data with absolute Likert scale ratings (1-7 or 1-5) for individual responses. While noisier than pairwise comparisons, Likert ratings enable margin-aware training—if yw received a score of 7 and yl a score of 2, the reward model should assign a larger margin than a 5 vs. 4 pair. The margin signal can be incorporated as:

L = -log σ((r(yw) - r(yl)) - m), where m is proportional to the Likert score difference.

AI-Assisted Labeling: RLAIF & Constitutional AI

Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF) replaces human annotators with a strong LLM judge. Anthropic's Constitutional AI uses a set of principles (the "constitution") to guide the AI judge. The pipeline: (1) generate response pairs, (2) prompt a judge LLM with the constitution and the pair, (3) extract the preference. This scales to millions of comparisons at a fraction of the cost. However, RLAIF introduces systematic biases—LLM judges favor verbose responses, exhibit position bias (preferring the first option), and can amplify the judge model's own biases.

Data Quality Metrics

Inter-annotator agreement quantifies label reliability. Key metrics:

Cohen's Kappa (κ)

Measures agreement between exactly two annotators, correcting for chance agreement: κ = (po - pe) / (1 - pe), where po is observed agreement and pe is expected chance agreement. Values: κ < 0.2 (poor), 0.2-0.4 (fair), 0.4-0.6 (moderate), 0.6-0.8 (substantial), >0.8 (excellent). Production RLHF data typically targets κ ≥ 0.6.

Krippendorff's Alpha (α)

Generalizes to any number of annotators with missing data: α = 1 - Do/De, where Do is observed disagreement and De is expected disagreement. Handles ordinal, nominal, and ratio scales. More robust than Fleiss' kappa for variable annotator assignments. Target α ≥ 0.667 for tentative conclusions, α ≥ 0.8 for reliable data.

Warning: Low inter-annotator agreement (κ < 0.4) on a subset of prompts often indicates ambiguous prompts or underspecified rubrics rather than annotator incompetence. These ambiguous examples, if included, inject noise that degrades reward model calibration. Filter or relabel them.

2  Bradley-Terry Preference Model

The Bradley-Terry (BT) model provides the probabilistic foundation for learning from pairwise preferences. Originally developed for ranking chess players, it assigns a latent "strength" parameter to each item and models the probability that one item is preferred over another.

Mathematical Formulation

Given a reward function r(x, y) that assigns a scalar score to each (prompt, response) pair, the BT model defines the probability that response yw is preferred over yl conditioned on prompt x:

P(yw ≻ yl | x) = σ(r(x, yw) - r(x, yl))

where σ is the sigmoid function σ(z) = 1/(1+e-z). This means preferences depend only on the difference in rewards, making the model invariant to adding a constant to all rewards (translation invariance). The reward function is unique only up to an additive constant.

Loss Function Derivation

Given dataset D of preference triples, the negative log-likelihood under the BT model is:

L(θ) = -𝔼(x, yw, yl) ~ D [ log σ(rθ(x, yw) - rθ(x, yl)) ]

Expanding: when r(x, yw) ≫ r(x, yl), σ(Δ) → 1 and -log(1) → 0 (low loss). When r(x, yw) ≈ r(x, yl), σ(0) = 0.5 and -log(0.5) = log(2) ≈ 0.693 (moderate loss). When r(x, yw) ≪ r(x, yl), the loss approaches infinity. The gradient with respect to θ pushes chosen responses to higher rewards and rejected responses to lower rewards.

Connection to ELO Ratings

The ELO rating system used in chess is a special case of the BT model with a scaling factor. In ELO, P(A beats B) = 1 / (1 + 10(RB-RA)/400), which is equivalent to σ((RA - RB) · ln(10)/400). The BT model uses natural scaling (base e) while ELO uses base 10 with a 400-point scale factor. This equivalence is why reward model scores are sometimes reported as "ELO scores" in LLM leaderboards like Chatbot Arena.

Key insight: The BT model assumes transitivity—if A ≻ B and B ≻ C, then A ≻ C. Human preferences can be intransitive (cyclic), especially when different quality dimensions conflict (helpfulness vs. safety). This assumption limits the BT model. Thurstone models and Plackett-Luce models offer alternatives for richer preference structures.

3  Reward Model Architecture

Modern reward models are built by repurposing a pretrained language model as a feature extractor and appending a lightweight scalar projection head. The architecture choice and pooling strategy significantly impact training stability and final accuracy.

Pretrained LLM as Backbone

The reward model backbone is typically initialized from the same pretrained checkpoint as the policy model (or a comparable one). Using the same model family ensures the reward model's internal representations align with the policy's output distribution. Common choices: LLaMA-7B/13B for open-source, GPT-3/3.5 scale for proprietary systems. The language modeling head (unembedding matrix projecting to vocabulary) is removed entirely.

Scalar Reward Head

A single linear layer projects the pooled hidden state h ∈ ℝd to a scalar reward: r = WTh + b, where W ∈ ℝd and b ∈ ℝ. Some implementations omit the bias term to enforce that the reward depends purely on the hidden representation. The head is initialized with small random weights (σ = 0.01) to prevent large initial reward magnitudes that destabilize early training.

Pooling Strategies

Last-Token Pooling

Uses the hidden state at the last token position (typically EOS or a padding token). This is the default in most implementations (InstructGPT, LLaMA-based RMs). The autoregressive attention mask ensures the last token attends to all preceding tokens, making it a natural summary representation. Works well because causal LMs compress full-sequence information into the final position.

Mean Pooling

Averages hidden states across all non-padding token positions: h = (1/T) Σ ht. More robust to sequence length variations and less sensitive to the specific content of the final token. However, it dilutes the signal from critical tokens (e.g., factual errors or harmful content) across the entire sequence. Generally performs 1-2% worse than last-token pooling for causal LMs.

Architecture note: OpenAI's InstructGPT uses a 6B parameter reward model (based on GPT-3 6B) with last-token pooling. Anthropic trains reward models at multiple scales (e.g., 13B, 52B) and ensembles them for robustness. The reward model need not be the same size as the policy—a smaller RM can effectively guide a larger policy, though accuracy improves with scale.

RewardModel Implementation

import torch import torch.nn as nn from transformers import AutoModel, AutoTokenizer class RewardModel(nn.Module): # Reward model: pretrained LLM backbone + scalar reward head def __init__(self, backbone_name: str, freeze_backbone: bool = False): super().__init__() self.backbone = AutoModel.from_pretrained(backbone_name) hidden_dim = self.backbone.config.hidden_size # Scalar projection head: hidden_dim → 1 self.reward_head = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, 1, bias=False) nn.init.normal_(self.reward_head.weight, std=0.01) if freeze_backbone: for param in self.backbone.parameters(): param.requires_grad = False def forward(self, input_ids, attention_mask): # Extract hidden states from backbone outputs = self.backbone( input_ids=input_ids, attention_mask=attention_mask, output_hidden_states=True ) hidden_states = outputs.last_hidden_state # (B, T, d) # Last-token pooling: find last non-padding position seq_lengths = attention_mask.sum(dim=1) - 1 # (B,) batch_indices = torch.arange(hidden_states.size(0)) pooled = hidden_states[batch_indices, seq_lengths] # (B, d) # Project to scalar reward reward = self.reward_head(pooled).squeeze(-1) # (B,) return reward def compute_preference_loss(self, chosen_ids, chosen_mask, rejected_ids, rejected_mask): # Compute pairwise ranking loss for preference learning r_chosen = self.forward(chosen_ids, chosen_mask) r_rejected = self.forward(rejected_ids, rejected_mask) # Bradley-Terry loss: -log σ(r_w - r_l) loss = -nn.functional.logsigmoid(r_chosen - r_rejected).mean() accuracy = (r_chosen > r_rejected).float().mean() return loss, accuracy

4  Training Procedure

Training a reward model requires careful batch construction, learning rate tuning, and regularization to prevent overfitting to the relatively small preference dataset (typically 50K-500K comparisons, compared to billions of tokens for pretraining).

Batch Construction

Each training batch contains paired (chosen, rejected) responses for the same prompt. Critical implementation detail: both responses in a pair must appear in the same batch to compute the pairwise loss. The standard approach: for batch size B, sample B prompts and load both chosen and rejected for each, yielding 2B forward passes. Shuffling is done at the prompt level, not the individual response level.

Margin-Based Losses

The vanilla BT loss treats all preference pairs equally. In practice, some pairs have a clear quality gap (rating 7 vs. 2) while others are near-ties (rating 5 vs. 4). Margin-based losses incorporate this signal:

Lmargin = -log σ(r(yw) - r(yl) - m(yw, yl))

where m is a margin proportional to the quality gap. LLaMA-2 uses a margin derived from the "safety" and "helpfulness" metadata associated with each pair. This margin-aware training produces better-calibrated reward models with more interpretable score distributions.

Training Hyperparameters

  • Learning rate: 1e-5 to 5e-6 (much lower than pretraining). Cosine schedule with 3-10% linear warmup.
  • Epochs: 1-2 passes over the preference data. More epochs risk overfitting—the model memorizes specific comparison pairs rather than learning generalizable quality signals.
  • Batch size: 64-512 paired examples (128-1024 forward passes). Larger batches stabilize gradients for the pairwise loss.
  • Weight decay: 0.01-0.1. Higher weight decay regularizes the reward head specifically.
  • Max sequence length: 2048-4096 tokens. Consistent with the policy model's context window.

Training Loop Implementation

import torch from torch.optim import AdamW from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import CosineAnnealingLR def train_reward_model(model, dataloader, num_epochs=1, lr=1e-5): # Configure optimizer with differential learning rates backbone_params = [p for n, p in model.named_parameters() if not n.startswith("reward_head")] head_params = [p for n, p in model.named_parameters() if n.startswith("reward_head")] optimizer = AdamW([ {"params": backbone_params, "lr": lr, "weight_decay": 0.01}, {"params": head_params, "lr": lr * 10, "weight_decay": 0.1}, ]) scheduler = CosineAnnealingLR(optimizer, T_max=len(dataloader) * num_epochs) model.train() for epoch in range(num_epochs): total_loss, total_acc, n_batches = 0.0, 0.0, 0 for batch in dataloader: chosen_ids = batch["chosen_input_ids"].cuda() chosen_mask = batch["chosen_attention_mask"].cuda() rejected_ids = batch["rejected_input_ids"].cuda() rejected_mask = batch["rejected_attention_mask"].cuda() # Forward pass: compute pairwise ranking loss loss, accuracy = model.compute_preference_loss( chosen_ids, chosen_mask, rejected_ids, rejected_mask ) # Backward pass with gradient clipping optimizer.zero_grad() loss.backward() torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0) optimizer.step() scheduler.step() total_loss += loss.item() total_acc += accuracy.item() n_batches += 1 if n_batches % 100 == 0: avg_loss = total_loss / n_batches avg_acc = total_acc / n_batches print(f"Epoch {epoch} | Batch {n_batches} | " f"Loss: {avg_loss:.4f} | Acc: {avg_acc:.3f}") return model
Overfitting risk: Reward models are prone to overfitting because preference datasets are orders of magnitude smaller than pretraining corpora. Signs of overfitting: training accuracy approaching 100% while validation accuracy plateaus or drops, reward score variance collapsing, or the model assigning near-identical scores to all responses. Early stopping on validation set accuracy is essential.

5  Reward Hacking & Calibration

Reward hacking (also called reward over-optimization) occurs when the policy exploits imperfections in the learned reward model to achieve high reward scores without genuinely improving response quality. This is a manifestation of Goodhart's law: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

Reward Over-Optimization

As PPO training progresses, the policy increasingly deviates from its initial distribution. At moderate KL divergence (KL ≈ 5-15 nats), proxy reward and true (human) reward are correlated. Beyond a critical KL threshold, the proxy reward continues increasing while the true reward decreases—the policy has found adversarial inputs that exploit the reward model's blind spots. Empirically, this manifests as responses that are excessively verbose, repetitive, or sycophantic.

KL-Constrained Optimization

The standard mitigation is a KL penalty between the current policy πθ and the reference policy πref (the SFT model):

maximize 𝔼x~D, y~πθ[ r(x,y) - β · KL(πθ(·|x) ‖ πref(·|x)) ]

The coefficient β controls the trade-off: higher β keeps the policy closer to the SFT model (conservative, avoids reward hacking), lower β allows more exploration (aggressive, higher risk). In practice, β is adaptively adjusted: increase β when reward starts increasing faster than validation human preference accuracy, decrease β when the policy is too conservative.

Reward Model Ensembles

Training multiple reward models (N=3-5) with different random seeds, data subsets, or architectures provides uncertainty estimates. During PPO, the effective reward is: reff = mean(r1,...,rN) - λ · std(r1,...,rN). The penalty term λ·std discourages the policy from exploiting regions where the reward models disagree (high uncertainty = likely out-of-distribution). Anthropic uses this approach with λ calibrated on a held-out validation set.

Calibration Techniques

  • Temperature scaling: Post-hoc rescaling of reward logits using a temperature parameter T optimized on validation data: rcal = r/T. Ensures that σ(Δr) reflects the true probability of human agreement.
  • Platt scaling: Fits a logistic regression a·r + b on validation pairs. More flexible than temperature scaling by allowing both scaling and shift.
  • Reward normalization: During PPO, normalize rewards using a running mean and standard deviation computed over recent batches. Prevents reward magnitude drift.

OOD Detection

  • Ensemble disagreement: Flag inputs where std(r1,...,rN) exceeds a threshold. These are inputs the reward model is uncertain about and potentially OOD.
  • Embedding distance: Compute the Mahalanobis distance of the pooled hidden state from the training distribution. Inputs far from the training manifold are unreliable.
  • Reward clipping: Clip absolute reward values to [-Rmax, Rmax] during PPO. Prevents the policy from chasing extreme reward values that are likely extrapolation artifacts.
Practical insight: The "Best-of-N" (rejection sampling) approach offers a reward-hacking-resistant alternative to PPO. Generate N responses, score each with the reward model, and select the highest-scoring one. This avoids policy optimization entirely—no gradients flow through the reward model—limiting the attack surface for reward hacking. However, it is N× more expensive at inference time.

6  Evaluation & Benchmarking

Evaluating a reward model requires going beyond simple accuracy metrics to assess calibration, bias, and robustness. A reward model that achieves 70% accuracy on held-out preferences but is well-calibrated and unbiased may outperform one with 75% accuracy that exhibits systematic biases.

Accuracy on Held-Out Preferences

The primary metric: given unseen (prompt, chosen, rejected) triples, how often does the reward model assign a higher score to the chosen response? Baseline performance depends on task difficulty and annotator agreement. If inter-annotator agreement is κ=0.7, a reward model achieving 72% accuracy is near the human ceiling. Report accuracy stratified by difficulty (clear wins vs. close calls), topic, and response length.

Agreement with Human Judgments

Beyond pairwise accuracy, measure rank correlation (Spearman's ρ, Kendall's τ) between reward model scores and human Likert ratings across a set of prompts. This tests whether the reward model's ordinal ranking matches human quality ordering, not just pairwise preferences. Target Spearman's ρ ≥ 0.6 for production deployment.

Reward Distribution Analysis

Visualize the distribution of reward scores across the training and validation sets. A healthy reward model produces a roughly normal distribution with clear separation between chosen and rejected response distributions. Red flags: bimodal distributions (the model is clustering rather than ranking), collapsed distributions (all scores near 0), or heavy tails (extreme scores for outlier responses).

Probing for Biases

Reward models inherit biases from the preference data and the pretrained backbone. Systematic probes include:

  • Length bias: Generate response pairs that are semantically identical but differ in length (e.g., via paraphrasing). A biased model assigns higher rewards to longer responses regardless of content. Measure correlation between reward score and response length, controlling for quality.
  • Sycophancy bias: Test whether the model rewards responses that agree with the user's stated opinion even when the opinion is factually incorrect. Construct prompts with wrong premises and measure if the model prefers agreeing vs. correcting responses.
  • Format bias: Compare rewards for responses with markdown formatting, bullet points, and headers vs. plain text with identical content. Some reward models strongly prefer formatted responses regardless of substance.
  • Position bias: For RLAIF-generated data, check if the reward model inherits the judge LLM's tendency to prefer the first (or last) response in a comparison.

Benchmark Suites

RewardBench

A standardized benchmark by the Allen AI Institute for evaluating reward models. Tests: chat (helpfulness), safety (refusal appropriateness), reasoning (math/logic correctness), and chat-hard (subtle quality differences). Reports per-category accuracy and a weighted overall score. State-of-the-art models achieve ~90% on chat but only ~65% on chat-hard, showing that distinguishing subtle quality differences remains challenging.

Chatbot Arena / LMSYS

Uses crowd-sourced human preferences from real conversations. Computes ELO ratings for models based on head-to-head comparisons. Reward model quality can be assessed by correlation between reward scores and Chatbot Arena ELO rankings across models. A well-calibrated reward model should rank models in an order consistent with their ELO ratings. Also useful for monitoring reward model drift over time.

Evaluation pitfall: High accuracy on the same preference data distribution used for training does not guarantee good RLHF outcomes. The reward model must generalize to the on-policy distribution—responses generated by the evolving PPO policy, which diverges from the SFT distribution over training. Always evaluate on fresh preference data collected from policy rollouts, not just a held-out split of the original dataset.
