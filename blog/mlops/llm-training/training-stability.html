Training Stability: Loss Spikes, NaN, Recovery

MLOps Series LLM Training Infrastructure

Training large language models at scale is an exercise in controlled chaos. A 175B-parameter run on 1024 GPUs can consume $4M+ in compute, and a single unrecovered instability event can waste days of wall-clock time. This post dissects the taxonomy of training instabilities, builds robust detection and monitoring systems, and implements automatic recovery strategies used in production training pipelines at organizations like Google DeepMind, Meta FAIR, and Anthropic.

Common Training Instabilities

Training instabilities in LLMs manifest in several distinct failure modes, each with characteristic signatures in the loss curve, gradient statistics, and activation distributions. Understanding these patterns is essential for building resilient training infrastructure.

Loss Spikes

Loss spikes are sudden, dramatic increases in the training loss—often 2-10× the running average—followed by either gradual recovery or complete divergence. They are the most visible symptom of instability and occur in virtually every large-scale training run. Google's PaLM paper documented ~20 loss spikes during training of the 540B model, each requiring manual intervention or automatic rollback.

Key characteristic: Loss spikes are often transient if the learning rate is low enough and the optimizer has sufficient momentum. However, at scale, even a recovered spike can shift the model into a worse loss basin, permanently degrading final performance by 0.1-0.5% on downstream benchmarks.

Gradient Explosions

Gradient explosions occur when the gradient norm grows exponentially across layers during backpropagation. In a Transformer with L layers, the gradient at layer l scales roughly as ∏(i=l to L) ||∂h_i/∂h_{i-1}||. If per-layer Jacobian norms exceed 1.0 consistently, gradients at early layers can exceed fp16 range (65504) and overflow to Inf or NaN.

NaN and Inf Propagation

Once a single NaN or Inf enters the computation graph, it propagates irreversibly through all downstream operations. In mixed-precision training with fp16 forward passes, the most common sources are: softmax overflow when attention logits exceed ~88 (exp(88) ≈ 1.65×10³⁸, near fp32 max), division by zero in LayerNorm when variance collapses, and loss scaling overflow when the dynamic loss scaler's scale factor grows too large.

Critical: NaN detection must happen before the optimizer step. Once NaN gradients are applied to parameters, the entire model state is corrupted and the only recovery is checkpoint rollback. This is why frameworks like Megatron-LM check for NaN in the gradient reduction step.

Slow Divergence

Unlike dramatic spikes, slow divergence is a gradual increase in loss over hundreds or thousands of steps. It's harder to detect because individual step-to-step changes are within normal variance. Root causes include learning rate schedules that don't decay fast enough, embedding norm growth that shifts the model's representational geometry, or accumulated numerical errors in optimizer states stored in fp16.

Root Cause Analysis

Diagnosing the root cause of instability is the most time-consuming part of large-scale training. Each failure mode has multiple potential causes, and the interaction between distributed training, mixed precision, and data composition creates a combinatorial space of failure triggers.

Learning Rate Miscalibration

The most common cause of instability is a learning rate that is too high for the model's current loss landscape curvature. The critical learning rate η_crit ≈ 2/λ_max, where λ_max is the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian. For Transformers, λ_max grows during training as the model sharpens its predictions, meaning a fixed learning rate that was stable at step 10k can become unstable at step 100k. This is why cosine decay schedules are essential—not just for convergence, but for stability.

Too-High Learning Rate

Symptoms: Periodic loss spikes with increasing frequency. Gradient norms exhibit high variance (CV > 0.5). The model oscillates around the minimum rather than converging.

Fix: Reduce peak LR by 2-3×, extend warmup from 2000 to 5000+ steps, use a more aggressive decay schedule.

Bad Data Batches

Symptoms: Isolated, non-periodic spikes. Loss returns to baseline within 5-20 steps without intervention. Gradient norms spike on specific data shards.

Fix: Implement batch-level loss monitoring, skip batches with loss > 5× moving average, add data validation in the preprocessing pipeline.

Numerical Precision Issues

Mixed-precision training with fp16 (IEEE 754 half-precision) has a dynamic range of 6.1×10⁻⁵ to 65504 and ~3.3 decimal digits of precision. BF16 (bfloat16) trades precision for range: same exponent bits as fp32 (8 bits) giving a range up to 3.39×10³⁸, but only ~2.4 decimal digits of precision. The critical implication is that fp16 underflows gradients below 6.1×10⁻⁵ to zero, which silently kills learning for parameters with small gradients (early embedding layers, bias terms).

Industry practice: Most production systems (Megatron-LM, GPT-NeoX, PaLM) use bf16 for forward/backward passes and fp32 for optimizer states and gradient accumulation. This gives the range safety of bf16 while maintaining precision where it matters most—in the Adam momentum and variance estimates.

Attention Logit Growth

In standard scaled dot-product attention, the logits are computed as QK^T/√d_k. As training progresses, the norms of Q and K vectors tend to grow, causing attention logits to increase. When logits exceed ~88 in fp32 (or ~11 in fp16), the softmax computation overflows because exp(x) exceeds the representable range. This is a well-documented failure mode observed in GPT-3, PaLM, and Chinchilla training runs.

Embedding Norm Growth

Token embeddings and positional embeddings can grow unboundedly during training because there is no explicit constraint on their norm. As embedding norms grow, the input to the first Transformer layer increases in magnitude, which amplifies through residual connections. The embedding norm at step t typically grows as O(√t) under SGD, but Adam's normalization can accelerate this to O(t) in pathological cases.

Detection and Monitoring

Robust detection infrastructure is the foundation of training stability. The goal is to detect instability within 1-5 steps of onset, before NaN propagation corrupts model state. This requires monitoring at multiple levels: global loss, per-layer gradient norms, activation statistics, and optimizer state health.

Gradient Norm Tracking

The global gradient norm (L2 norm of the flattened gradient vector) is the single most informative stability metric. In a stable training run, it follows a predictable distribution: roughly log-normal with a slowly decreasing mean as the model converges. A spike in gradient norm of >3σ from the running mean is a reliable early warning of impending loss spike.

import torch from collections import deque import math class GradNormTracker: # Tracks gradient norms with exponential moving average def __init__(self, window=1000, sigma_threshold=3.0): self.window = window self.sigma_threshold = sigma_threshold self.history = deque(maxlen=window) self.ema_mean = None self.ema_var = None self.alpha = 0.01 # EMA decay factor def update(self, grad_norm: float) -> dict: self.history.append(grad_norm) if self.ema_mean is None: self.ema_mean = grad_norm self.ema_var = 0.0 return {"spike": False, "grad_norm": grad_norm} # Update exponential moving average and variance delta = grad_norm - self.ema_mean self.ema_mean += self.alpha * delta self.ema_var = (1 - self.alpha) * (self.ema_var + self.alpha * delta ** 2) ema_std = math.sqrt(self.ema_var + 1e-8) z_score = abs(delta) / ema_std is_spike = z_score > self.sigma_threshold return { "spike": is_spike, "grad_norm": grad_norm, "z_score": z_score, "ema_mean": self.ema_mean, "ema_std": ema_std, }

Loss Spike Detection

Loss spike detection requires comparing the current loss against a robust baseline. Using a simple moving average is insufficient because it's sensitive to outliers. Instead, use an exponential moving average with a long decay (α=0.001) combined with a median filter over a short window. A spike is declared when the current loss exceeds max(2× EMA, EMA + 4σ).

Per-Layer Monitoring

Global gradient norm can mask per-layer pathologies. A single layer with exploding gradients might contribute only 10% of the global norm but be in a completely unstable regime. Production systems monitor: gradient norm per layer group (embedding, attention, FFN, final LM head), activation mean and variance per layer, and the ratio of parameter update magnitude to parameter magnitude (should stay between 10⁻⁴ and 10⁻²).

Performance overhead: Per-layer monitoring adds ~2-5% overhead to training throughput due to additional all-reduce operations for distributed gradient statistics. In production, teams often monitor a random 10% subset of layers per step and rotate which layers are monitored, reducing overhead to <0.5%.

Gradient Clipping Strategies

Gradient clipping is the primary defense against gradient explosions. It bounds the gradient magnitude before the optimizer applies the update, preventing catastrophically large parameter changes. The choice of clipping strategy and threshold significantly impacts both stability and convergence speed.

Global Gradient Norm Clipping

The most widely used strategy. The global gradient norm ||g||₂ is computed across all parameters, and if it exceeds a threshold τ, all gradients are scaled by τ/||g||₂. This preserves the relative direction of the gradient while bounding its magnitude. The key advantage is that it treats the gradient as a single vector in parameter space, maintaining the direction that the optimizer would take.

def clip_grad_norm_global(parameters, max_norm, norm_type=2.0): # Compute total gradient norm across all parameters total_norm = torch.norm( torch.stack([torch.norm(p.grad, norm_type) for p in parameters]), norm_type ) # Scale gradients if norm exceeds threshold clip_coef = max_norm / (total_norm + 1e-6) if clip_coef < 1.0: for p in parameters: p.grad.mul_(clip_coef) return total_norm

Per-Parameter Value Clipping

An alternative approach clips each gradient element independently: g_i = clamp(g_i, -τ, τ). This is simpler but fundamentally changes the gradient direction, which can slow convergence. It's primarily useful when specific parameters (e.g., the final logit projection) consistently have disproportionately large gradients.

Adaptive Clipping

Adaptive gradient clipping (AGC), introduced in the NFNet paper, clips gradients based on the ratio of gradient norm to parameter norm: if ||g_l||/||w_l|| > λ for layer l, the gradient is clipped to λ·||w_l||. The intuition is that a parameter update should not change the parameter by more than a fraction λ of its current magnitude. Typical values are λ ∈ [0.01, 0.1].

Global Norm Clipping

Threshold: Typically 1.0 for LLMs. GPT-3 used 1.0, PaLM used 1.0, LLaMA used 1.0.

Pros: Preserves gradient direction, well-understood interaction with Adam optimizer, single hyperparameter.

Cons: Doesn't account for parameter scale differences, can under-clip small layers while over-clipping large ones.

Adaptive Clipping (AGC)

Threshold: λ = 0.01 to 0.1, unitless ratio. Layer-aware by construction.

Pros: Automatically adapts to different layer scales, enables training without batch normalization, reduces sensitivity to learning rate.

Cons: More complex, interacts non-trivially with weight decay, less studied at >100B scale.

Choosing Clip Thresholds

The optimal clip threshold depends on the model architecture, optimizer, learning rate, and training stage. A practical approach: run a short calibration phase (1000 steps) without clipping, record the gradient norm distribution, and set the clip threshold at the 95th-99th percentile. For most Transformer LLMs with Adam and LR ~3×10⁻⁴, a global norm clip of 1.0 is a robust default. Values below 0.3 can significantly slow convergence; values above 5.0 provide little protection.

Automatic Recovery

Automatic recovery transforms training instability from a catastrophic failure into a temporary slowdown. The key principle: never corrupt model state. This means detecting instability before applying optimizer updates and having a fallback plan at every step.

Checkpoint Rollback

The most robust recovery strategy: revert to the last known-good checkpoint and resume training with modified hyperparameters. Google's PaLM training used this approach for all 20 loss spikes during the 540B run. The procedure is: (1) detect spike via loss > 2× EMA, (2) halt training across all workers, (3) load the checkpoint from N steps before the spike, (4) skip the data batch window that triggered the spike, (5) optionally reduce learning rate by 10-50%, (6) resume training.

Checkpoint frequency trade-off: More frequent checkpoints (every 100 steps) minimize rollback waste but consume storage bandwidth. At scale with 175B parameters at fp32, each checkpoint is ~700 GB. Distributed checkpointing with tensor-parallel sharding reduces per-node I/O but requires all nodes to be available for recovery.

Batch Skipping

For instabilities caused by bad data batches (corrupted text, adversarial sequences, extreme length outliers), the simplest recovery is to skip the problematic batch. The decision is made before the optimizer step: if the loss for the current batch exceeds a threshold (e.g., 5× running average), the gradients are zeroed and the step is skipped. This is lightweight but only effective when the instability source is data-related, not systemic.

Learning Rate Reduction

After a detected instability event, temporarily reducing the learning rate helps the optimizer navigate the high-curvature region that triggered the spike. A common strategy: multiply the current LR by 0.5, maintain the reduced LR for 500-1000 steps, then linearly ramp back to the scheduled LR over another 500 steps. This creates a "healing window" where the model can recover without risking re-triggering the instability.

Z-Loss Regularization

Z-loss adds a penalty term proportional to the log of the sum of exponentials of the logits: L_z = α · log²(Σ exp(z_i)). This directly penalizes large logit values, preventing the softmax overflow that causes NaN. The PaLM paper used α = 10⁻⁴ and found it eliminated most attention-logit-related instabilities. The overhead is negligible (<0.1% compute) because the logsumexp is already computed for the cross-entropy loss.

def z_loss(logits, coeff=1e-4): # Penalize large logit magnitudes to prevent softmax overflow # logits shape: (batch_size, seq_len, vocab_size) log_z = torch.logsumexp(logits, dim=-1) # (batch, seq) loss = coeff * (log_z ** 2).mean() return loss def compute_total_loss(logits, targets, z_loss_coeff=1e-4): # Standard cross-entropy plus z-loss regularization ce_loss = torch.nn.functional.cross_entropy( logits.view(-1, logits.size(-1)), targets.view(-1), ignore_index=-100 ) z_reg = z_loss(logits, z_loss_coeff) return ce_loss + z_reg, {"ce_loss": ce_loss.item(), "z_loss": z_reg.item()}

Advanced Stabilization Techniques

Beyond reactive recovery, proactive stabilization techniques modify the architecture or parameterization to prevent instabilities from occurring in the first place. These are increasingly standard in models trained at 100B+ scale.

QK-Norm (Query-Key Normalization)

QK-Norm applies LayerNorm to the query and key vectors before computing attention logits: Attention = softmax(LN(Q)·LN(K)^T / √d_k). By normalizing Q and K to unit variance, the attention logit magnitude is bounded regardless of training progress. This eliminates the attention logit growth problem entirely. Google's Gemini and ViT-22B both use QK-Norm.

Embedding Scaling

Instead of allowing embeddings to grow freely, scale them by a fixed factor at initialization and after each update. The simplest approach: multiply the embedding output by √d_model (as in the original Transformer paper) combined with a maximum norm constraint. Alternatively, use parametric scaling where a learned scalar γ modulates the embedding magnitude, initialized to a small value (0.1-1.0).

Pre-Norm (GPT-style)

Structure: x + Sublayer(LN(x)). LayerNorm is applied before each sublayer. The residual stream carries unnormalized activations.

Stability: More stable because the sublayer input is always normalized. Gradient flow through the residual is unimpeded by normalization.

Caveat: Can lead to representation collapse in very deep models because the residual stream dominates sublayer contributions.

Post-Norm (Original Transformer)

Structure: LN(x + Sublayer(x)). LayerNorm is applied after the residual addition.

Stability: Less stable during training—requires careful warmup and lower learning rates. Gradient magnitudes vary more across layers.

Advantage: Often achieves better final performance because sublayer outputs are not suppressed by normalization.

μP (Maximal Update Parameterization)

μP is a principled approach to parameterization that ensures hyperparameters transfer across model widths. Under standard parameterization (SP), the optimal learning rate, initialization scale, and other hyperparameters change when you scale model width. Under μP, each parameter group has a width-dependent learning rate multiplier: embeddings use η/d, hidden layers use η/d, and the output layer uses η/d, where d is the model width. This means you can tune hyperparameters on a small (128-width) model and directly transfer them to the full-scale (12288-width) model.

μP in practice: Cerebras and Microsoft have validated μP transfer across 3 orders of magnitude in model width. The key implementation detail is that μP requires different learning rate multipliers per parameter group in the optimizer—not a single global learning rate. Most frameworks require custom optimizer wrappers to implement this correctly.

Full Stability Monitor Implementation

The following implementation combines all the detection and recovery strategies into a production-grade StabilityMonitor class. It integrates with a standard PyTorch training loop and supports distributed training via gradient norm all-reduce.

import torch import math import logging from collections import deque from dataclasses import dataclass, field from typing import Optional, Dict, List logger = logging.getLogger(__name__) @dataclass class StabilityConfig: # Detection thresholds loss_spike_sigma: float = 4.0 # z-score threshold for loss spike grad_spike_sigma: float = 3.0 # z-score threshold for grad norm spike loss_spike_multiplier: float = 2.0 # absolute multiplier threshold nan_check_enabled: bool = True # Recovery parameters max_consecutive_skips: int = 5 # max batch skips before rollback lr_reduction_factor: float = 0.5 # LR multiplier on spike recovery lr_recovery_steps: int = 500 # steps to ramp LR back up ema_alpha: float = 0.001 # EMA decay for loss tracking grad_ema_alpha: float = 0.01 # EMA decay for grad norm tracking checkpoint_interval: int = 200 # steps between stability checkpoints class StabilityMonitor: def __init__(self, config: StabilityConfig): self.config = config self.step = 0 self.loss_ema = None self.loss_var = 0.0 self.grad_ema = None self.grad_var = 0.0 self.consecutive_skips = 0 self.lr_recovery_remaining = 0 self.original_lr = None self.last_good_checkpoint = None self.spike_events: List[Dict] = [] def check_nan(self, loss, model) -> bool: # Check for NaN/Inf in loss and gradients if not self.config.nan_check_enabled: return False if torch.isnan(loss) or torch.isinf(loss): logger.error(f"NaN/Inf detected in loss at step {self.step}") return True for name, param in model.named_parameters(): if param.grad is not None: if torch.isnan(param.grad).any() or torch.isinf(param.grad).any(): logger.error(f"NaN/Inf gradient in {name} at step {self.step}") return True return False def compute_grad_norm(self, model) -> float: total_norm = 0.0 for p in model.parameters(): if p.grad is not None: total_norm += p.grad.data.norm(2).item() ** 2 return math.sqrt(total_norm) def _update_ema(self, value, ema, var, alpha): if ema is None: return value, 0.0 delta = value - ema new_ema = ema + alpha * delta new_var = (1 - alpha) * (var + alpha * delta ** 2) return new_ema, new_var def detect_loss_spike(self, loss_val: float) -> bool: if self.loss_ema is None: self.loss_ema = loss_val return False std = math.sqrt(self.loss_var + 1e-8) z_score = (loss_val - self.loss_ema) / std if std > 0 else 0 absolute_spike = loss_val > self.config.loss_spike_multiplier * self.loss_ema statistical_spike = z_score > self.config.loss_spike_sigma return absolute_spike or statistical_spike def detect_grad_spike(self, grad_norm: float) -> bool: if self.grad_ema is None: self.grad_ema = grad_norm return False std = math.sqrt(self.grad_var + 1e-8) z_score = (grad_norm - self.grad_ema) / std if std > 0 else 0 return z_score > self.config.grad_spike_sigma def step_check(self, loss, model, optimizer) -> str: # Returns: "ok", "skip_batch", or "rollback" self.step += 1 loss_val = loss.item() # Phase 1: NaN check (most critical) if self.check_nan(loss, model): return "rollback" # Phase 2: Gradient norm analysis grad_norm = self.compute_grad_norm(model) grad_spike = self.detect_grad_spike(grad_norm) self.grad_ema, self.grad_var = self._update_ema( grad_norm, self.grad_ema, self.grad_var, self.config.grad_ema_alpha ) # Phase 3: Loss spike detection loss_spike = self.detect_loss_spike(loss_val) self.loss_ema, self.loss_var = self._update_ema( loss_val, self.loss_ema, self.loss_var, self.config.ema_alpha ) if loss_spike or grad_spike: self.consecutive_skips += 1 self.spike_events.append({ "step": self.step, "loss": loss_val, "grad_norm": grad_norm, "type": "loss+grad" if (loss_spike and grad_spike) else "loss" if loss_spike else "grad" }) logger.warning(f"Spike at step {self.step}: loss={loss_val:.4f}, grad={grad_norm:.4f}") if self.consecutive_skips >= self.config.max_consecutive_skips: return "rollback" return "skip_batch" else: self.consecutive_skips = 0 return "ok" def reduce_lr(self, optimizer): # Temporarily reduce learning rate for recovery if self.original_lr is None: self.original_lr = optimizer.param_groups[0]["lr"] for pg in optimizer.param_groups: pg["lr"] *= self.config.lr_reduction_factor self.lr_recovery_remaining = self.config.lr_recovery_steps def maybe_restore_lr(self, optimizer): # Linearly ramp LR back to original after recovery if self.lr_recovery_remaining > 0 and self.original_lr is not None: self.lr_recovery_remaining -= 1 current_lr = optimizer.param_groups[0]["lr"] ramp = (self.original_lr - current_lr) / (self.lr_recovery_remaining + 1) for pg in optimizer.param_groups: pg["lr"] += ramp if self.lr_recovery_remaining == 0: self.original_lr = None

Now integrating the StabilityMonitor into a full training loop with checkpoint management, batch skipping, and automatic recovery:

import torch import os import logging logger = logging.getLogger(__name__) def save_checkpoint(model, optimizer, step, path): torch.save({ "model": model.state_dict(), "optimizer": optimizer.state_dict(), "step": step }, path) def load_checkpoint(model, optimizer, path): ckpt = torch.load(path, map_location="cpu") model.load_state_dict(ckpt["model"]) optimizer.load_state_dict(ckpt["optimizer"]) return ckpt["step"] def train_with_stability(model, optimizer, dataloader, config): monitor = StabilityMonitor(StabilityConfig()) scaler = torch.cuda.amp.GradScaler() ckpt_dir = "checkpoints/stability" os.makedirs(ckpt_dir, exist_ok=True) step = 0 for epoch in range(config.num_epochs): for batch in dataloader: step += 1 # Save periodic stability checkpoint if step % monitor.config.checkpoint_interval == 0: path = f"{ckpt_dir}/ckpt_{step}.pt" save_checkpoint(model, optimizer, step, path) monitor.last_good_checkpoint = path # Forward pass with mixed precision with torch.cuda.amp.autocast(dtype=torch.bfloat16): logits = model(batch["input_ids"]) loss, loss_dict = compute_total_loss( logits, batch["labels"] ) # Backward pass optimizer.zero_grad() scaler.scale(loss).backward() scaler.unscale_(optimizer) # Stability check BEFORE optimizer step action = monitor.step_check(loss, model, optimizer) if action == "ok": # Normal path: clip and step torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), 1.0) scaler.step(optimizer) scaler.update() monitor.maybe_restore_lr(optimizer) elif action == "skip_batch": # Bad batch: zero grads, skip optimizer step optimizer.zero_grad() logger.warning(f"Skipping batch at step {step}") elif action == "rollback": # Critical instability: rollback to checkpoint if monitor.last_good_checkpoint is not None: logger.error(f"Rolling back to {monitor.last_good_checkpoint}") step = load_checkpoint( model, optimizer, monitor.last_good_checkpoint ) monitor.reduce_lr(optimizer) monitor.consecutive_skips = 0 else: raise RuntimeError("No checkpoint available for rollback") # Log metrics every 100 steps if step % 100 == 0: logger.info( f"Step {step}: loss={loss_dict['ce_loss']:.4f}, " f"z_loss={loss_dict['z_loss']:.6f}, " f"grad_ema={monitor.grad_ema:.4f}, " f"lr={optimizer.param_groups[0]['lr']:.2e}" )
Production considerations: In distributed training, the stability check must be coordinated across all ranks. Use an all-reduce on a boolean "skip" flag so all workers agree on whether to skip a batch or rollback. Inconsistent decisions across ranks will desynchronize the data pipeline and corrupt gradient accumulation.
Testing your stability system: Inject synthetic instabilities during development—multiply a random gradient by 1000× every N steps—to verify that your detection, skip, and rollback logic works end-to-end before running on expensive GPU clusters. Never ship a stability monitor that hasn't been tested with synthetic spikes.
