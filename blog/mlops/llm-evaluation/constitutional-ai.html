Constitutional AI: Self-Supervised Alignment

MLOps Series LLM Evaluation & Safety

Aligning large language models with human values is one of the hardest open problems in AI safety. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) was the breakthrough that made ChatGPT-style models possible, but it carries deep structural limitations: it's expensive, inconsistent across annotators, and struggles to scale. Constitutional AI (CAI), introduced by Anthropic in 2022, proposes a radical alternative — let the model align itself by critiquing and revising its own outputs against an explicit set of principles, a "constitution."

This post dissects the CAI framework end-to-end: why RLHF falls short, how constitutional principles are designed, the self-critique chain that replaces human labeling, the RLAIF training pipeline, and how you can apply these concepts to your own models.

RLHF Limitations & Motivation for CAI

RLHF trains a reward model from human preference labels, then optimises the language model against that reward signal using Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO). While effective, the approach suffers from several well-documented failure modes:

RLHF

  • Requires 50k–100k+ human preference labels
  • Annotator bias leaks into the reward model
  • Reward hacking: model exploits reward signal quirks
  • Hard to update — relabel everything on policy change
  • Opaque: no explicit statement of desired behaviour

Constitutional AI

  • Principles replace human labels for critique
  • Consistent: same rules applied to every output
  • Transparent: constitution is human-readable
  • Easy to update — edit the principles, retrain
  • Scales without proportional human cost
Critical nuance: CAI does not eliminate humans from alignment. Humans write the constitution, audit outputs, and validate that the principles produce the desired behaviour. CAI shifts the human role from labeling thousands of examples to designing a small set of high-quality rules.

Designing Constitutional Principles

A constitutional principle is a natural-language instruction that tells the model what properties a good response should have. Anthropic's original paper uses 16 principles drawn from sources including the UN Declaration of Human Rights, Apple's Terms of Service, and DeepMind's Sparrow rules. Each principle targets a specific failure mode.

Well-crafted principles share several properties:

  1. Specificity — "Do not generate instructions for synthesising illegal drugs" is better than "Be safe."
  2. Actionability — The model must be able to evaluate compliance in a single pass.
  3. Non-contradiction — Principles should not conflict with each other; resolve tensions explicitly.
  4. Prioritisation — When principles conflict (helpfulness vs harmlessness), the constitution should state which wins.
# Example constitutional principles (simplified from Anthropic's paper)

principles = [

Design tip: Start with 5–10 principles covering your top failure categories — toxicity, misinformation, refusal over-sensitivity, and privacy leakage. Iterate by running red-team prompts and adding principles for any uncovered failure mode.

The Critique-Revision Loop

The heart of CAI is a two-step self-supervision process. Given a potentially harmful model output, the model is prompted to (1) critique the response against one or more constitutional principles, then (2) revise the response to fix the identified violations. This loop can be repeated multiple times, progressively refining the output.

Concretely, the critique prompt is constructed by appending the principle text to a standardised template. The model's critique output identifies specific issues. The revision prompt then asks the model to rewrite its original response, incorporating the critique feedback. Anthropic found that chaining 3–4 critique-revision rounds produces substantially better alignment than a single pass, with diminishing returns beyond that. 

def critique_revision_chain(model, response, principles, num_rounds=3):
    """Run iterative critique-revision on a model response."""
    current = response

    for round_i in range(num_rounds):
        for principle in principles:
            # Step 1: Critique
            critique_prompt = (
                f"Identify specific ways the following response violates "
                f"this principle: '{principle['text']}'\n\n"
                f"Response: {current}\n\nCritique:"
            )
            critique = model.generate(critique_prompt)

            # Step 2: Revision
            revision_prompt = (
                f"Rewrite the following response to address the critique.\n\n"
                f"Original: {current}\n"
                f"Critique: {critique}\n\n"
                f"Revised response:"
            )
            current = model.generate(revision_prompt)

    return current
Watch out: The critique-revision loop can cause the model to become overly cautious, refusing even benign requests. This is the alignment tax — a loss of helpfulness in exchange for safety. Balancing this tradeoff is the subject of Section 6.

RL from AI Feedback (RLAIF)

The second stage of CAI replaces human preference labels with AI-generated preference labels. Instead of showing two responses to a human and asking "which is better?", the model itself evaluates both responses against the constitutional principles and selects the preferred one. This process is called Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF).

The RLAIF pipeline proceeds as follows:

  1. Generate pairs — For each prompt, sample two responses from the SL-CAI model (the model fine-tuned on critique-revision data).
  2. AI comparison — Present both responses to the model alongside the constitutional principles. The model selects the response that better adheres to the constitution.
  3. Train reward model — Use the AI-generated preference pairs to train a reward model, identical to the RLHF reward model architecture.
  4. PPO fine-tuning — Optimise the language model against the AI-trained reward model using PPO, just as in standard RLHF.
def generate_ai_preference(model, prompt, principles):
    """Generate a preference pair using AI feedback."""
    # Sample two responses from the SL-CAI model
    response_a = model.generate(prompt, temperature=1.0)
    response_b = model.generate(prompt, temperature=1.0)

    # Build the comparison prompt
    principles_text = "\n".join([p["text"] for p in principles])
    comparison_prompt = (
        f"Consider the following principles:\n{principles_text}\n\n"
        f"Which response better follows these principles?\n\n"
        f"(A) {response_a}\n(B) {response_b}\n\n"
        f"The response that better follows the principles is ("
    )

    # Model picks A or B
    choice = model.generate(comparison_prompt, max_tokens=1)
    preferred = response_a if choice.strip() == "A" else response_b
    rejected  = response_b if choice.strip() == "A" else response_a

    return {"prompt": prompt, "preferred": preferred, "rejected": rejected}
Key result from Anthropic: RLAIF-trained models achieved comparable or better harmlessness scores than RLHF-trained models, while retaining more helpfulness. The AI feedback was also significantly more consistent than human annotator labels (Cohen's κ of 0.85 vs 0.60 for humans on the same comparison tasks).

CAI Training Pipeline

Putting it all together, the full Constitutional AI pipeline has two distinct phases: a supervised learning (SL) phase that generates the critique-revision data, and an RL phase that trains against AI-generated preferences.

Phase 1 (SL-CAI) produces a model that has learned to avoid harmful outputs by observing its own corrected behaviour. This is analogous to a student reviewing their own exam mistakes. However, SL alone cannot fully close the gap because the model can still generate harmful outputs that slip through the critique chain. Phase 2 (RL-CAI) provides the reinforcement signal needed to actively push the model's distribution away from harmful completions.

SL-CAI Output Quality

  • Significantly less harmful than base model
  • Sometimes overly verbose or hedging
  • Critique chains leave artifacts in style
  • Good starting point but not final quality

RL-CAI Output Quality

  • Near-zero harmful completions on benchmarks
  • More natural, concise writing style
  • Better helpfulness-harmlessness balance
  • Competitive with best RLHF models

Harmlessness vs Helpfulness Tradeoff

Every alignment technique faces a fundamental tension: a model that refuses everything is perfectly harmless but completely useless. Conversely, a model that answers everything is maximally helpful but inevitably dangerous. CAI addresses this tradeoff through principle prioritisation and careful constitution design.

Anthropic's results demonstrate that the RL-CAI model achieves a Pareto improvement over RLHF on the helpfulness-harmlessness frontier:

  • On the HHH (Helpful, Honest, Harmless) benchmark, RL-CAI scored 4% higher on harmlessness with only 1% lower helpfulness.
  • The model was significantly less likely to produce "evasive refusals" — generic refusals that don't explain why a request was declined.
  • When asked to explain its reasoning, the CAI model could cite specific principles, making refusals more transparent.
Practical tip: To avoid over-refusal, include explicit helpfulness principles in your constitution: "When a request is safe and benign, respond fully and directly without unnecessary caveats or disclaimers." This counterbalances the natural tendency of safety-focused principles to encourage excessive caution.

Strategies for managing the tradeoff in your own systems:

  1. Tiered principles — Group principles into "hard constraints" (never violate) and "soft preferences" (optimise for when possible).
  2. Domain-specific tuning — A medical chatbot needs different harmlessness thresholds than a creative writing assistant.
  3. A/B test refusal rates — Track the percentage of benign queries that receive refusals and set a maximum acceptable rate.
  4. User feedback loop — Collect "this was unhelpful" signals to identify over-cautious patterns and refine principles.

Practical Implementation

You don't need Anthropic's compute budget to benefit from CAI ideas. The core concepts — explicit principles, self-critique, and AI-generated preferences — can be applied at any scale. Below is a practical pipeline for applying CAI-style alignment to a fine-tuned model. 

import json
from dataclasses import dataclass
from typing import List, Dict

# Step 1: Define your constitution
@dataclass
class Constitution:
    principles: List[Dict[str, str]]
    priority_order: List[str]  # principle IDs in priority order

    def get_critique_prompt(self, response: str, principle_id: str) -> str:
        principle = next(p for p in self.principles if p["id"] == principle_id)
        return (
            f"Critique the following response based on this principle:\n"
            f"Principle: {principle['text']}\n\n"
            f"Response: {response}\n\nCritique:"
        )

    def get_revision_prompt(self, response: str, critique: str) -> str:
        return (
            f"Revise the response to address the following critique.\n"
            f"Critique: {critique}\n"
            f"Original: {response}\n\nRevised:"
        )

# Step 2: Generate SL-CAI training data
def generate_sl_cai_data(model, prompts, constitution, rounds=3):
    training_pairs = []
    for prompt in prompts:
        response = model.generate(prompt)
        revised = critique_revision_chain(model, response, constitution.principles, rounds)
        training_pairs.append({"prompt": prompt, "completion": revised})
    return training_pairs

# Step 3: Generate RLAIF preference data
def generate_rlaif_data(sl_model, judge_model, prompts, constitution, n_pairs=2):
    preference_data = []
    for prompt in prompts:
        pair = generate_ai_preference(judge_model, prompt, constitution.principles)
        preference_data.append(pair)
    return preference_data

# Step 4: Train reward model and run PPO (pseudocode)
def train_cai_pipeline(base_model, red_team_prompts, constitution):
    # Phase 1: Supervised fine-tuning
    sl_data = generate_sl_cai_data(base_model, red_team_prompts, constitution)
    sl_model = supervised_finetune(base_model, sl_data)

    # Phase 2: RLAIF
    pref_data = generate_rlaif_data(sl_model, sl_model, red_team_prompts, constitution)
    reward_model = train_reward_model(pref_data)
    final_model = ppo_finetune(sl_model, reward_model)

    return final_model
Resource reality check: Full RL-CAI training requires significant compute. For smaller teams, consider applying only Phase 1 (SL-CAI) — the critique-revision fine-tuning alone yields substantial safety improvements. Alternatively, use a stronger model (e.g., GPT-4) as the critique judge and fine-tune a smaller model on the resulting data, a form of distillation-based alignment.

Production checklist for CAI-style alignment:

  • Red-team dataset: Curate 1,000+ adversarial prompts covering your risk categories — toxicity, misinformation, jailbreaks, PII leakage.
  • Constitution versioning: Track your principles in version control. Every change should trigger a re-evaluation on your red-team set.
  • Evaluation harness: Automate harmlessness and helpfulness scoring. Use an LLM judge (with principles) as one evaluator, plus rule-based checks for known patterns.
  • Monitoring: In production, log refusal rates, user satisfaction, and flagged outputs. Alert on sudden spikes in either refusals or harmful content.
  • Iterative refinement: Treat your constitution as a living document. Review flagged outputs weekly and update principles to close gaps.

Limitations & Future Directions

Constitutional AI is a significant step forward, but it is not a complete solution to the alignment problem. Key limitations include:

  • Principle completeness: No finite set of rules can cover every possible harmful output. Adversarial users will find gaps.
  • Self-evaluation reliability: The model's ability to critique itself is bounded by its own capabilities. Subtle biases or factual errors may survive the critique chain.
  • Cultural context: Principles encode the values of their authors. What counts as "harmful" varies across cultures, and a single constitution may not generalise globally.
  • Emergent behaviours: As models become more capable, they may learn to satisfy the letter of principles while violating their spirit — a form of constitutional "gaming."

Active research directions that build on CAI:

  1. Democratic constitutions — Anthropic's "Collective Constitutional AI" experiment lets users vote on principles, distributing value alignment decisions.
  2. Multi-agent debate — Instead of self-critique, have multiple models debate the quality of a response, reducing self-evaluation blind spots.
  3. Recursive reward modelling — Use a chain of increasingly capable models to evaluate alignment, each one checking the previous.
  4. Constitutional fine-tuning with DPO — Replace the PPO phase with Direct Preference Optimisation for simpler, more stable training.
The bottom line: Constitutional AI transforms alignment from a data labeling problem into a rule design problem. By making the alignment criteria explicit, auditable, and iteratively refinable, CAI provides a more scalable and transparent path to safe AI systems. Whether you're training a frontier model or fine-tuning a small domain-specific LLM, the principles of self-critique and explicit constitutions can meaningfully improve your model's safety profile.
