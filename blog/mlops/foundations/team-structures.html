ML Team Structures

MLOps Series Foundations

Introduction

Building a great ML model is only half the battle. How you organize the people who build, deploy, and maintain those models often determines whether ML delivers sustained business value or slowly becomes shelf-ware.

Conway's Law tells us that the architecture of a system mirrors the communication structures of the organization that produces it. ML systems are no exception. A team that is siloed away from product engineers will produce hand-off-heavy workflows, longer feedback loops, and models that drift in production without anyone noticing.

In this post — number 6 of 12 in the MLOps Foundations series — we explore the most common ML team topologies, the trade-offs of each, and a decision framework to help you pick (or evolve towards) the structure that fits your organization's maturity and goals.

Core principle: The "best" team structure is the one that minimizes the distance between an insight in data and a change in the product. Everything else is secondary.

Key Roles

Before choosing a topology, it helps to define the roles that will fill it. Titles vary between companies, but the underlying responsibilities cluster into four archetypes.

Platform ML Engineers

Platform ML Engineers build and operate the shared infrastructure that every ML project depends on — feature stores, training pipelines, model registries, serving frameworks, and monitoring dashboards. They care deeply about reliability, latency, and developer experience. 

// Platform ML Engineer — typical responsibilities
class PlatformMLEngineer {
  build_feature_store()          // shared feature computation
  maintain_training_cluster()     // GPU scheduling, cost tracking
  operate_model_registry()        // versioning, lineage, access control
  provide_serving_framework()     // low-latency inference APIs
  build_monitoring_dashboards()   // data drift, model quality alerts
}

Applied ML Engineers

Applied ML Engineers sit closest to the product. They translate business requirements into model specifications, select appropriate algorithms, and own the end-to-end lifecycle from training through production deployment.

Data Scientists

Data Scientists focus on exploration and analysis. They discover patterns, build prototypes, and validate hypotheses. In many organizations the Data Scientist hands a prototype to an ML Engineer for "productionization," though modern teams increasingly expect Data Scientists to own deployment too.

ML Researchers

ML Researchers push the frontier — experimenting with novel architectures, loss functions, and training strategies. Their work has a longer feedback loop and a higher failure rate, but successful breakthroughs can become lasting competitive advantages.

Common anti-pattern: Merging all four roles into a single "ML Engineer" title. The breadth of skills needed is enormous, and expecting one person to do everything leads to burnout and mediocre infrastructure and mediocre models.

Centralized Teams

In the centralized model, a single ML team serves every business unit. Product teams submit requests — "we need a churn model," "we need a recommendation engine" — and the ML team prioritizes, builds, and delivers.

Advantages

  • Consistency: Shared tooling, standards, and review processes across all projects.
  • Knowledge sharing: ML practitioners sit together and learn from each other.
  • Efficient resource use: GPUs, feature pipelines, and datasets are shared rather than duplicated.

Disadvantages

  • Bottleneck: A single team can't scale to meet every product team's demands.
  • Context loss: ML engineers rarely develop deep domain expertise in any one product.
  • Prioritization politics: Which business unit gets ML resources this quarter?
When it works best: Early-stage ML organizations (fewer than ~10 ML practitioners) where standardization and knowledge-building outweigh speed of delivery.

Embedded Teams

The embedded model places ML engineers directly inside product teams. Each product squad has its own ML capability, reporting to the product lead rather than an ML manager.

✅ Strengths

  • Deep domain knowledge — the ML engineer lives and breathes the product.
  • Short feedback loops — changes go from notebook to production within the same sprint.
  • Aligned incentives — ML work is directly tied to product metrics.

⚠️ Weaknesses

  • Duplicated infrastructure — every team reinvents the training pipeline.
  • Isolation — ML engineers lose touch with the broader ML community inside the company.
  • Uneven quality — standards vary widely between squads.
// Embedded team — typical request flow
product_squad.plan_sprint()
  → embedded_ml_eng.scope_feature(user_story)
  → embedded_ml_eng.train_model(local_pipeline)
  → embedded_ml_eng.deploy(squad_infra)       // no cross-team dependency
  → product_squad.measure_impact()
Watch out: Embedded ML engineers often get pulled into general backend work when there is no active ML project. Over time this erodes their ML skills and makes hiring harder — candidates want to do ML, not fix CRUD endpoints.

Platform Team Model

The platform team model is a hybrid: a dedicated ML Platform team builds and maintains shared infrastructure, while Applied ML Engineers and Data Scientists live inside product teams but consume the platform's APIs and tools.

Why this is popular at scale

The platform model gives product teams autonomy while eliminating duplicated infrastructure. The ML Platform team acts as an internal product team — its customers are the Applied ML Engineers in each squad. 

// Platform team model — request flow
applied_ml_eng.define_features(feature_store_sdk)
applied_ml_eng.submit_training_job(platform_api)
platform.schedule_gpu(job)               // platform owns scheduling
applied_ml_eng.register_model(registry)
platform.deploy_canary(model, traffic=5%)  // platform owns rollout
platform.monitor(model, alerts)            // platform owns observability
Tip: Treat the platform team's APIs like a public product — versioned, documented, with SLAs. If internal customers have to read the source code to use your platform, you've already failed.

Interfaces Between Teams

Regardless of which topology you choose, the interfaces between teams determine how smoothly work flows. Poorly defined interfaces create ambiguity, duplicated effort, and finger-pointing when something breaks.

API Contracts

The platform team should expose well-defined APIs — REST or gRPC — with versioning, backward compatibility guarantees, and clear deprecation policies. Every interaction should go through these APIs, never through direct database access or shared file systems. 

// Example: Model deployment contract
interface DeploymentContract {
  model_artifact:   URI           // S3 / GCS path to model
  model_version:    SemVer        // e.g. 2.4.1
  serving_config: {
    max_latency_ms: int           // p99 target
    min_throughput:  int           // requests/sec
    canary_percent:  float         // initial traffic split
  }
  owner:            TeamID        // who gets paged?
  rollback_policy:  auto | manual // on quality regression
}

Communication Patterns

Synchronous

  • Weekly cross-team stand-ups
  • Design reviews for new model architectures
  • Incident war-rooms when production models degrade

Asynchronous

  • RFCs and design docs shared in a central wiki
  • Slack channels per model domain (#ml-search, #ml-ads)
  • Automated alerts routed to the right team via PagerDuty

Ownership Boundaries

A clear RACI matrix prevents "who owns this?" arguments at 2 AM. A typical split:

  • Platform team owns: infrastructure reliability, GPU scheduling, serving SLAs, monitoring pipeline.
  • Applied ML team owns: model quality, feature engineering, A/B test design, business impact.
  • Data Engineering owns: data pipelines, data quality, schema evolution, warehouse SLAs.
Gray zone alert: Feature pipelines often fall between Data Engineering and ML Platform. Define ownership explicitly in your team charter — the ambiguity will bite you during an outage.

Scaling ML Teams

Team structures are not static. As an organization's ML maturity grows, the structure must evolve. Most companies follow a predictable trajectory:

Stage 1 — The First Data Scientist

A single Data Scientist joins a product team. They use Jupyter notebooks, push models to production via ad-hoc scripts, and own everything end-to-end. This works for one or two models.

Stage 2 — Centralized ML Team

ML headcount grows to 3–8. A centralized team forms, bringing consistency but also creating a request queue. Deployment is still manual or semi-automated.

Stage 3 — Platform Emergence

At 8–20 ML practitioners, the pain of duplicated infrastructure becomes acute. A small platform sub-team (2–3 engineers) splits off to build shared tooling. Applied ML engineers begin to embed in product squads.

Stage 4 — Full Platform + Embedded

Beyond 20 ML practitioners, the platform team becomes a first-class product team with its own PM. Every product squad has at least one Applied ML Engineer. The centralized ML team may still exist as a "center of excellence" for research and best practices. 

// Maturity evolution pseudo-code
if ml_headcount < 3:
    use_embedded()             // one DS per product team
elif ml_headcount < 8:
    use_centralized()          // build shared standards
elif ml_headcount < 20:
    split_platform_team()      // dedicated infra engineers
else:
    full_platform_embedded()   // platform + embedded applied ML
Tip: Don't skip stages. Each stage builds institutional knowledge that the next stage depends on. Jumping straight to a platform team without first experiencing the pain of duplication leads to over-engineering.

Choosing Your Structure

There is no one-size-fits-all answer. Use the decision framework below to evaluate which topology fits your current situation — and revisit it every 6–12 months as your team grows.

Decision Framework

Choose Centralized If…

  • You have fewer than 8 ML practitioners.
  • ML is a new discipline in the organization.
  • You need consistent standards and tooling first.
  • Product teams don't yet know how to consume ML.

Choose Embedded If…

  • Speed of iteration matters more than consistency.
  • Product teams have strong engineering cultures.
  • Models are deeply product-specific (e.g., NLP for legal).
  • You can accept some infrastructure duplication.

Choose Platform + Embedded If…

  • You have 10+ ML practitioners across multiple teams.
  • Infrastructure duplication is causing real pain.
  • You need both speed and consistency.
  • You can staff a dedicated platform team (≥ 3 engineers).

Hybrid Considerations

  • Many orgs blend models — e.g., centralized research + embedded applied.
  • Reporting lines matter: dotted-line to ML leadership preserves community.
  • Rotate engineers between platform and applied roles every 12–18 months.
  • Invest in internal ML guilds regardless of formal structure.

Anti-Patterns to Avoid

// Common organizational anti-patterns
anti_pattern "Ivory Tower"
  // Research team builds models nobody deploys

anti_pattern "Shadow ML"
  // Product engineers train models in secret, no review or monitoring

anti_pattern "Platform Without Customers"
  // Platform team builds infra before understanding user needs

anti_pattern "The Hero"
  // One senior ML engineer owns every model — single point of failure
Final thought: The goal is not to land on the "perfect" org chart. It's to create feedback loops that let your team detect problems early, share knowledge freely, and ship models that actually move business metrics. Revisit your structure as you grow — what works at 5 people will break at 50.
