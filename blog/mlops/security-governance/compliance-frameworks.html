Compliance: EU AI Act, NIST, ISO

MLOps Series Security & Governance

The regulatory landscape for AI is evolving rapidly. In 2024 alone, the EU AI Act became law, NIST finalized its AI Risk Management Framework, and ISO published 42001 as the first international management system standard for AI. For ML engineers and platform teams, these frameworks translate into concrete technical requirements: logging, documentation, testing, monitoring, and governance controls that must be built into the ML system—not bolted on after the fact.

This post breaks down the three major compliance frameworks, explains what they require in practical engineering terms, and provides automation strategies for continuous compliance verification.

Why It Matters: Non-compliance with the EU AI Act can result in fines up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover—whichever is higher. Understanding these frameworks is not optional for teams deploying AI in regulated markets.

EU AI Act

The EU AI Act is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation. It takes a risk-based approach, classifying AI systems into four tiers with progressively stricter requirements. The Act applies to any organization deploying AI systems that affect EU citizens, regardless of where the organization is headquartered.

Risk Categories

  • Unacceptable Risk (Banned) — Social scoring by governments, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces (with narrow exceptions), manipulation of vulnerable groups, emotion recognition in workplaces/schools.
  • High Risk — Biometric identification, critical infrastructure management, education access, employment decisions, credit scoring, law enforcement, migration/asylum processing, justice administration. These require full compliance with Chapter III obligations.
  • Limited Risk — Chatbots, deepfake generators, emotion recognition systems. Must comply with transparency obligations (users must know they're interacting with AI).
  • Minimal Risk — Spam filters, AI-enabled video games, inventory management. No specific obligations beyond existing law.
Regulatory Alert: General-purpose AI models (including foundation models like GPT-4) have their own set of obligations under the Act. Providers must publish training data summaries, comply with copyright law, and—for models with "systemic risk"—conduct adversarial testing and report serious incidents.

High-Risk Technical Requirements

For high-risk AI systems, the EU AI Act mandates the following technical controls. Each maps directly to MLOps engineering work:

  1. Risk Management System (Art. 9) — Continuous identification, analysis, and mitigation of risks throughout the system lifecycle. Implement automated risk scoring in your CI/CD pipeline.
  2. Data Governance (Art. 10) — Training, validation, and test datasets must be relevant, representative, free of errors, and complete. Document data provenance and bias assessments.
  3. Technical Documentation (Art. 11) — Detailed system description, design specifications, development process, monitoring capabilities, and performance metrics. Maintain versioned documentation alongside model artifacts.
  4. Record-Keeping (Art. 12) — Automatic logging of events throughout the system lifetime. Logs must enable traceability from input to output.
  5. Transparency (Art. 13) — System must be sufficiently transparent that deployers can interpret outputs and use them appropriately.
  6. Human Oversight (Art. 14) — Design the system so humans can effectively oversee its operation, including the ability to override or reverse decisions.
  7. Accuracy, Robustness, Cybersecurity (Art. 15) — Declared accuracy levels, resilience to adversarial attacks, and protection against unauthorized access.

NIST AI RMF

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) provides a voluntary, flexible framework for managing AI risks. While not legally binding like the EU AI Act, it is widely adopted as a best-practice standard in the US and is referenced by federal procurement policies. Many organizations use NIST AI RMF as the foundation for their internal AI governance programs.

Core Functions

The NIST AI RMF organizes AI risk management into four core functions, each with subcategories:

  • GOVERN — Establish organizational AI governance structures, policies, and accountability. Define roles (AI Risk Officer, Model Validation Team), create an AI use-case registry, and set risk tolerance thresholds.
  • MAP — Contextualize the AI system within its operational environment. Identify stakeholders, intended uses, known limitations, and potential failure modes. This is where you document your system's risk profile.
  • MEASURE — Quantify risks using metrics, testing, and monitoring. Implement bias audits, robustness testing, performance benchmarks, and fairness metrics. Automate measurement in CI/CD.
  • MANAGE — Prioritize and act on identified risks. Implement mitigations, establish incident response procedures, and monitor residual risk over time.

EU AI Act

  • Legally binding regulation
  • Risk-based classification (4 tiers)
  • Specific technical mandates for high-risk
  • Fines up to €35M or 7% revenue
  • Applies to EU market participants
  • Conformity assessment required

NIST AI RMF

  • Voluntary framework
  • Function-based (Govern/Map/Measure/Manage)
  • Flexible—adapts to organizational context
  • No direct penalties
  • Referenced by US federal procurement
  • Self-assessment approach

NIST AI RMF Playbook

NIST provides a companion Playbook with suggested actions for each subcategory. For ML engineers, the most actionable subcategories are: MEASURE 2.5 (AI system fairness assessment), MEASURE 2.6 (robustness/reliability testing), MAP 3.5 (documentation of known limitations), and MANAGE 2.2 (mechanisms to supersede or deactivate the system). Map these directly to your MLOps pipeline stages.

ISO 42001 & Related Standards

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 is the first international standard specifying requirements for an AI Management System (AIMS). It follows the ISO high-level structure (Annex SL), making it compatible with existing management systems like ISO 27001 (information security) and ISO 9001 (quality management). Organizations already certified to these standards can integrate AI governance into their existing frameworks.

Key Requirements

  • AI Policy — Executive-level commitment to responsible AI development and deployment, communicated across the organization.
  • AI Risk Assessment — Systematic identification and evaluation of risks specific to AI systems, including data bias, model drift, adversarial vulnerability, and unintended consequences.
  • AI System Impact Assessment — Evaluation of potential impacts on individuals, groups, and society. Similar to a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) but broader in scope.
  • AI System Lifecycle Management — Controls across design, development, deployment, operation, and retirement. Maps closely to MLOps lifecycle stages.
  • Third-Party AI Management — Due diligence for AI systems or components sourced from external providers (APIs, pre-trained models, data vendors).
Related Standards: ISO 42001 sits within a family of AI standards. ISO 23894 provides AI risk management guidance, ISO 42005 covers AI impact assessment, and ISO 22989 defines AI terminology. Together they form a comprehensive standards ecosystem for AI governance.

Certification Path

ISO 42001 certification follows the standard ISO audit process: gap analysis, implementation of required controls, internal audit, and then Stage 1 (documentation review) and Stage 2 (operational audit) by an accredited certification body. Certification is valid for 3 years with annual surveillance audits. For ML teams, this means maintaining continuous evidence of compliance—automated documentation, version-controlled policies, and auditable pipelines are essential.

Documentation Requirements

All three frameworks converge on a common theme: comprehensive, maintained documentation. The challenge for ML teams is that traditional software documentation practices are insufficient. ML documentation must also cover data provenance, model behavior characterization, fairness analysis, and known limitations.

Minimum Documentation Set

  1. System Description — Purpose, intended use, target users, deployment context, and architecture diagram.
  2. Data Documentation — Data sources, collection methods, preprocessing steps, bias assessments, representativeness analysis, and versioning (use Model Cards and Datasheets for Datasets formats).
  3. Model Documentation — Architecture, training procedure, hyperparameters, evaluation metrics, known failure modes, and performance across demographic groups.
  4. Risk Assessment — Identified risks, likelihood and impact scores, mitigation strategies, residual risk acceptance rationale.
  5. Monitoring Plan — Metrics monitored in production, drift detection thresholds, alerting rules, escalation procedures, and human oversight mechanisms.
  6. Incident Response Plan — Procedures for model failures, data breaches, adversarial attacks, and performance degradation. Include rollback procedures and communication templates.

Common Mistake: Generating documentation once at deployment and never updating it. Compliance frameworks require that documentation be maintained throughout the system's lifetime. Automate documentation generation in your CI/CD pipeline so it stays in sync with the actual system.

Compliance Mapping

Organizations operating across jurisdictions often need to satisfy multiple frameworks simultaneously. A compliance mapping identifies overlapping requirements so you can implement a single control that satisfies multiple frameworks, avoiding redundant work.

Cross-Framework Mapping

The table below maps key technical requirements across the three major frameworks. Each row represents a technical control, and the columns show which framework article or function mandates it:

Data Governance Controls

  • EU AI Act: Art. 10 (Data and data governance)
  • NIST RMF: MAP 2.1, MEASURE 2.5
  • ISO 42001: Annex B.3 (Data for AI systems)
  • Implementation: Data versioning, bias audits, schema validation, lineage tracking

Logging & Traceability

  • EU AI Act: Art. 12 (Record-keeping)
  • NIST RMF: MEASURE 2.6, MANAGE 4.1
  • ISO 42001: Clause 9.1 (Monitoring, measurement)
  • Implementation: Immutable audit logs, trace IDs, hash-chain verification

Risk Assessment

  • EU AI Act: Art. 9 (Risk management system)
  • NIST RMF: MAP (all), MEASURE (all)
  • ISO 42001: Clause 6.1 (Actions to address risks)
  • Implementation: Automated risk scoring, failure mode analysis, adversarial testing

Human Oversight

  • EU AI Act: Art. 14 (Human oversight)
  • NIST RMF: GOVERN 1.3, MANAGE 2.2
  • ISO 42001: Annex B.5 (AI system operation)
  • Implementation: Override mechanisms, confidence thresholds, human-in-the-loop workflows

Implementation Strategy: Build compliance checks as a reusable library that plugs into your CI/CD pipeline as a deployment gate. Start with the highest-risk requirements (EU AI Act Art. 9-15 for high-risk systems), then expand coverage to NIST and ISO requirements incrementally. A single compliance check suite can satisfy all three frameworks.

Compliance is not a one-time checkbox—it is a continuous process. As your models evolve, as regulations are updated, and as new risks emerge, your compliance controls must adapt. Automate everything you can, maintain living documentation, and treat compliance as a first-class engineering concern alongside performance and reliability.
