GDPR for ML: Right to Explanation, Erasure

MLOps Series Security & Governance

Introduction

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) has fundamentally reshaped how organisations build and deploy machine learning systems in the European Union and beyond. Unlike traditional software, ML models memorise patterns from personal data, creating unique compliance challenges that span the entire model lifecycle.

When a data subject exercises their rights under GDPR, the implications ripple through your entire ML pipeline: from training data stores and feature engineering to deployed model weights and cached predictions. This post explores the key GDPR provisions that directly affect ML systems and offers practical engineering strategies for compliance.

Scope: This post covers the technical and architectural implications of GDPR for ML engineers. It is not legal advice. Always consult qualified legal counsel for your specific jurisdiction and use case.

GDPR Overview for ML Systems

Several GDPR articles have direct consequences for machine learning practitioners. Understanding these articles is the first step to building compliant systems.

Key Articles

Articles 13–15 (Transparency & Access) require that data subjects be informed about automated decision-making, including "meaningful information about the logic involved." For ML systems, this means you must be able to explain why a model made a particular prediction.

Article 17 (Right to Erasure) grants individuals the right to have their personal data deleted. When that data was used to train a model, the question becomes: does the model itself constitute personal data?

Article 22 (Automated Decision-Making) gives data subjects the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing that produce legal or similarly significant effects. This has profound implications for credit scoring, hiring, and insurance ML models.

Article 22 is not a blanket ban. Automated decisions are permitted when they are necessary for a contract, authorised by law, or based on explicit consent. However, even when permitted, safeguards must exist — including the right to obtain human intervention and to contest the decision.

Personal Data in the ML Context

GDPR's definition of personal data is broad: any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person. In ML, personal data can appear in:

  • Training datasets — the raw records used to fit the model
  • Feature stores — engineered features derived from personal records
  • Model weights — neural networks can memorise individual training examples
  • Prediction logs — input/output pairs cached for monitoring
  • Embedding spaces — dense vector representations of user behaviour

Right to Explanation

The right to explanation derives primarily from Articles 13(2)(f), 14(2)(g), and 15(1)(h), which require controllers to provide "meaningful information about the logic involved" in automated decision-making. Recital 71 further clarifies that data subjects should have the right to "obtain an explanation of the decision reached."

What Constitutes a Sufficient Explanation?

The regulation does not prescribe a specific technical approach. In practice, organisations must balance faithfulness (how accurately the explanation reflects the model's reasoning) with intelligibility (how understandable it is to a non-technical person).

Global Explanations

Describe the model's overall behaviour and which features matter most across all predictions. Useful for auditors and regulators.

Methods: SHAP summary plots, permutation importance, partial dependence plots.

Local Explanations

Explain a single prediction for a specific individual. Required when a data subject asks "why was my loan denied?"

Methods: LIME, SHAP waterfall, counterfactual explanations.

Counterfactual Explanations

Counterfactuals are particularly GDPR-friendly because they answer the question: "What would need to change for a different outcome?" For example: "Your loan would have been approved if your income were €5,000 higher."

# Generate counterfactual explanation for a denied loan import dice_ml # Create a DiCE explainer with the trained model explainer = dice_ml.Dice(data_interface, model_interface) # Generate 3 diverse counterfactuals for the denied applicant counterfactuals = explainer.generate_counterfactuals( query_instance=denied_applicant, total_CFs=3, desired_class="approved", features_to_vary=["income", "employment_length", "debt_ratio"] ) # Format for data subject communication for cf in counterfactuals.cf_examples_list: print(cf.final_cfs_df)
Practical tip: Pre-compute explanations for common decision outcomes and cache them. When a data subject requests an explanation, you can deliver it within the 30-day GDPR response window without running expensive post-hoc analysis.

Right to Erasure

Article 17 grants the "right to be forgotten" — data subjects can request deletion of their personal data. For ML systems, this raises a critical question: is deleting the training record enough, or must the model itself be updated?

Erasure Scope

A thorough erasure response for ML must address multiple layers:

  • Raw data — remove the individual's records from training datasets and backups
  • Derived features — purge computed features in the feature store
  • Prediction logs — delete inference records containing the individual's data
  • Model weights — assess whether the model has memorised the individual's data
  • Downstream systems — propagate deletion to any systems consuming model outputs

Approaches to Model-Level Erasure

Full Retraining

Remove the data, retrain from scratch. Gold standard for compliance but computationally expensive — impractical for large models retrained infrequently.

Cost: High  |  Guarantee: Strong

Machine Unlearning

Surgically remove the influence of specific records from model weights without full retraining. Active research area with emerging practical methods.

Cost: Low–Medium  |  Guarantee: Varies

SISA Training

Sharded, Isolated, Sliced, and Aggregated training. Partition data into shards, train sub-models independently. To "forget," retrain only the affected shard.

Cost: Medium  |  Guarantee: Strong per shard

Differential Privacy

Train with DP guarantees so individual records have bounded influence. Does not eliminate memorisation but provides mathematical privacy bounds.

Cost: Low (at inference)  |  Guarantee: Probabilistic

Machine Unlearning

Machine unlearning is the process of removing the influence of specific training samples from a learned model without retraining from scratch. This is a rapidly evolving field with several promising approaches.

Exact vs. Approximate Unlearning

Exact unlearning produces a model that is statistically indistinguishable from one that was never trained on the removed data. Approximate unlearning relaxes this guarantee, accepting a bounded divergence measured by metrics like KL divergence or membership inference attack success rate.

SISA Training (Sharded Unlearning)

The SISA framework partitions training data into disjoint shards. Each shard trains an independent constituent model, and predictions are aggregated (e.g., majority vote or averaging). When a deletion request arrives, only the shard containing the target record is retrained.

# SISA Training: Sharded, Isolated, Sliced, Aggregated class SISATrainer: def __init__(self, n_shards=10, slices_per_shard=5): self.n_shards = n_shards self.slices_per_shard = slices_per_shard self.shard_models = {} self.data_index = {} # maps record_id → (shard, slice) def train(self, dataset): shards = partition(dataset, self.n_shards) for shard_id, shard_data in enumerate(shards): slices = split_into_slices(shard_data, self.slices_per_shard) model = create_base_model() for slice_id, slice_data in enumerate(slices): model.incremental_train(slice_data) save_checkpoint(model, shard_id, slice_id) self.shard_models[shard_id] = model def unlearn(self, record_id): shard_id, slice_id = self.data_index[record_id] # Only retrain from the affected slice onward model = load_checkpoint(shard_id, slice_id - 1) remaining_slices = get_slices(shard_id, from=slice_id) for s in remaining_slices: filtered = remove_record(s, record_id) model.incremental_train(filtered) self.shard_models[shard_id] = model def predict(self, x): preds = [m.predict(x) for m in self.shard_models.values()] return aggregate(preds)

Gradient-Based Unlearning

For models trained with SGD, influence functions approximate the effect of removing a training point. The Newton update step adjusts model parameters to approximate the leave-one-out retrained model:

# Approximate unlearning via influence functions def influence_unlearn(model, data_to_forget, full_train_data): # Compute the Hessian of the loss over the full dataset H = compute_hessian(model, full_train_data) H_inv = matrix_inverse(H) # or use LiSSA approximation # Compute gradient of the loss on the data to forget grad_forget = compute_gradient(model, data_to_forget) # Newton update: remove the influence of forgotten data delta_params = H_inv @ grad_forget / len(full_train_data) # Apply the parameter update for param, delta in zip(model.parameters(), delta_params): param.data += delta return model
Scalability concern: Computing the full Hessian is O(p²) in the number of parameters. For modern deep learning models with millions of parameters, approximate methods like LiSSA (Linear time Stochastic Second-order Algorithm) or Fisher information-based approaches are necessary.

Implementation Strategies

Building GDPR-compliant ML systems requires architectural decisions made early in the design process, not retrofitted after deployment.

Data Lineage Tracking

Maintain a complete lineage graph from raw records through features to model versions. When an erasure request arrives, you must be able to trace exactly which models, features, and predictions were influenced by the target record.

# Data lineage registry for GDPR compliance class LineageRegistry: def register_training_run(self, model_id, dataset_version, record_ids): # Map every training record to the models it influenced for rid in record_ids: self.db.insert("lineage", { "record_id": rid, "model_id": model_id, "dataset_version": dataset_version, "trained_at": now() }) def find_affected_models(self, record_id): # Identify all models trained on a specific record return self.db.query( "SELECT model_id FROM lineage WHERE record_id = ?", record_id ) def process_erasure(self, record_id): affected = self.find_affected_models(record_id) for model in affected: self.schedule_unlearning(model, record_id) self.delete_raw_data(record_id) self.purge_features(record_id) self.delete_prediction_logs(record_id)

Retraining Cadence Strategy

Rather than retraining on every erasure request, batch deletions and retrain periodically. This reduces cost while maintaining compliance — GDPR allows a 30-day response window.

Immediate Retraining

Retrain on every deletion request. Guarantees immediate compliance but is prohibitively expensive for large models or frequent requests.

Best for: Small models, low request volume.

Batched Retraining

Accumulate deletion requests and retrain periodically (e.g., weekly). Balances compliance with cost. Most practical for production systems.

Best for: Most production deployments.

Architecture Checklist

  • ☑ Maintain a record-to-model lineage graph
  • ☑ Implement data subject lookup APIs across all storage layers
  • ☑ Use SISA or sharded training to minimise retraining scope
  • ☑ Pre-compute local explanations for high-stakes decisions
  • ☑ Store model training provenance (dataset version, hyperparameters, code hash)
  • ☑ Deploy membership inference tests to verify unlearning effectiveness
  • ☑ Automate erasure propagation to downstream systems
  • ☑ Set up audit logging for all GDPR request processing
Remember: GDPR compliance is not a one-time checkbox — it is an ongoing architectural commitment. Design your ML systems with privacy as a first-class concern, and the operational burden of compliance becomes manageable.
