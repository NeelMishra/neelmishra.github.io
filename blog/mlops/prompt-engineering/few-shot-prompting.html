Few-Shot Prompting: In-Context Learning

MLOps Series Prompt Engineering

What Is In-Context Learning

In-context learning (ICL) is the ability of large language models to learn a task at inference time by conditioning on a handful of input–output examples provided directly in the prompt—without any gradient updates. The term was introduced in the GPT-3 paper (Brown et al., 2020), where the authors demonstrated that scaling model size dramatically improved the ability to "learn" from examples in the context window.

The key insight is that LLMs don't literally learn new parameters; instead, they locate and activate existing latent capabilities by recognizing the pattern structure of the examples. Research by Min et al. (2022) showed that even with randomly shuffled labels, models still improve on formatting—suggesting that ICL partly works by demonstrating the input–output format, not just the mapping itself.

Zero-shot vs. Few-shot vs. Many-shot: Zero-shot provides only an instruction. Few-shot adds 2–8 demonstration examples. Many-shot (50–200+ examples) leverages expanded context windows in models like GPT-4 Turbo and Claude 3 to push performance further, often approaching fine-tuning quality for structured tasks.

Few-shot prompting occupies a sweet spot: it offers strong task adaptation without training costs, data pipelines, or deployment complexity. The quality of your examples, however, matters enormously—often more than the instruction text itself.

Example Selection Strategies

Choosing the right examples is the single most impactful lever in few-shot prompting. Research consistently shows that example selection can swing accuracy by 20–30 percentage points on the same model with the same number of shots.

Semantic Similarity

The most effective general-purpose strategy is to select examples that are semantically similar to the test input. Liu et al. (2022) demonstrated this in their KATE (Knn-Augmented in-conText Example selection) method: embed your example pool and your test query with the same encoder, then retrieve the k nearest neighbors as demonstrations.

# Semantic similarity-based example selection
import numpy as np
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer

model = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")

def select_by_similarity(query, example_pool, k=4):
    """Select k most similar examples to the query."""
    q_emb = model.encode([query])
    pool_texts = [ex["input"] for ex in example_pool]
    pool_embs = model.encode(pool_texts)

    # Cosine similarity
    sims = np.dot(pool_embs, q_emb.T).flatten()
    sims /= (np.linalg.norm(pool_embs, axis=1) *
             np.linalg.norm(q_emb))

    top_k = np.argsort(sims)[-k:][::-1]
    return [example_pool[i] for i in top_k]

Diversity and Coverage

Similarity alone can cause redundancy—you might select four near-identical examples that demonstrate only one sub-pattern. A better approach balances similarity with diversity:

🎯 Similarity-Only

Pros: Strong when test inputs are clustered. Simple to implement.

Cons: Redundant examples. Fails on edge cases. Doesn't cover label space.

Best for: Narrow, well-defined tasks like sentiment on product reviews.

🌐 Diversity-Aware

Pros: Covers label space. Robust to distribution shift. Reduces bias.

Cons: More complex retrieval. May include less relevant examples.

Best for: Multi-class classification, complex reasoning tasks.

Label Coverage

For classification tasks, always ensure your examples cover every target label. If you're doing 4-class sentiment, include at least one example per class. Research by Zhao et al. (2021) showed that omitting a label from demonstrations causes models to almost never predict that label—a catastrophic failure mode.

Majority-label bias: If 6 of 8 examples share the same label, the model becomes biased toward predicting that label regardless of the input. Always balance your label distribution in demonstrations, or explicitly add a "Note: labels are uniformly distributed" instruction.

Task-Aware Selection

For reasoning tasks, select examples that demonstrate the specific reasoning pattern required. Su et al. (2023) showed that selecting examples with similar reasoning chains (not just similar surface inputs) improved chain-of-thought performance by 5–12% on math benchmarks. Consider encoding both the question type and solution strategy when building your retrieval index.

Ordering Effects

The order in which you present examples to an LLM has a measurable impact on performance. Lu et al. (2022) found that reordering the same set of few-shot examples could swing accuracy from near-random (54%) to near-optimal (93%) on SST-2 sentiment classification. Understanding these biases is essential for reliable few-shot systems.

Recency Bias

Models tend to weight examples closer to the test input (the end of the prompt) more heavily. This is the recency bias—a well-documented property of autoregressive language models where recent tokens have stronger influence on the next-token distribution. Practically, this means your most representative example should be placed last, immediately before the query.

Primacy Effects

The first example also gets disproportionate attention—it sets the "frame" for how the model interprets the task. If your first example is an outlier or ambiguous, it can poison the entire set. Use a clear, prototypical example as the opener to anchor the model's understanding of the task format.

Optimal Ordering Strategy

Based on the research, a reliable ordering heuristic is:

  1. Position 1: A clear, prototypical example that anchors the task format.
  2. Middle positions: Examples sorted by increasing similarity to the query, ensuring label diversity.
  3. Last position: The most similar example to the test input—this has the strongest influence due to recency bias.
# Ordering examples by ascending similarity (most similar last)
def order_examples(examples, query, encoder):
    """Order examples: prototype first, most similar last."""
    q_emb = encoder.encode([query])
    e_embs = encoder.encode([e["input"] for e in examples])

    sims = cosine_similarity(e_embs, q_emb).flatten()
    sorted_idx = np.argsort(sims)  # ascending → most similar last

    # Put the most "prototypical" example first
    prototype_idx = find_prototype(examples)
    ordered = [examples[prototype_idx]]
    ordered += [examples[i] for i in sorted_idx if i != prototype_idx]
    return ordered
Practical tip: If you can't compute similarity dynamically, a simple heuristic is to always place examples in a fixed order that alternates labels (positive, negative, positive, negative…). This alone eliminates most majority-label bias.

Formatting Best Practices

How you format few-shot examples matters almost as much as which examples you choose. Consistent, clearly delimited examples reduce ambiguity and help the model distinguish between demonstration content and the actual query.

Use Explicit Delimiters

Separate each example with clear boundaries. The model needs to understand where one demonstration ends and the next begins. Common delimiter patterns include triple dashes (---), numbered headers, or XML-like tags.

❌ Poor Formatting

Classify the sentiment.
The food was amazing. Positive
Service was slow. Negative
The ambiance was nice.

No clear boundary between examples. The model may confuse "The ambiance was nice" as part of the last example rather than the query.

✅ Good Formatting

Classify the sentiment as Positive or Negative.

Input: The food was amazing.
Sentiment: Positive
---
Input: Service was slow and rude.
Sentiment: Negative
---
Input: The ambiance was nice.
Sentiment:

Clear delimiters, consistent field names, and the query follows the same pattern with an incomplete output.

Consistent Structure

Every example should follow the exact same template. If one example uses "Input:" and another uses "Text:", the model receives a mixed signal. Key structural rules:

  • Identical field names across all examples (Input/Output, Question/Answer, etc.)
  • Same ordering of fields within each example
  • Matching output granularity—if some examples return single words and others return paragraphs, the model's output length becomes unpredictable
  • Include the query in the same format with an empty or partial output field to signal where generation should begin

Structured Output Formats

For tasks requiring structured outputs (JSON, tables, specific formats), include the exact structure in every example. Models are remarkably good at mirroring formatting—if all examples output valid JSON, the test query will almost always produce valid JSON too.

# Structured output few-shot template
TEMPLATE = """Extract entities from the text as JSON.

Text: Apple CEO Tim Cook announced new MacBooks at the event in Cupertino.
Entities: {{"people": ["Tim Cook"], "orgs": ["Apple"], "locations": ["Cupertino"], "products": ["MacBooks"]}}
---
Text: Satya Nadella discussed Azure growth during the earnings call in Seattle.
Entities: {{"people": ["Satya Nadella"], "orgs": ["Microsoft"], "locations": ["Seattle"], "products": ["Azure"]}}
---
Text: {input_text}
Entities:"""
Delimiter hierarchy: For complex prompts, use a hierarchy: === to separate the instruction from examples, --- between examples, and newlines between fields within an example. This three-level structure rarely causes ambiguity.

Python Implementation

Building few-shot prompts programmatically allows you to dynamically select, order, and format examples per query. Below is a complete pipeline that ties together the strategies discussed above.

from dataclasses import dataclass
from typing import List, Optional
import numpy as np

@dataclass
class Example:
    input_text: str
    output_text: str
    label: Optional[str] = None
    embedding: Optional[np.ndarray] = None

class FewShotBuilder:
    """Builds few-shot prompts with selection and ordering."""

    def __init__(self, examples: List[Example],
                 encoder=None, k: int = 4,
                 delimiter: str = "---",
                 input_prefix: str = "Input",
                 output_prefix: str = "Output"):
        self.examples = examples
        self.encoder = encoder
        self.k = k
        self.delimiter = delimiter
        self.input_prefix = input_prefix
        self.output_prefix = output_prefix
        self._precompute_embeddings()

    def _precompute_embeddings(self):
        """Cache embeddings for the entire example pool."""
        if self.encoder is None:
            return
        texts = [e.input_text for e in self.examples]
        embs = self.encoder.encode(texts)
        for ex, emb in zip(self.examples, embs):
            ex.embedding = emb

    def select(self, query: str,
               strategy: str = "similarity") -> List[Example]:
        """Select k examples using the chosen strategy."""
        if strategy == "similarity":
            return self._select_similar(query)
        elif strategy == "diverse":
            return self._select_diverse(query)
        elif strategy == "random":
            idx = np.random.choice(
                len(self.examples), self.k, replace=False)
            return [self.examples[i] for i in idx]

    def _select_similar(self, query: str) -> List[Example]:
        q_emb = self.encoder.encode([query])[0]
        pool = np.array([e.embedding for e in self.examples])
        sims = pool @ q_emb / (
            np.linalg.norm(pool, axis=1) * np.linalg.norm(q_emb))
        top_k = np.argsort(sims)[-self.k:]
        return [self.examples[i] for i in top_k]

    def _select_diverse(self, query: str) -> List[Example]:
        """MMR-style: balance similarity and diversity."""
        q_emb = self.encoder.encode([query])[0]
        pool = np.array([e.embedding for e in self.examples])
        sims = pool @ q_emb / (
            np.linalg.norm(pool, axis=1) * np.linalg.norm(q_emb))
        selected, remaining = [], list(range(len(pool)))
        for _ in range(self.k):
            if not selected:
                best = remaining[np.argmax(sims[remaining])]
            else:
                sel_embs = pool[selected]
                mmr_scores = []
                for idx in remaining:
                    redundancy = np.max(sel_embs @ pool[idx])
                    mmr = 0.7 * sims[idx] - 0.3 * redundancy
                    mmr_scores.append(mmr)
                best = remaining[np.argmax(mmr_scores)]
            selected.append(best)
            remaining.remove(best)
        return [self.examples[i] for i in selected]

    def build_prompt(self, query: str,
                     instruction: str = "",
                     strategy: str = "similarity") -> str:
        """Full pipeline: select → order → format."""
        examples = self.select(query, strategy)
        # Order by ascending similarity (most similar last)
        if self.encoder:
            q_emb = self.encoder.encode([query])[0]
            examples.sort(key=lambda e:
                np.dot(e.embedding, q_emb))
        parts = [instruction] if instruction else []
        for ex in examples:
            parts.append(
                f"{self.input_prefix}: {ex.input_text}\n"
                f"{self.output_prefix}: {ex.output_text}")
        parts.append(
            f"{self.input_prefix}: {query}\n"
            f"{self.output_prefix}:")
        return f"\n{self.delimiter}\n".join(parts)

Usage with OpenAI's API:

from openai import OpenAI
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer

# Setup
encoder = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")
pool = [
    Example("I love this product!", "Positive", "pos"),
    Example("Terrible experience, never again.", "Negative", "neg"),
    Example("Best purchase I've made this year.", "Positive", "pos"),
    Example("Completely broken on arrival.", "Negative", "neg"),
    Example("It's okay, nothing special.", "Neutral", "neu"),
    Example("Works as expected, fair price.", "Neutral", "neu"),
]

builder = FewShotBuilder(pool, encoder, k=4,
    input_prefix="Review", output_prefix="Sentiment")

prompt = builder.build_prompt(
    query="The battery life is disappointing.",
    instruction="Classify the sentiment as Positive, Negative, or Neutral.",
    strategy="diverse")

client = OpenAI()
response = client.chat.completions.create(
    model="gpt-4",
    messages=[{"role": "user", "content": prompt}],
    max_tokens=5, temperature=0)
print(response.choices[0].message.content)

Dynamic Example Retrieval

In production systems, you rarely hardcode examples. Instead, you maintain an example store—a vector database or indexed pool—and retrieve examples dynamically per query. This is often called retrieval-augmented few-shot prompting.

Architecture

Key implementation decisions for the retrieval layer:

  • Embedding model: Use the same encoder for both indexing and query-time retrieval. Sentence-transformers or OpenAI's text-embedding-3-small work well.
  • Index backend: FAISS for low-latency in-memory retrieval, Pinecone or Weaviate for managed cloud stores, ChromaDB for lightweight prototyping.
  • Cache layer: Cache prompt constructions for repeated or near-identical queries to avoid redundant embedding + retrieval calls.
  • Token budget: Dynamically adjust k based on remaining token budget after the instruction and query. Longer examples → fewer shots.
# Token-aware dynamic k selection
import tiktoken

def compute_max_examples(instruction, query, pool,
                          max_tokens=3800, model="gpt-4"):
    enc = tiktoken.encoding_for_model(model)
    fixed = len(enc.encode(instruction + query))
    budget = max_tokens - fixed - 100  # reserve for output

    # Greedily add examples until budget is exhausted
    selected = []
    for ex in pool:
        cost = len(enc.encode(ex.input_text + ex.output_text)) + 10
        if budget - cost < 0:
            break
        selected.append(ex)
        budget -= cost
    return selected

Limitations & When to Fine-Tune

Few-shot prompting is powerful but has fundamental constraints. Understanding these helps you decide when to invest in fine-tuning instead.

Few-Shot Prompting

  • No training required—instant iteration
  • Works across models (vendor-agnostic)
  • Limited by context window size
  • Per-query cost of embedding + retrieval
  • Sensitive to example selection & ordering
  • Struggles with highly specialized domains

Fine-Tuning

  • Requires training data & compute
  • Locked to specific model + version
  • No context window overhead for examples
  • Lower per-query inference cost
  • Consistent behavior across queries
  • Better for domain-specific patterns

When Few-Shot Falls Short

Consider fine-tuning when you observe these signals:

  • Performance plateau: Adding more examples or better selection strategies no longer improves accuracy—you've hit the ceiling of what ICL can do for your task.
  • Domain-specific formatting: The model consistently fails to produce outputs in your custom schema despite clear examples (e.g., proprietary JSON schemas, medical coding formats).
  • Latency sensitivity: The extra tokens from demonstrations increase latency beyond your SLA. Fine-tuned models with shorter prompts are faster per query.
  • Cost at scale: If you're making millions of calls per day, the additional prompt tokens for demonstrations add up. A fine-tuned model with a minimal prompt can be 60–80% cheaper.
The 80/20 rule: Few-shot prompting can typically get you to ~80% of fine-tuned performance on most tasks. If that last 20% matters (medical diagnosis, financial compliance, safety-critical systems), invest in fine-tuning. If you're building a prototype or handling diverse, changing tasks, few-shot is almost always the right starting point.

Hybrid Approaches

In practice, the best systems combine both. Use few-shot prompting during development and early production, collect labeled data from model outputs (with human review), and fine-tune once you have enough examples and a stable task definition. The few-shot examples then become your evaluation benchmark for the fine-tuned model.

Summary: Few-shot prompting is the fastest path from idea to working system. Master example selection (semantic similarity + diversity), respect ordering effects (anchor first, most similar last), use consistent formatting with clear delimiters, and build your prompt pipeline programmatically. When performance plateaus, graduate to fine-tuning with the data you've collected along the way.
