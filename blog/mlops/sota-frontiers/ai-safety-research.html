AI Safety: Mechanistic Interpretability, Alignment

MLOps Series SOTA & Frontiers

Why Mechanistic Interpretability?

As language models scale to hundreds of billions of parameters, a critical question emerges: what are these models actually computing? Mechanistic interpretability aims to reverse-engineer the algorithms learned by neural networks — not just correlating inputs and outputs, but understanding the internal computational structure that transforms inputs into predictions.

The stakes are high. Without understanding why a model produces a particular output, we cannot reliably predict when it will fail, deceive, or produce harmful content. Interpretability is the foundation on which all other safety guarantees are built.

Core thesis: Neural networks learn interpretable algorithms. These algorithms are composed of discrete computational units — circuits — that can be identified, understood, and potentially modified. The goal is not just to explain outputs post-hoc, but to build a mechanistic understanding that enables prediction of model behavior on novel inputs.

The three pillars of the interpretability research agenda are: (1) feature identification — what concepts does the model represent? (2) circuit discovery — how do features compose into algorithms? (3) intervention — can we modify behavior by editing internal representations?

Linear Probes & Representation Analysis

The simplest interpretability tool is the linear probe: train a linear classifier on top of frozen model activations to test whether a particular concept is linearly represented. If a simple linear model can predict a concept from hidden states, that concept is likely explicitly encoded in the representation space.

# Linear probing for concept detection
import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class LinearProbe(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, hidden_dim, num_classes):
        super().__init__()
        self.linear = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, num_classes)

    def forward(self, hidden_states):
        return self.linear(hidden_states)

def train_probe(model, dataset, layer_idx, concept_labels):
    # Extract activations at target layer
    activations, labels = [], []
    for text, label in dataset:
        with torch.no_grad():
            outputs = model(text, output_hidden_states=True)
        h = outputs.hidden_states[layer_idx][:, -1, :]  # last token
        activations.append(h)
        labels.append(label)

    probe = LinearProbe(h.shape[-1], num_classes=len(set(labels)))
    # Train with cross-entropy loss on (activations, labels)
    # High accuracy → concept is linearly represented at this layer
    return train_classifier(probe, activations, labels)

Key findings from probing studies:

  • Layerwise progression: Early layers encode surface features (token identity, position). Middle layers develop syntactic structure (part-of-speech, dependency relations). Later layers encode semantic and task-specific concepts (sentiment, factual knowledge).
  • Linear representation hypothesis: Many high-level concepts — truthfulness, sentiment, language identity, even ethical judgments — are encoded as linear directions in activation space (Li et al., 2023; Marks & Tegmark, 2023).
  • Superposition: Models represent far more concepts than they have dimensions. Features are stored in superposition — overlapping, non-orthogonal directions — which makes extraction difficult but enables extraordinary capacity.
Probe pitfall: A probe's accuracy doesn't prove the model uses that representation. A concept might be linearly decodable but never causally influence the model's output. Always pair probing with causal interventions (activation patching, ablation) to confirm functional relevance.

Circuit Discovery: Induction Heads & IOI

A circuit in the mechanistic interpretability sense is a minimal subgraph of the full computational graph that is sufficient to explain a specific model behavior. Discovering circuits means identifying which attention heads, MLP neurons, and connections implement a particular algorithm.

Induction Heads

The canonical example of circuit discovery is the induction head (Olsson et al., 2022). Induction heads implement a simple but powerful algorithm: "if token A was previously followed by token B, and token A appears again, predict B." This is the core mechanism behind in-context learning in transformers.

The induction circuit consists of two attention heads working in tandem:

  • Previous-token head (Layer L): Attends from position i to position i−1, copying information about what comes before each token into the residual stream.
  • Induction head (Layer L+1): Uses the copied information to search for previous occurrences of the current token's predecessor, then attends to the token that followed it.
# Induction head detection via pattern matching
def detect_induction_heads(model, repeated_sequence):
    # Feed model: [A B C D ... A B C D ...]
    # Induction heads will attend from second A to first B
    tokens = repeated_sequence + repeated_sequence
    with torch.no_grad():
        _, attentions = model(tokens, output_attentions=True)

    induction_scores = {}
    for layer_idx, attn in enumerate(attentions):
        for head_idx in range(attn.shape[1]):
            # Measure attention from second-half positions
            # to one-after-previous-occurrence positions
            score = compute_induction_score(attn[0, head_idx])
            induction_scores[(layer_idx, head_idx)] = score

    # Heads with high scores implement the induction algorithm
    return sorted(induction_scores.items(), key=lambda x: -x[1])

The IOI Circuit

The Indirect Object Identification (IOI) circuit (Wang et al., 2022) is the most detailed circuit analysis to date. Given "When Mary and John went to the store, John gave a drink to ___", the model correctly predicts "Mary." The circuit involves 26 attention heads across multiple layers, organized into functional groups:

Name Mover Heads

Attend to the indirect object name ("Mary") and copy it to the output position. These are the final "effector" heads.

S-Inhibition Heads

Suppress the subject name ("John") by writing a negative signal, preventing the Name Movers from copying the wrong name.

Duplicate Token Heads

Detect that "John" appears twice, flagging which name is the repeated subject vs. the unique indirect object.

Previous Token Heads

Provide positional context so the circuit can distinguish the first vs. second mention of each name.

Why IOI matters: It demonstrated that transformers learn interpretable, modular algorithms — not inscrutable statistical associations. Each head has a clear functional role, and ablating any component degrades performance in predictable ways. This gives evidence that mechanistic interpretability can scale to real model behaviors.

Sparse Autoencoders for Feature Extraction

The superposition hypothesis states that models represent more features than they have dimensions by encoding features as nearly-orthogonal directions in activation space. This creates a core challenge: individual neurons are polysemantic — they activate for multiple unrelated concepts. Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) aim to disentangle this superposition.

# Sparse Autoencoder for feature extraction
class SparseAutoencoder(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model, n_features, k_sparse):
        super().__init__()
        # n_features >> d_model (overcomplete basis)
        self.encoder = nn.Linear(d_model, n_features)
        self.decoder = nn.Linear(n_features, d_model)
        self.k = k_sparse  # only top-k features active

    def forward(self, x):
        # Encode: project to high-dimensional feature space
        h = relu(self.encoder(x))

        # Sparsify: keep only top-k activations
        topk_vals, topk_idx = torch.topk(h, self.k, dim=-1)
        sparse_h = torch.zeros_like(h)
        sparse_h.scatter_(-1, topk_idx, topk_vals)

        # Decode: reconstruct original activation
        x_hat = self.decoder(sparse_h)
        return x_hat, sparse_h

    def loss(self, x, x_hat, sparse_h):
        # Reconstruction + L1 sparsity penalty
        recon_loss = mse_loss(x, x_hat)
        sparsity_loss = sparse_h.abs().sum(dim=-1).mean()
        return recon_loss + self.lambda_sparse * sparsity_loss

How SAEs decompose superposition:

  • Overcomplete dictionary: If activations live in ℝd, the SAE learns an overcomplete dictionary of n ≫ d features. Each feature is a direction in activation space that corresponds to a single interpretable concept.
  • Sparsity constraint: For any given input, only a small number of features are active (typically 10–100 out of 10,000+). This forces each feature to specialize.
  • Monosemanticity: Unlike raw neurons which are polysemantic, SAE features tend to be monosemantic — each feature responds to one coherent concept (e.g., "Golden Gate Bridge," "Python code," "expressions of sycophancy").
Scaling behavior: As SAE width increases (more features), reconstruction loss decreases and feature interpretability improves — but training cost scales linearly. Current state-of-the-art SAEs train 16M+ features on frontier model activations, requiring significant compute. The key open question: do SAEs find all features, or only the easy-to-extract ones?

Anthropic's Interpretability Research

Anthropic has made mechanistic interpretability a central pillar of their safety strategy, producing some of the most significant results in the field. Their research progression demonstrates how interpretability scales from toy models to frontier systems.

Scaling Monosemanticity (2024)

The Scaling Monosemanticity paper applied sparse autoencoders to Claude 3 Sonnet and extracted millions of interpretable features. Key findings included:

  • Abstract concepts as features: Features corresponding to "inner conflict," "deceptive behavior," "code vulnerability patterns," and "sycophantic responses" were discovered — not just surface-level pattern matching but deep semantic concepts.
  • The Golden Gate Bridge feature: A single feature that activated strongly for all mentions and depictions of the Golden Gate Bridge. When artificially amplified, the model became obsessed with the bridge — inserting it into every response — demonstrating that features are causally linked to behavior.
  • Safety-relevant features: Features for dangerous knowledge (bioweapons, cyberattacks), deception, and manipulation were identified. This opens the door to feature-level monitoring as a safety mechanism.

Features in Claude (2025)

Building on scaling monosemanticity, Anthropic published a comprehensive analysis of features in Claude 3.5 Sonnet using an even larger SAE (34 million features). New contributions included:

  • Multi-layer feature circuits: Tracing how features compose across layers. A "planning" feature in early layers feeds into "deceptive strategy" features in later layers — enabling detection of emergent deceptive reasoning.
  • Feature universality: Many features appear in independently-trained models, suggesting they reflect genuine structure in language rather than training artifacts.
  • Feature-based interventions: Suppressing specific features at inference time to eliminate dangerous capabilities without retraining. This is more targeted than RLHF-based refusals.
Open challenge: Current SAE methods capture only a fraction of model computation. Residual reconstruction error means important features may be missing. The "dark matter" of neural network computation — the part SAEs cannot yet explain — could contain the most dangerous behaviors.

Steering Vectors & Representation Engineering

Representation engineering (Zou et al., 2023) takes a control-theoretic approach to model behavior: instead of interpreting individual features, identify directions in activation space that correspond to behavioral properties, then add or subtract these directions at inference time to steer the model.

# Steering vector extraction and application
def extract_steering_vector(model, positive_prompts, negative_prompts, layer):
    # Collect activations for contrasting prompt sets
    pos_acts = mean([get_activation(model, p, layer) for p in positive_prompts])
    neg_acts = mean([get_activation(model, p, layer) for p in negative_prompts])

    # The steering vector is the difference in means
    steering_vec = pos_acts - neg_acts
    return steering_vec / steering_vec.norm()

def steer_generation(model, prompt, steering_vec, layer, alpha=1.5):
    # Hook into the target layer and add the steering vector
    def hook(module, input, output):
        # Scale and add the steering direction
        output[:, :, :] += alpha * steering_vec
        return output

    handle = model.layers[layer].register_forward_hook(hook)
    result = model.generate(prompt, max_new_tokens=200)
    handle.remove()
    return result

# Example: make model more/less honest
honesty_vec = extract_steering_vector(
    model,
    positive=["I'll give you the honest truth...", ...],
    negative=["I'll tell you what you want to hear...", ...],
    layer=15
)
# alpha > 0 → more honest, alpha < 0 → less honest
output = steer_generation(model, query, honesty_vec, layer=15, alpha=2.0)

Applications of steering vectors:

Safety Steering

Add "refusal" or "harmlessness" vectors to make models more cautious. More fine-grained than system prompts — operates directly on internal representations.

Truthfulness Control

Identified truthfulness directions in models (Li et al., 2023). Adding the truth vector improves factual accuracy on TruthfulQA by 10–20% without retraining.

Personality & Tone

Steer along dimensions like formality, empathy, or creativity. Enables per-user behavior customization without fine-tuning separate models.

Capability Elicitation

Some capabilities may be "latent" — present in weights but not expressed. Steering vectors can probe for hidden capabilities, a critical safety concern.

Advantage over fine-tuning: Steering vectors require zero gradient updates. They can be composed (add honesty + reduce sycophancy), applied at inference time, and removed instantly. The "alignment tax" is near-zero — typically <1% latency overhead.

Activation Patching & Causal Tracing

Activation patching (also called causal tracing) is the gold standard for establishing causal relationships between internal components and model outputs. The idea: run the model on two inputs (clean and corrupted), then selectively replace activations at specific positions and layers to determine which components are causally responsible for a behavior.

# Activation patching for causal tracing
def activation_patch(model, clean_input, corrupt_input, target_pos):
    # Step 1: get clean run activations
    clean_cache = model.run_with_cache(clean_input)
    # Step 2: get corrupted run output (baseline)
    corrupt_logits = model(corrupt_input)

    results = {}
    for layer in range(model.n_layers):
        for component in ["attn_out", "mlp_out"]:
            # Step 3: run corrupted input but patch in
            # the clean activation at (layer, component)
            patched_logits = model.run_with_hooks(
                corrupt_input,
                hooks=[(f"blocks.{layer}.{component}",
                        lambda act, hook:
                            replace_at(act, clean_cache[hook.name], target_pos))]
            )
            # Measure how much patching recovers the clean output
            recovery = logit_diff(patched_logits) - logit_diff(corrupt_logits)
            results[(layer, component)] = recovery

    return results  # heatmap: which (layer, component) matters most

What activation patching reveals:

  • Localization: Factual recall concentrates in mid-layer MLPs. Subject identification uses early attention heads. Output formatting depends on late-layer attention.
  • Redundancy: Models often have backup circuits. Ablating one circuit shifts computation to redundant pathways — making safety interventions harder than they first appear.
  • Composition: Attention heads compose in specific patterns. Layer 3 head 7 might write information that Layer 10 head 2 reads — forming a multi-step algorithm spanning many layers.
Scalability challenge: Activation patching requires O(layers × components × positions) forward passes. For a 70B-parameter model with 80 layers, this means thousands of forward passes per example. Techniques like attribution patching (using gradients as a first-order approximation) reduce cost to ~3 forward passes but sacrifice some accuracy.

Red Teaming & Adversarial Robustness

Red teaming is the practice of systematically attacking AI systems to discover failure modes before deployment. It bridges interpretability research and practical safety by stress-testing the guardrails that alignment techniques impose.

Major red teaming approaches:

  • Human red teaming: Domain experts (security researchers, ethicists, subject-matter specialists) craft adversarial inputs. Highest quality but expensive and slow. Anthropic, OpenAI, and DeepMind maintain dedicated red teams for pre-deployment testing.
  • Automated red teaming: Use a separate "attacker" LLM to generate adversarial prompts. Perez et al. (2022) showed that LLMs can find novel jailbreaks by optimizing against the target model's safety classifier. Scales much better than human-only approaches.
  • Gradient-based attacks: The Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) attack (Zou et al., 2023) optimizes adversarial suffixes by computing gradients through the model. Found universal adversarial strings that bypass safety training on multiple models simultaneously.
  • Multi-turn attacks: Sophisticated adversaries use multi-turn conversations to gradually steer models past safety guardrails. Crescendo attacks slowly escalate harmful requests over 5–10 turns, exploiting the model's tendency to maintain conversational coherence.

White-Box Testing

Access: Full model weights and activations.

Methods: Gradient-based attacks, activation analysis, internal feature probing.

Strength: Can find worst-case vulnerabilities.

Black-Box Testing

Access: API-only, no internal access.

Methods: Prompt engineering, multi-turn manipulation, encoding tricks.

Strength: Reflects real-world attack surface.

The arms race: Every generation of safety training patches known attacks — but new attacks keep emerging. GCG bypasses RLHF refusals. Many-shot jailbreaking (Anthropic, 2024) uses long contexts to overwhelm safety training. The fundamental question: can safety training ever be provably robust, or is it inherently a cat-and-mouse game?

Constitutional AI & Scalable Oversight

Constitutional AI (CAI) (Bai et al., 2022) addresses a fundamental alignment challenge: RLHF requires human feedback for every behavior you want to train, which doesn't scale. CAI replaces per-example human judgments with a set of principles (a "constitution") that the model uses to self-critique and self-improve.

# Constitutional AI training pipeline
def constitutional_ai_pipeline(model, constitution, prompts):
    # Phase 1: Supervised self-critique (SL-CAI)
    for prompt in prompts:
        initial = model.generate(prompt)  # may be harmful

        # Model critiques its own output against principles
        critique = model.generate(
            f"Critique this response according to: {constitution}\n"
            f"Response: {initial}"
        )

        # Model revises based on its own critique
        revised = model.generate(
            f"Revise the response to address: {critique}"
        )
        finetune_on(model, prompt, revised)

    # Phase 2: RLAIF — RL from AI feedback
    preference_model = train_preference_model(
        pairs=generate_comparison_pairs(model, constitution)
    )
    # Use AI-generated preferences instead of human labels
    ppo_train(model, preference_model)

Scalable Oversight

As models become more capable than their human overseers in specific domains, scalable oversight becomes critical. How do you supervise a system that can outthink you?

  • Debate: Two AI systems argue opposing positions while a human judge evaluates. Even if the human can't solve the problem directly, they can assess which argument is more compelling. Theoretical guarantees show debate can verify any polynomial-time computation.
  • Recursive reward modeling: Use AI assistants to help humans provide better reward signals. The assistant decomposes complex evaluations into simpler sub-questions that humans can answer reliably.
  • Iterated amplification: Christiano (2018) proposed using teams of AI agents, each supervised by slightly less capable agents, creating a recursive chain down to human-level oversight.
  • Weak-to-strong generalization: Burns et al. (2023, OpenAI) showed that strong models fine-tuned with weak supervision can exceed the weak supervisor's performance — suggesting that alignment signals generalize. A GPT-4-level model fine-tuned with GPT-2-level labels recovers much of GPT-4's capabilities.
Constitutional AI in practice: Claude's character and safety behavior are largely shaped by CAI. The constitution includes principles about helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty. The key advantage over pure RLHF: the principles are transparent and auditable. You can read the constitution and predict what behaviors it encourages. This transparency is itself a safety property.

The Alignment Tax & Future Directions

The alignment tax is the performance cost of making models safe. If safety training makes models less capable, labs face competitive pressure to underinvest in alignment. The central goal of alignment research is to reduce this tax to zero — making safe models as capable (or more capable) than unsafe ones.

Current alignment tax estimates:

Low/Negative Tax

RLHF/CAI: Aligned models are often more useful than base models because they follow instructions better. The alignment process improves helpfulness.

Steering vectors: <1% inference overhead. No capability degradation when properly calibrated.

Measurable Tax

Over-refusal: Safety-trained models refuse 5–15% of benign queries that resemble harmful ones. This "false positive" rate is the most visible alignment tax.

Capability suppression: Some dangerous-but-dual-use capabilities (chemistry knowledge, code exploitation) are deliberately reduced, limiting legitimate use cases.

Future research directions that could reduce the alignment tax:

  • Mechanistic anomaly detection: Use interpretability to monitor internal feature activations in real-time. Flag when "deception" or "harmful intent" features activate above a threshold, rather than relying on output classifiers.
  • Formal verification for neural networks: Prove properties about model behavior mathematically. Currently limited to small models and simple properties, but theoretical frameworks are advancing.
  • Process-based supervision: Instead of evaluating only final outputs, reward models for safe reasoning processes. A model that reaches the right answer via sound reasoning is safer than one that reaches it via unsafe shortcuts.
  • Cooperative AI: Train models to be transparent about their uncertainty and to defer to humans when they are unsure. This requires models that can accurately represent their own epistemic state — a deep connection to interpretability research.
  • Governance and evaluation: Standardized safety benchmarks (MACHIAVELLI, HarmBench, SafetyBench), third-party auditing frameworks, and deployment-time monitoring create an ecosystem where alignment is measurable and enforceable.
The convergence hypothesis: Mechanistic interpretability, steering vectors, constitutional AI, and scalable oversight are converging toward a unified safety stack. Interpretability gives us visibility into what models compute. Steering vectors give us control. Constitutional AI gives us principles. Scalable oversight gives us verification. The future of AI safety lies in composing these techniques into defense-in-depth architectures where no single technique needs to be perfect.

AI safety is not a solved problem — but it is a tractable one. The tools described in this module represent real, measurable progress toward understanding and controlling the behavior of increasingly capable AI systems. For MLOps practitioners, the takeaway is clear: safety is not a post-hoc addition. It must be integrated into the training pipeline, the inference stack, and the monitoring infrastructure from day one.
