The AI Hardware Landscape

AI model performance is fundamentally gated by hardware. The race to train and serve ever-larger models has spawned an extraordinary diversity of silicon — from NVIDIA's GPU juggernaut to purpose-built architectures like Groq's LPU and Cerebras' wafer-scale engine. Choosing the right hardware isn't just a procurement decision; it defines your training throughput, inference latency, cost per token, and ultimately what models you can feasibly run.

The key metrics that matter for AI workloads are:

  • Peak FLOPS (FP16/BF16/FP8): Raw compute throughput — how many operations per second the chip can sustain.
  • Memory capacity (HBM): How large a model fits on one chip without offloading or sharding.
  • Memory bandwidth (TB/s): How fast data moves between HBM and compute units — the real bottleneck for inference.
  • Interconnect bandwidth: How fast chips talk to each other — critical for distributed training and tensor parallelism.
  • Power efficiency (FLOPS/W): Operational cost and data center density constraints.
  • Cost per token: The only metric that matters for production deployment economics.
Why bandwidth matters more than FLOPS. For LLM inference, the dominant cost is moving weights from memory to compute units. A 70B parameter model at FP16 is 140 GB — at 3.35 TB/s (H100), you move those weights ~24 times per second. That's your token generation rate ceiling, regardless of how many TFLOPS the chip has. This is why memory bandwidth is the most important metric for inference.

NVIDIA: H100 to B200

NVIDIA dominates the AI accelerator market with roughly 80–90% market share. Their Hopper and Blackwell architectures set the benchmark everyone else is measured against. Understanding the evolution from H100 → H200 → B100 → B200 reveals where the bottlenecks are shifting.

H100 SXM — The Workhorse

  • 990 BF16 TFLOPS — still the most widely deployed training GPU.
  • 80 GB HBM3 at 3.35 TB/s — fits a 65B parameter model in FP16 on 8 GPUs.
  • 900 GB/s NVLink — 4th generation, 18 links per GPU.
  • Mature software ecosystem: CUDA, cuDNN, TensorRT all heavily optimized.
  • Cloud pricing: ~$2.50–3.50/hr per GPU (as of mid-2024).

H200 SXM — Memory Upgrade

  • Same 990 TFLOPS — identical Hopper die, no compute increase.
  • 141 GB HBM3e at 4.8 TB/s — 76% more capacity, 43% more bandwidth.
  • Fits Llama 70B in FP16 on 4 GPUs (vs. 8 on H100).
  • Inference throughput gains of 30–45% purely from memory bandwidth.
  • Drop-in replacement for H100 — same power, same NVLink, same software.

B100 — Blackwell Entry

  • 1800 BF16 TFLOPS — 1.8× H100, new Blackwell tensor cores.
  • 192 GB HBM3e at 8 TB/s — 2.4× memory bandwidth of H100.
  • FP4 support: 7 PFLOPS for low-precision inference at scale.
  • 1800 GB/s NVLink — 5th gen, doubles H100's interconnect.
  • Same 700W TDP as Hopper — better perf/watt.

B200 — The Flagship

  • 2250 BF16 TFLOPS — 2.3× H100, highest density GPU ever.
  • 4500 FP8 TFLOPS — designed for mixed-precision training at scale.
  • 9 PFLOPS FP4 — purpose-built for massive inference deployments.
  • 1000W TDP — requires liquid cooling and new rack designs (GB200 NVL72).
  • GB200 NVL72: 72 B200 GPUs + 36 Grace CPUs in one rack = 1.4 exaFLOPS FP4.
The GB200 NVL72 changes deployment economics. A single rack delivers 1.4 exaFLOPS of FP4 inference compute with 13.5 TB of unified HBM. This is enough to serve a 27-trillion-parameter MoE model from one rack. The catch: each rack consumes 120 kW and costs an estimated $2–3M. The shift from GPU-level to rack-level purchasing is a fundamental change in how AI infrastructure is procured.

Google TPU v5e & v5p

Google's Tensor Processing Units take a fundamentally different approach: they're ASIC accelerators available exclusively through Google Cloud, designed to tightly integrate with JAX/XLA and Google's distributed training infrastructure. The v5 generation splits into two variants — v5e for cost-efficient inference and v5p for maximum training throughput.

TPU v5e — Cost-Efficient

  • 197 BF16 TFLOPS per chip — lower than H100, but much cheaper.
  • 16 GB HBM2e — limited capacity, optimized for smaller models or heavy sharding.
  • Interconnect: ICI (Inter-Chip Interconnect) at 1.6 Tbps per chip — very fast chip-to-chip.
  • Pods of 256 chips connected via ICI — no external switches needed.
  • Cost: ~$1.20/hr per chip — roughly 2.5× cheaper per chip than H100.
  • Sweet spot: serving Gemma/PaLM-scale models, fine-tuning <30B parameter models.

TPU v5p — Performance

  • 459 BF16 TFLOPS per chip — 2.3× the v5e, competitive with H100 per-chip.
  • 95 GB HBM2e — closer to H100's capacity.
  • Interconnect: 4.8 Tbps ICI — 3× the v5e, enables massive model parallelism.
  • Pods scale to 8,960 chips — a single pod delivers over 4 exaFLOPS BF16.
  • Used internally to train Gemini Ultra and PaLM 2.
  • Key advantage: no NVLink/NVSwitch tax — ICI is native, flat bandwidth across the pod.

AMD MI300X

AMD's Instinct MI300X is the most credible GPU challenger to NVIDIA. It competes aggressively on specs, particularly memory capacity (192 GB HBM3 — the largest of any GPU) and bandwidth (5.3 TB/s). The software ecosystem (ROCm) has improved significantly but remains the Achilles' heel.

MI300X Strengths

  • 192 GB HBM3 — 2.4× the H100's 80 GB. Fits Llama 70B FP16 on 1 GPU.
  • 5.3 TB/s memory bandwidth — 58% more than H100.
  • 1307 BF16 TFLOPS — 32% more raw compute than H100.
  • Chiplet architecture: 8 XCDs on 4 IODs with unified memory.
  • Pricing: 15–25% cheaper than equivalent H100 instances.

MI300X Challenges

  • ROCm maturity: Flash Attention, Triton, and vLLM support have improved but lag CUDA by 6–12 months.
  • Interconnect: Infinity Fabric at 896 GB/s — competitive but lacks NVSwitch-class fabric.
  • Training at scale: Few published results at >1000 GPU scale.
  • Compiler stack: HIP is CUDA-compatible but not CUDA-identical — edge cases cause bugs.
  • Adoption gated by software, not silicon — the hardware is excellent.
MI300X for inference is a strong value play. For LLM inference where memory capacity and bandwidth dominate, the MI300X's 192 GB and 5.3 TB/s make it objectively superior to H100 on paper. Cloud providers like Oracle and Microsoft Azure have deployed MI300X for inference workloads. The ROCm + vLLM integration is now production-viable for standard transformer architectures.

Groq LPU: Deterministic SRAM

Groq's Language Processing Unit (LPU) is the most architecturally distinct chip in this comparison. It abandons HBM entirely in favor of on-chip SRAM only, uses a deterministic execution model with no caches, no branch prediction, and no out-of-order execution. The result: inference latency that is predictable to the nanosecond, with throughput that demolishes traditional GPUs on a per-chip basis.

# Groq LPU Architecture — fundamentally different from GPUs

class GroqLPU:
    architecture = "TSP (Tensor Streaming Processor)"
    memory_type  = "SRAM only — no HBM, no DRAM, no caches"
    sram_per_chip = "230 MB"       # vs 80 GB HBM on H100
    sram_bandwidth = "80 TB/s"    # vs 3.35 TB/s HBM on H100
    int8_tops     = 750              # competitive per-chip compute
    tdp_watts     = 300              # much lower than H100's 700W

    # The key insight: SRAM is ~20x faster than HBM
    # But SRAM is ~100x more expensive per bit
    # So you get 230 MB instead of 80 GB

    def execution_model(self):
        # Deterministic: every instruction takes exactly N cycles
        # No caches → no cache misses → no latency variation
        # Compiler schedules everything at compile time
        # Result: inference latency is PREDICTABLE and REPEATABLE
        return "compile-time scheduled, cycle-deterministic"

    def model_sharding(self, model_size_gb):
        # 230 MB per chip → a 70B model (140 GB FP16) needs ~600 chips
        # But at INT8: 70 GB → ~300 chips
        # Groq racks: 576 chips per rack
        chips_needed = model_size_gb * 1024 / 230
        return f"Needs {int(chips_needed)} LPU chips"

Groq's strengths are narrowly focused but extraordinary:

  • Inference latency: 500+ tokens/second per user for Llama 70B — 10–18× faster than H100-based serving. This makes real-time conversational AI qualitatively different.
  • Determinism: Because there are no caches or dynamic scheduling, every inference run takes exactly the same time. This is critical for SLA-bound production deployments where p99 latency matters.
  • Power efficiency: 300W per chip vs. 700W for H100. At 80 TB/s SRAM bandwidth, the FLOPS-per-watt on bandwidth-bound workloads is exceptional.

Groq's limitations are equally significant:

  • No training: The deterministic model and SRAM-only design make it unsuitable for backpropagation and gradient updates.
  • Model size ceiling: 230 MB per chip means large models require hundreds of chips coordinating, increasing inter-chip communication overhead.
  • Limited availability: Groq hardware is only available via GroqCloud API — no on-prem deployment currently.
  • Narrow workloads: Optimized for autoregressive transformer inference. CNNs, diffusion models, and non-standard architectures aren't well supported.
Groq rewrites the latency-throughput trade-off. Traditional GPU serving must choose between low latency (small batch) and high throughput (large batch). Groq's deterministic architecture achieves both simultaneously — every token takes the same time regardless of batch size. This isn't an incremental improvement; it's a fundamentally different operating point that enables new application categories like real-time voice agents with sub-200ms response times.

Cerebras WSE-3

Cerebras takes the opposite approach to chiplet designs: they build the entire wafer as a single chip. The Wafer-Scale Engine 3 (WSE-3) is the largest chip ever made — 46,225 mm² of silicon (56× larger than an H100 die), containing 4 trillion transistors, 900,000 AI-optimized cores, and 44 GB of on-chip SRAM distributed across those cores.

WSE-3 Specifications

  • 900,000 cores on a single 300mm wafer.
  • 44 GB SRAM distributed on-chip — no HBM, no off-chip memory bottleneck.
  • 21 PB/s internal memory bandwidth — 6,000× an H100's HBM bandwidth.
  • 125 PFLOPS sparse compute / ~62.5 PFLOPS dense (peak).
  • CS-3 system: WSE-3 + MemoryX (off-chip storage) + SwarmX (interconnect fabric).

When Cerebras Makes Sense

  • Training large sparse models: The massive core count + on-chip bandwidth excels at sparsity.
  • Eliminating parallelism complexity: One wafer = one device. No tensor/pipeline/data parallelism to manage.
  • Research workloads: Fast iteration on novel architectures without distributed systems headaches.
  • Genomics, simulation, drug discovery: Non-LLM workloads that benefit from extreme memory bandwidth.
  • MemoryX enables training models up to 24 trillion parameters by streaming weights from external memory.
# Cerebras vs. GPU cluster: the parallelism tax

def parallelism_overhead(num_gpus, model_params_B):
    # Traditional GPU cluster for a 70B model on 64 H100s:
    tensor_parallel = 8         # within node (NVLink)
    pipeline_parallel = 2       # across nodes
    data_parallel = 4           # replicas
    communication_overhead = 0.15  # ~15% of time spent on comms
    bubble_overhead = 0.05        # pipeline bubble waste
    effective_utilization = 1 - communication_overhead - bubble_overhead
    # = 80% effective MFU

    # Cerebras CS-3 for the same 70B model:
    # Model fits on one wafer (44 GB SRAM + MemoryX streaming)
    # No TP/PP/DP overhead — just run the forward pass
    cerebras_utilization = 0.95  # near-theoretical MFU

    return {
        "gpu_cluster_mfu": effective_utilization,
        "cerebras_mfu": cerebras_utilization,
        "cerebras_advantage": cerebras_utilization / effective_utilization,
    }

# The advantage grows with model size (more parallelism = more overhead)

Memory Hierarchy & HBM Bottleneck

Every AI accelerator has a memory hierarchy, and understanding it explains why inference is memory-bound and why different chips make different trade-offs. The hierarchy from fastest to slowest:

# Memory hierarchy in AI accelerators (approximate figures for H100)

class MemoryHierarchy:
    levels = [
        {"name": "Registers",    "size": "~27 MB",  "bw": "~100 TB/s",   "latency": "1 cycle"},
        {"name": "L1/Shared Mem", "size": "~33 MB",  "bw": "~33 TB/s",    "latency": "~20 cyc"},
        {"name": "L2 Cache",      "size": "50 MB",   "bw": "~12 TB/s",    "latency": "~100 cyc"},
        {"name": "HBM3",          "size": "80 GB",   "bw": "3.35 TB/s",   "latency": "~400 cyc"},
        {"name": "NVLink (peer)", "size": "640 GB",  "bw": "0.9 TB/s",    "latency": "~2 µs"},
        {"name": "PCIe/Network",  "size": "TB+",     "bw": "0.05 TB/s",   "latency": "~10 µs"},
    ]

    def arithmetic_intensity(self, flops_per_byte):
        # Arithmetic intensity = FLOPS / Bytes moved
        # LLM inference (autoregressive): ~1-2 FLOPS/byte
        # LLM training (batch matmul):    ~100-200 FLOPS/byte
        # Threshold for H100: 990 TFLOPS / 3.35 TB/s ≈ 295 FLOPS/byte
        # If your workload is below 295 → memory-bound
        # If above 295 → compute-bound
        if flops_per_byte < 295:
            return "memory-bound (most inference)"
        else:
            return "compute-bound (large batch training)"

The critical insight is the arithmetic intensity threshold. For the H100, you need ~295 FLOPS per byte of data moved to keep the tensor cores fully busy. Autoregressive LLM inference has an arithmetic intensity of ~1–2 FLOPS/byte (each weight is loaded from memory, multiplied once, and discarded). This means inference is 100–300× below the compute-bound threshold — you could remove 99% of the H100's tensor cores and inference speed wouldn't change, because the bottleneck is memory bandwidth.

This explains every chip's design philosophy. Groq uses SRAM (80 TB/s) instead of HBM (3.35 TB/s) to directly attack the bandwidth bottleneck. Cerebras distributes 44 GB SRAM across 900K cores for 21 PB/s aggregate bandwidth. AMD's MI300X has 192 GB HBM3 at 5.3 TB/s — more bandwidth within the GPU paradigm. Each is a different answer to the same question: how do we move weights to compute faster?

Interconnects: NVLink, NVSwitch, ICI

For multi-chip and multi-node training, the interconnect determines how efficiently you can shard models across devices. The interconnect bandwidth relative to compute determines the maximum tensor parallelism degree before communication becomes the bottleneck.

    interconnects = {

            "tp_limit":      72,  # massive TP domain
        },
        "Google ICI (TPU v5p)": {

            "topology":      "3D torus — up to 8960 chips in one pod",
            "cross_node":    "ICI is the only interconnect (no PCIe/IB)",
            "tp_limit":      8960, # entire pod is one flat domain
        },
        "AMD Infinity Fabric (MI300X)": {

            "topology":      "8 GPUs via xGMI links",

    }

# Key insight: GB200 NVL72's 72-GPU NVLink domain eliminates
# the traditional "NVLink within node, InfiniBand across nodes" split.
# This means tensor parallelism across 72 GPUs at full NVLink speed —
# enabling TP=72 for multi-trillion parameter models.

Why interconnect topology matters for model training:

  • Tensor Parallelism (TP) requires all-reduce operations that are latency-sensitive. TP degree is limited by the NVLink/ICI domain size. H100 = TP≤8, GB200 NVL72 = TP≤72, TPU v5p = TP across entire pod.
  • Pipeline Parallelism (PP) sends activations point-to-point. Latency matters more than bandwidth. PP works across InfiniBand but introduces pipeline bubbles.
  • Data Parallelism (DP) requires all-reduce of gradients once per step. Bandwidth-sensitive but latency-tolerant — can overlap with compute.
  • Expert Parallelism (EP) for MoE models requires all-to-all communication. This is the most demanding pattern because every chip must talk to every other chip — ICI's flat topology gives TPUs a structural advantage here.
The interconnect determines your parallelism strategy. On H100 DGX clusters, teams use TP=8 (within node) × PP=4–8 (across nodes) × DP=N. On TPU v5p pods, teams often use TP=much larger values because ICI bandwidth is uniform across the pod. GB200 NVL72 bridges this gap by making 72 GPUs act like one giant "node." Your training recipe is fundamentally shaped by your interconnect.

The cost equation is shifting rapidly. In 2023, H100 inference cost ~$10/M tokens for 70B models. When to Use Which Hardware

There is no single "best" accelerator — the right choice depends on your workload, scale, team expertise, and deployment model. Here's a decision framework:

Choose NVIDIA H100/H200

  • Training at any scale — the safest choice with the deepest ecosystem.
  • PyTorch-first teams — CUDA is unmatched for compatibility and debugging.
  • Multi-cloud or on-prem deployment — available everywhere.
  • Rapid prototyping — every library, every framework, every tutorial assumes CUDA.
  • When to pick H200 over H100: Inference workloads or training models >30B where HBM capacity is the limit.

Choose NVIDIA B200 / GB200

  • Next-gen training runs — if procuring hardware in 2025+.
  • Inference at massive scale — FP4 support makes the $/token unbeatable at the rack level.
  • Multi-trillion parameter models — the 72-GPU NVLink domain enables unprecedented TP.
  • Be prepared for new cooling, power, and rack requirements.

Choose Google TPU v5

  • You're already on GCP and using JAX/Flax.
  • Large-scale training (1000+ chip pods) where ICI topology shines.
  • Cost-sensitive inference — v5e is ~2.5× cheaper per chip-hour than H100.
  • MoE models — ICI's flat topology is ideal for expert parallelism.
  • Avoid if: PyTorch-only team, multi-cloud requirement, need for custom CUDA kernels.

Choose AMD MI300X

  • Inference where 192 GB HBM and 5.3 TB/s bandwidth directly reduce cost.
  • Budget-conscious — 15–25% cheaper than equivalent H100 instances.
  • Standard architectures — ROCm + vLLM works well for vanilla transformers.
  • Avoid if: Cutting-edge training, custom CUDA kernels, or Flash Attention variants that haven't been ported.

Choose Groq LPU

  • Ultra-low latency inference — real-time agents, voice AI, interactive coding assistants.
  • SLA-critical services where p99 latency determinism matters more than throughput.
  • You're okay with API-only access (no self-hosted deployment).
  • Avoid if: Training workloads, large context windows, non-transformer architectures.

Choose Cerebras WSE-3

  • Eliminating distributed systems complexity — one wafer, no parallelism to manage.
  • Sparse model training — the 900K cores and 21 PB/s bandwidth thrive on sparsity.
  • Research labs iterating on novel architectures — faster experiment turnaround.
  • Avoid if: Production inference at scale, standard dense transformers, tight budget (high upfront cost).
# Decision flowchart for hardware selection

def choose_hardware(workload, scale, budget, team_stack):
    if workload == "training":
        if team_stack == "JAX" and scale > 1000:
            return "TPU v5p pods"
        elif scale > 10000 and budget == "unlimited":
            return "GB200 NVL72 racks"
        else:
            return "H100/H200 clusters"  # safest default

    elif workload == "inference":
        if budget == "minimum_cost":
            return "MI300X or Groq API"
        elif budget == "minimum_latency":
            return "Groq LPU"
        elif scale > 100_000_users:
            return "B200 / GB200 NVL72 (FP4)"
        elif team_stack == "GCP":
            return "TPU v5e"
        else:
            return "H200 with vLLM"   # balanced default

    elif workload == "research":
        if "sparsity" in team_stack:
            return "Cerebras CS-3"
        return "H100 (ecosystem wins for experimentation)"
The hardware landscape is fragmenting — and that's a good thing. The era of "just buy H100s" is ending. In 2025, the optimal infrastructure stack will likely be heterogeneous: NVIDIA GPUs for training, Groq or purpose-built ASICs for latency-critical inference, TPUs for cost-optimized batch inference, and Cerebras for sparse research workloads. The teams that win will be those who match hardware to workload, not those who default to the most expensive option.
