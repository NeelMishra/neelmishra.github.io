GPU Cluster Management

MLOps Series Platform Engineering

Cluster Overview

A well-managed GPU cluster is the backbone of any ML platform. GPUs are expensive—an 8×H100 node costs $25–$40/hour—so maximising utilisation while maintaining fairness across teams is critical. This post covers the scheduling, quota, and monitoring strategies that mature ML platform teams use to keep GPU utilisation above 80% without starving any single team.

Utilisation target: World-class GPU clusters run at 80–90% utilisation. Below 70% means money is being wasted. Above 95% means queues are too long and researchers are blocked. The sweet spot requires careful tuning of quotas, preemption policies, and scheduling algorithms.

Scheduling Strategies

GPU scheduling for ML differs from traditional CPU scheduling because training jobs are long-running, gang-scheduled (all-or-nothing), and often cannot tolerate partial allocation. The three most common scheduling strategies are FIFO, fair-share, and gang scheduling.

FIFO Scheduling

Jobs are executed in arrival order. Simple to implement but leads to head-of-line blocking where a large job prevents smaller jobs from running even when resources are partially available.

Gang Scheduling

All pods for a distributed training job are scheduled simultaneously or not at all. This prevents resource deadlocks where two multi-node jobs each hold half the required GPUs and neither can proceed.

# Kubernetes gang scheduling with Volcano # volcano-job.yaml apiVersion: batch.volcano.sh/v1alpha1 kind: Job metadata: name: distributed-training spec: minAvailable: 4 # gang: all 4 pods must schedule together schedulerName: volcano queue: ml-training policies: - event: PodEvicted action: RestartJob tasks: - replicas: 4 name: worker template: spec: containers: - name: trainer image: my-registry/train:latest resources: limits: nvidia.com/gpu: 8 # 8 GPUs per pod

Backfill Scheduling

While large jobs wait for enough GPUs, smaller jobs are “backfilled” into idle slots. This dramatically improves utilisation without delaying the large job’s start time. Both Slurm and Volcano support backfill scheduling natively.

Pitfall: Without gang scheduling, a 4-node training job requesting 32 GPUs can partially allocate 16 GPUs and block indefinitely waiting for the remaining 16. This wastes both the allocated GPUs and the wall-clock time of the job. Always enable gang scheduling for multi-node training.

Quota Management

Quotas partition the cluster’s GPU capacity across teams, projects, or priority classes. Without quotas, a single team can monopolise the entire cluster. The three quota models are:

Hard Quotas

Each team gets a fixed GPU allocation. Team A gets 32 GPUs, Team B gets 64, etc. Unused capacity is not available to other teams.

Pros: Predictable. Easy to budget.
Cons: Low utilisation when teams are idle.

Soft Quotas with Borrowing

Each team has a guaranteed minimum. When a team is idle, its unused GPUs are lent to other teams. When the owning team submits jobs, borrowed GPUs are reclaimed via preemption.

Pros: High utilisation. Fair.
Cons: Complex preemption logic needed.

# Kubernetes ResourceQuota for GPU limits per namespace apiVersion: v1 kind: ResourceQuota metadata: name: team-alpha-gpu-quota namespace: team-alpha spec: hard: requests.nvidia.com/gpu: "32" # guaranteed minimum limits.nvidia.com/gpu: "64" # burst maximum --- # Kueue ClusterQueue for fair-share with borrowing apiVersion: kueue.x-k8s.io/v1beta1 kind: ClusterQueue metadata: name: ml-training spec: cohort: shared-gpus # enables borrowing across queues resourceGroups: - coveredResources: ["nvidia.com/gpu"] flavors: - name: h100 resources: - name: nvidia.com/gpu nominalQuota: 32 # guaranteed borrowingLimit: 32 # can borrow up to 32 more

Fair-Share Policies

Fair-share scheduling considers each team’s historical usage when making scheduling decisions. Teams that have consumed more than their share are deprioritised; teams that have consumed less are boosted. This creates an equitable system over time, even when demand is bursty.

The fair-share priority score is typically calculated as:

# Fair-share priority calculation def fair_share_priority(team, window_hours=168): """Lower score = higher priority (less historical usage)""" allocated = get_gpu_hours_consumed(team, window_hours) target = get_gpu_hours_target(team, window_hours) # Ratio of actual usage to target allocation usage_ratio = allocated / max(target, 1) # Teams below their target get priority boost if usage_ratio < 1.0: return usage_ratio * 0.5 # strong boost else: return usage_ratio # natural decay
Decay windows: A 7-day rolling window is common. Short windows (24h) are too reactive and penalise teams that just completed a large training run. Long windows (30d) are too slow to respond to changes in team activity. Tune the window based on your training job durations.

Slurm implements fair-share natively via the fairshare priority factor. In Kubernetes, Kueue and Volcano both support fair-share scheduling with configurable decay and weight parameters.

Monitoring & Observability

You cannot manage what you cannot measure. GPU monitoring requires specialised tooling because standard CPU metrics (load average, memory) do not capture GPU-specific concerns like SM occupancy, memory bandwidth utilisation, and NVLink throughput.

Key Metrics

Utilisation Metrics

  • GPU SM Active (%): Percentage of time at least one warp is active.
  • GPU Memory Used (GB): VRAM consumption. Alerts when near OOM.
  • Tensor Core Active (%): Time spent on matrix ops. Low values suggest CPU-bound code.
  • NVLink Bandwidth (GB/s): Inter-GPU communication throughput.

Cluster Metrics

  • Queue Depth: Number of pending jobs. High values indicate capacity pressure.
  • Wait Time (P50/P99): Time from submission to job start.
  • Preemption Rate: Frequency of job evictions. High rates frustrate users.
  • Utilisation by Team: Fairness tracking across organisational units.
# Prometheus scrape config for DCGM exporter # prometheus.yml scrape_configs: - job_name: "dcgm" scrape_interval: 15s kubernetes_sd_configs: - role: pod relabel_configs: - source_labels: [__meta_kubernetes_pod_label_app] regex: nvidia-dcgm-exporter action: keep --- # Grafana alert rule for low GPU utilisation # Alert if GPU SM active < 30% for 30 minutes groups: - name: gpu-alerts rules: - alert: LowGPUUtilisation expr: avg_over_time(DCGM_FI_PROF_SM_ACTIVE[30m]) < 0.3 for: 30m labels: severity: warning annotations: summary: "GPU {{ $labels.gpu }} on {{ $labels.node }} is underutilised"
Common trap: nvidia-smi reports “GPU Utilization” as the percentage of time a kernel is running, not the percentage of compute capacity used. A kernel that uses 1% of SMs still shows 100% utilisation in nvidia-smi. Use DCGM metrics (DCGM_FI_PROF_SM_ACTIVE, DCGM_FI_PROF_SM_OCCUPANCY) for accurate measurements.

Slurm vs Kubernetes

The two dominant GPU cluster orchestrators are Slurm (traditional HPC) and Kubernetes (cloud-native). Each has distinct strengths, and many organisations run both.

Slurm

  • Strengths: Purpose-built for HPC. Mature fair-share, gang scheduling, and preemption.
  • Job model: sbatch scripts with resource requests. Simple and familiar to researchers.
  • Networking: First-class InfiniBand and MPI support. Zero overhead for NCCL.
  • Scaling: Proven at 10,000+ node clusters (ORNL, NERSC).
  • Weakness: Poor container and microservice support. No native auto-scaling.

Kubernetes

  • Strengths: Cloud-native. Excellent for serving, CI/CD, and mixed workloads.
  • Job model: Pods and Jobs with GPU resource limits. Requires operators for gang scheduling.
  • Networking: Requires RDMA device plugin and network operator for InfiniBand.
  • Scaling: Native auto-scaling with Cluster Autoscaler or Karpenter.
  • Weakness: GPU scheduling less mature. Requires Volcano/Kueue for fair-share.
# Slurm job script for multi-node distributed training #!/bin/bash #SBATCH --job-name=llm-train #SBATCH --nodes=4 #SBATCH --ntasks-per-node=8 #SBATCH --gpus-per-node=8 #SBATCH --partition=h100 #SBATCH --time=72:00:00 #SBATCH --account=team-alpha module load cuda/12.2 nccl/2.18 srun torchrun \ --nnodes=$SLURM_NNODES \ --nproc_per_node=8 \ --rdzv_backend=c10d \ --rdzv_endpoint=$SLURM_NODELIST:29500 \ train.py \ --model llama-7b \ --batch-size 64 \ --gradient-accumulation 4

For teams that want the best of both worlds, projects like SlurmK8s and NVIDIA Run:ai bridge the gap by running Slurm-style scheduling on top of Kubernetes infrastructure, combining HPC scheduling with cloud-native auto-scaling and container management.

Recommendation: Use Slurm for dedicated on-prem GPU clusters focused on large-scale training. Use Kubernetes for cloud-based clusters, inference serving, and mixed ML/non-ML workloads. Consider hybrid approaches (Run:ai, Kueue) when you need HPC-grade scheduling on Kubernetes.
