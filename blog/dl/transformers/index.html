Transformers: A Deep Dive

Introduced in the landmark 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need," the Transformer architecture fundamentally changed how we build sequence models. By replacing recurrence with a purely attention-based mechanism, Transformers unlocked massive parallelism during training and achieved state-of-the-art results across natural language processing, computer vision, speech recognition, and protein structure prediction. Today, nearly every frontier model, from large language models to vision transformers, is built on this foundation.

This series breaks the Transformer down into its core components, examines each in detail, and traces how the original design has branched into the diverse family of architectures powering modern AI.

The Big Picture

At its core, the Transformer is an elegant answer to a single question: how can a model relate every element of a sequence to every other element, efficiently and in parallel? The attention mechanism provides the answer by computing pairwise relevance scores across all positions, allowing information to flow freely without the bottleneck of sequential processing. Layer normalization and residual connections stabilize the deep stacking of these attention blocks, while position encodings inject the ordering information that pure attention lacks. The feed-forward layers then transform attended representations independently at each position, adding the nonlinear capacity the model needs. Encoder-only variants like BERT leverage bidirectional context for understanding tasks, decoder-only variants like GPT use causal masking for generation, and full encoder-decoder models like T5 handle sequence-to-sequence problems. Understanding each of these components in isolation, and how they interact when composed, is the key to reasoning about why Transformers work, when they fail, and how to adapt them to new domains.
