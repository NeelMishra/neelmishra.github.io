    The Binomial Theorem

    May 2026 · 16 min read

    The binomial coefficients we built in Part 4 are not just counters of subsets. They are also the coefficients you get when you expand $(x + y)^n$. The connection between the algebra and the combinatorics is the binomial theorem — and once you see why the bridge exists, almost every "messy expansion" problem becomes routine.

    Statement

    Binomial theorem. For any non-negative integer $n$ and any reals (or complex numbers, or elements of a commutative ring) $x, y$,

    $\displaystyle (x + y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^{n-k} y^k.$

    Equivalently, you can index by the power of $x$ and write

    $\displaystyle (x + y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^{k} y^{n-k},$

    using the symmetry $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}$.

    Combinatorial Proof

    Write $(x + y)^n$ explicitly as the product of $n$ identical factors:

    $(x + y)(x + y)(x + y) \cdots (x + y) \quad\text{($n$ factors).}$

    To expand the product into a sum of monomials, the distributive law tells you to pick either $x$ or $y$ from each factor and multiply your choices. A typical term in the expansion looks like $y \cdot x \cdot x \cdot y \cdot x \cdot \dots$, which after rearrangement is some power of $x$ times some power of $y$.

    Suppose we end up choosing $y$ from exactly $k$ of the $n$ factors and $x$ from the remaining $n - k$. The product is then $x^{n-k} y^k$. How many ways can we make those choices? We are picking $k$ of the $n$ factors to take $y$ from — that is exactly the number of $k$-subsets of an $n$-set, namely $\binom{n}{k}$.

    So the coefficient of $x^{n-k} y^k$ in the expansion is $\binom{n}{k}$. Summing over all valid $k$ gives the binomial theorem. $\Box$

    Why the algebraic side and the counting side agree. Algebra "remembers" how often each monomial appears. Combinatorics counts the number of ways to produce that monomial by independent choice. Both routes count the same thing — the binomial theorem is just the statement that they agree.

    ▶ Visual: Expanding $(x + y)^3$

    Step through the 8 ways to pick one term from each factor of $(x+y)^3$. Group by how many $y$'s appear and you recover the binomial coefficients $\binom{3}{0}, \binom{3}{1}, \binom{3}{2}, \binom{3}{3}$.

    Worked: $(x + y)^4$

    Read the row $n = 4$ of Pascal's triangle: $1, 4, 6, 4, 1$. So

    $(x + y)^4 = x^4 + 4x^3 y + 6 x^2 y^2 + 4 x y^3 + y^4.$

    You can verify the coefficient of $x^2 y^2$ directly: we need to pick $y$ from 2 of the 4 factors. There are $\binom{4}{2} = 6$ ways to do that. Pleasingly, that is the same 6 you would compute by brute multiplication.

    Useful Substitutions

    Setting $x = y = 1$

    $\displaystyle 2^n = (1 + 1)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} \cdot 1^{n-k} \cdot 1^{k} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k}.$

    This is the row sum we mentioned in Part 4. The binomial theorem turns it from a curiosity into a one-line proof.

    Setting $x = 1, y = -1$

    $\displaystyle 0 = (1 - 1)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} (-1)^k$ for $n \ge 1$.

    Splitting into even-$k$ and odd-$k$ sums shows that there are equally many even-sized and odd-sized subsets of an $n$-set when $n \ge 1$.

    Differentiating with respect to $x$

    Treat $y$ as a constant. Differentiate both sides of the identity $(1 + x)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^k$ with respect to $x$:

    $n (1 + x)^{n-1} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} k \binom{n}{k} x^{k-1}.$

    Set $x = 1$ to get

    $\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^{n} k \binom{n}{k} = n \cdot 2^{n-1}.$

    This is also the answer to "pick a committee from $n$ people and designate a chair" counted in two ways: $\binom{n}{k}$ for the committee, $k$ for the chair, summed over all $k$ — versus $n$ for the chair (any of the $n$ people), then $2^{n-1}$ for the rest.

    Integrating

    Integrate $(1 + x)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^k$ from $0$ to $1$:

    $\displaystyle \dfrac{2^{n+1} - 1}{n + 1} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \dfrac{\binom{n}{k}}{k + 1}.$

    Both calculus and combinatorics deliver the same identities — pick whichever proof you find faster.

    Beyond Two Variables: A Preview

    What about $(x + y + z)^n$? Each factor in the product offers three choices instead of two, so the expansion is

    $\displaystyle (x + y + z)^n = \sum_{a + b + c = n} \binom{n}{a, b, c} x^a y^b z^c$

    where $\binom{n}{a, b, c} = \dfrac{n!}{a!\,b!\,c!}$ is a multinomial coefficient. We tackle these in Part 6.

    A Ross-style Application

    How many subsets of $\{1, 2, \dots, n\}$ have an even number of elements?

    From the alternating-sum identity above, $\sum_{k} (-1)^k \binom{n}{k} = 0$. Splitting odd and even $k$:

    $\sum_{k\text{ even}} \binom{n}{k} = \sum_{k\text{ odd}} \binom{n}{k}.$

    And the total is $\sum_k \binom{n}{k} = 2^n$. So each side equals $2^{n-1}$. There are exactly $2^{n-1}$ even-sized subsets when $n \ge 1$.

    Heads-up: General Binomial Theorem

    For non-integer or negative $\alpha$, the same theorem works as a power series in $|x| < 1$:

    $(1 + x)^\alpha = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{\alpha}{k} x^k,$

    where the generalised binomial coefficient is

    $\binom{\alpha}{k} = \dfrac{\alpha (\alpha - 1)(\alpha - 2) \cdots (\alpha - k + 1)}{k!}.$

    This is how Newton extended the theorem to fractional and negative powers. We will not need it for elementary probability, but it is good to know it exists — it powers Taylor expansions of $\sqrt{1 + x}$, $1/(1 - x)$, and a great deal of generating-function algebra.

    Common Mistakes

    • Swapping $x^{n-k}$ and $y^k$. Always check which variable's exponent matches the binomial coefficient subscript. Both conventions appear in the wild — pick one and stick with it.
    • Treating $(x - y)^n$ as $(x + y)^n$. When $y$ is replaced by $-y$, every odd-power term flips sign. The expansion is $\sum (-1)^k \binom{n}{k} x^{n-k} y^k$.
    • Dropping the $\binom{n}{0}$ or $\binom{n}{n}$ term. They are both 1 and easy to forget when summing manually.
    • Assuming the formula needs $x, y$ to be numbers. The proof uses only the distributive law and commutativity of multiplication — it works for symbolic variables, matrices that commute, polynomials, and many other settings.

    Summary

    • $(x + y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^{n-k} y^k$.
    • Coefficient of $x^{n-k} y^k$ is the number of ways to choose which $k$ factors contribute $y$.
    • Substitutions like $x = y = 1$ or $x = 1, y = -1$ instantly produce row-sum and alternating-sum identities.
    • Differentiation and integration of the identity $(1 + x)^n = \sum \binom{n}{k} x^k$ give weighted sum identities.
    • Generalises to multinomials (Part 6) and to non-integer exponents via Newton's series.
