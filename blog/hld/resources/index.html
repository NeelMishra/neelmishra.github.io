    How to Get Good at System Design: The Complete Resource List

    June 2026 · 35 min read

    This post gathers the best material on High-Level Design and distributed systems from across GitHub and the wider internet into one place: a learning roadmap, the full concept curriculum, the highest-signal repositories, books, courses, YouTube channels, websites, the classic papers, practice problems, an interview framework, the latency numbers worth memorizing, the most common mistakes, and a week-by-week study plan. System design rewards breadth plus judgment, so the goal here is to map the territory, then point at deliberate practice. The short version: learn the building blocks (load balancers, caches, databases, queues), learn the trade-offs, then design real systems out loud, over and over.

    How to use this list. Read the roadmap, pick one repository and one book as anchors (most people choose system-design-primer and Designing Data-Intensive Applications), then spend the bulk of the time designing problems from the ladder at the end. Star counts are approximate as of mid-2026 and only signal popularity, not a ranking of quality.

    The Roadmap: How Getting Good Actually Works

    System-design skill is the ability to assemble a small set of building blocks under a set of constraints, and to defend the trade-offs. It builds up in layers:

    1. Fundamentals. Scalability, availability, reliability, latency versus throughput, and the CAP theorem give the vocabulary for every later decision.
    2. Building blocks. Load balancers, caches, databases (SQL and NoSQL), CDNs, message queues, and API gateways are the Lego pieces every design is made of.
    3. Distributed systems theory. Replication, partitioning, consensus, and consistency models explain why the building blocks behave as they do under failure.
    4. Trade-offs. Strong versus eventual consistency, push versus pull, batch versus stream: senior answers live here, not in naming components.
    5. Estimation. Back-of-the-envelope math for QPS, storage, and bandwidth turns a vague design into a sized one.
    6. Deliberate practice. Designing real systems end to end, out loud, under time pressure, is the step that turns knowledge into skill.

    The Concept Curriculum

    This is the syllabus nearly every strong system-design resource converges on. Work through it roughly in this order.

    1. Core Concepts

    Scalability (vertical versus horizontal), availability and the nines, reliability, single points of failure, latency versus throughput versus bandwidth, consistent hashing, the CAP theorem and PACELC, failover, and fault tolerance.

    2. Networking Fundamentals

    The OSI model, IP and DNS, proxies versus reverse proxies, HTTP and HTTPS, TCP versus UDP, load-balancing algorithms, and checksums.

    3. API Fundamentals

    REST versus GraphQL versus gRPC, API gateways, WebSockets and webhooks, idempotency, rate limiting, pagination, and versioning.

    4. Database Fundamentals

    ACID transactions, SQL versus NoSQL, indexing, sharding and partitioning, replication, read and write scaling, the major database families (key-value, document, column, graph, time-series, search), and bloom filters.

    5. Caching

    Cache-aside, read-through, write-through, and write-behind strategies, eviction policies (LRU, LFU, TTL), distributed caching, hot keys, cache stampede, and CDNs.

    6. Asynchronous Communication

    Publish-subscribe, message queues, brokers like Kafka and RabbitMQ, delivery guarantees, and change data capture.

    7. Distributed Systems and Microservices

    Replication and partitioning, leader election and consensus (Raft, Paxos), quorums, vector clocks, heartbeats and health checks, service discovery, gossip protocols, distributed locking, idempotency, the saga pattern, two-phase commit, circuit breakers, and distributed tracing.

    8. Architectural Patterns

    Client-server, monolith versus microservices, serverless, event-driven architecture, CQRS and event sourcing, and peer-to-peer.

    9. The Trade-offs That Matter

    Vertical versus horizontal scaling, strong versus eventual consistency, latency versus throughput, push versus pull, long polling versus WebSockets, batch versus stream processing, stateful versus stateless, REST versus RPC, and synchronous versus asynchronous communication. A senior answer is mostly a sequence of these decisions, made on purpose.

    The Classic Papers

    The interview rarely demands papers, but reading a handful is the fastest route to real depth. These are the foundational ones, browsable through awesome-distributed-systems and the MIT 6.824 reading list.

    • The Google File System and MapReduce — the origin of modern big-data infrastructure.
    • Bigtable and Dynamo — the two ancestral designs behind most NoSQL stores (column and key-value).
    • Spanner — globally distributed, externally consistent transactions with TrueTime.
    • In Search of an Understandable Consensus Algorithm (Raft) and Paxos Made Simple — how replicas agree. Start with Raft via raft.github.io.
    • Cassandra, Kafka, and ZooKeeper / Chubby — the workhorses of storage, streaming, and coordination.
    • Time, Clocks, and the Ordering of Events (Lamport) — the foundation of reasoning about distributed time.

    The Interview Framework

    Strong system-design answers follow the same arc. Practicing the process beats memorizing any single design.

    1Clarify requirements. Separate functional from non-functional (scale, latency, availability, consistency), and pin the scope.

    2Estimate. Back-of-the-envelope QPS, storage, and bandwidth. Sizing drives every later choice.

    3Define the API. A few endpoints make the contract concrete.

    4Sketch the high-level design. Clients, load balancer, services, datastores, and caches as boxes and arrows.

    5Design the data model. Pick the storage type, schema, and partitioning key.

    6Deep dive. Scale the bottleneck: sharding, replication, caching, queues, and consistency choices.

    7Address failures and trade-offs. Single points of failure, bottlenecks, monitoring, and what was deliberately given up.

    Latency Numbers Worth Knowing

    Estimation separates a vague design from a sized one. Memorize these orders of magnitude (the classic "latency numbers every engineer should know").

    OperationApprox. latency
    L1 cache reference~1 ns
    Main memory reference~100 ns
    Read 1 MB sequentially from memory~10 µs
    SSD random read~100 µs
    Read 1 MB sequentially from SSD~1 ms
    Round trip within the same data center~0.5 ms
    Disk (HDD) seek~10 ms
    Round trip across continents~150 ms

    Companion rules of thumb: roughly 2.5 million seconds in a month, a day is about 86,400 seconds, and one server handles on the order of a few thousand simple requests per second before it needs help.

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    • Skipping requirements and estimation. Designing before sizing produces a solution to an unstated problem.
    • Naming components instead of justifying them. Saying "add Kafka" is not a design; explaining why a queue is needed is.
    • Ignoring trade-offs. Every choice (consistency, push versus pull, SQL versus NoSQL) gives something up; senior answers say what.
    • One giant database. Not addressing sharding, replication, and the read/write split when scale demands it.
    • Forgetting failure. No single-point-of-failure analysis, no monitoring, no graceful degradation.
    • Over-engineering. Reaching for microservices, multi-region, and exotic stores when the stated scale does not need them.
    • Staying too high level. Never going deep on the one component the interviewer cares about.

    A Study Plan

    WeeksFocus
    Weeks 1–2Fundamentals and building blocks: load balancing, caching, SQL versus NoSQL, CAP, consistent hashing. Read the system-design-primer concepts.
    Weeks 3–4Data systems: replication, partitioning, transactions, consistency. Read the core chapters of Designing Data-Intensive Applications.
    Week 5Asynchronous and distributed: queues, pub-sub, consensus, idempotency, the saga pattern. Watch a few real-architecture videos.
    Weeks 6–8One design per day from the ladder, timed, warm-up to hard. Always finish with estimation, a deep dive, and a failure analysis.
    The one habit that matters. Design a full system out loud every day, then compare against a reference architecture and redo the weak part. A few weeks of that builds more intuition than months of passive reading. Everything above just feeds that loop.
