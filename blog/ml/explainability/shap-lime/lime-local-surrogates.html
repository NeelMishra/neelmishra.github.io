    LIME: Local Surrogate Explanations

    July 2026 · 18 min read

    LIME stands for Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations. The method explains one prediction by sampling perturbed points near the row, querying the black-box model on those points, and fitting a simple interpretable surrogate that is weighted toward the original row.

    The LIME Objective

    Let $f$ be the black-box model and $g$ be a simple model, often a sparse linear model. Around a row $x_0$, LIME solves

    $$\arg\min_g \sum_i \pi_{x_0}(z_i)\left(f(z_i)-g(z_i)\right)^2 + \Omega(g),$$

    where $z_i$ are perturbations, $\pi_{x_0}$ is a locality kernel, and $\Omega(g)$ penalizes complexity. The coefficients of $g$ become the explanation.

    ▶ LIME Fits a Local Line

    Nearby perturbations receive larger weights. The fitted line explains the black-box curve only around the highlighted row, not globally.

    Algorithm Steps

    choose row x0
sample perturbed rows around x0
query black-box model f on each perturbation
weight each perturbation by distance to x0
fit a simple weighted surrogate g
read coefficients of g as local feature effects

    Pitfalls

    LIME's flexibility is also its weakness. The explanation can change when the perturbation distribution changes, when the kernel width changes, or when the surrogate family changes. For tabular data, unrealistic perturbations can query the model on rows that never occur in the real distribution.

    Local means local. A LIME coefficient is not a global feature effect. It says what the surrogate needed in one neighborhood to mimic the black-box model.
