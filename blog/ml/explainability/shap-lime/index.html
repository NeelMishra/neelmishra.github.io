    SHAP & LIME: Local Explainability

    July 2026 · 17 min read

    SHAP and LIME both explain one prediction at a time. They treat the trained model as a function that can be queried, then answer a local question: around this row, which features push the prediction up or down? The methods differ in the surrogate problem they solve. LIME fits a simple model near the row. SHAP assigns feature credit by averaging marginal contributions over coalitions.

    The Shared Setting

    Assume a trained model f(x) and a row x0. The explainer can query the model on modified rows, but it does not need to know how the model is implemented. This is why SHAP and LIME are called model-agnostic when used in their black-box forms.

    ▶ One Prediction, Two Explanation Views

    The center point is the row being explained. LIME looks at nearby perturbations and fits a local line. SHAP looks at coalitions of present and absent features and allocates credit.

    Quick Comparison

    Method Core idea Strength Main risk
    LIME Fit an interpretable local surrogate weighted by closeness to the row. Simple, fast, flexible, and easy to explain. Sensitive to perturbation design, distance kernel, and surrogate instability.
    SHAP Assign additive feature credits using Shapley values. Strong axioms: local accuracy, symmetry, dummy feature, and consistency. Expensive in exact form, and dependent-feature semantics are subtle.
