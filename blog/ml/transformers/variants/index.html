    Transformer Variants

    ML Series · March 2026

    From One Architecture to Many

    The original transformer from "Attention Is All You Need" (2017) was an encoder-decoder model designed for machine translation. Within two years, researchers discovered that you could take the encoder half, the decoder half, or the full model and adapt each to fundamentally different tasks. This produced three distinct families of transformer architectures, each with its own strengths and training paradigms.

    The encoder processes input bidirectionally and produces contextual representations. The decoder generates output autoregressively, one token at a time. The full encoder-decoder model combines both for sequence-to-sequence tasks. Understanding which family a model belongs to tells you immediately what it is good at and how it was trained.

    The Three Families

    Every major transformer model falls into one of three architectural categories:

    • Encoder-only models process the entire input at once with full bidirectional attention. They excel at understanding tasks: classification, named entity recognition, semantic similarity. BERT is the canonical example.
    • Decoder-only models generate text left to right using causal (masked) attention. They are the foundation of modern language generation. GPT is the canonical example.
    • Encoder-decoder models use a full encoder to process the input and a full decoder to generate the output, with cross-attention connecting the two. They are designed for sequence-to-sequence tasks: translation, summarization, question answering. T5 and BART are the canonical examples.

    Family Tree of Transformer Variants

    Encoder-Only: Understanding Text

    Encoder-only models remove the decoder entirely and use only the encoder stack. The key advantage is bidirectional attention: every token can attend to every other token in the input, both left and right. This makes encoder-only models excellent at tasks that require understanding the full context of a sentence.

    BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) established this paradigm in 2018. It is pretrained with two objectives: Masked Language Modeling (MLM), where random tokens are masked and the model predicts them, and Next Sentence Prediction (NSP), where the model determines whether two sentences are consecutive in the original text.

    After pretraining, BERT is fine-tuned on downstream tasks by adding a task-specific head on top of the encoder output. For classification, you take the [CLS] token representation. For token-level tasks like NER, you use each token's representation. This fine-tuning paradigm proved remarkably effective across dozens of NLP benchmarks.

    Decoder-Only: Generating Text

    Decoder-only models remove the encoder and use only the decoder stack with causal masking. Each token can only attend to tokens that came before it (and itself). This left-to-right constraint makes the model naturally suited for generation: given a prefix, predict the next token, then use that token as part of the prefix for the next prediction.

    GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) established this paradigm. The training objective is simple: predict the next token. Despite this simplicity, scaling decoder-only models to billions of parameters and trillions of training tokens produced the large language models that dominate modern NLP. GPT-3 demonstrated that a sufficiently large decoder-only model can perform tasks it was never explicitly trained for, simply by conditioning on a few examples in the prompt (few-shot learning).

    The decoder-only family now includes GPT-4, LLaMA, PaLM, Mistral, and most other large language models. The architectural simplicity (no encoder, no cross-attention) makes these models easier to scale and train efficiently.

    Encoder-Decoder: Transforming Sequences

    Encoder-decoder models retain the full original architecture. The encoder processes the input with bidirectional attention. The decoder generates the output autoregressively, attending both to its own previous tokens (via causal self-attention) and to the encoder's output (via cross-attention).

    T5 (Text-to-Text Transfer Transformer) unified all NLP tasks into a text-to-text format: the input is a text string, the output is a text string. Translation becomes "translate English to French: [sentence]". Classification becomes "classify: [sentence]" with the output being the class label as text. This uniform framing lets a single encoder-decoder model handle any task.

    BART combines a bidirectional encoder (like BERT) with an autoregressive decoder (like GPT). It is pretrained by corrupting text in various ways (masking, deletion, permutation, rotation) and training the model to reconstruct the original. This makes BART particularly strong at text generation tasks like summarization.

    Choosing the Right Variant

    The choice of variant depends on the task:

    • Understanding tasks (classification, extraction, similarity): use an encoder-only model. Bidirectional attention gives the richest representations of input text.
    • Generation tasks (dialogue, story writing, code completion): use a decoder-only model. Causal attention is the natural fit for sequential generation.
    • Sequence-to-sequence tasks (translation, summarization, structured output): use an encoder-decoder model. The encoder captures the input context, and the decoder generates the output conditioned on it.
    • Vision tasks: use a vision transformer when you have large labeled datasets or can leverage pretrained checkpoints. For small datasets with limited compute, convolutional architectures may still be more practical.

    Historical Timeline

    The transformer family expanded rapidly:

    • 2017: Original Transformer (Vaswani et al.)
    • 2018: GPT (Radford et al.), BERT (Devlin et al.)
    • 2019: GPT-2, RoBERTa, ALBERT, T5, BART
    • 2020: GPT-3, Vision Transformer (ViT)
    • 2021: DeBERTa, PaLM planning begins
    • 2022: PaLM, LLaMA planning begins
    • 2023: GPT-4, LLaMA, Mistral

    Each new model built on the successes and lessons of its predecessors. BERT showed that pretraining works. GPT-3 showed that scale works. ViT showed that the architecture generalizes beyond language. The family continues to grow.
