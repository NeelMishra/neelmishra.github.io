    Tokenization: BPE, WordPiece, SentencePiece

    March 2026 · ML Series · Preprocessing

    Why Subword Tokenization

    Transformers process sequences of discrete tokens, not raw text. The method used to split text into tokens has a direct impact on model quality, vocabulary size, and computational cost. The three naive approaches all fail:

    • Character-level. Every letter is a token. The vocabulary is tiny (a few hundred entries), but sequences become extremely long. A sentence of 20 words might become 100+ tokens. Long sequences are expensive: self-attention scales quadratically, and the model must learn to compose characters into meaningful units from scratch.
    • Word-level. Every whitespace-separated word is a token. This produces short sequences, but the vocabulary must include every word form the model will encounter. Morphological variations (run, running, runs, runner) each need separate entries. Rare words and proper nouns create an open vocabulary problem: the model cannot represent words it has never seen.
    • Character n-grams. Fixed-length character windows. This produces a manageable vocabulary but breaks words at arbitrary points with no linguistic meaning.

    Subword tokenization solves the open vocabulary problem while keeping sequences short. Common words (the, is, and) remain single tokens. Rare words are split into meaningful subunits: "unhappiness" becomes "un" + "happiness" or "un" + "hap" + "pi" + "ness", depending on the algorithm and vocabulary size. The model sees enough examples of each subword to learn useful representations, and any new word can be decomposed into known subwords.

    Key insight: Subword tokenization is a compression algorithm applied to language. Frequent byte sequences get short codes (single tokens), and rare sequences get longer codes (multiple tokens). This is the same principle behind Huffman coding and arithmetic coding.

    Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)

    BPE was originally a data compression algorithm. Its adaptation for NLP (Sennrich et al., 2016) starts with a character-level vocabulary and iteratively merges the most frequent adjacent pair of tokens into a new token. The algorithm:

    // BPE Training Algorithm
function train_bpe(corpus, num_merges):
    // Step 1: Initialize vocabulary with individual characters
    vocab = set(all_characters_in(corpus))
    splits = split_each_word_into_characters(corpus)

    for i in range(num_merges):
        // Step 2: Count all adjacent pairs
        pair_counts = count_adjacent_pairs(splits)

        // Step 3: Find the most frequent pair
        best_pair = argmax(pair_counts)

        // Step 4: Merge that pair everywhere in the corpus
        splits = merge_pair(splits, best_pair)
        vocab.add(best_pair.left + best_pair.right)

    return vocab, merge_rules

    Worked Example

    Consider a small corpus with word frequencies:

    • "low" : 5, "lowest" : 2, "newer" : 6, "wider" : 3

    Initial character vocabulary: {l, o, w, e, s, t, n, r, i, d}

    After splitting each word into characters (with end-of-word marker):

    • l o w </w> : 5
    • l o w e s t </w> : 2
    • n e w e r </w> : 6
    • w i d e r </w> : 3

    Merge 1: The most frequent pair is (e, r) with count 9 (6 from "newer" + 3 from "wider"). Merge into "er".

    Merge 2: Now (er, </w>) appears 9 times. Merge into "er</w>".

    Merge 3: (l, o) appears 7 times. Merge into "lo".

    Merge 4: (lo, w) appears 7 times. Merge into "low".

    After four merges, "low" is a single token. "lowest" is "low" + "e" + "s" + "t" + "</w>". The merge operations are stored as ordered rules and applied in the same order during inference.

    WordPiece

    WordPiece (Schuster and Nakajima, 2012) is the tokenization algorithm used by BERT and related models. It is similar to BPE in structure: start with characters, iteratively merge pairs. The difference is the merge criterion.

    BPE merges the most frequent pair. WordPiece merges the pair that maximizes the likelihood of the training corpus. Specifically, it chooses the pair (a, b) that maximizes:

    score(a, b) = freq(ab) / (freq(a) · freq(b))

    This is equivalent to maximizing the mutual information between a and b. A pair is merged when the two tokens appear together much more often than you would expect from their individual frequencies. This tends to produce more linguistically meaningful merges than pure frequency counting.

    WordPiece uses a special prefix to indicate subword continuation. A word like "playing" that gets split into two tokens becomes: "play" and "##ing". The "##" prefix indicates that this token is a continuation of a previous token, not the start of a new word. During detokenization, tokens with "##" are concatenated to the preceding token without a space.

    // WordPiece encoding of "unhappiness"
Input:  "unhappiness"
Lookup: "unhappiness" not in vocab
        "un" in vocab           // start fresh
        "##happiness" not in vocab
        "##hap" in vocab        // continuation
        "##pi" in vocab         // continuation
        "##ness" in vocab       // continuation
Output: ["un", "##hap", "##pi", "##ness"]
    Key insight: The difference between BPE and WordPiece is subtle but meaningful. BPE is greedy about frequency, while WordPiece is greedy about statistical association. In practice, both produce similar vocabularies, but WordPiece tends to keep morphological boundaries (prefixes, suffixes) more intact because morphemes have high mutual information with their stems.

    SentencePiece

    SentencePiece (Kudo and Richardson, 2018) addresses a fundamental limitation of BPE and WordPiece: they require pre-tokenized input. Before running BPE, you must split the text into words using whitespace or language-specific rules. This fails for languages without whitespace (Chinese, Japanese, Thai) and introduces a dependency on language-specific preprocessing.

    SentencePiece treats the input as a raw sequence of Unicode characters, including spaces. Spaces are not used as word boundaries; instead, they are replaced with a special character (the Unicode block element, often displayed as an underscore). The entire text is then segmented into subwords directly from the raw character sequence.

    // SentencePiece treats space as just another character
Input:  "New York is great"
Tokens: ["_New", "_York", "_is", "_great"]

// A word in the middle of another word has no underscore
Input:  "unhappiness"
Tokens: ["_un", "happiness"]    // or other splits

// Works for any language without modification
Input:  "東京は素晴らしい"
Tokens: ["_東京", "は", "素晴らし", "い"]

    SentencePiece supports both BPE and Unigram as its underlying segmentation algorithm. The Unigram model works in reverse compared to BPE: it starts with a large vocabulary and iteratively removes tokens that contribute least to the corpus likelihood. This produces a probabilistic model that can generate multiple possible tokenizations for the same input, which can be useful for data augmentation during training.

    Vocabulary Size Tradeoffs

    Vocabulary size is a fundamental design choice that affects model size, sequence length, and representation quality. The tradeoffs:

    Too small (e.g., 1,000 tokens)

    • Nearly character-level tokenization. Sequences are very long.
    • The model must learn to compose meaning from individual characters, which requires more layers and more computation per word.
    • Self-attention cost scales quadratically with sequence length, so long sequences are expensive.
    • The embedding matrix is small, so total model size decreases.

    Too large (e.g., 500,000 tokens)

    • Many tokens appear rarely in the training data. Their embeddings are poorly trained.
    • The embedding matrix and output projection layer become very large (each token needs a d-dimensional vector). For d = 4096, a vocabulary of 500k requires 2 billion parameters just for the embedding layer.
    • The softmax over the full vocabulary at each position becomes a computational bottleneck.

    The sweet spot

    Most modern models use vocabulary sizes between 30,000 and 100,000 tokens. GPT-2 uses 50,257. LLaMA uses 32,000. Common English words are single tokens, and rare words are split into 2 to 4 pieces. This balances sequence length, embedding quality, and computational cost.

    Special Tokens

    Every tokenizer includes special tokens that carry structural or control meaning rather than linguistic content. These tokens are assigned fixed IDs and are never produced by the merge or split algorithm. They are inserted explicitly during data preprocessing.

    Token Purpose Used by
    [PAD] Padding. Fills shorter sequences to a uniform length within a batch. Masked out during attention. All models with batched inference
    [CLS] Classification token. Placed at the start of input. Its final hidden state is used as the aggregate sequence representation for classification tasks. BERT, RoBERTa
    [SEP] Separator. Marks the boundary between two segments (e.g., question and passage in QA tasks). BERT, RoBERTa
    [MASK] Masking token for masked language modeling. Replaces a token during training; the model predicts the original. BERT, RoBERTa, ALBERT
    [BOS] Beginning of sequence. Signals that generation should start. Also called <s> in some tokenizers. GPT, LLaMA, T5
    [EOS] End of sequence. Signals that the sequence is complete. Stops autoregressive generation. GPT, LLaMA, T5
    [UNK] Unknown token. Used as a fallback when a character is not in the vocabulary. Rare with subword tokenizers. Most models

    The choice of special tokens varies between models and affects how inputs are formatted. BERT expects [CLS] + tokens + [SEP] for single sequences, and [CLS] + tokens_A + [SEP] + tokens_B + [SEP] for pairs. GPT-style models typically only use [BOS] and [EOS]. Getting the special token format wrong is a common source of bugs when fine-tuning pretrained models.

    Key insight: From the model's perspective, special tokens are just tokens with learned embeddings. The model learns what [CLS] means through training: its embedding evolves to aggregate information from the full sequence. There is nothing inherently special about the token itself; its function comes entirely from how it is used during training.

    Practical Considerations

    When training a new tokenizer or choosing a pretrained one:

    • Train on your domain. A tokenizer trained on English web text will poorly handle code, mathematical notation, or non-Latin scripts. If your training data differs significantly from the tokenizer's training data, consider retraining the tokenizer on a representative corpus.
    • Byte-level BPE (used by GPT-2 and later) operates on UTF-8 bytes instead of Unicode characters. This guarantees that any input can be tokenized (no [UNK] tokens), at the cost of occasionally splitting characters into multiple byte tokens for non-ASCII text.
    • Never modify a tokenizer after training the model. The model's embeddings are tied to specific token IDs. Adding, removing, or reordering tokens will corrupt the embeddings. If you must add tokens (e.g., domain-specific terms), resize the embedding matrix and initialize the new rows, but understand that performance on the new tokens will be poor until fine-tuned.
