    Boosting & AdaBoost

    June 2026 · 18 min read

    Bagging in Part 5 built its trees independently and averaged them to cut variance. Boosting does the opposite: it builds learners in sequence, and each new one is steered toward the examples the current ensemble still gets wrong. The classic instance, AdaBoost, combines a crowd of "weak" learners, each barely better than chance, into a single strong classifier. This part states the algorithm precisely, shows it running, explains why its peculiar weight update is exactly right, and connects it to gradient boosting.

    Weak Learners and the Idea

    A weak learner is any model that does just a little better than guessing. The standard choice is a decision stump, a depth-one tree that thresholds a single feature. No stump can capture much on its own, but boosting orchestrates many of them. The plan is simple to state: keep a weight on every training point, fit a stump that does well on the current weights, then increase the weight of every point the stump misclassified so the next stump is forced to attend to them. The final prediction is a weighted vote of all the stumps, where better stumps get a louder voice.

    The AdaBoost Algorithm

    Work with labels $y_i \in \{-1, +1\}$. Start with uniform weights $w_i = 1/N$. For each round $t = 1, \dots, T$:

    1. Fit a stump $h_t$ minimizing the weighted error $\varepsilon_t = \sum_i w_i \,\mathbb{1}[h_t(x_i) \ne y_i]$.
    2. Give it a vote $\displaystyle \alpha_t = \tfrac12 \ln\frac{1 - \varepsilon_t}{\varepsilon_t}$.
    3. Reweight: $w_i \leftarrow w_i \, e^{-\alpha_t y_i h_t(x_i)}$, then renormalize so the weights sum to $1$.

    The final classifier is the sign of the weighted vote:

    $$H(x) = \operatorname{sign}\!\left(\sum_{t=1}^{T} \alpha_t \, h_t(x)\right).$$

    Two details carry the whole method. The vote $\alpha_t$ grows as the error $\varepsilon_t$ shrinks: a stump with $\varepsilon = 0.5$ earns $\alpha = 0$ (it is worthless and silenced), while a near-perfect stump earns a large $\alpha$. The reweighting term $e^{-\alpha_t y_i h_t(x_i)}$ is less than $1$ when the stump is right on point $i$ (since $y_i h_t = +1$) and greater than $1$ when it is wrong, so correctly classified points get lighter and misclassified points get heavier. The next round therefore concentrates exactly where the last one failed.

    Watch the point sizes: a point the early stumps misclassify swells round after round until some later stump is bribed, by its now-large weight, into getting it right. The combined accuracy climbs past what any single stump achieves, even though every stump on its own is mediocre. That is the whole promise of boosting, made concrete.

    Why the Weight Update Is Exactly Right

    AdaBoost is not a heuristic; it is stage-wise minimization of the exponential loss

    $$L = \sum_{i} \exp\!\big(-y_i \, F(x_i)\big), \qquad F(x) = \sum_t \alpha_t h_t(x).$$

    Adding one term $\alpha h$ to $F$ multiplies each point's loss by $e^{-\alpha y_i h(x_i)}$, which is precisely the AdaBoost reweighting factor: the weights are the current per-point exponential losses. Minimizing the new total loss over $\alpha$, for a fixed stump with weighted error $\varepsilon$, gives

    $$\frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha}\Big(e^{-\alpha}(1-\varepsilon) + e^{\alpha}\varepsilon\Big) = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \alpha = \tfrac12\ln\frac{1-\varepsilon}{\varepsilon}.$$

    So both the reweighting and the famous $\alpha$ formula drop straight out of greedily minimizing exponential loss, one weak learner at a time. As long as every round finds a stump with $\varepsilon_t < 0.5$ (better than chance), the training error is provably driven down at an exponential rate.

    Boosting can still overfit. The exponential loss punishes confidently wrong points harshly, so AdaBoost is sensitive to label noise and mislabeled outliers, whose weights can blow up. In practice the number of rounds $T$ is a regularizer chosen by validation, and shallow stumps keep each learner weak on purpose.

    From AdaBoost to Gradient Boosting

    Seeing AdaBoost as loss minimization opens the door to a generalization. Why stick to exponential loss, or to $\pm 1$ classification? Gradient boosting replaces the cleverly engineered weight update with a direct one: at each round, fit the next learner to the negative gradient of whatever differentiable loss is in use, evaluated at the current predictions. For squared-error regression that negative gradient is just the residual $y_i - F(x_i)$, so gradient boosting becomes "fit the next tree to the residuals", which is the form built and tested in From Scratch · C++ · Boosting. AdaBoost is then recognizable as the special case using exponential loss.
