    NLP — Recommended Papers

    Rough-draft reading list · 12 papers, chronological

    Rough draft. The entries below are stubs — each links to the paper and gives a one-paragraph pitch. Proper write-ups will land later as part of the “Recommended Papers” series.

    An opinionated reading list for anyone working through the CS336 curriculum (or just trying to understand how modern language models came to be). The papers are listed chronologically; click any title for the (currently stub) per-paper page.

    The list

    • Prediction and Entropy of Printed English

      Shannon · 1950 (published 1951) · Bell System Technical Journal

      This is the paper that invented the language model. Shannon framed English as a stochastic source, defined its entropy, and ran human-prediction experiments to estimate it. Every probabilistic language model since — from n-grams to GPT — is doing a version of what Shannon proposed here.

    • A Neural Probabilistic Language Model

      Bengio et al. · 2003 · JMLR

      The first neural language model. Before this paper, language models were n-grams with smoothing. Bengio et al. proposed learning continuous word embeddings and feeding them through an MLP to predict the next word — the recipe every modern LM is a descendant of.

    • Sequence to Sequence Learning with Neural Networks

      Sutskever et al. · 2014 · NeurIPS

      Established the encoder-decoder paradigm that dominates modern conditional generation: encode the input into a fixed-length vector with one RNN, decode the output with another. Even though the Transformer eventually replaced the LSTM backbone, the encoder/decoder framing survived.

    • Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate

      Bahdanau et al. · 2015 · ICLR

      Introduced attention — the mechanism the entire Transformer architecture is built from. The single-vector bottleneck of vanilla seq2seq vanishes once the decoder can soft-attend to every encoder state at every step.

    • Neural Machine Translation of Rare Words with Subword Units

      Sennrich, Haddow & Birch · 2016 · ACL

      The paper that brought byte-pair encoding to NLP. BPE remains the tokenizer of choice for GPT-2/3/4, Llama, Mistral, and most modern frontier LMs. The CS336 lecture's tokenization section is largely a tour of this idea.

    • Attention Is All You Need

      Vaswani et al. · 2017 · NeurIPS

      The paper of the era. Showed that you can drop the recurrence entirely and rely on self-attention + feedforward blocks stacked deep. Faster to train (parallelism over time steps), scales better, and powers essentially every frontier LM in 2026.

    • Deep Contextualized Word Representations

      Peters et al. · 2018 · NAACL

      ELMo proved the “pretrain a deep language model, then plug it into downstream tasks” recipe before BERT made it canonical. The first crisp demonstration that contextual word embeddings dominate static ones (word2vec, GloVe).

    • BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding

      Devlin et al. · 2019 · NAACL

      The paper that defined “modern NLP” for several years. Cast every classification task as fine-tuning a single pretrained Transformer encoder, set state of the art across the GLUE board, and made pretraining + fine-tuning the default research workflow.

    • Language Models are Unsupervised Multitask Learners

      Radford et al. · 2019 · OpenAI Technical Report

      The first crisp demonstration that a large enough autoregressive Transformer trained on web text can do zero-shot task transfer — no fine-tuning required. The intellectual bridge from BERT-style pretraining to GPT-3 in-context learning.

    • Language Models are Few-Shot Learners

      Brown et al. · 2020 · NeurIPS

      Discovered in-context learning — that a 175B-parameter autoregressive LM can perform new tasks from a handful of examples in the prompt, with no parameter updates at all. The paradigm shift from fine-tuning to prompting was born here.

    • Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models

      Kaplan et al. · 2020 · Preprint (arXiv)

      Showed that test loss for autoregressive LMs follows clean power laws in model size, dataset size, and compute — over many orders of magnitude. This is the paper that made “just scale it” a defensible engineering plan rather than a hope.

    • Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models

      Hoffmann et al. · 2022 · NeurIPS

      Corrected Kaplan et al.’s scaling laws and showed that previous frontier models (GPT-3, PaLM) were dramatically under-trained. The headline finding — data should scale roughly in lockstep with parameters — reshaped how every frontier lab budgets compute.
