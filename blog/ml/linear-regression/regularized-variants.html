    Regularized Linear Regression Variants

    July 2026 · 23 min read

    Regularization adds a penalty on coefficient size to the least-squares objective. The penalty deliberately biases coefficients toward simpler solutions, which sounds like it should hurt, and on the training set it does. It helps on new data because it trades a controlled amount of bias for an often much larger reduction in variance, which is exactly the trade Part 2 hinted at when a nearly-singular $X^\top X$ let noise get amplified into wild coefficients.

    Motivation: Why Penalize at All

    Ordinary least squares is the best linear unbiased estimator (BLUE), meaning it has the smallest variance among unbiased estimators that are linear in $y$ (the Gauss-Markov theorem), but that guarantee is conditional: it requires the model to be correctly specified as linear in the parameters, the errors to have zero conditional mean given the features (exogeneity), the errors to have constant variance and zero pairwise covariance with each other (homoscedasticity plus uncorrelated errors, together called a spherical error covariance, $\text{Cov}(\varepsilon\mid X)=\sigma^2 I$), and the design matrix to have full column rank (no perfect multicollinearity). Homoscedasticity alone is not sufficient for BLUE, correlated errors (for instance from time-series or grouped structure) invalidate the guarantee just as much as unequal variances do, even if every error has the same variance. Gauss-Markov says nothing about non-linear estimators, and it does not require normally distributed errors, that assumption is only needed later for exact finite-sample confidence intervals and hypothesis tests, not for the unbiasedness or variance-optimality claim itself. Even when every one of those assumptions holds, "smallest variance among unbiased estimators" is not the same as "small in absolute terms." When features are correlated or numerous relative to the sample size, Part 2 showed that $X^\top X$ becomes ill-conditioned and OLS coefficients can swing wildly with small changes to the data, even though predictions can remain comparatively stable. A biased estimator that shrinks coefficients toward zero can have dramatically lower variance, and if the variance reduction outweighs the bias introduced, the biased estimator wins on expected prediction error. This is the bias-variance tradeoff, and it is the entire justification for everything in this post.

    Always scale first, and handle the intercept separately. Ridge, Lasso, and Elastic Net all penalize coefficient size directly. That penalty is only fair across features if they are on comparable scales, so features should be standardized (Part 3 of the data-preparation series) before fitting any of them. The intercept must also be kept out of the penalty entirely, since it exists to absorb the mean of $y$ and has no reason to be pulled toward zero; the next section makes that precise, because writing the design matrix $X$ with a leading column of ones (the convention used throughout Part 2) and then penalizing $\|w\|^2$ over every coordinate of $w$, including the one multiplying that ones column, would silently and incorrectly penalize the intercept too.

    Ridge Regression

    To keep the intercept out of the penalty, work with centered data. Let $\bar x\in\mathbb R^d$ be the column means of the original predictors (excluding the ones column) and $\bar y$ the mean of $y$. Define the centered design matrix $\tilde X$ (each column with its mean subtracted) and centered response $\tilde y = y-\bar y\mathbf 1$. Fit the penalized regression on $(\tilde X,\tilde y)$ with no intercept column and a slope vector $w\in\mathbb R^d$:

    $$J_{\text{ridge}}(w) = \|\tilde y-\tilde Xw\|^2 + \lambda\sum_j w_j^2 = \|\tilde y-\tilde Xw\|^2+\lambda\|w\|^2.$$

    Differentiating and setting the gradient to zero, the same way Part 2 derived the plain normal equations:

    $$\nabla J_{\text{ridge}} = -2\tilde X^\top(\tilde y-\tilde Xw)+2\lambda w = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; (\tilde X^\top \tilde X+\lambda I)w = \tilde X^\top \tilde y \;\Longrightarrow\; w = (\tilde X^\top \tilde X+\lambda I)^{-1}\tilde X^\top \tilde y.$$

    Because $\tilde X$ has no ones column, $\lambda I$ only ever touches the slope coefficients, never an intercept, so there is nothing left to fix. The intercept in the original coordinates is recovered afterward as $b=\bar y-\bar x^\top w$, which is exactly the condition that forces the fitted hyperplane to pass through the centroid $(\bar x,\bar y)$ of the data. Everything below is written in terms of this $w$ and this $\tilde X$; an equivalent way to see the same thing without centering is to keep the ones column but penalize with a matrix $P=\operatorname{diag}(0,1,1,\dots,1)$ instead of $I$, solving $w=(X^\top X+\lambda P)^{-1}X^\top y$, which zeros out the penalty on the intercept's own coordinate directly. The two constructions give the same slopes and the same intercept; centering is more common in practice because it also avoids ever having to invert a matrix with a very different-scaled ones column mixed in with standardized features.

    Adding $\lambda I$ before inverting is what makes this useful: even if $\tilde X^\top \tilde X$ is singular or near-singular (the exact multicollinearity failure from Part 2), every eigenvalue of $\tilde X^\top \tilde X+\lambda I$ is shifted up by $\lambda$, so the matrix is always invertible for any $\lambda>0$. Writing $\tilde X^\top \tilde X$ in terms of its eigenvalues $d_j^2\ge 0$, the ridge solution shrinks the OLS solution's component along each eigenvector by a factor $\dfrac{d_j^2}{d_j^2+\lambda}$. Directions with a large eigenvalue (well-determined by the data) are barely shrunk; directions with a small eigenvalue (the unstable, poorly-determined directions that caused the original instability) are shrunk the most. Ridge does not treat every coefficient equally; it specifically suppresses the directions that were least trustworthy to begin with.

    A Bayesian reading. Suppose each slope carries an independent Gaussian prior belief $w_j \sim \mathcal N(0,\tau^2)$, the intercept carries a flat, unpenalized prior (consistent with excluding it from the penalty above), and the observation noise is $\tilde y\mid \tilde X,w \sim \mathcal N(\tilde Xw,\sigma^2 I)$. The maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate maximizes $p(w\mid \tilde X,\tilde y)\propto p(\tilde y\mid \tilde X,w)\,p(w)$. Taking the negative log of that product and dropping constants gives exactly $\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\tilde y-\tilde Xw\|^2 + \frac{1}{2\tau^2}\|w\|^2$, which is the ridge objective with $\lambda=\sigma^2/\tau^2$. Ridge regression is what a belief that "slopes are probably small" looks like once it is turned into a point estimate; a stronger prior belief (smaller $\tau^2$) corresponds to a larger $\lambda$. This is the online-learning connection flagged at the end of Part 3.

    Lasso Regression

    Lasso replaces the penalty with an $\ell_1$ norm, using the same centering convention as Ridge above so the intercept never enters the penalty: fit $\tilde X$ and $\tilde y$ (each predictor column and the response centered to zero mean), then recover $b=\bar y-\bar x^\top w$ afterward.

    $$J_{\text{lasso}}(w) = \tfrac12\|\tilde y-\tilde Xw\|^2 + \lambda\sum_j|w_j|$$

    (using the common convention of a $\tfrac12$ in front of the squared error, which only rescales $\lambda$ relative to the plain-SSE form used for Ridge above, and keeps the formulas below clean). Because $|w_j|$ is not differentiable at $w_j=0$, there is generally no closed-form solution the way there is for Ridge. There is one instructive special case: if the columns of $\tilde X$ are orthonormal ($\tilde X^\top \tilde X=I$, meaning each column also has unit norm, not merely unit variance), the objective separates into $d$ independent one-dimensional problems, and each has the closed-form solution

    $$w_j^\star = S(z_j,\lambda) = \operatorname{sign}(z_j)\max(|z_j|-\lambda,\,0), \qquad z_j = (\tilde X^\top \tilde y)_j = w_{j,\text{OLS}},$$

    the soft-thresholding operator: shrink the OLS estimate toward zero by $\lambda$, and if that overshoots past zero, clip it to exactly zero. This is the key structural difference from ridge's shrink factor $d_j^2/(d_j^2+\lambda)$, which multiplies the estimate and can approach zero but, for finite $\lambda$, never exactly reaches it. Soft-thresholding subtracts, and a subtraction can reach zero and stop.

    Real feature sets are not orthonormal, so Lasso is solved with coordinate descent: cycle through coefficients one at a time, holding every other coefficient fixed, and apply the same soft-thresholding idea to the partial residual. A common source of confusion here is scale: standardizing a column to unit variance makes $x_j^\top x_j$ equal to $n-1$ (or $n$, depending on which variance convention is used), not $1$, since $x_j^\top x_j=\sum_i x_{ij}^2$ is a sum of $n$ roughly-unit-sized squared terms, so it grows with the sample size. The clean formula above only applies when columns are scaled to unit $\ell_2$ norm ($\|x_j\|_2=1$), which is a stronger condition than unit variance and depends on $n$. Without that extra normalization step, the coordinate update needs its own denominator:

    $$w_j \leftarrow \frac{S\big(x_j^\top r^{(j)},\,\lambda\big)}{x_j^\top x_j}, \qquad r^{(j)} = \tilde y - \tilde Xw + x_j w_j \text{ (the residual with feature $j$'s own contribution added back)}.$$
    // coordinate descent for Lasso on centered data
    repeat until coefficients stop changing:
    for j in 1..d:
    r_partial = y_c - Xc·w + x_j * w_j // add back feature j's own contribution
    z_j = dot(x_j, r_partial) // simple regression of the partial residual on x_j
    denom_j = dot(x_j, x_j) // = 1 only if x_j has unit L2 norm, not just unit variance
    w_j = soft_threshold(z_j, lambda) / denom_j // sign(z_j) * max(|z_j| - lambda, 0) / denom_j

    Each coordinate update is exactly the one-dimensional soft-threshold formula above, applied to "how much of the leftover signal, after removing every other feature's contribution, lines up with feature $j$," and rescaled by that feature's own squared norm. Cycling through all coefficients repeatedly converges to the global optimum, because the Lasso objective, while not differentiable everywhere, is still convex. In practice, libraries differ in exactly how they fold $n$ into the objective (for instance, whether the squared-error term is divided by $n$ or $2n$) and in exactly how they standardize columns before fitting, so a $\lambda$ value tuned in one library is not automatically the same penalty strength in another; always re-run cross-validation rather than porting a numeric $\lambda$ across implementations.

    Elastic Net

    Elastic Net combines both penalties, again on centered $(\tilde X,\tilde y)$:

    $$J_{\text{enet}}(w) = \tfrac12\|\tilde y-\tilde Xw\|^2 + \lambda\Big(\alpha\|w\|_1 + \tfrac{1-\alpha}{2}\|w\|_2^2\Big), \qquad \alpha\in[0,1],$$

    which mirrors the parameterization used by scikit-learn and glmnet: $\lambda$ (called alpha in scikit-learn, confusingly) sets the overall penalty strength, and $\alpha$ (scikit-learn's l1_ratio) sets the mix between Lasso ($\alpha=1$) and Ridge ($\alpha=0$). The two libraries do not use numerically identical conventions, though: they differ in whether the squared-error term is averaged over $n$, in the default variance convention used to standardize columns, and in how the intercept and centering are handled internally, so a specific $\lambda$ value fit in one library will not reproduce the same effective penalty in the other. Treat $\lambda$ as library-specific and re-tune it by cross-validation rather than copying a numeric value across tools. The reason to want both penalties at once is a specific failure mode of pure Lasso: when several features are highly correlated, Lasso tends to arbitrarily pick one of them and zero out the rest, which is unstable across resamples of the data (a different, equally correlated feature could easily have been picked instead). The small ridge component breaks ties between correlated features by encouraging them to share the coefficient roughly equally instead of picking one arbitrarily, a behavior called the grouping effect. Elastic Net is the common default in settings with many correlated features and far more features than rows, such as genomic or text data.

    Constraint-Region Geometry

    Each penalized objective has an equivalent constrained form: minimize $\|\tilde y-\tilde Xw\|^2$ subject to $\|w\|_2^2\le t$ for Ridge, or $\|w\|_1\le t$ for Lasso, where $t$ and $\lambda$ are in one-to-one correspondence. The unconstrained squared-error objective has elliptical contours centered at the OLS solution $w_{\text{OLS}}$: every point on one ellipse has the same SSE. The constrained solution is the point where the smallest such ellipse first touches the allowed region ($\|w\|_2\le\sqrt t$, a circle in two dimensions, a ball in general; or $\|w\|_1\le t$, a diamond in two dimensions, a cross-polytope in general).

    A circle has no flat edges or corners, so the point where a growing ellipse first touches it is generic: it moves continuously as $t$ shrinks and, for finite $t>0$, essentially never lands exactly on an axis, so Ridge coefficients shrink smoothly but stay nonzero. A diamond is different: it has flat edges and corners sitting exactly on the coordinate axes. At a corner, many different edge orientations meet, so a whole range of possible ellipse shapes and orientations, elongated in many different directions, can all reach the diamond's boundary at that same corner first. Touching a point in the middle of a flat edge, by contrast, requires the ellipse to be oriented in one very specific way relative to that edge's fixed direction. Corners are simply easier targets to hit first across the range of ellipse shapes that correlated, differently-scaled features tend to produce, which is why Lasso solutions land exactly on the axes so often in practice. When the point of contact is a corner, every coordinate off that axis is exactly zero. That is the entire geometric reason Lasso produces sparse solutions and Ridge does not; it has nothing to do with the specific value 1 or 2 in the exponent and everything to do with the constraint region having corners on the axes.

    Choosing Lambda

    The penalty strength $\lambda$ (and, for Elastic Net, the mixing parameter $\alpha$) is a hyperparameter, not something least squares can solve for: setting $\lambda=0$ always minimizes the penalized objective at least as well on the training data as any $\lambda>0$, so choosing $\lambda$ by training error alone would always pick zero regularization. Instead $\lambda$ is chosen by validation or, more commonly, $k$-fold cross-validation: fit the model at each candidate $\lambda$ on $k-1$ folds, evaluate on the held-out fold, average across folds, and pick the $\lambda$ with the best average held-out score (or, for extra stability, the largest $\lambda$ within one standard error of the best score, since simpler models are preferable when performance is statistically indistinguishable). Fitting the model at many $\lambda$ values and tracking how each coefficient moves is exactly the regularization path in the animation above, and Ridge's closed form and Lasso's coordinate descent are both cheap enough to repeat across a grid of dozens of $\lambda$ values without much cost. Too little regularization behaves like ordinary least squares and keeps its variance problem; too much regularization underfits by forcing genuinely useful coefficients toward zero alongside the noisy ones.
