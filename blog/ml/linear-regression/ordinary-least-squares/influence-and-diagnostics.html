    OLS Diagnostics: Leverage, Influence, and Model Failure

    July 2026 · 19 min read

    The broader series' Assumptions and Diagnostics post covers the full LINE checklist and its standard plots. This part goes narrower and deeper on one specific, high-stakes question that page only introduces: which individual rows are actually steering the fitted coefficients, by how much, and what to do once you have found one. Every number below is computed on one small dataset with a single planted high-leverage point, so the mechanics can be checked by hand.

    Errors Versus Residuals, Again

    Part 4 defined the true, unobservable error $\varepsilon_i=y_i-x_i^\top w$ for the true (unknown) $w$. This part works entirely with the observable residual $e_i=y_i-x_i^\top\hat w$, which is only an estimate of $\varepsilon_i$ and, unlike $\varepsilon_i$, satisfies exact algebraic identities by construction ($\sum_ie_i=0$ and $X^\top e=0$, from Part 2 and Part 3's orthogonality results) regardless of whether any modeling assumption holds. Diagnostics work by treating patterns in $e_i$ as evidence about $\varepsilon_i$, not because the two are the same object, but because a badly behaved $e_i$ is strong evidence of a badly behaved $\varepsilon_i$, or of a genuinely unusual row.

    The Hat Matrix and Leverage

    The fitted values are a linear function of $y$ alone: $\hat y = X\hat w = X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y = Hy$, where $H=X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top$ is the hat matrix (it "puts the hat on" $y$). Its diagonal entries, $h_{ii}$, are each row's leverage: how much row $i$'s own $y_i$ value contributes to its own fitted value, $\partial\hat y_i/\partial y_i=h_{ii}$, entirely independent of what $y_i$ actually is. Leverage depends only on where a row sits in predictor space, never on its target value. Two properties are always true whenever an intercept is included: $0\le h_{ii}\le 1$ for every row, and $\sum_ih_{ii}=p$ (the number of parameters), so the average leverage is $p/n$; a common rule of thumb flags $h_{ii}$ above roughly $2p/n$ or $3p/n$ as noteworthy.

    Running example

    $n=8$ points, $x=[1,2,3,4,5,6,7,15]$ (seven points roughly on a line, an eighth planted far out at $x=15$), $y=[3.1,4.9,7.2,8.8,11.3,12.8,15.1,22.0]$. Fitting $\hat y=b+wx$ gives $w=1.3501$, $b=3.3930$. Leverages: [0.268, 0.210, 0.167, 0.139, 0.126, 0.128, 0.145, 0.817]. Average leverage is $p/n=2/8=0.25$; the last point's leverage of 0.817 is more than $3\times$ that average and close to the theoretical ceiling of 1.

    Studentized Residuals

    Raw residuals are not directly comparable across rows, because $\text{Var}(e_i)=\sigma^2(1-h_{ii})$ depends on $h_{ii}$: a high-leverage row's residual has systematically smaller variance than a low-leverage row's, even under identical assumptions. Internally studentized residuals correct for this using the fit's own $\hat\sigma^2$:

    $$r_i = \frac{e_i}{\hat\sigma\sqrt{1-h_{ii}}}.$$

    This still has a subtle flaw: $e_i$ itself was part of what produced $\hat\sigma^2$, so an outlier inflates the very yardstick used to judge it, understating its own unusualness. Externally studentized residuals (also called deleted or jackknife residuals) fix this by using $\hat\sigma^2_{(i)}$, the residual variance from refitting with row $i$ removed:

    $$t_i = \frac{e_i}{\hat\sigma_{(i)}\sqrt{1-h_{ii}}} = e_i\sqrt{\frac{n-p-1}{SSE(1-h_{ii})-e_i^2}},$$

    where the right-hand form is an algebraic shortcut that avoids literally refitting $n$ times. $t_i$ follows a $t$-distribution with $n-p-1$ degrees of freedom under the model, making it directly usable for an outlier test.

    On the running example

    Internally studentized residuals: [-1.21, -0.85, -0.17, 0.00, 0.78, 0.88, 1.54, -2.43]. The last point already looks large internally. Its externally studentized residual is $t_8=-17.78$, dramatically larger, because removing it drops $\hat\sigma_{(8)}$ to $0.216$ (versus $\hat\sigma=1.582$ for the full fit): the outlier had been inflating the very variance estimate meant to judge it.

    Cook's Distance

    Leverage measures how unusual a row's predictors are; the residual measures how surprising its target value is. Neither alone predicts how much the fitted coefficients would actually move if that row were dropped; Cook's distance combines both into a single, calibrated measure of overall influence:

    $$D_i = \frac{e_i^2}{p\cdot\hat\sigma^2}\cdot\frac{h_{ii}}{(1-h_{ii})^2} = \frac{r_i^2}{p}\cdot\frac{h_{ii}}{1-h_{ii}}.$$

    A common rule of thumb treats $D_i>1$ (or, more conservatively, $D_i>4/n$) as worth investigating. On the running example, Cook's distances are [0.270, 0.096, 0.003, 0.000, 0.044, 0.057, 0.201, 13.18]: every ordinary point is comfortably under the $4/n=0.5$ threshold, while the planted point's $D_8=13.18$ exceeds it by more than $26\times$, and exceeds the stricter $D_i>1$ threshold by more than $13\times$. This single number is already enough to flag row 8 as dominating the fit, before looking at anything else.

    DFFITS and DFBETAS

    Cook's distance summarizes influence on the fitted values as a single number per row. Two related diagnostics break that influence down further. DFFITS measures the standardized change in row $i$'s own fitted value when it is removed:

    $$\text{DFFITS}_i = t_i\sqrt{\frac{h_{ii}}{1-h_{ii}}}, \qquad \text{flag when } |\text{DFFITS}_i| > 2\sqrt{p/n}.$$

    DFBETAS goes one step further and measures the standardized change in one specific coefficient:

    $$\text{DFBETAS}_{j}(i) = \frac{\hat w_j-\hat w_{j(i)}}{\hat\sigma_{(i)}\sqrt{\left[(X^\top X)^{-1}\right]_{jj}}}, \qquad \text{flag when } |\text{DFBETAS}_j(i)| > 2/\sqrt n.$$

    On the running example, for the slope coefficient and row 8: the threshold is $2\sqrt{2/8}=1.0$ for DFFITS and $2/\sqrt8=0.707$ for DFBETAS. The actual values are $\text{DFFITS}_8=-37.56$ and $\text{DFBETAS}_{w}(8)=-34.57$, both roughly $35$-$40\times$ their flagging thresholds. Removing row 8 alone would move the slope from $1.350$ to $1.996$, a 48% change from a single one-eighth of the dataset, which is exactly what such an extreme DFBETAS value is designed to warn about before you ever refit anything by hand.

    Supporting Plots and Formal Tests

    The residuals-vs-fitted, normal Q-Q, and scale-location plots, and the Breusch-Pagan/White and Durbin-Watson tests, are covered in depth in the broader series' Assumptions and Diagnostics post and are not repeated here; they remain exactly as relevant for an OLS-specific workflow as for the general case, since leverage and Cook's distance are complementary to those checks, not replacements for them. What is specific to this part is the leverage-versus-studentized-residual plot: plotting $h_{ii}$ on one axis and $t_i$ (or $r_i$) on the other, often with Cook's distance contours overlaid, makes the two-dimensional nature of influence visible at a glance, a point with high leverage but a small residual sits far to the right but near the horizontal zero line, and only a point that is unusual on both axes at once lands inside a high-Cook's-distance contour.

    Validate Before You Delete

    A large Cook's distance or DFFITS is a reason to look, not a license to delete. The correct order of operations:

    1. Audit the row for a data or measurement error first. A transposed digit, a unit mismatch, or a sensor fault is a legitimate reason to correct or remove a row, but that determination comes from investigating the data's provenance, not from the size of $D_i$ alone.
    2. If the row is valid, ask whether it represents a real, if rare, regime. A genuinely extreme but correctly recorded observation carries real information (often disproportionately more information than an ordinary row, precisely because of its high leverage) and deleting it can bias the model toward the common case at the expense of the tails that may matter most for the application.
    3. Prefer a model change over silent deletion when the point is valid. Robust regression or quantile regression (Robust and Quantile Regression) down-weight extreme residuals systematically and transparently, rather than hand-picking which rows to exclude, which is difficult to justify after the fact and easy to (even unintentionally) tune toward a preferred result.
    4. Check generalization on held-out data before and after any change. Refit with and without the disputed row on a training split, and compare predictive performance on a separate held-out split; a row whose removal only improves the fit on the same data used to make the decision is not evidence the removal was correct, only that the training data was fit more closely, which is a different and less useful property.

    On the running example, row 8 warrants exactly this workflow: check whether $x=15,\,y=22.0$ is a real, correctly recorded case (perhaps a genuinely larger example the other seven rows simply do not represent) before concluding the linear relationship estimated from the other seven points is "the" relationship and this row is merely noise; the truth could easily run the other way, with the first seven points under-representing the full relationship's curvature at larger $x$.
