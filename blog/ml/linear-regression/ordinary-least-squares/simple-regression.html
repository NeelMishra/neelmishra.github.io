    Simple OLS: The Closed-Form Line

    July 2026 · 14 min read

    Simple regression, one predictor and an intercept, is where the OLS closed form is easiest to derive by hand and easiest to check by hand. Everything here generalizes to the multiple-predictor case in Part 3, but the one-predictor case is worth deriving carefully on its own, because the intuition for "slope equals covariance over variance" is what makes the matrix version in Least Squares Geometry feel inevitable rather than merely correct.

    The Model and the Objective

    The model is $\hat y_i = b + wx_i$ for $i=1,\dots,n$. OLS chooses $b,w$ to minimize the sum of squared residuals:

    $$L(b,w)=\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - b - wx_i)^2.$$

    $L$ is a quadratic, convex function of $(b,w)$, so its unique minimum (when $x$ is not constant) is found by setting both partial derivatives to zero.

    Deriving the Closed Form

    Differentiate with respect to $b$ first:

    $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial b} = -2\sum_i (y_i-b-wx_i) = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \sum_i y_i = nb + w\sum_i x_i.$$

    Dividing by $n$ gives the first normal equation directly in terms of the means $\bar x,\bar y$:

    $$b = \bar y - w\bar x. \tag{1}$$

    This says the fitted line always passes through the point of means $(\bar x,\bar y)$, for any slope $w$; the intercept is whatever value makes that true. Now differentiate with respect to $w$:

    $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w} = -2\sum_i x_i(y_i-b-wx_i) = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \sum_i x_iy_i = b\sum_i x_i + w\sum_i x_i^2.$$

    Substitute (1) for $b$ and expand $\sum_i x_i = n\bar x$:

    $$\sum_i x_iy_i = (\bar y-w\bar x)\,n\bar x + w\sum_i x_i^2 = n\bar x\bar y - w n\bar x^2 + w\sum_i x_i^2.$$

    Collecting the $w$ terms:

    $$\underbrace{\sum_i x_iy_i - n\bar x\bar y}_{S_{xy}} = w\underbrace{\left(\sum_i x_i^2 - n\bar x^2\right)}_{S_{xx}} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad w = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}. \tag{2}$$

    Equations (1) and (2) are the entire closed form. In words: the slope is the sample covariance of $x$ and $y$ divided by the sample variance of $x$ (the factor of $n-1$ or $n$ used to define "variance" and "covariance" cancels, so it does not matter which convention you use as long as it is consistent), and the intercept is whatever keeps the line through the point of means.

    Centered-Sum Identities

    $S_{xx}$ and $S_{xy}$ above were defined in their computational (uncentered) form, $\sum x_i^2 - n\bar x^2$ and $\sum x_iy_i-n\bar x\bar y$, but they are exactly equal to the centered sums:

    $$S_{xx} = \sum_i (x_i-\bar x)^2, \qquad S_{xy} = \sum_i (x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y).$$

    To see why, expand the centered form of $S_{xy}$:

    $$\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y) = \sum_i x_iy_i - \bar y\sum_i x_i - \bar x\sum_i y_i + n\bar x\bar y = \sum_i x_iy_i - n\bar x\bar y - n\bar x\bar y + n\bar x\bar y = \sum_i x_iy_i - n\bar x\bar y,$$

    using $\sum_i x_i = n\bar x$ and $\sum_i y_i = n\bar y$. The identical argument with $y_i$ replaced by $x_i$ gives the $S_{xx}$ identity. The centered form is the one worth internalizing conceptually ("variance of $x$", "covariance of $x$ and $y$"); the uncentered form is occasionally more convenient for hand or streaming computation, but is also more prone to catastrophic cancellation in floating point when $\bar x$ is large relative to the spread of $x$, a point Part 6 returns to.

    Residual Orthogonality

    The two normal equations are not just a solving trick, they are equivalent to two exact geometric facts about the residuals $e_i = y_i - b - wx_i$ of the fitted line, true for any dataset once $b,w$ solve (1) and (2):

    $$\sum_i e_i = 0, \qquad \sum_i x_ie_i = 0.$$

    The first is just the $b$-normal-equation rearranged: $\sum_i(y_i-b-wx_i)=0$ is exactly $\sum_i e_i=0$. The second is the $w$-normal-equation rearranged the same way. Geometrically, the residual vector is orthogonal to both the constant vector $\mathbf 1$ and the predictor vector $x$, i.e. orthogonal to every column of the design matrix, which is precisely the projection picture in Least Squares Geometry specialized to one predictor. A useful consequence: whenever an intercept is included, residuals always sum to exactly zero regardless of how good or bad the fit is, so "do the residuals average to zero" is never informative about model quality; only their pattern against $x$ or $\hat y$ is.

    ▶ Building the Line One Point at a Time

    Step through adding points to a running dataset. At each step the centered sums $S_{xx}, S_{xy}$ and the fitted $w,b$ are fully recomputed from equations (1)-(2). Watch $w$ and $b$ stabilize as $n$ grows, and check residual orthogonality on the final step.

    The R-Squared Relationship

    Define $S_{yy}=\sum_i(y_i-\bar y)^2$ (the total sum of squares, $SST$). Using $\hat y_i = b+wx_i$ and orthogonality, the total sum of squares splits exactly:

    $$S_{yy} = \underbrace{\sum_i(\hat y_i-\bar y)^2}_{SSR} + \underbrace{\sum_i(y_i-\hat y_i)^2}_{SSE},$$

    with no cross term, because the cross term is $2\sum_i(\hat y_i-\bar y)e_i$ and both $\sum_i e_i=0$ and $\sum_i \hat y_ie_i=0$ (a short calculation combining the two orthogonality facts above). $R^2 = 1-SSE/SST = SSR/SST$ measures the fraction of $y$'s variance explained by the line. In the single-predictor case only, $R^2$ has a second, purely algebraic identity worth knowing:

    $$R^2 = \frac{S_{xy}^2}{S_{xx}S_{yy}} = r_{xy}^2,$$

    the squared Pearson correlation coefficient between $x$ and $y$. This identity is specific to simple regression; with more than one predictor, $R^2$ is no longer simply the square of any single pairwise correlation, which is one of the reasons Part 3 needs the FWL theorem to talk precisely about one predictor's contribution net of the others.

    Edge Cases That Break the Formula

    Zero variance in x

    If every $x_i$ equals the same constant, $S_{xx}=0$ and $w=S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ divides by zero: the slope is not merely unstable, it is undefined. Geometrically the design matrix $[\mathbf 1, x]$ has rank 1, not 2, because the $x$-column is just a scalar multiple of the constant column, so there is no unique way to split the fit between an intercept and a slope. This is the one-predictor instance of the rank-deficiency problem that Linear Regression: Foundations flags in general and that Part 6 handles numerically for the many-predictor case.

    Intercept suppression (regression through the origin)

    Forcing $b=0$ changes the objective to $\min_w\sum_i(y_i-wx_i)^2$, whose closed form is the uncentered ratio $w=\dfrac{\sum_i x_iy_i}{\sum_i x_i^2}$, not $S_{xy}/S_{xx}$. Two consequences are easy to miss: first, the residual-orthogonality guarantee $\sum_i e_i=0$ no longer holds, because there is no intercept normal equation to produce it, so residuals from a no-intercept fit can have any nonzero mean. Second, the usual $R^2=1-SSE/SST$ can come out negative or otherwise misleading, because $SST$ measured around $\bar y$ is not the right baseline when the model itself is not allowed to fit $\bar y$; software that supports no-intercept fits typically reports an "uncentered $R^2$" instead, and the two numbers are not comparable. Suppress the intercept only when a zero intercept is a genuine, theoretically justified feature of the problem (for instance, a physical relationship known to pass through the origin), not as a default or a way to inflate $R^2$.

    Worked Numeric Example

    Eight points, $x=1,\dots,8$ and $y=[2.8,\,3.9,\,4.6,\,5.9,\,6.5,\,7.6,\,8.4,\,9.5]$:

    QuantityValue
    $\bar x$4.500
    $\bar y$6.150
    $S_{xx}$42.000
    $S_{xy}$39.500
    $S_{yy}$ (SST)37.260

    $b = \bar y - w\bar x$1.9179

    The fitted line is $\hat y = 1.918 + 0.9405x$. Every value above was computed directly from equations (1) and (2); nothing was rounded before the fact, only in the display.
