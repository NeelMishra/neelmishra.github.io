    OLS Inference: Standard Errors, Tests, and Intervals

    July 2026 · 19 min read

    Part 4 established that $\hat w$ has mean $w$ and covariance $\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}$, under a stated set of assumptions. Inference is what turns that abstract sampling distribution into a specific number you can report: a standard error, a $p$-value, an interval. Every quantity in this part is computed once, on the dataset already used in Part 2, so the numbers can be checked directly.

    Residual Variance and Degrees of Freedom

    $\sigma^2$ in Part 4's formula is unknown and must be estimated from the residuals. The natural estimator is:

    $$\hat\sigma^2 = \frac{SSE}{n-p}, \qquad SSE=\sum_i e_i^2,$$

    where $p$ is the number of estimated parameters (including the intercept). Dividing by $n-p$ rather than $n$ is what makes $\hat\sigma^2$ unbiased: fitting $p$ parameters uses up $p$ degrees of freedom from the $n$ residuals (they must satisfy $p$ exact orthogonality constraints, $X^\top e=0$, from the normal equations), leaving only $n-p$ of them free to vary. Dividing by $n$ instead systematically underestimates $\sigma^2$, more severely so when $p$ is large relative to $n$.

    On the Part 2 dataset

    $n=8$, $p=2$ (intercept and slope), $SSE=0.1112$, so $\hat\sigma^2=0.1112/6=0.01853$, and $\hat\sigma=0.1361$.

    The Coefficient Covariance Matrix

    Plugging $\hat\sigma^2$ into Part 4's variance formula gives the estimated covariance matrix of the coefficients:

    $$\widehat{\text{Cov}}(\hat w) = \hat\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1},$$

    whose diagonal entries are the squared standard errors, $SE(\hat w_j)=\sqrt{\left[\hat\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}\right]_{jj}}$, and whose off-diagonal entries are the covariances between different coefficients, needed for the $F$-test below and for any hypothesis involving more than one coefficient at once (for instance, testing $w_1=w_2$). For the simple-regression case specifically, this reduces to:

    $$SE(\hat w) = \sqrt{\frac{\hat\sigma^2}{S_{xx}}}, \qquad SE(\hat b) = \sqrt{\hat\sigma^2\left(\frac1n+\frac{\bar x^2}{S_{xx}}\right)}.$$

    On the running dataset: $SE(\hat w)=\sqrt{0.01853/42.00}=0.02101$, and $SE(\hat b)=\sqrt{0.01853\cdot(1/8+4.5^2/42.00)}=0.10607$.

    t-Tests for a Single Coefficient

    To test $H_0: w_j=0$ against $H_1: w_j\ne 0$, form:

    $$t_j = \frac{\hat w_j}{SE(\hat w_j)},$$

    which follows a $t$-distribution with $n-p$ degrees of freedom under $H_0$ and the Gauss-Markov assumptions plus normal errors (or approximately, in large samples, without normal errors). On the running dataset, $t_w = 0.9405/0.02101=44.77$ and $t_b=1.9179/0.10607=18.08$, both enormous relative to the 95% critical value $t_{0.975,6}=2.447$, so both coefficients are significant at any conventional level; the two-sided $p$-values are $p_w\approx 8\times10^{-9}$ and $p_b\approx 2\times10^{-6}$. A 95% confidence interval for the slope is $\hat w\pm t_{0.975,6}\cdot SE(\hat w) = 0.9405\pm2.447\cdot0.02101=(0.889,\,0.992)$.

    Joint F-Tests

    A $t$-test asks about one coefficient in isolation. An $F$-test asks whether a group of coefficients is jointly zero, comparing the full model's $SSE_{\text{full}}$ to a restricted model's $SSE_{\text{restricted}}$ (the same model with $q$ coefficients forced to zero):

    $$F = \frac{(SSE_{\text{restricted}}-SSE_{\text{full}})/q}{SSE_{\text{full}}/(n-p)} \sim F_{q,\,n-p} \text{ under } H_0.$$

    The most common special case restricts every slope to zero at once (the "is this model useful at all" test), comparing to the intercept-only model, whose $SSE_{\text{restricted}}$ is exactly $SST$.

    Worked example on Part 3's two-predictor housing dataset

    $SST=20.939$, $SSE_{\text{full}}=0.2294$ (with both $x_1,x_2$), $q=2$, $n-p=8-3=5$. Then $F=\dfrac{(20.939-0.2294)/2}{0.2294/5}=225.7$, against a critical value $F_{0.95,(2,5)}=5.79$: the two predictors are jointly, overwhelmingly significant ($p\approx1.3\times10^{-5}$). This is a genuinely different question from the individual $t$-tests on $w_1$ and $w_2$ separately (which were significant here too, but need not always agree with the joint test: two correlated predictors can each look individually weak, with inflated standard errors from Part 4's multicollinearity discussion, while still being jointly significant).

    Confidence Intervals Versus Prediction Intervals

    At a new point $x_0$, two different intervals are both built from the same fitted value $\hat y_0=x_0^\top\hat w$, but answer different questions and use different variance terms:

    $$\text{CI (mean response): } \hat y_0 \pm t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-p}\cdot\hat\sigma\sqrt{h_0}, \qquad \text{PI (new observation): } \hat y_0 \pm t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-p}\cdot\hat\sigma\sqrt{1+h_0},$$

    where $h_0=x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0$ (the same leverage quantity used for diagnostics in Part 7, evaluated at a new point rather than a training row). The confidence interval asks how precisely the true average of $y$ is known at $x_0$; the prediction interval asks the harder question of where one new $y_0$ will actually land. The extra "$+1$" inside the prediction interval's square root is the variance of a genuinely new error term $\varepsilon_0$: even a perfectly known regression line still sits underneath irreducible noise, and that noise does not shrink as $n$ grows, only the line's own uncertainty ($h_0$) does. The prediction interval is therefore always at least as wide as the confidence interval at the same $x_0$, and the gap between them never closes, no matter how much training data accumulates.

    ▶ CI and PI as x0 Moves

    Same fitted line as Part 2, $\hat y=1.918+0.9405x$, $n=8$, $\hat\sigma=0.1361$. Step through four values of $x_0$: two inside the training range, one at its edge, and one well outside it (extrapolation). Watch both bands widen together, and watch the gap between them stay driven by the fixed "+1" term.

    Both intervals rely on the errors being at least approximately normal for the $t$-quantile to be exact in finite samples. With enough data, the central limit theorem makes $\hat w$ itself approximately normal regardless of the true error distribution, which rescues the confidence interval asymptotically. It does not rescue the prediction interval the same way: the CLT is a statement about averages of many errors converging to normal, not about any single future error $\varepsilon_0$ becoming normal, and $\varepsilon_0$ never gets averaged with anything. A Gaussian-shaped prediction interval is therefore only as good as the assumption that the errors themselves are close to normal, no matter how large $n$ is; when residuals show heavy tails or skew (the Q-Q plot in Part 7), prefer an empirical-residual interval, a bootstrap interval, or quantile regression fit directly at the target tail probabilities.

    Robust Standard Errors

    When Part 4's assumption 4 (spherical errors) fails but exogeneity still holds, $\hat w$ remains unbiased, but $\hat\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}$ is the wrong covariance formula. Heteroscedasticity-consistent ("robust", "sandwich", or White) standard errors replace $\hat\sigma^2I$ inside the sandwich with a diagonal matrix of the squared residuals themselves, without ever assuming a shared variance across rows:

    $$\widehat{\text{Cov}}_{\text{robust}}(\hat w) = (X^\top X)^{-1}\left(\sum_i e_i^2\,x_ix_i^\top\right)(X^\top X)^{-1}.$$

    The displayed formula is the original HC0 estimator. HC1 multiplies the middle term by $n/(n-p)$ for a degrees-of-freedom correction; HC2 replaces each $e_i^2$ by $e_i^2/(1-h_{ii})$; and HC3 uses $e_i^2/(1-h_{ii})^2$, applying a stronger correction to high-leverage rows. HC2 or HC3 is often preferable in small samples, especially when leverage varies substantially, but the chosen variant should always be named in a report rather than hidden behind the generic word "robust." Every version keeps the OLS point estimate $\hat w$ unchanged and only corrects its reported uncertainty; none fixes endogeneity bias, and none is a substitute for GLS if the goal is a lower-variance point estimate rather than a correctly sized interval around the existing one.

    A concrete illustration

    On a separate synthetic dataset ($n=40$, $x$ evenly spaced from 1 to 10, error standard deviation growing linearly with $x$), the classical formula gives $SE(\hat b)=0.927$ and $SE(\hat w)=0.152$, while HC0 gives $SE(\hat b)=0.654$ and $SE(\hat w)=0.162$: the intercept's classical standard error is roughly 42% too large here, while the slope's is roughly 6% too small. Neither direction is universal; heteroscedasticity can inflate or deflate the naive standard error depending on exactly how the variance pattern relates to the predictor values, which is precisely why the correction has to be estimated from the data rather than guessed at.

    Multiple Testing and Model-Selection Caveats

    A single pre-specified $t$-test at $\alpha=0.05$ has, by construction, a 5% chance of a false positive when the null is actually true. Testing many candidate predictors and reporting only the ones that come out significant inflates that rate far above 5% for the "surviving" claims. A direct simulation makes the size of the problem concrete: generating 1,000 independent datasets where $x$ and $y$ are pure, unrelated noise and testing $H_0:w=0$ each time rejects the null in 4.1% of trials, matching the nominal 5% rate for one honest test. But if 20 unrelated candidate predictors are tried against the same $y$ and only the "winner" is reported, roughly $20\times0.05=1$ spurious significant result is expected from chance alone, and it will look exactly like a real effect in isolation. Two practical consequences: first, a coefficient's $p$-value from a model built by trying many predictors and keeping the significant ones (stepwise selection or extensive manual search) is no longer calibrated to 5%, and should either be corrected (Bonferroni, Benjamini-Hochberg) or validated on data the selection process never saw. Second, statistical significance is not the same as practical importance: with enough data, even a tiny, practically irrelevant coefficient becomes "significant," so the coefficient's size and its confidence interval matter at least as much as its $p$-value.

    Practical Reporting

    • Report the coefficient, its standard error, and a confidence interval, not a $p$-value alone; the interval communicates the range of effect sizes still consistent with the data, which a $p$-value by itself does not.
    • State which standard error was used (classical or robust) and why; if the diagnostics in Part 7 suggest heteroscedasticity, report robust standard errors, not classical ones.
    • Report whether a prediction interval or a confidence interval is the relevant quantity for the use case; they are not interchangeable, and the wider one (PI) is the honest one for "what will this one new case look like."
    • If the model or its predictors were chosen after looking at many candidates, say so, and treat the reported $p$-values as descriptive rather than as a calibrated hypothesis test, or validate on held-out data.
