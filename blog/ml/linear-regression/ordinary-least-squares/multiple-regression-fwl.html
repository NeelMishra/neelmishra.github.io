    Multiple OLS and the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell Theorem

    July 2026 · 17 min read

    Part 2 derived $w=S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ for one predictor. With several predictors, no single ratio of sums works anymore, because predictors can be correlated with each other, and a coefficient has to somehow account for that. The Frisch-Waugh-Lovell (FWL) theorem is the precise statement of how it does: every multiple-regression coefficient is exactly a simple regression slope like Part 2's, just computed on variables that have already had every other predictor's influence subtracted out.

    The Design Matrix and Partial Effects

    With $d$ predictors, stack an intercept column of ones and the $n$ observations of each predictor into an $n\times(d+1)$ design matrix $X=[\mathbf 1,\,x_1,\,\dots,\,x_d]$, and write the model as $\hat y = Xw$ where $w=(b,w_1,\dots,w_d)^\top$. OLS minimizes $\|y-Xw\|^2$, and provided $X$ has full column rank, the unique minimizer solves the normal equations:

    $$X^\top X w = X^\top y \quad\Longrightarrow\quad w = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y.$$

    The geometry behind this equation, and why "full column rank" is exactly the condition that makes $X^\top X$ invertible, is covered in Least Squares Geometry; this part focuses on what the entries of $w$ mean once you have them.

    Each coefficient $w_j$ is a partial effect: the change in $\hat y$ per one-unit change in $x_j$, with every other column of $X$ held fixed at its current value. This is a strictly stronger statement than "the relationship between $x_j$ and $y$", because it explicitly nets out whatever the other predictors are doing. The next section makes precise how much that netting-out can change a coefficient relative to a simple regression that ignores it.

    The Frisch-Waugh-Lovell Theorem

    Partition the design matrix into the one predictor of interest, $x_1$, and everything else, $X_2=[\mathbf 1,x_2,\dots,x_d]$, so $X=[x_1,X_2]$ and $y=x_1w_1+X_2w_2+e$. The FWL theorem states that $w_1$, the exact coefficient from the full multiple regression, is recovered by the following three-step procedure:

    1. Regress $x_1$ on $X_2$ (which includes the intercept) and keep the residuals $\tilde x_1 = x_1 - X_2(X_2^\top X_2)^{-1}X_2^\top x_1$, i.e. the part of $x_1$ that $X_2$ cannot explain.
    2. Regress $y$ on $X_2$ and keep the residuals $\tilde y = y - X_2(X_2^\top X_2)^{-1}X_2^\top y$, the part of $y$ that $X_2$ cannot explain.
    3. Regress $\tilde y$ on $\tilde x_1$ (a simple regression, exactly Part 2's formula, and with no separate intercept needed because both residual series already have mean zero by construction): the resulting slope equals $w_1$ exactly, and the residuals of this final simple regression equal the residuals of the original full multiple regression, entry for entry.

    Sketch of why. Let $M_2 = I - X_2(X_2^\top X_2)^{-1}X_2^\top$ be the "residual-maker" matrix for $X_2$ (it projects any vector onto the space orthogonal to $X_2$'s columns). Then $\tilde x_1=M_2x_1$ and $\tilde y=M_2y$. Multiplying the fitted decomposition $y=x_1w_1+X_2w_2+e$ through by $M_2$ gives $M_2y=M_2x_1\,w_1+M_2e$. Here $M_2X_2=0$ by construction, and $M_2e=e$ exactly because the full OLS residual $e$ is orthogonal to every column of $X_2$. Therefore $\tilde y=\tilde x_1w_1+e$ with the same coefficient and the same residual vector as the full regression. A simple regression of $\tilde y$ on $\tilde x_1$ recovers exactly that $w_1$ by Part 2's formula; symmetry and idempotence of $M_2$ make this an algebraic identity, not an approximation.

    ▶ FWL in Three Steps, on a Concrete Dataset

    A tiny housing dataset, $n=8$: $x_1$ = floor area (100 sq ft), $x_2$ = age (years), $y$ = price ($100k). $x_1$ and $x_2$ are correlated ($r=-0.888$: newer houses tend to be bigger). Step through residualizing $x_1$ on $x_2$, residualizing $y$ on $x_2$, then regressing the residuals.

    Worked Example: Full Numbers

    Same dataset. The full multiple regression (fit directly via the normal equations, no residualizing) gives:

    $$\hat y = 1.797 + 0.2935\,x_1 - 0.0691\,x_2.$$
    QuantityValue
    Full regression coefficient on $x_1$0.29348
    FWL: slope of $\tilde y$ on $\tilde x_1$0.29348 (identical to machine precision)
    Simple regression of $y$ on $x_1$ alone (ignoring $x_2$)0.39633
    $\text{corr}(x_1,x_2)$−0.888

    The simple regression's coefficient (0.396) and the multiple regression's partial effect (0.293) disagree by 35%, purely because $x_1$ and $x_2$ are correlated. Both numbers are "correct" answers to different questions: 0.396 answers "how much bigger is price for a bigger house, ignoring age", 0.293 answers "how much bigger is price for a bigger house, among houses of the same age." The next section makes the size of that gap exact.

    Omitted-Variable Bias

    What if $x_2$ (age) were never collected, and only the simple regression of $y$ on $x_1$ were run? Its coefficient $w_1^{\text{simple}}$ relates to the full-model coefficients by an exact identity:

    $$w_1^{\text{simple}} = w_1^{\text{full}} + w_2^{\text{full}}\cdot\delta_1, \qquad \delta_1 = \text{slope of regressing } x_2 \text{ on } x_1.$$

    The bias term $w_2^{\text{full}}\cdot\delta_1$ vanishes only when at least one of two things is true: $x_2$ has no partial effect on $y$ once $x_1$ is included ($w_2^{\text{full}}=0$), or $x_2$ is uncorrelated with $x_1$ ($\delta_1=0$). If neither holds, an omitted variable that affects $y$ and correlates with an included predictor silently biases that predictor's coefficient, and no amount of additional data fixes it, because the bias does not come from sampling noise, it comes from a structural gap in what was measured.

    Checking the identity on this dataset

    $\delta_1 = -1.4884$ (older houses tend to be smaller, in this data). Then $w_1^{\text{full}} + w_2^{\text{full}}\cdot\delta_1 = 0.29348 + (-0.06910)(-1.4884) = 0.29348+0.10285 = 0.39633$, matching $w_1^{\text{simple}}=0.39633$ exactly. Age was doing real work (its own partial effect $-0.0691$ is nonzero) and is correlated with floor area, so omitting it visibly inflates the apparent effect of size on price.

    This is exactly the condition the series overview refers to when it says a coefficient needs "no omitted confounders" to be read causally: omitted-variable bias is the formal mechanism behind that requirement, not a vague warning.

    Interactions and Nonlinear Bases

    "Linear in the parameters" does not mean "linear in the raw inputs." An interaction term $x_1x_2$ is just another column computed from the data before fitting, so the model $\hat y = b+w_1x_1+w_2x_2+w_3(x_1x_2)$ is still ordinary least squares on the design matrix $[\mathbf 1,x_1,x_2,x_1x_2]$; only the interpretation changes. With an interaction present, $w_1$ alone is no longer "the effect of $x_1$"; the effect of a one-unit change in $x_1$ on $\hat y$ becomes $w_1+w_3x_2$, which now depends on the value of $x_2$. Reporting $w_1$ by itself as "the" effect of $x_1$ when an interaction term is in the model is a common and avoidable misread; the partial-effect formula must be evaluated at a specific $x_2$, or averaged over a stated distribution of $x_2$, to mean anything. The same principle extends to any basis expansion (polynomials, splines, logs): the FWL theorem above applies unchanged to any column of the design matrix, engineered or raw, because its derivation never used anything about where the columns came from.

    The Dummy-Variable Trap

    Encoding a categorical predictor with $k$ levels as $k$ separate 0/1 dummy columns, alongside an intercept, breaks full column rank: the $k$ dummy columns always sum to a column of all ones, which already exists as the intercept column. Concretely, for a 3-level category $\{A,B,C\}$:

    RowIntercept$D_A$$D_B$$D_C$
    category A1100
    category B1010
    category C1001

    $D_A+D_B+D_C$ equals the intercept column on every row, so these four columns have rank 3, not 4: the design matrix is rank-deficient by exactly one, and $(X^\top X)^{-1}$ does not exist. The standard fix is to drop one level as a reference category (say $D_A$), keeping $D_B,D_C$ plus the intercept; the dropped level's effect is then absorbed into the intercept, and each remaining dummy's coefficient is the partial effect of that category relative to the reference category, not relative to zero. An equivalent fix drops the intercept instead and keeps all $k$ dummies, in which case each coefficient is that category's full mean effect rather than a contrast; the two parameterizations fit identical predictions but the coefficients mean different things and are not directly comparable across the two encodings.

    Prediction versus causality, restated for this model. Even with $x_2$ correctly included and the dummy trap avoided, $w_1^{\text{full}}=0.293$ is only a causal "effect of floor area on price" if there is no further omitted confounder correlated with both floor area and price, if floor area is not itself partly a consequence of price (reverse causality is implausible here, but common in other settings such as marketing spend and sales), and if floor area is measured accurately. Absent verifying those conditions, 0.293 is a correct and useful partial-effect estimate for prediction and for describing this dataset, not a licensed causal claim.
