    Solving OLS Reliably: Normal Equations, QR, and SVD

    July 2026 · 18 min read

    Every solver in this part computes exactly the same mathematical answer, $\hat w=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y$, whenever that answer is well defined. In exact arithmetic they would be indistinguishable. In floating point they are not, and the gap between them can be the difference between a usable coefficient and a number with no correct digits at all. This part is about that gap: where it comes from, how large it can get, and which solver to reach for.

    The Normal Equations, Without an Explicit Inverse

    Never literally invert $X^\top X$ to get $\hat w$. Computing a matrix inverse is both slower and less accurate than solving the equivalent linear system directly. In practice, forming $A=X^\top X$ and $c=X^\top y$ and then solving $Aw=c$ as a linear system (via Cholesky factorization, since $A$ is symmetric positive definite whenever $X$ has full column rank) is both faster and more numerically sound than computing $A^{-1}$ and multiplying. This is a general rule, not specific to regression: "solve $Aw=c$" is a better-conditioned operation than "compute $A^{-1}$, then multiply," because forming the explicit inverse accumulates additional rounding error that solving directly avoids.

    Why Squaring X Squares the Condition Number

    The condition number $\kappa(X)=\sigma_{\max}(X)/\sigma_{\min}(X)$ (ratio of largest to smallest singular value) measures how much a small relative error in the input can be amplified in the solution; a matrix with $\kappa(X)=10^k$ can lose up to roughly $k$ decimal digits of accuracy when used to solve a linear system in floating point. Since the singular values of $X^\top X$ are exactly the squares of the singular values of $X$ ($X=U\Sigma V^\top\Rightarrow X^\top X=V\Sigma^2V^\top$), the condition number of $X^\top X$ is the condition number of $X$, squared:

    $$\kappa(X^\top X) = \kappa(X)^2.$$

    This identity is exact in real-number arithmetic, and it is the single most important number in this part: any solver that explicitly forms $X^\top X$ (Cholesky on the normal equations) inherits this squaring, while a solver that works with $X$ directly (QR, SVD) does not.

    Pivoted QR Decomposition

    QR decomposition factors $X=QR$, where $Q$ has orthonormal columns ($Q^\top Q=I$) and $R$ is upper triangular. Substituting into the normal equations and using $Q^\top Q=I$:

    $$X^\top Xw = X^\top y \;\Longrightarrow\; R^\top Q^\top QR\,w = R^\top Q^\top y \;\Longrightarrow\; Rw = Q^\top y,$$

    which is solved by simple back-substitution, since $R$ is triangular, and crucially $X^\top X$ is never formed. $\kappa(R)=\kappa(X)$, not $\kappa(X)^2$, so QR-based solving inherits only the original conditioning of $X$, not its square. Column pivoting (choosing, at each step, to eliminate the column with the largest remaining norm first) additionally makes QR reliable in the presence of near-collinear or numerically weak columns, and directly exposes near-rank-deficiency: if a diagonal entry of $R$ becomes tiny relative to the others after pivoting, that column contributes almost no new information beyond the earlier ones, a numerical, continuous analogue of Part 4's exact-rank-deficiency check.

    SVD, the Pseudoinverse, and Rank Tolerance

    The singular value decomposition $X=U\Sigma V^\top$ gives the most numerically robust route to $\hat w$, via the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse:

    $$\hat w = X^+y, \qquad X^+ = V\Sigma^+U^\top,$$

    where $\Sigma^+$ inverts each nonzero singular value and leaves zero (or numerically negligible) singular values as zero rather than dividing by them. This is the one solver in this part that degrades gracefully under exact or near-exact rank deficiency: instead of failing outright (normal equations, whose $X^\top X$ is not invertible) or returning an arbitrarily large, meaningless answer (QR, whose triangular solve divides by a near-zero diagonal entry), the SVD approach explicitly chooses a rank tolerance $\tau$ (typically scaled to machine precision times the largest singular value) and treats every singular value below it as exactly zero, returning the minimum-norm solution among the (now explicitly acknowledged) infinitely many minimizers. This is the correct behavior specifically because, as the Foundations post notes, a rank-deficient design matrix does not have a unique minimizing coefficient vector at all; the SVD makes that fact visible and handles it by convention (minimum norm) instead of hiding it behind a numerically fragile inverse.

    The Cholesky Trade-off

    Cholesky factorization of $X^\top X=LL^\top$ (forming the normal equations, then factoring the resulting small $p\times p$ matrix) is the fastest of the three approaches and is entirely reasonable whenever $X$ is well-conditioned and $p$ is much smaller than $n$: the condition-number squaring above is a real cost, but a modest one when $\kappa(X)$ was already small. It stops being reasonable exactly when $\kappa(X)$ is large enough that squaring it pushes past the point where floating-point precision can still represent the answer, which is precisely the worked example below.

    For small-to-moderate, well-conditioned, dense problems, the constant-factor differences above rarely matter and Cholesky's simplicity wins. For very large sparse problems (millions of rows, where $X^\top X$ would be both expensive to form and to store densely, or would destroy sparsity), matrix-free iterative solvers that only ever multiply by $X$ and $X^\top$ (never forming either $X^\top X$ or a dense factorization) are the standard approach, typically combined with a preconditioner and, in practice, some form of regularization (Regularized Variants) both for stability and because such large, wide problems are frequently ill-conditioned or rank-deficient in practice.

    Scaling and Centering Before Solving

    Conditioning is not an intrinsic property of a dataset, it is a property of how the columns happen to be represented. A predictor recorded as raw calendar years (around 2,000,000 when measured in some absurd but not unheard-of unit, or simply a large offset from zero) creates an artificially huge condition number purely from where the number sits on the real line, with no genuine ill-posedness in the underlying relationship. Centering each predictor by its mean (and optionally scaling by its standard deviation) before solving removes this artificial inflation entirely. The fitted values are unchanged, and mapping the coefficients back to the original units recovers the same original-unit slope; the coefficient shown on the centered or standardized scale naturally has a different numerical value and intercept interpretation. This is the same caution Part 2 raised about the uncentered computational formula for $S_{xy}$ being more prone to floating-point cancellation than its centered counterpart, now shown to have a dramatic, measurable effect on the condition number itself.

    The normal-equations intercept is off by more than 5, on a true value of 3, an error of over 170%, while QR and SVD both recover the true coefficients to 4-5 correct significant digits. This is not a contrived instability; it is the direct, measured consequence of $\kappa(X^\top X)\approx8.4\times10^{22}$ exceeding the roughly $10^{16}$ digits of precision double-precision floating point can represent, by more than six orders of magnitude. As the scaling section above notes, simply centering $x$ before fitting collapses $\kappa(X)$ from $2.90\times10^{11}$ down to $3.45$, at which point every solver in this part, including plain Cholesky on the normal equations, recovers the exact answer without issue.
