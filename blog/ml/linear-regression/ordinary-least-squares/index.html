    Ordinary Least Squares: A Complete Guide

    July 2026 · 15 min read

    The broader Linear Regression series treats ordinary least squares (OLS) as one ingredient among several: geometry, optimization, regularization, robust losses, and diagnostics all share space with it. This sub-series does the opposite. It isolates OLS itself and asks the questions a working practitioner actually needs answered: where does the closed-form solution come from, why does it decompose the way it does with more than one predictor, what does it guarantee about repeated samples, how do you attach honest uncertainty to it, how do you compute it without losing precision, and how do you tell when it has quietly failed. Nothing here duplicates the broader series' pages on geometry, gradient descent, regularization, or robust losses; instead, every part below links out to them at the point where they become the natural next step.

    Prerequisites. This guide assumes the model definition and residual/least-squares setup from Linear Regression: Foundations. If the phrase "design matrix" or "sum of squared residuals" is unfamiliar, start there first. The projection view in Least Squares Geometry is complementary background for Parts 3 and 6 below but is not required reading before them.

    The OLS Workflow, End to End

    OLS is not a single step, it is a short pipeline, and most practical mistakes come from skipping a stage rather than from getting the arithmetic wrong. The stages are the same whether the model has one predictor or fifty:

    1. Specify the model. Decide which columns belong in the design matrix $X$: raw features, polynomial terms, interactions, dummy-coded categories. This choice is a modeling decision, not a numerical one, and it is made before any fitting happens.
    2. Check that $X$ has full column rank. If two columns are exact linear combinations of each other (a duplicated feature, or a dummy trap from over-coding a categorical variable), the coefficient vector is not unique, no matter how the equations are solved. See Part 3 for the dummy trap and Part 6 for near-rank-deficiency.
    3. Solve for the coefficients. Normal equations, QR decomposition, or the SVD/pseudoinverse all solve the same minimization problem; they differ only in numerical reliability, covered in Part 6.
    4. Diagnose the fit. Residual plots, leverage, and Cook's distance (Part 7) reveal whether a handful of rows are silently dictating the answer, and whether the model shape is even appropriate.
    5. Quantify uncertainty. Standard errors, $t$ and $F$ tests, and confidence/prediction intervals (Part 5) turn a point estimate into a statement with a stated error rate, but only if the Gauss-Markov assumptions (Part 4) are at least approximately true.
    6. Decide what the coefficients mean, and whether OLS was even the right tool. A coefficient is always a valid description of the fitted plane; it is only a causal effect under identification assumptions well beyond curve-fitting, covered below.

    When OLS Is (and Is Not) the Right Estimator

    OLS is the right default when the target is continuous, the conditional mean is plausibly linear in the parameters (after any basis expansion), and a single interpretable set of weighted-sum coefficients is genuinely useful. It stops being the right default well before any assumption is literally violated to zero:

    Use plain OLS when

    The target is continuous, the functional form and exogeneity assumptions are defensible, errors are roughly spherical, predictors have full rank, and you want the best linear unbiased fit.

    Use weighted or generalized least squares when

    The error variance is known or estimable and changes across rows (heteroscedasticity), or errors are correlated across rows (clustered or time-series data). See the broader series' geometry page for weighted least squares.

    Use Ridge, Lasso, or Elastic Net when

    Predictors are numerous or highly collinear, and a small, controlled amount of bias is an acceptable trade for a large reduction in coefficient variance. See Regularized Variants.

    Use robust or quantile regression when

    A handful of outliers dominate the squared-error objective, or the conditional mean itself is not the quantity worth predicting. See Robust and Quantile Regression.

    Crucially, none of these alternatives is "OLS with a tweak" in the sense of still being ordinary least squares; each changes what is being minimized or what is assumed about the errors. Calling a ridge fit or a robust fit "OLS" is a common and misleading shorthand: this guide is specifically about the unweighted, unpenalized, squared-error estimator, and every alternative above is framed as a deliberate departure from it, not a variant of it.

    Prediction Is Not Causal Interpretation

    An OLS coefficient $\hat w_j$ always has one guaranteed meaning: it is the change in the fitted value $\hat y$ for a one-unit change in $x_j$, holding the other columns of $X$ fixed at whatever values they take. That statement is true by construction, for any dataset, whether or not any statistical assumption holds. It is a purely descriptive fact about the fitted hyperplane.

    Reading $\hat w_j$ as "the causal effect of $x_j$ on $y$" is a much stronger claim, and it requires assumptions that curve-fitting alone never supplies: that every confounder correlated with both $x_j$ and $y$ is already included in $X$ (no omitted-variable bias, formalized with the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell theorem in Part 3), that $x_j$ is not itself partly caused by $y$ (no reverse causality or simultaneity), and that $x_j$ is measured without systematic error. When these identification assumptions hold, $\hat w_j$ can estimate a causal effect; obtaining a valid standard error additionally requires the sampling and covariance conditions developed in Parts 4 and 5. When identification fails, $\hat w_j$ is still a perfectly good description of the fitted plane and a potentially useful input to a prediction, but it is not a causal effect, no matter how small its $p$-value is. Statistical significance is a statement about sampling noise; it says nothing about whether the coefficient is confounded.

    A model built purely to predict $y$ well on new rows does not need these causal identification assumptions, and can legitimately include proxies or mediators when they are genuinely available at inference time. It must not use a consequence of $y$ that will only be known after $y$ occurs, since that is target leakage rather than predictive information. A model built to answer "what would happen to $y$ if we changed $x_j$" needs the identification assumptions, and skipping this distinction is one of the most common misuses of a fitted regression in practice.
