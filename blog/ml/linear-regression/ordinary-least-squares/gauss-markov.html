    Gauss-Markov and the Sampling Properties of OLS

    July 2026 · 18 min read

    Everything so far in this series has been algebra: given a dataset, here is the formula for $w$. This part asks a different kind of question, about the estimator itself rather than any one fitted line: if the data were drawn again from the same underlying process, how would $\hat w$ behave across those repetitions? The Gauss-Markov theorem answers that question precisely, under a specific list of assumptions, and it is worth stating that list exactly rather than from memory, because most misuses of OLS come from silently assuming a weaker or stronger condition than the one actually needed.

    The Assumptions, Stated Precisely

    Write the data-generating process as $y=Xw+\varepsilon$, where $X$ is treated as fixed (or the analysis is conditioned on it) and $\varepsilon$ is the true, unobservable error. Four assumptions are needed for the results in this part, and it matters which result needs which assumption:

    1. Linearity in the parameters. The true conditional mean of $y$ given $X$ is $Xw$ for some fixed $w$; the model is not misspecified in functional form (after any basis expansion has already been applied to build $X$).
    2. Full column rank. $X$ is $n\times p$ with $\text{rank}(X)=p$, so $X^\top X$ is invertible and $w$ is uniquely identified. Part 3's dummy-variable trap and Part 6's near-rank-deficiency are both violations of this assumption in degree.
    3. Exogeneity (zero conditional mean of the errors). $E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0$. This is the assumption that delivers unbiasedness on its own; it fails under omitted-variable bias (Part 3), reverse causality, or systematic measurement error in $X$.
    4. Spherical errors (homoscedastic and uncorrelated). $\text{Var}(\varepsilon\mid X)=\sigma^2I$: every error has the same variance $\sigma^2$, and no two errors covary. This is the assumption specifically needed for the BLUE guarantee below, on top of the first three.

    Normality of $\varepsilon$ is deliberately not in this list. It is not required for unbiasedness, for the variance formula, or for BLUE; it is required only for the coefficients to be exactly normal (and hence for exact, finite-sample $t$ and $F$ tests), which is a topic for Part 5, not for this part's results.

    Deriving Unbiasedness

    Substitute the true model into the OLS formula:

    $$\hat w = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top(Xw+\varepsilon) = w + (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top\varepsilon.$$

    Taking the conditional expectation given $X$ (which is fixed, so it passes through the linear terms unchanged):

    $$E[\hat w\mid X] = w + (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top E[\varepsilon\mid X] = w + (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top\cdot 0 = w.$$

    Unbiasedness uses only assumptions 1-3. It does not use assumption 4 at all: OLS remains unbiased under heteroscedasticity or autocorrelated errors, as long as exogeneity still holds. This is worth remembering precisely, because it is common to see heteroscedasticity described as something that "biases" OLS; it does not, it only affects the variance formula and BLUE, covered next.

    Deriving the Variance

    From $\hat w - w = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top\varepsilon$, and using assumption 4 for $\text{Var}(\varepsilon\mid X)=\sigma^2I$:

    $$\text{Var}(\hat w\mid X) = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top\,\text{Var}(\varepsilon\mid X)\,X(X^\top X)^{-1} = \sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top X(X^\top X)^{-1} = \sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}.$$

    This is the covariance matrix used throughout Part 5 to build standard errors, and it is only valid, as written, under assumption 4. When the errors are heteroscedastic or autocorrelated, the true covariance of $\hat w$ is instead the sandwich form $(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top\,\text{Var}(\varepsilon\mid X)\,X(X^\top X)^{-1}$ with a non-scalar middle term, which is what robust standard errors (Part 5) estimate directly instead of assuming it collapses to $\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}$.

    The Gauss-Markov Theorem: OLS Is BLUE

    Under all four assumptions, the Gauss-Markov theorem states that OLS is the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator (BLUE): among every estimator of the form $\tilde w = Cy$ that is linear in $y$ and unbiased for every possible $w$, OLS has the smallest sampling variance, coefficient by coefficient (and more strongly, for every linear combination $a^\top w$).

    Sketch of the proof. Write any other linear unbiased estimator as $\tilde w = \left[(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top + D\right]y$ for some matrix $D$. Unbiasedness for every possible $w$ forces $DX=0$ (otherwise $E[\tilde w\mid X]$ would depend on $w$ through an extra $DXw$ term that OLS does not have). Then:

    $$\text{Var}(\tilde w\mid X) = \sigma^2\left[(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top+D\right]\left[X(X^\top X)^{-1}+D^\top\right] = \sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1} + \sigma^2DD^\top,$$

    where the cross terms vanish exactly because $DX=0$. Since $DD^\top$ is positive semi-definite (it is a matrix times its own transpose) and equals the zero matrix only when $D=0$, $\text{Var}(\tilde w\mid X)-\text{Var}(\hat w_{\text{OLS}}\mid X)=\sigma^2DD^\top\succeq 0$. That is precisely the statement that OLS has the smallest variance in the class, with equality only when $\tilde w$ is OLS itself.

    What BLUE Does Not Mean

    BLUE is a narrow, specific guarantee, and it is routinely overread. It does not mean:

    • Lowest variance among all estimators. Only among linear and unbiased ones. Ridge regression (Regularized Variants) is still linear in $y$, but it is biased, and it can have strictly lower mean squared error than OLS precisely by trading a little bias for a large variance reduction, especially under multicollinearity. BLUE says nothing about that trade-off, because it does not compare OLS to biased estimators at all.
    • Normally distributed coefficients. BLUE is proven using only the mean and variance of $\varepsilon$, never its distributional shape. $\hat w$ is exactly normal only if $\varepsilon$ is exactly normal; otherwise $\hat w$'s distribution is only approximately normal, and only for large $n$ (the asymptotic result covered below).
    • Small variance in absolute terms. BLUE is a comparison to other estimators in the same class; $\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}$ can still be enormous if $X$ is severely collinear (see below), even though no other linear unbiased estimator does better.
    • Correct standard errors under any assumption failure. BLUE, and the specific formula $\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}$, both require assumption 4. If it fails, OLS can still be unbiased (assumptions 1-3 alone) while no longer being BLUE and while its usual standard errors are simply wrong.

    Consistency: The Asymptotic Claim

    Unbiasedness ($E[\hat w]=w$ for every fixed $n$) and consistency ($\hat w\to w$ in probability as $n\to\infty$) are different claims with different assumption requirements, and it is worth keeping them separate. Unbiasedness needs the strict, conditional version of exogeneity, $E[\varepsilon_i\mid X]=0$ for every observation $i$ using the entire design matrix. Consistency needs only a weaker, large-sample condition, that each predictor is asymptotically uncorrelated with its own error, $\frac1n\sum_i x_i\varepsilon_i\to 0$ in probability, alongside $\frac1nX^\top X$ converging to a fixed invertible matrix. The weaker condition is enough because as $n\to\infty$, $\hat w-w=\left(\frac1nX^\top X\right)^{-1}\frac1nX^\top\varepsilon$, and the second factor converges to zero under the weaker condition even if it is not exactly zero, in expectation, for every finite $n$. This distinction matters in practice: a model can be consistent (correct in the limit) while still noticeably biased in a small sample, and the two properties are checked differently, one asymptotically and one exactly.

    Multicollinearity and Variance Inflation

    Assumption 2 (full rank) is a binary, all-or-nothing requirement: either $X$ has full column rank or it does not, and if it does not, $w$ is not identified at all, not merely imprecise. Imperfect multicollinearity, where predictors are strongly but not perfectly correlated, is a completely different and milder problem: $w$ remains identified and unbiased, but its variance can become very large. For predictor $j$, the variance from assumption 4's formula can be rewritten as:

    $$\text{Var}(\hat w_j) = \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{jj}\left(1-R_j^2\right)}, \qquad \text{VIF}_j = \frac1{1-R_j^2},$$

    where $S_{jj}=\sum_i(x_{ij}-\bar x_j)^2$ and $R_j^2$ is the $R^2$ from regressing $x_j$ on every other predictor (an application of the FWL theorem from Part 3: $S_{jj}(1-R_j^2)$ is exactly the sum of squares of $x_j$'s FWL residual $\tilde x_j$). The variance inflation factor $\text{VIF}_j$ is how much larger $\hat w_j$'s variance is compared to a hypothetical dataset where $x_j$ were uncorrelated with every other predictor.

    Checking the VIF formula on Part 3's housing dataset

    Regressing $x_1$ (floor area) on $[1,x_2]$ gives $R_1^2=0.788$, so $\text{VIF}_1 = 1/(1-0.788)=4.727$: the correlation between floor area and age nearly quintuples the variance of the floor-area coefficient relative to an uncorrelated design. Plugging in directly, $\text{Var}(\hat w_1)=\sigma^2/(S_{11}(1-R_1^2))=0.0016748$, which matches $\sigma^2\cdot\left[(X^\top X)^{-1}\right]_{11}$ computed from the full $3\times3$ matrix exactly. As a rule of thumb, $\text{VIF}_j>5$ (some practitioners use 10) is commonly flagged as worth investigating, though the right threshold depends on how much variance the application can tolerate.

    Because multicollinearity only inflates variance, not bias, its "fix" is different from an omitted-variable-bias fix: dropping a genuinely relevant correlated predictor reintroduces the omitted-variable bias from Part 3, so the standard remedies are instead more data, combining or removing a truly redundant predictor, or accepting a small, controlled amount of bias via Ridge regression (Regularized Variants) in exchange for a large variance reduction.

    When Assumption 4 Fails: Heteroscedasticity and Autocorrelation

    If $\text{Var}(\varepsilon_i\mid X)$ varies across rows (heteroscedasticity) or $\text{Cov}(\varepsilon_i,\varepsilon_j\mid X)\ne 0$ for $i\ne j$ (autocorrelation, common in time series or clustered data), assumptions 1-3 can still hold, so OLS remains unbiased. It also remains consistent when the sample-moment and dependence regularity conditions from the consistency section continue to hold; arbitrary, persistent dependence does not guarantee that result automatically. What breaks immediately is BLUE and the standard-error formula: $\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}$ is no longer the true covariance of $\hat w$, and the estimator that is BLUE under the true, non-spherical covariance is instead Generalized Least Squares (GLS), which reweights observations by (the inverse of) their true error covariance, of which the broader series' weighted least squares is the diagonal special case. Using plain OLS standard errors when assumption 4 actually fails typically understates the true uncertainty when errors are positively autocorrelated, and can distort it in either direction under heteroscedasticity, which is why Part 5 covers robust standard errors as a way to keep the unbiased point estimate while fixing only the (in)correct uncertainty quantification, without switching estimators entirely.

    ▶ Repeated Sampling: Where Do the Estimates Land?

    True model: $y=2+1.5x+\varepsilon$, $\varepsilon\sim N(0,1)$, $x=1,\dots,10$ fixed across draws. Each step draws a fresh noise vector and refits OLS. Step through eight repeated samples and watch the fitted lines scatter around the true line, while the running average of $\hat w_1$ homes in on 1.5.

    The theoretical variance from assumption 4's formula, $\text{Var}(\hat w_1)=\sigma^2\left[(X^\top X)^{-1}\right]_{11}=0.01212$ for this design, is not something eight samples can confirm tightly (the animation's own 8-sample variance, 0.01291, is already in the right neighborhood but noisy). Repeating the same simulation 20,000 times gives an empirical mean of $\hat w_1$ of 1.4998 and an empirical variance of 0.01204, both extremely close to the theoretical values of 1.5 and 0.01212. That gap between what eight samples show and what twenty thousand samples confirm is itself the practical content of "unbiased in expectation, with a specific sampling variance": both properties are exact statements about the estimator's distribution across repeated samples, not about any single sample's estimate looking exactly like the truth.
