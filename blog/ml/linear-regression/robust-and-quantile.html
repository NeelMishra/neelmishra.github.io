    Robust and Quantile Regression

    July 2026 · 21 min read

    Why Squared Error Breaks

    Ordinary least squares minimizes $\sum_i e_i^2$, and squaring is exactly what makes a single bad row dangerous: doubling a residual quadruples its contribution to the loss, so the optimizer will happily move the fitted line noticeably, away from where most of the data actually sits, if doing so shrinks one enormous residual even a little. A point far from the mean of the features (high leverage) is especially dangerous in combination with a large residual, since it has more room to tilt the line's slope rather than just its intercept. The animation below makes this concrete: adding a single outlier at the far end of the feature range visibly rotates the ordinary least squares line, while a robust loss barely moves.

    Least Absolute Deviations

    Least absolute deviations (LAD) replaces the squared loss with the absolute loss:

    $$\min_{b,w}\sum_i |y_i-b-w^\top x_i|.$$

    Where OLS estimates the conditional mean of $y$ given $x$, LAD estimates the conditional median. The median is famously insensitive to the magnitude of outliers, only their count and sign: moving the single largest value in a dataset to be ten times larger does not move the median at all, while it moves the mean substantially. The cost is that $|e|$ is not differentiable at $e=0$, so there is no closed form; LAD is typically solved either as a linear program (the absolute-value objective can be rewritten with slack variables as a linear objective under linear constraints) or, as covered next, by iteratively reweighted least squares with weights $1/(|e_i|+\epsilon)$ for a small constant $\epsilon>0$, which keeps the weight finite for rows that currently sit exactly on the fitted line.

    Huber Regression

    Huber loss interpolates between squared and absolute loss: quadratic (like OLS) for small residuals, where the extra sensitivity of squared loss is harmless and even helpful for precision, and linear (like LAD) for large residuals, where a runaway squared penalty is what causes the problem in the first place.

    $$L_\delta(e)=\begin{cases}\frac12e^2,& |e|\le\delta\\ \delta(|e|-\frac12\delta),& |e|>\delta.\end{cases}$$

    The threshold $\delta$ is a tuning parameter: small $\delta$ behaves closer to LAD (more robust, less efficient when errors really are Gaussian), large $\delta$ behaves closer to OLS. A common default sets $\delta = 1.345\,\hat\sigma$, where $\hat\sigma$ is a robust estimate of the residual scale, not the raw median absolute deviation itself: $\hat\sigma \approx \mathrm{MAD}/0.6745$, where $\mathrm{MAD}$ is the median absolute deviation of the residuals and the constant $0.6745$ rescales it so that $\hat\sigma$ estimates the standard deviation consistently when the errors truly are Gaussian (for a Gaussian, the population MAD is about $0.6745\sigma$, so dividing by that constant undoes the shrinkage). With that scaling, $\delta=1.345\hat\sigma$ is calibrated to be about 95% as statistically efficient as OLS when the errors truly are Gaussian, while still bounding the influence of outliers.

    Huber loss is differentiable everywhere, but its gradient is not linear in $w$, so there is no direct closed form either. The standard algorithm is iteratively reweighted least squares (IRLS), and it is a direct application of Part 2's weighted least squares:

    // IRLS for Huber regression
    w = ols_fit(X, y) // start from the ordinary least squares solution
    repeat until w stops changing:
    e = y - X·w
    weight_i = 1 if |e_i| ≤ delta else delta / |e_i|, for each row i
    w = weighted_least_squares(X, y, weight) // Part 2's (X^T W X)^-1 X^T W y

    Rows with a small residual get weight $1$ and are treated exactly as OLS would treat them; rows with a large residual are downweighted in proportion to how far outside $\delta$ they sit, so a residual of $10\delta$ counts a tenth as much as a residual of $\delta$. Refitting a weighted least squares problem with the new weights changes the residuals, so the weights are recomputed and the process repeats until the coefficients stabilize. LAD is the limiting case of this same idea with weights $1/(|e_i|+\epsilon)$ instead of the Huber weights, downweighting every row in inverse proportion to its residual with no quadratic region at all. Note that downweighting outliers is exactly what makes Huber's estimate diverge from a literal conditional mean under contamination: Huber and IRLS do not recover the true conditional mean of a contaminated distribution, they estimate a robust central trend that deliberately trades a small amount of that textbook-mean property away in exchange for resisting the pull of a few extreme rows, which is the entire point of reaching for it over OLS.

    RANSAC

    RANSAC (random sample consensus) is a different strategy entirely: instead of reshaping the loss function, it searches over subsets of the data. It assumes a majority of the points are "inliers" following one consistent linear relationship, and a minority are arbitrary outliers that share no structure.

    // RANSAC, repeated for a fixed number of trials
    repeat N times:
    sample a minimal random subset of rows (2 points for a line)
    fit a line to just that subset
    count inliers: points within tolerance ε of this line
    keep this fit if it has the most inliers seen so far
    finally: refit ordinary least squares using only the winning inlier set

    RANSAC can tolerate a much higher fraction of outliers than Huber or LAD, since a subset fit is only ever influenced by the handful of points sampled into it, but it needs a tolerance $\epsilon$ to define "inlier," is not deterministic, and only produces a good answer if the true inlier fraction is high enough that a clean random subset is found within the trial budget.

    Quantile Regression

    Every loss so far has targeted a single number per row: the mean (OLS, Ridge), a robust central trend close to but not identical to the mean (Huber), or the median (LAD). Quantile regression targets an arbitrary conditional quantile $\tau\in(0,1)$ using the pinball loss (also called check loss):

    $$\rho_\tau(e) = \begin{cases}\tau\, e, & e\ge 0\\ (\tau-1)\,e, & e<0\end{cases}, \qquad e = y-\hat y.$$

    This penalizes underprediction ($y>\hat y$, so $e>0$) with weight $\tau$ and overprediction ($y<\hat y$, so $e<0$) with weight $1-\tau$. At $\tau=0.5$ both weights equal $\frac12$ and pinball loss is exactly half of LAD's absolute loss, recovering the median as a special case. For $\tau=0.9$, underprediction is penalized nine times more heavily than overprediction, which pushes the fitted line up until only about $10\%$ of points fall above it: the defining property of the 90th percentile. Fitting several quantiles at once (say $\tau=0.1$ and $\tau=0.9$) produces a conditional quantile band, a data-driven interval-shaped object directly from the model, without assuming a particular error distribution the way a Gaussian-based confidence interval around the mean would; whether that band actually behaves like a calibrated prediction interval on new data is a separate question from having fit it, and is exactly what the coverage check below is for.

    Quantile crossing. If each quantile is fit independently, as the simple description above implies, there is no constraint stopping the fitted 10th-percentile line from crossing above the fitted 90th-percentile line for some values of $x$, especially out near the edges of the training range or with limited data, which is a logical impossibility (a lower quantile cannot exceed a higher one) and a visible sign that the fit is not to be trusted there. The simplest fix is a post-processing step called rearrangement: at each $x$, sort the predicted quantiles into increasing order, which removes the crossing but is a patch applied after the fact rather than a property the fit was ever asked for. A stronger fix is to fit all the desired quantiles jointly, with an explicit non-crossing constraint built into the optimization, which costs more to fit but guarantees monotonicity by construction rather than by repair. Either way, an interval built from two independently fit quantiles is only as good as those two quantiles are calibrated: always check its coverage on held-out data (for a nominal 80% interval from $\tau=0.1$ and $\tau=0.9$, roughly 80% of held-out points should actually fall inside it) rather than assuming the nominal level is met just because the model fit without error.

    Choosing a Loss for the Question

    QuestionLossSensitive to outliers?
    What is the average outcome?Squared error (OLS)Yes, quadratically
    What is the average outcome, but a few rows are unreliable?HuberBounded (linear past $\delta$)
    What is the typical (middle) outcome?Absolute error (LAD)No, only sign matters past a point
    Most points follow a pattern, some are unrelated noise?RANSACVery robust, ignores outlier subsets entirely
    What is a specific percentile, or an interval?Pinball / quantile lossDepends on $\tau$, controllable
    Choose the loss for the question, not out of habit. Squared error asks for the mean, absolute error asks for the median, and pinball loss asks for a chosen quantile. Reaching for OLS by default and only checking residuals afterward, which is exactly Part 6's topic, is how outliers and skew go unnoticed until they matter.
