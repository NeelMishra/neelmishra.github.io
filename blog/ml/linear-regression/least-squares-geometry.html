    Least Squares Geometry

    July 2026 · 22 min read

    In matrix form, linear regression writes every prediction at once as $\hat y = Xw$, where each row of $X$ is one observation's feature vector (with a column of ones prepended so the intercept $b$ is just the first entry of $w$) and $\hat y$ stacks all $n$ predictions. The columns of $X$ span every prediction vector the model is capable of producing, no matter what $w$ is chosen. Least squares finds the point in that column space closest to the target vector $y$. This single picture explains, at once, why the normal equations look the way they do, why redundant features cause instability, and why the intercept-plus-slope model from Part 1 is the same object as multiple, polynomial, and basis-expanded regression.

    Projection View, and the Same Answer from Calculus

    The fitted vector $\hat y = Xw$ is constrained to lie in the column space of $X$. Least squares picks the $w$ that makes $\hat y$ the point in that space closest to $y$ in ordinary Euclidean distance. Geometrically, the closest point in a subspace to an external vector is always its orthogonal projection: the residual vector $r=y-\hat y$ must be perpendicular to every column of $X$, otherwise $\hat y$ could be nudged along that column to get closer to $y$. "Perpendicular to every column" is exactly the equation:

    $$X^\top(y-Xw)=0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad X^\top Xw = X^\top y.$$

    These are the normal equations. It is worth deriving the same result the other way, by calculus, to see both routes land on identical algebra. Write the sum of squared residuals as $J(w) = \|y-Xw\|^2 = y^\top y - 2w^\top X^\top y + w^\top X^\top X w$. Differentiating with respect to $w$ and setting the gradient to zero:

    $$\nabla_w J = -2X^\top y + 2X^\top Xw = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad X^\top Xw = X^\top y.$$

    Calculus finds the flat point of a bowl-shaped (convex) surface; geometry finds the nearest point in a subspace. Least squares is both at once, and that is not a coincidence: minimizing squared Euclidean distance to a subspace is, by construction, an orthogonal projection. When $X^\top X$ is invertible, both derivations give the unique solution $w = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y$.

    ▶ Projection onto the Model Space

    The target vector is projected onto the model's column space. The residual is orthogonal to the fitted vector, which is exactly the condition $X^\top r=0$ above.

    Design Matrices: Simple, Multiple, Polynomial, and Basis Expansion

    Part 1 distinguished simple, multiple, polynomial, and basis-expanded regression by what counts as a feature. In matrix form, that distinction is entirely about how $X$ is built; the optimization problem $\min_w\|y-Xw\|^2$ never changes. For $n$ observations and an intercept:

    $$ \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}1 & x_1\\ 1 & x_2\\ \vdots & \vdots\\ 1 & x_n\end{bmatrix}}_{\text{simple regression}} \qquad \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}1 & x_{1,1} & x_{1,2} & \cdots & x_{1,d}\\ 1 & x_{2,1} & x_{2,2} & \cdots & x_{2,d}\\ \vdots & & & & \vdots\\ 1 & x_{n,1} & x_{n,2} & \cdots & x_{n,d}\end{bmatrix}}_{\text{multiple regression}} \qquad \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}1 & t_1 & t_1^2 & t_1^3\\ 1 & t_2 & t_2^2 & t_2^3\\ \vdots & & & \vdots\\ 1 & t_n & t_n^2 & t_n^3\end{bmatrix}}_{\text{cubic polynomial regression}} $$

    The first column of ones is what lets $b$ live inside $w$ as $w_0$; it is itself a "feature," the constant function $1$. Multiple regression adds one column per raw measured feature. Polynomial regression instead derives several columns from a single raw input $t$, and basis expansion generalizes this to any fixed transform, $\phi_j(t)$, of the input, such as $\log t$, $\mathbb 1[t>0]$, or $\sin(2\pi t / 365)$ for a yearly cycle. All three are "linear regression" in the exact sense of this post: linear in $w$, whatever $X$ contains.

    This also clarifies what "enough independent information" meant earlier: it is a statement about the columns of $X$, regardless of where those columns came from. A polynomial of degree 9 fit to 10 points has as many columns as rows, and behaves exactly as unstably as 9 redundant raw features would, for the same algebraic reason covered next.

    Rank and Multicollinearity

    If the columns of $X$ are linearly independent (full column rank), $X^\top X$ is invertible and the normal-equation solution is unique. If columns are redundant, for instance one feature is a near-exact linear combination of others, many different coefficient vectors $w$ can produce the same fitted values $\hat y$. This is multicollinearity: predictions can remain stable even though individual coefficients become unstable, sign-flipping and blowing up in magnitude under tiny changes to the data, and lose any sensible interpretation as "the effect of this feature."

    Two practical measures make this concrete. The condition number $\kappa(X^\top X)$ is the ratio of its largest to smallest eigenvalue; a condition number of $10^6$ roughly means that errors in $y$ get amplified up to a million-fold in $w$. It is directly related to the condition number of $X$ itself (the ratio of $X$'s largest to smallest singular value): $\kappa(X^\top X)=\kappa(X)^2$, since the eigenvalues of $X^\top X$ are exactly the squared singular values of $X$. This squaring is the same phenomenon the numerics section below returns to, and it is the precise reason solvers that work directly with $X$ (QR, SVD) are more numerically stable than ones that explicitly form $X^\top X$ first. The variance inflation factor for feature $j$, $\text{VIF}_j = 1/(1-R_j^2)$ where $R_j^2$ is the $R^2$ from regressing feature $j$ on all other features, quantifies how much that one coefficient's variance is inflated by its correlation with the rest. A VIF above about 5 to 10 is the usual rule-of-thumb flag. Ridge regression (Part 4) is the standard fix: it adds a small constant to every eigenvalue of $X^\top X$, which caps how much noise can be amplified, at the cost of a little bias.

    Weighted Least Squares

    Ordinary least squares treats every row of $X$ as equally trustworthy. That is not always true: some observations may be averages of many measurements and therefore precise, others may be single noisy readings; some rows might represent one customer, others a whole market segment. Weighted least squares (WLS) assigns each row a weight $w_i>0$ (unrelated to the parameter vector $w$; here written with a subscript to avoid confusion) reflecting how much that row should count, and minimizes

    $$\min_{w}\sum_i w_i\,(y_i-x_i^\top w)^2 = \min_w (y-Xw)^\top W (y-Xw),$$

    where $W$ is the diagonal matrix of weights. This is still least squares, just under a rescaled inner product, so the same projection argument applies: the optimal residual must now be orthogonal to the columns of $X$ under the $W$-weighted inner product, giving the generalized normal equations

    $$X^\top W X\, w = X^\top W y \quad\Longrightarrow\quad w = (X^\top W X)^{-1}X^\top W y.$$

    Ordinary least squares is the special case $W=I$. The most common use of WLS is exactly the failure mode Part 6 calls heteroscedasticity: when the noise variance of $y_i$ differs across rows (known or estimated as $\sigma_i^2$), the statistically efficient choice is $w_i=1/\sigma_i^2$, so noisier rows are down-weighted rather than allowed to dominate the fit. The same reweighting mechanism, with weights chosen a different way, is also exactly how Part 5's Huber regression is computed in practice.

    Numerical Stability

    Solving the normal equations directly by forming $X^\top X$ and inverting it is algebraically correct but numerically fragile: forming $X^\top X$ squares the condition number of $X$ (precisely, $\kappa(X^\top X)=\kappa(X)^2$, so a design matrix with condition number $10^3$ produces a normal-equations matrix with condition number $10^6$), so a moderately ill-conditioned design matrix becomes a severely ill-conditioned one before inversion even starts. In floating point, this can turn a merely difficult problem into one that loses several digits of precision. QR decomposition sidesteps this entirely: rather than solving the normal equations at all, it factors $X=QR$ with $Q$ orthogonal and $R$ upper triangular, and solves the equivalent triangular system $Rw=Q^\top y$ directly, so $X^\top X$ is never formed and the squaring of the condition number never happens; the method works with $X$'s own condition number throughout. The singular value decomposition (SVD) goes further still, remaining stable and informative even when $X$ is rank-deficient (it exposes exactly which directions in feature space are unidentified, which neither the normal equations nor QR alone make as explicit). In from-scratch educational code, normal equations are useful because they show the algebra in the clearest possible form, and that is the version implemented in the companion C++ post; production numerical libraries default to QR or SVD-based solvers for exactly the reason above.

    Core geometry. Least squares is not magic curve fitting. It is nearest-point projection in a vector space of possible predictions, whether that space is spanned by raw features, polynomial terms, arbitrary basis functions, or a reweighted version of any of these.
