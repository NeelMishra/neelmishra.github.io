    Gradient Descent for Linear Regression

    July 2026 · 19 min read

    Closed-form least squares (Part 2) is elegant, but iterative optimization is often more practical: it scales to large datasets, adapts to streaming data, stays efficient with very many features, and generalizes cleanly to the regularized and robust losses in Parts 4 and 5, none of which always have a closed form. Gradient descent updates the parameters by repeatedly moving a small step opposite the slope of the loss.

    Gradient

    Using mean squared error,

    $$J(w)=\frac{1}{2n}\|Xw-y\|^2,$$

    the gradient is

    $$\nabla J(w)=\frac{1}{n}X^\top(Xw-y).$$

    The update is $w \leftarrow w-\eta\nabla J(w)$, where $\eta$ is the learning rate. Notice that setting $\nabla J(w)=0$ recovers $X^\top Xw = X^\top y$, exactly the normal equations from Part 2. Gradient descent and the closed-form solution are aiming at the same point; they only differ in how they get there. For a single parameter, $J$ is precisely a parabola (as it is for every parameter direction of the true multivariate loss, since $J$ is a convex quadratic), which is why the animation below can show the entire mechanism on a 1-D curve without losing anything essential.

    ▶ Walking Down the Loss Surface

    Each step moves the parameter downhill by η times the slope. Pick a learning rate, then step or play through the same starting point. Too small is slow, too large overshoots and can diverge.

    Batch, SGD, and Mini-Batch

    • Batch gradient descent: uses all $n$ rows for every update. The gradient is exact, so the path down is smooth, but each step costs a full pass over the data.
    • Stochastic gradient descent (SGD): uses one row at a time. Each step is cheap and lets the model start improving before it has even seen the whole dataset, but the gradient estimate is noisy, so the path zig-zags and never fully settles: it converges to a small region around the optimum, not the optimum itself, unless the learning rate is decayed toward zero.
    • Mini-batch gradient descent: uses a small batch (commonly tens to a few thousand rows). It is the practical default because it balances gradient noise against per-step cost, and it maps efficiently onto vectorized and GPU hardware.

    Because SGD never fully settles at a fixed learning rate, practical schedules decay $\eta$ over time (for example $\eta_t = \eta_0 / (1+kt)$), which trades early speed for late-stage precision, letting noisy early steps make fast progress while shrinking steps late in training avoid perpetual jitter around the minimum.

    Feature Scaling

    Gradient descent is sensitive to feature scale. If one feature is measured in dollars and another in years, the loss surface becomes a long narrow valley rather than a round bowl: the gradient points almost, but not quite, at the minimum, so descent zig-zags across the narrow direction. This is precisely the condition-number problem from Part 2's discussion of multicollinearity, viewed through an optimization lens instead of a projection lens: the Hessian of $J$ is $\frac{1}{n}X^\top X$, and a poorly scaled or poorly conditioned $X$ makes that Hessian's eigenvalues span a wide range, which slows gradient descent in exactly the directions where the closed-form solution would also be least numerically stable. Standardization (Part 3 of the data-preparation series covers this scaler in depth) makes the valley rounder and updates more stable.

    Rule of thumb. If an iterative linear model is slow or unstable, scale features before tuning the learning rate; tuning the learning rate first, on unscaled data, is treating the symptom instead of the cause.

    Closed Form Versus Iterative: A Complexity Tradeoff

    The normal-equation solution costs roughly $O(nd^2 + d^3)$: build $X^\top X$ ($O(nd^2)$) and invert or factor it ($O(d^3)$). Gradient descent costs $O(nd)$ per iteration (batch) for however many iterations convergence takes. When $d$ is small, say tens of features, the $d^3$ term is negligible and closed form is simply faster, with no learning rate to tune and no convergence to verify. When $d$ is large, hundreds of thousands of columns from a polynomial expansion, a text or genomic feature set, or a one-hot encoded categorical with many levels, $d^3$ becomes prohibitive and even $d^2$ storage for $X^\top X$ may not fit in memory, while gradient descent's cost stays linear in $d$. The crossover is data-dependent, but as a working heuristic: prefer the closed form (or QR/SVD from Part 2) below a few thousand features, and prefer iterative methods above that, or whenever the data does not fit in memory at once.

    Online and Streaming Updates

    SGD's one-row-at-a-time update is also the natural fit for online learning: data that arrives continuously, where refitting from scratch on the full history after every new row would be wasteful or impossible. Two directions are worth knowing about, without needing their full derivations here. Recursive least squares (RLS) maintains the exact closed-form solution incrementally, updating $w$ and a running estimate of $(X^\top X)^{-1}$ after each new row in $O(d^2)$ time, rather than $O(d^3)$ per full refit; it is the online analogue of Part 2's normal equations, in the same way SGD is the online analogue of batch gradient descent. Bayesian linear regression takes a different online view: instead of a point estimate $w$, it tracks a full probability distribution over $w$, starting from a prior and updating to a posterior one row (or batch) at a time via Bayes' rule, with the update available in closed form when the prior and noise are both Gaussian. That posterior's mode, under a Gaussian prior centered at zero, turns out to be exactly the ridge regression estimate from Part 4; ridge regression's penalty term is what a Gaussian belief that coefficients are probably small looks like once translated out of probability and into an optimization objective.
