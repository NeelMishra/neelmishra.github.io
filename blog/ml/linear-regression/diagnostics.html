    Linear Regression Assumptions and Diagnostics

    July 2026 · 22 min read

    Linear regression is useful precisely because its failure modes are visible. Residuals should look like unstructured noise scattered around zero; when a pattern remains in them, the model is telling you, directly and legibly, that the linear story is incomplete. This part covers what has to be true for OLS to be valid in the first place, the plots and tests that check each assumption, and what to do when a check fails, tying together the repairs already introduced across this series: weighted least squares (Part 2), regularization (Part 4), and robust losses (Part 5).

    The Assumptions, and Why Each One Is Load-Bearing

    A convenient mnemonic is LINE: Linearity, Independence, Normality, Equal variance. LINE is a good starting checklist, but it is not the complete list: two more requirements sit underneath the whole framework and are usually assumed silently rather than named. Each assumption protects a different guarantee, so it matters which one breaks.

    • Full rank (no perfect multicollinearity): the design matrix $X$ has full column rank, so $X^\top X$ is invertible and the coefficient vector is uniquely identified (Part 1's note on rank deficiency, Part 2's condition number and VIF). This is not a bias-or-variance issue, it is about the problem having a unique answer at all: under perfect collinearity, infinitely many coefficient vectors fit the data identically well, so "the" coefficient simply does not exist. Imperfect but severe multicollinearity is a milder, different problem: the coefficients are still identified, but their variance can become enormous, which is what Part 2's diagnostics and Part 4's Ridge or Elastic Net address; do not confuse the two failure modes, one destroys identifiability and the other only inflates uncertainty around a coefficient that still technically exists.
    • Zero conditional mean of the errors (exogeneity): $E[\varepsilon_i\mid X]=0$, meaning the features carry no systematic information about the sign or size of the true error. This is the assumption that actually delivers unbiasedness, and it is the one most often silently violated in practice, by omitted variables correlated with both $y$ and a feature, by measurement error in the features, or by feedback where $y$ itself influences $x$. Unlike the other assumptions here, no amount of extra data, transformation, weighting, or regularization repairs a broken exogeneity assumption; it requires better-specified features or a fundamentally different identification strategy.
    • Linearity: the true relationship between features and the conditional mean of $y$ is (after any basis expansion from Part 1) linear in the parameters. If it is not, coefficients are biased, not just imprecise: the model is fitting the wrong shape, and no amount of additional data fixes that, only additional or transformed features do (Part 1's polynomial and basis-expansion regression).
    • Independence: the true errors are statistically independent of one another, a stronger requirement than merely being uncorrelated. The classical Gauss-Markov variance calculation only needs the weaker zero-pairwise-covariance condition; combined with equal variance, that gives the spherical covariance $\operatorname{Cov}(\varepsilon\mid X)=\sigma^2I$ defined in Part 4. Full independence additionally rules out non-linear dependence (for instance, error magnitudes clustering in time even if their signs do not correlate), which zero covariance alone permits. Violations are common in time series (today's error predicts tomorrow's, whether through correlation or through more general dependence) or grouped data (rows from the same customer or sensor share unexplained structure). Either kind of violation is not about predictions being wrong; it is about standard errors being wrong; dependent errors make coefficients look far more precise than they are, inflating confidence in $t$-tests and confidence intervals.
    • Normality (of the errors, not the features): needed for exact small-sample $t$ and $F$ tests and prediction intervals. With enough data, the central limit theorem makes coefficient estimates approximately normal regardless, so this assumption matters most for small samples and for the width of prediction intervals, not for point predictions themselves.
    • Equal variance (homoscedasticity): the spread of the errors does not depend on the fitted value or on any feature. Provided exogeneity still holds, OLS remains unbiased under heteroscedasticity, but it is no longer the lowest-variance unbiased estimator, and its standard errors are wrong; heteroscedasticity and a broken exogeneity assumption are separate failures that are frequently conflated, and only the latter causes bias. Part 2's weighted least squares is the direct fix once the variance pattern is known or estimable.

    One more distinction worth being precise about: everything above is a statement about the true, unobservable errors $\varepsilon_i = y_i - f(x_i)$ for the true (unknown) function $f$, not the fitted residuals $e_i = y_i-\hat y_i$ that diagnostics actually compute and plot. Residuals are estimates of the errors, and by construction they satisfy algebraic identities the true errors need not, for instance OLS residuals are always exactly orthogonal to every column of $X$ regardless of whether any assumption above holds, which is why they sum to exactly zero whenever an intercept is fit. Diagnostics work because well-behaved residuals are strong evidence of well-behaved errors, not because the two are literally the same object; a model can produce deceptively tidy residuals on the exact data it was fit to while still violating an assumption that only shows up on new data (Part 6's validation point below).

    Note what is conspicuously absent: OLS does not require the features themselves to be normally distributed, only the errors, and only for inference rather than for point prediction. This is one of the most common misreadings of the assumptions.

    Diagnostic Plots

    Each assumption above has a standard plot built to expose exactly its violation.

    • Residuals versus fitted values. The default first check. Random scatter around a horizontal zero line is good; any visible curve, funnel, or isolated point is a specific, named problem, covered in the table below.
    • Normal Q-Q plot. Sorted residuals plotted against the quantiles a normal distribution would predict for them. Points falling on the diagonal support normality; systematic curvature at the ends indicates heavier or lighter tails than a normal distribution, which widens or narrows how much prediction intervals should really be trusted.
    • Scale-location plot. $\sqrt{|\text{standardized residual}|}$ against fitted values. This is a residuals-vs-fitted plot specifically rescaled to make heteroscedasticity (a trend in spread, rather than in sign) easier to see, since it removes the cancellation between positive and negative residuals.
    • Leverage versus standardized residual, with Cook's distance. Leverage $h_{ii}$ (the $i$-th diagonal entry of the hat matrix $H=X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top$, so named because $\hat y = Hy$) measures how unusual row $i$'s features are, independent of its $y$ value. Cook's distance combines leverage with the residual to measure how much the entire fit would change if row $i$ were deleted:
    $$D_i = \frac{e_i^2}{p\cdot\text{MSE}}\cdot\frac{h_{ii}}{(1-h_{ii})^2},$$

    where $p$ is the number of parameters and MSE is the residual mean squared error. A point needs both an unusual residual and high leverage to get a large $D_i$; a large residual at a typical feature value, or a typical residual at an extreme feature value, individually moves the fit much less than the combination.

    Formal tests, as corroborating evidence rather than a replacement for the plots. Plots can be ambiguous, especially with modest sample sizes, since different reviewers can disagree about how much curvature or funnel-shape counts as "visible." Formal statistical tests turn the same question into a number: the Breusch-Pagan test (or White's test, which does not assume a specific functional form for how the variance changes) regresses squared residuals on the features and tests whether they carry significant explanatory power, directly formalizing what the scale-location plot shows visually; the Durbin-Watson statistic tests residuals for first-order autocorrelation, formalizing what a residuals-versus-row-order plot shows for time-ordered data. Treat both as supporting evidence alongside the plots and domain knowledge, not a substitute for either: with a large sample, these tests can reject on a real but practically irrelevant departure from an assumption, and with a small sample they can fail to reject a real and practically important one; no test can tell you whether a curved pattern reflects a feature you have access to and simply have not added yet, or a fundamental limit of a linear model.

    Confidence Intervals Versus Prediction Intervals

    These two intervals answer different questions and are routinely confused. A confidence interval for the mean response at a point $x_0$ asks how precisely the average $y$ is known, across all the rows that could ever look exactly like $x_0$. A prediction interval asks the harder, usually more practically relevant question: what range will one individual new $y$ at $x_0$ actually fall into? Under the LINE-plus-exogeneity-and-full-rank assumptions above, both intervals are built from the same fitted value $\hat y_0=x_0^\top\hat w$ and the same estimated error variance $\hat\sigma^2$, but with different variance terms:

    $$\text{CI: } \hat y_0 \pm t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-p}\cdot\hat\sigma\sqrt{x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0}, \qquad \text{PI: } \hat y_0 \pm t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-p}\cdot\hat\sigma\sqrt{1+x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0},$$

    where $t_{1-\alpha/2,\,n-p}$ denotes the $(1-\alpha/2)$ quantile of the $t$-distribution with $n-p$ degrees of freedom, the value that leaves probability $\alpha/2$ in the upper tail (written this way rather than as $t_{\alpha/2}$ to avoid any ambiguity about which tail the subscript refers to).

    The prediction interval's extra $+1$ inside the square root is not a safety margin tacked on for caution, it is the variance of a genuinely new error term: predicting one new observation carries the fitted line's own uncertainty (the part both intervals share, since even a perfectly known line still sits on top of an unknown coefficient estimate) plus the irreducible noise $\varepsilon_0$ that a fresh draw of $y_0$ still carries even if the true coefficients were known exactly. Because of that extra term, the prediction interval is always strictly wider than the confidence interval at the same $x_0$, and the gap between them never closes, not even as $n\to\infty$: more data shrinks $x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0$ toward zero, pinning the line down almost exactly, but it cannot shrink an individual future observation's own noise. Both intervals rely on the assumptions above, and normality specifically is needed for the $t$-distribution to be exact in finite samples, but the two intervals do not degrade the same way once normality is dropped: with enough data, the coefficient estimates $\hat w$ are asymptotically normal by the central limit theorem (with an appropriate covariance estimator in place of $\hat\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}$, robust to heteroscedasticity if needed), so the confidence interval for the mean response remains approximately calibrated. The prediction interval is a different story: it also has to describe the distribution of one brand-new error $\varepsilon_0$, and no amount of additional data makes a single future error "more normal," since the CLT is a statement about averages, not about individual draws. A Gaussian prediction interval is therefore not generally asymptotically calibrated when the true errors are heavy-tailed or skewed, no matter how large $n$ gets; when that is a concern, prefer an empirical-residual interval (using the actual quantiles of the residual distribution instead of a Gaussian shape), quantile regression (Part 5) fit directly at the desired tail probabilities, a bootstrap prediction interval, or a conformal prediction interval, all of which are built to be calibrated under the data's real error distribution rather than an assumed one. Both intervals still widen automatically for $x_0$ far from the training data's centroid, since $x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0$ grows there, correctly reflecting the added risk of extrapolation.

    Validation, Not Just In-Sample Diagnostics

    Good diagnostics are not only about training residuals. A model with beautiful in-sample residual plots can still fail on new data if features leak future information, or if preprocessing (scalers, imputers, the choice of $\lambda$ from Part 4's cross-validation) was fit on the full dataset instead of on the training split alone. Split the data first, fit every piece of preprocessing on the training split only, and evaluate residual diagnostics and error metrics on genuinely held-out data. A residual plot on training data answers "does the model describe the data it was fit to"; a residual plot on held-out data answers the question that actually matters, "does the model describe data it has not seen."

    A Practical Checklist

    Across this series, the questions worth asking about any linear fit, roughly in the order to ask them:

    • Are the features doing the modeling job, or does the residual-versus-fitted plot show curvature that basis expansion or polynomial terms (Part 1) should absorb instead?
    • Are any features redundant enough to blow up coefficient variance (Part 2's condition number and VIF), and if so, is Ridge or Elastic Net (Part 4) called for?
    • Does the loss surface make gradient descent painfully slow (Part 3), suggesting feature scaling before touching the learning rate?
    • Does the scale-location plot show growing spread, calling for weighted least squares (Part 2) or a variance-stabilizing transform?
    • Does the leverage-versus-residual plot show one or two points with outsized Cook's distance, calling for an audit of that row and possibly a robust loss (Part 5) instead of squared error?
    • Is the mean even the right thing to predict, or would a specific quantile or interval (Part 5) answer the actual question better?
    • Was every diagnostic and every preprocessing choice validated on held-out data, not just admired on the training set?
    The residual plot is the first debugging tool. It usually shows, directly, whether to add features, transform variables, reweight observations, regularize, switch losses, or conclude that linear regression has genuinely run out of room and a nonlinear model is warranted.
