    Binary Entropy

    July 2026 · 16 min read

    Binary entropy is the entropy of a two-outcome distribution $(p,1-p)$:

    $$H(p) = -p\log_2 p - (1-p)\log_2(1-p).$$

    This single curve explains why balanced binary nodes are maximally uncertain, why pure decision-tree leaves have zero entropy, and why class imbalance lowers uncertainty even before any feature split is considered.

    The Shape of the Curve

    The curve is symmetric because swapping the names of the two classes changes $p$ to $1-p$ but leaves uncertainty unchanged. It is zero at $p=0$ and $p=1$, where the outcome is certain. It reaches one bit at $p=0.5$, where both outcomes are equally likely.

    ▶ Sweep the Binary Entropy Curve

    The marker moves from a pure class-0 node to a pure class-1 node. Entropy is largest in the middle, where both classes are equally likely.

    Concavity and Mixing

    Binary entropy is concave. Mixing two distributions cannot reduce average uncertainty below the weighted average of their entropies. In decision trees this is why splitting can be measured by entropy reduction: a useful split separates one mixed parent into children that are, on average, less mixed.

    Derivative Intuition

    The slope is

    $$H'(p)=\log_2\frac{1-p}{p}.$$

    The slope is positive when $p<0.5$, zero at $p=0.5$, and negative when $p>0.5$. Near the edges, a tiny amount of minority class sharply increases uncertainty. Near the middle, moving a little left or right changes uncertainty more slowly because the distribution is already highly mixed.

    Entropy is not error rate. A node with class proportions $(0.9,0.1)$ has only $10\%$ majority-class error, but its entropy is about $0.469$ bits. Entropy measures uncertainty in the full distribution, not just the final majority prediction.

    From Binary to Multiclass

    The multiclass formula is the same sum over all classes. For $K$ equally likely classes, entropy is $\log_2 K$. A balanced four-class node has $2$ bits of entropy, because two yes/no questions are needed on average to identify one of four equally likely labels.
