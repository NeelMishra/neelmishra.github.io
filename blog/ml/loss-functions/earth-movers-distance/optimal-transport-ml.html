    Optimal Transport and EMD in Machine Learning

    July 2026 · 16 min read

    Earth Mover's Distance becomes powerful when outcomes have geometry: pixels, embeddings, spatial locations, ranked values, or continuous features. It compares distributions by asking how much movement is needed, not just how much probability differs in matching bins.

    Wasserstein GANs

    Wasserstein GANs replace a saturated divergence with a transport-style objective. When generated and real distributions are separated, the Wasserstein distance can still provide a meaningful gradient because it reflects how far samples must move.

    Distribution Shift

    EMD can detect shifts where mass moves smoothly. For example, if prediction scores drift upward by a small amount, EMD reflects a small movement. Bin-wise divergences may overreact if mass crosses bin boundaries.

    Cost Matrices

    The key modeling choice is the ground cost. In images it may be pixel distance. In embeddings it may be cosine or Euclidean distance. If the ground cost does not match the problem, the EMD value can be misleading.

    Tradeoff

    Optimal transport is often more expensive than KL or JS. Entropic regularization and Sinkhorn iterations are common approximations when exact transport is too slow.
