    KL Divergence Foundations

    July 2026 · 18 min read

    KL divergence compares two probability distributions. Think of $P$ as the distribution that actually generates the data, and $Q$ as the distribution predicted by a model. KL asks a direct question:

    If outcomes really come from $P$, how badly surprised would a model using $Q$ be?

    If $Q$ gives high probability to the outcomes that actually happen under $P$, KL is small. If $Q$ gives low probability to outcomes that often happen under $P$, KL is large.

    The formula is

    $$D_{KL}(P\|Q)=\sum_i p_i\log_2\frac{p_i}{q_i}.$$

    Read it one outcome at a time. The term $p_i$ says how often outcome $i$ happens in the real distribution. The ratio $p_i/q_i$ says whether the model underestimates or overestimates that outcome. The logarithm turns that mismatch into a surprise penalty. The sum averages that penalty over the outcomes that actually occur.

    A Tiny Weather Example

    Suppose the real weather distribution is $P=(0.8,0.2)$ for sun and rain. A good model should also put about $80\%$ probability on sun and $20\%$ on rain. Compare three model guesses:

    Model distribution Q Interpretation KL from P to Q
    $(0.8,0.2)$ Matches the real distribution. $0$ bits
    $(0.5,0.5)$ Too uncertain. It underestimates sunny days. about $0.278$ bits
    $(0.2,0.8)$ Mostly backwards. It expects rain when sun is common. about $1.2$ bits

    The last model is punished heavily because sunny days happen often under $P$, but the model assigns them only $20\%$ probability. KL does not just ask whether the most likely class is correct. It checks the whole probability distribution.

    What Do the Bits Mean?

    The unit "bits" comes from the base-2 logarithm. It is a unit of surprise, not something that requires understanding prefix codes. The coding story is optional: if a model assigns low probability to common events, it pays extra surprise. That extra average surprise is KL divergence.

    This is also why KL connects to entropy and cross-entropy:

    $$H(P,Q)-H(P)=D_{KL}(P\|Q).$$

    In plain language: cross-entropy is the model's average surprise on real data, entropy is the unavoidable uncertainty in the data itself, and KL is the extra surprise caused by using the wrong probabilities.

    ▶ KL as Extra Surprise

    The true distribution P stays fixed. Step through model distributions Q. KL shrinks as Q stops being surprised by outcomes that are common under P.

    Direction Matters

    The order $D_{KL}(P\|Q)$ means "judge model $Q$ using real data from $P$." It punishes $Q$ harshly when $Q$ assigns tiny probability to events that actually occur under $P$. The reversed quantity, $D_{KL}(Q\|P)$, asks a different question: if the model generates samples from $Q$, how surprising are those samples under $P$? The next post focuses on why this reversal changes behavior.

    KL is not a distance metric. It is nonnegative, but it is not symmetric and does not satisfy the triangle inequality.
