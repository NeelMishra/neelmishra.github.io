    OLS, Normal Equations, and Projection Geometry

    July 2026 · 14 min read

    The previous part fixed the estimand: linear regression targets the best linear approximation to $E[Y\mid X]$. This part derives the estimator itself, $\hat{\beta}$, first through calculus on the least-squares objective, then again through the geometry of orthogonal projection. Both routes end at the same formula, and each explains a different part of why the formula behaves the way it does.

    Deriving the Normal Equations

    The objective is the residual sum of squares as a function of $\beta$,

    $$\text{RSS}(\beta) = (y-X\beta)^\top(y-X\beta) = y^\top y - 2\beta^\top X^\top y + \beta^\top X^\top X \beta.$$

    Differentiating with respect to $\beta$ and setting the gradient to zero gives

    $$\nabla_\beta\, \text{RSS}(\beta) = -2X^\top y + 2X^\top X\beta = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad X^\top X\,\hat{\beta} = X^\top y.$$

    These are the normal equations. When $X$ has full column rank, $X^\top X$ is invertible, and

    $$\hat{\beta} = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y.$$

    The Hessian of $\text{RSS}(\beta)$ is $2X^\top X$, which is positive semidefinite for any $X$ and positive definite whenever $X$ has full column rank. Because the objective is convex, any $\hat{\beta}$ satisfying the normal equations is a global minimizer, not merely a stationary point; the first-order condition is sufficient here, not just necessary, which is why setting the gradient to zero is enough without a separate check of a second-order condition in the scalar-optimization sense.

    Specializing to One Predictor

    With one predictor, differentiating $\sum_i(y_i-a-bx_i)^2$ separately gives $\sum_i e_i=0$ and $\sum_i x_ie_i=0$. The first equation gives $a=\bar y-b\bar x$; substitution into the second gives the familiar formulas,

    $$\hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_i (x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{\sum_i (x_i-\bar{x})^2} = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}, \qquad \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y}-\hat{\beta}_1\bar{x}.$$

    A worked six-point example fixes these formulas to numbers that the animation below reuses directly. With $x=(1,2,3,4,5,6)$ and $y=(2.2,3.8,5.1,5.6,7.4,8.3)$, the sums are $S_{xx}=17.5$ and $S_{xy}=20.9$, giving $\hat{\beta}_1 = 20.9/17.5 = 1.1943$ and $\hat{\beta}_0 = 5.4 - 1.1943(3.5) = 1.22$.

    The Normal Equations Are an Orthogonality Statement

    Writing the normal equations as $X^\top(y-X\hat\beta)=0$ shows that they say something geometric: the residual vector $e=y-X\hat\beta$ has zero dot product with every column of $X$. For simple regression, this is two conditions at once, $\sum_i e_i = 0$ (residuals orthogonal to the intercept column) and $\sum_i e_i x_i = 0$ (residuals orthogonal to $x$). The first condition holds automatically whenever the intercept is chosen optimally for a given slope, $\hat\beta_0=\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x$, for any value of that slope. The second condition is what pins down the slope itself: it holds only at the least-squares optimum, and fails, in a specific and measurable way, for every other candidate slope.

    ▶ Exact Vector Projection onto a Stated Column Space

    The target is $y=(3,1,2)$ and the stated subspace is $\mathcal S=\operatorname{span}\{(1,1,0),(0,1,1)\}$. The animation derives the exact projection, residual, orthogonality checks, and Pythagorean norm identity without a slope grid.

    For $X=[u\ v]$, the normal equations give $\hat\beta=(5/3,2/3)$, hence $\hat y=(5/3)u+(2/3)v=(5/3,7/3,2/3)$. The residual is $e=y-\hat y=(4/3,-4/3,4/3)$, and $e^\top u=e^\top v=0$. Exact arithmetic verifies $\|y\|^2=14$, $\|\hat y\|^2=26/3$, and $\|e\|^2=16/3$, so $14=26/3+16/3$.

    The Hat Matrix as an Orthogonal Projection

    Substituting $\hat\beta=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y$ into $\hat y = X\hat\beta$ gives

    $$\hat y = X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y = Hy, \qquad H := X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top,$$

    where $H$ is the hat matrix. $H$ is symmetric ($H^\top=H$) and idempotent ($HH=H$, since applying the same projection twice changes nothing further), which together are exactly the algebraic definition of an orthogonal projection matrix. $H$ projects $y$ onto $\text{col}(X)$, the column space of $X$: $\hat y=Hy$ is, among all vectors in $\text{col}(X)$, the one closest to $y$ in Euclidean distance, and that closest point is unique because $\text{col}(X)$ is a linear subspace. The residual $e=(I-H)y$ is the projection of $y$ onto the orthogonal complement of $\text{col}(X)$, which is exactly the geometric restatement of the normal equations: $e$ is perpendicular to every vector in $\text{col}(X)$, including every column of $X$ individually and every linear combination of them.

    Property of $H$Consequence
    $H^\top = H$$H$ is self-adjoint with respect to the standard inner product, a requirement for an orthogonal (as opposed to oblique) projection.
    $H H = H$Projecting an already-fitted vector changes nothing: $H\hat y = H(Hy) = Hy = \hat y$.
    $\text{trace}(H) = p+1$The trace of a projection matrix equals the dimension of the subspace it projects onto, here the number of columns of $X$; the diagonal entries $h_{ii}$ are the leverage values used later for influence diagnostics.
    Eigenvalues of $H$ are $0$ or $1$Any projection matrix has eigenvalues restricted to $\{0,1\}$: $1$ on $\text{col}(X)$, $0$ on its orthogonal complement.

    This picture also explains why $R^2$ can never decrease when a new predictor column is added, a fact that surprises many people the first time they see it. Adding a column can only enlarge $\text{col}(X)$ (or leave it unchanged if the new column is already a linear combination of the existing ones), and projecting onto a larger subspace can only move the projection closer to $y$, never farther. That same fact is exactly why $R^2$ alone cannot be used to justify adding predictors, since it improves mechanically even when the added predictor carries no real signal, which is the motivation behind adjusted $R^2$ and out-of-sample validation covered later in this track.

    Fitted and Residual Decomposition

    Every response vector decomposes uniquely as $y=\hat y+e$ with $\hat y\in\operatorname{col}(X)$ and $e\in\operatorname{col}(X)^\perp$, even when coefficients themselves are not unique. Orthogonality yields the Pythagorean identity $\|y\|^2=\|\hat y\|^2+\|e\|^2$. With an intercept, centering produces the related identity $\sum_i(y_i-\bar y)^2=\sum_i(\hat y_i-\bar y)^2+\sum_i e_i^2$. Without an intercept, the centered SST identity and the usual interpretation of $R^2$ can fail even though the uncentered vector identity remains valid.

    Counterexample. For a through-origin fit, residuals need not sum to zero because the constant vector is absent from $\operatorname{col}(X)$. Reporting the centered $R^2=1-\text{SSE}/\text{SST}$ can then produce a negative value or a misleading decomposition despite a valid least-squares projection.

    Interview trap. A frequent request is to derive $\hat\beta$ "from scratch," and an equally frequent slip is forgetting to justify invertibility of $X^\top X$. $X^\top X$ is invertible exactly when $X$ has full column rank, meaning no column of $X$ is an exact linear combination of the others. Perfect multicollinearity, for example an intercept column plus two predictor columns where one predictor is a constant multiple of the other, makes $X^\top X$ singular, and $\hat\beta$ from the closed form is not defined. This is not a rare edge case in interview settings; it is a standard follow-up question, and the correct response names the rank condition explicitly rather than treating the inverse as automatically available.

    Near-Collinearity: Defined but Unstable

    A related but distinct situation is when $X^\top X$ is invertible in principle but nearly singular in practice, because two columns are highly, though not perfectly, correlated. The formula for $\hat\beta$ remains mathematically valid, but the entries of $(X^\top X)^{-1}$ become very large, so small changes in the data can swing $\hat\beta$ substantially, and floating-point computation of the inverse becomes numerically unreliable. This case is common enough in real design matrices that it gets a dedicated page later in this track, in Multicollinearity, VIF, and the FWL Theorem, together with the numerically stable solvers, QR and SVD based rather than direct matrix inversion, that keep computation reliable even as columns approach collinearity.

    Assumptions and edge cases. Least squares needs finite numeric inputs; unique coefficients need full column rank. Rank deficiency still has a unique fitted projection but infinitely many coefficient vectors, conventionally resolved by a pseudoinverse or a constraint. Weighted least squares changes the inner product, so Euclidean orthogonality becomes $X^\top We=0$. The from-scratch C++ implementation includes dimension and rank checks and focused solver components in From Scratch · C++ · Ordinary Least Squares.
