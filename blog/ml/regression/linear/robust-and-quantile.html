    Robust and Quantile Regression

    July 2026 · 13 min read

    Squared loss gives large residuals very large weight, so one extreme response can pull the fitted line strongly. Absolute, Huber, and pinball losses reduce this sensitivity or target a different part of the outcome distribution. No loss is always best; each answers a different question.

    Squared, Absolute, and Huber Loss

    $$\rho_\delta(r)=\begin{cases}\frac12r^2,&|r|\le\delta,\\ \delta(|r|-\frac12\delta),&|r|>\delta.\end{cases}\qquad \psi_\delta(r)=\rho_\delta'(r)=\begin{cases}r,&|r|\le\delta,\\ \delta\operatorname{sign}(r),&|r|>\delta.\end{cases}$$

    Huber loss behaves like squared loss for small residuals and like absolute loss for large ones. LAD minimizes absolute residuals and targets the conditional median. Huber gives a compromise between mean fitting and tail resistance. Neither protects against an extreme predictor row, because leverage comes from $X$, not only from the residual.

    How One Extreme Point Changes the Fit

    The score $\psi(r)=\rho'(r)$ describes how strongly one residual pulls on the coefficients. Adding a tiny amount of contamination at $(x_0,y_0)$ changes the fitted parameter at the rate

    $$\psi(y_0-x_0^\top\beta)\,x_0=M\,\frac{d\beta(F_\varepsilon)}{d\varepsilon}\bigg|_{\varepsilon=0},\qquad M=E_F\!\left[\psi'(y-x^\top\beta)\,xx^\top\right],$$

    Thus $\operatorname{IF}(x_0,y_0)=M^{-1}\psi(r_0)x_0$. Squared loss uses $\psi(r)=r$, so residual influence grows without limit. LAD uses only the sign of $r$, Huber clips the score, and pinball loss uses bounded asymmetric scores. The factor $x_0$ remains, so a very unusual predictor row can still have large influence.

    Pinball Loss and Conditional Quantiles

    Pinball loss at level $\tau$ penalizes under-prediction and over-prediction differently. Its minimum is the conditional $\tau$ quantile. At $\tau=0.5$, it becomes half of absolute loss, so quantile regression becomes median regression.

    Different quantile slopes show how different parts of the conditional distribution change with $X$. A 0.1 and 0.9 pair can form a prediction band, but its coverage must be checked on held-out data.

    Prediction Intervals versus Confidence Intervals

    A confidence interval for the mean at $x_0$ includes coefficient uncertainty. A prediction interval for one new outcome also includes fresh outcome noise:

    $$\operatorname{Var}(y_0-x_0^\top\hat\beta)=\sigma^2\left(1+x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0\right).$$

    With $x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0=0.05$, $\hat\sigma^2=4$, and $t\approx2$, the mean-interval half-width is about $0.89$, while the prediction-interval half-width is about $4.10$. More data can shrink uncertainty about the mean, but it cannot remove new-observation noise.

    Optimization, Scale, and Tuning

    LAD and pinball losses are convex but have a sharp point, so common solvers include linear programming and subgradient methods. Huber regression often uses IRLS, giving full weight to small residuals and weight $\delta/|r_i|$ to large ones.

    Trap. A fixed threshold such as $\delta=1.345$ has meaning only after residuals are expressed in compatible scale units. Multiplying the response by one thousand without scaling $\delta$ changes the estimator.

    A common Huber choice is $\delta=1.345\hat\sigma$, where $\hat\sigma$ comes from a robust scale such as MAD. Validation can also choose $\delta$ using Huber loss. Extreme quantiles near $0$ or $1$ have fewer relevant tail observations and much higher variance.

    Counterexamples, Edge Cases, and Production

    Changing the loss does not fix a missing curve or omitted variable. Robust response loss also does not control an extreme predictor row.

    Separate quantile lines may cross, so a predicted 90th percentile can fall below the median. Extreme quantiles are noisy, ties create nonunique solutions, and censored data needs a censoring-aware model.

    Useful monitoring includes matched loss, held-out quantile coverage, interval width, subgroup coverage, crossing rate, residual-scale drift, and the fraction of Huber residuals being clipped.
