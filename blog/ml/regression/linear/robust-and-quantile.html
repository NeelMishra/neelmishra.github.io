    Robust and Quantile Regression

    July 2026 · 13 min read

    Squared loss targets the conditional mean and gives residual $r$ a score proportional to $r$. Its unbounded influence makes one gross response error capable of dominating a fit. Absolute, Huber, and pinball losses alter both the target functional and the sensitivity to tails. Robustness is not a synonym for universal superiority: each loss answers a different statistical question.

    Squared, Absolute, and Huber Loss

    $$\rho_\delta(r)=\begin{cases}\frac12r^2,&|r|\le\delta,\\ \delta(|r|-\frac12\delta),&|r|>\delta.\end{cases}\qquad \psi_\delta(r)=\rho_\delta'(r)=\begin{cases}r,&|r|\le\delta,\\ \delta\operatorname{sign}(r),&|r|>\delta.\end{cases}$$

    Huber loss is quadratic near zero and linear in the tails, so its score is bounded. Least absolute deviations, or LAD, minimizes $\sum_i|r_i|$ and targets a conditional median. Huber generally targets a robust location near the mean under symmetric errors; asymmetry can shift that target. Neither method protects against arbitrary high-leverage contamination in $X$, because a large predictor vector can multiply even a bounded residual score.

    Influence Functions, Derived

    Every loss above is fit by solving an estimating equation $\sum_i\psi(y_i-x_i^\top\beta)x_i=0$ for the corresponding score $\psi=\rho'$. Consider the contaminated distribution $F_\varepsilon=(1-\varepsilon)F+\varepsilon\delta_{(x_0,y_0)}$, a point mass added at $(x_0,y_0)$ with weight $\varepsilon$, and let $\beta(F_\varepsilon)$ solve $E_{F_\varepsilon}[\psi(y-x^\top\beta)x]=0$. Differentiating that identity with respect to $\varepsilon$ at $\varepsilon=0$ and using $E_F[\psi(y-x^\top\beta)x]=0$ at the true $\beta$ gives

    $$\psi(y_0-x_0^\top\beta)\,x_0=M\,\frac{d\beta(F_\varepsilon)}{d\varepsilon}\bigg|_{\varepsilon=0},\qquad M=E_F\!\left[\psi'(y-x^\top\beta)\,xx^\top\right],$$

    so the influence function is $\operatorname{IF}(x_0,y_0)=M^{-1}\psi(y_0-x_0^\top\beta)\,x_0$. For smooth scores, $M$ uses the ordinary derivative. For LAD and pinball loss, it means the Jacobian of the expected score: the jump at residual zero contributes the conditional residual density, giving $M=2E[f_{\varepsilon\mid X}(0\mid X)xx^\top]$ for the stated LAD score and $M=E[f_{\varepsilon\mid X}(0\mid X)xx^\top]$ for pinball loss. Nonsingularity and positive density at the target quantile are required. This formula reproduces every entry in the table above: squared loss has $\psi(r)=r$, so influence grows without bound in the residual; LAD has $\psi(r)=\operatorname{sign}(r)$, bounded at $\pm1$ regardless of residual size; Huber clips $\psi$ at $\pm\delta$; and the pinball score $\psi_\tau(r)=\tau-\mathbf 1\{r<0\}$ is bounded in $[\tau-1,\tau]$. Critically, $x_0$ multiplies every case linearly. No pointwise residual loss bounds the influence of a high-leverage predictor vector; only bounding or downweighting $x_0$ itself, as in bounded-influence or leverage-trimmed estimators, can do that.

    Pinball Loss and Conditional Quantiles

    For residual $r=y-q$, pinball loss is $\rho_\tau(r)=r(\tau-\mathbf 1\{r<0\})$. Minimizing expected pinball loss yields any conditional $\tau$ quantile because its subgradient condition is $P(Y\le q\mid X)-\tau=0$ at a continuous distribution. At $\tau=0.5$, pinball loss is one half of absolute loss, so median regression and LAD coincide.

    Quantile slopes describe how a conditional quantile changes with predictors, not how individual outcomes move. Different slopes at $\tau=0.1$ and $\tau=0.9$ reveal distributional heterogeneity that a mean model hides. Prediction intervals based on two conditional quantiles require calibration checks: nominal 80 percent coverage does not follow merely from fitting the 0.1 and 0.9 quantiles on the training sample.

    Prediction Intervals versus Confidence Intervals

    A confidence interval for the mean response at $x_0$ targets the unknown conditional mean $x_0^\top\beta$ and uses only estimation uncertainty, $\operatorname{Var}(x_0^\top\hat\beta)=\sigma^2x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0$. A prediction interval for one new outcome $y_0=x_0^\top\beta+\varepsilon_0$ must also cover the fresh noise draw, adding $\sigma^2$ to the same variance:

    $$\operatorname{Var}(y_0-x_0^\top\hat\beta)=\sigma^2\left(1+x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0\right).$$

    Worked comparison. With $x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0=0.05$, $\hat\sigma^2=4$, and $t\approx2$, the confidence-interval half-width is $2\sqrt{4\times0.05}\approx0.89$, while the prediction-interval half-width is $2\sqrt{4\times1.05}\approx4.10$, over four times wider. As $n\to\infty$, $x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0\to0$ and the confidence interval shrinks toward zero width, but the prediction interval converges to a floor set by $\sigma^2$ alone: no amount of additional data removes irreducible outcome noise. Reporting a narrow confidence interval where a decision needs a prediction interval systematically understates real-world risk. The quantile-based interval from two fitted $\tau$ levels avoids a parametric Gaussian error shape, but it still assumes the fitted conditional quantiles are correctly specified and calibrated; it is not automatically distribution-free.

    Optimization, Scale, and Tuning

    LAD and pinball objectives are convex but nonsmooth. Linear programming, subgradient methods, and specialized interior-point solvers are standard. Huber regression can use iteratively reweighted least squares with weights $w_i=\psi_\delta(r_i)/r_i$, equal to $1$ inside the quadratic region and $\delta/|r_i|$ outside. The residual scale should be estimated robustly, often from MAD, before setting $\delta$.

    Trap. A fixed threshold such as $\delta=1.345$ has meaning only after residuals are expressed in compatible scale units. Multiplying the response by one thousand without scaling $\delta$ changes the estimator.

    Tuning $\delta$ has two common routes: the classical choice $\delta=1.345\hat\sigma$, with $\hat\sigma=\text{MAD}/0.6745$, targets 95 percent efficiency relative to OLS under exactly Gaussian errors; alternatively, a small grid of $\delta$ values can be cross-validated against a held-out robust loss such as Huber or pinball loss rather than RMSE, so the tuning criterion matches the estimator's own objective. Quantile levels are tuned similarly: extreme $\tau$ near 0 or 1 have few effectively relevant tail observations and high variance, so production systems typically fit a moderate, evenly spaced grid of $\tau$ values, cross-validate each on its own pinball loss, and monitor how many quantile pairs cross before trusting the resulting intervals.

    Counterexamples, Edge Cases, and Production

    Counterexample. Replacing OLS with LAD does not repair omitted nonlinear structure. A curved residual pattern remains misspecified under every pointwise loss. Likewise, a data-entry error in a high-leverage predictor can distort LAD because robust response loss alone does not bound leverage.

    Finite samples can have a whole interval of valid medians. Extreme quantiles have few effective tail observations and high variance. Ties produce subgradient sets. Separate quantile models can cross, so a predicted 90th percentile may fall below a predicted median. Noncrossing constraints, joint parameterizations, or monotone rearrangement address that incoherence. Censoring and truncation require dedicated models rather than a simple robust loss.

    Production evaluation should report loss matched to the estimand, empirical quantile coverage, interval width, subgroup coverage, crossing frequency, residual scale drift, and the proportion of Huber observations in the clipped region. A rising clipped fraction can signal drift or upstream corruption rather than successful robustness. Thresholds, quantile levels, scaling rules, and subgradient tolerances belong in the model artifact.
