    Multicollinearity, VIF, and the FWL Theorem

    July 2026 · 13 min read

    Multicollinearity is high correlation among the predictor columns of $X$. It does not violate any of the classical assumptions from the previous part on its own, provided $X$ retains full column rank, and it does not bias $\hat\beta$ or damage the model's overall predictive fit. What it damages is the precision of individual coefficients, and understanding exactly why clarifies what should, and should not, be done about it.

    What Multicollinearity Changes, and What It Leaves Alone

    $\text{Var}(\hat\beta\mid X)=\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}$ from the previous part already contains the whole story. When two columns of $X$ are highly correlated, $X^\top X$ becomes close to singular, and the entries of its inverse, in particular the diagonal entries that set the variance of each individual coefficient, become large. The fitted values $\hat y = Hy$ and the overall $R^2$ depend only on the column space $\text{col}(X)$, not on which particular basis of that space is used to represent it, so predictions and total explained variance are essentially unaffected by how correlated the columns happen to be, as long as the column space itself does not change. What becomes unreliable is the model's attempt to split credit for that explained variance among individual, highly correlated columns.

    Exact Rank Failure Versus Near Collinearity

    Exact collinearity means $\operatorname{rank}(X)

    Stable prediction is possible because opposite coefficient changes can cancel inside $x^\top\hat\beta$ for feature combinations resembling the training manifold. This is not a guarantee under distribution shift: a new row that separates previously correlated predictors can have high prediction variance. Joint tests and prediction can remain useful even when individual coefficient tests are weak.

    The Variance Inflation Factor

    For predictor $j$, write $R_j^2$ for the $R^2$ obtained by regressing $X_j$ on every other predictor in the model (including the intercept). The variance of $\hat\beta_j$ can be shown to equal

    $$\text{Var}(\hat\beta_j) = \frac{\sigma^2}{(n-1)\,\widehat{\text{Var}}(X_j)}\cdot\frac{1}{1-R_j^2},$$

    and the second factor is the variance inflation factor,

    $$\text{VIF}_j = \frac{1}{1-R_j^2}.$$

    $\text{VIF}_j=1$ when $X_j$ is uncorrelated with the other predictors, meaning the variance of $\hat\beta_j$ is exactly what it would be if $X_j$ were fit alone. As $R_j^2\to 1$, meaning $X_j$ becomes almost perfectly predictable from the other predictors, $\text{VIF}_j\to\infty$: the model has to disentangle the effect of $X_j$ from an increasingly narrow sliver of information that $X_j$ contributes and the other predictors do not.

    Worked numerical example. The exact four-row construction below exposes every FWL projection and dot product.

    ▶ Exact FWL Residualization

    For $z=(-1,-1,1,1)$, $x=(1,3,2,4)$, and $y=(5,7,11,17)$, each stage computes an exact projection on $[1,z]$. The final residual-on-residual slope equals the full-model coefficient on $x$ exactly.

    The full regression is $y=5+2x+3z+e$ with $e=(1,-1,-1,1)$. Projecting $x$ on $[1,z]$ gives fitted values $(2,2,3,3)$ and $\tilde x=(-1,1,-1,1)$. Projecting $y$ on the same controls gives $(6,6,14,14)$ and $\tilde y=(-1,1,-3,3)$. Therefore $\tilde x^\top\tilde y/\tilde x^\top\tilde x=8/4=2$, exactly the full-model coefficient.

    Interview trap. "Multicollinearity biases the coefficients" is a common but incorrect claim. Multicollinearity, on its own, changes variance, not bias; $\hat\beta$ remains unbiased under the classical assumptions regardless of how correlated the predictors are, as long as $X$ has full column rank. What multicollinearity destroys is the ability to trust any single coefficient's point estimate or sign in isolation, since a slightly different sample can produce a very different, even oppositely signed, split between two highly collinear coefficients while leaving predictions nearly unchanged.

    The Frisch-Waugh-Lovell Theorem

    The FWL theorem gives a precise sense in which a multiple regression coefficient is a partial effect, with the influence of the other predictors mathematically removed rather than merely "held constant" in a hand-wavy sense. For the model $y = X_1\beta_1 + X_2\beta_2 + \varepsilon$, the coefficient $\hat\beta_2$ can be recovered in three steps:

    1. Residualization of $y$ on $X_1$ produces the residual vector $\tilde y$.
    2. Residualization of $X_2$ on $X_1$ produces the residual matrix $\tilde X_2$.
    3. A regression of $\tilde y$ on $\tilde X_2$ produces a slope equal to $\hat\beta_2$ from the full multiple regression exactly, and its residuals equal those of the full regression exactly.

    Let $M_1=I-X_1(X_1^\top X_1)^{-1}X_1^\top$. The second block of the full normal equations is $X_2^\top(y-X_1\hat\beta_1-X_2\hat\beta_2)=0$. Solving the first block for $\hat\beta_1$ and substituting yields

    $$X_2^\top M_1X_2\hat\beta_2=X_2^\top M_1y,\qquad \hat\beta_2=(X_2^\top M_1X_2)^{-1}X_2^\top M_1y.$$

    Since $M_1$ is symmetric and idempotent, this is exactly OLS of $\tilde y=M_1y$ on $\tilde X_2=M_1X_2$. The full and partial regressions also have identical final residuals. The equality requires the relevant residualized columns to retain rank; if $M_1X_2=0$, $X_2$ contains no variation beyond the controls and its coefficient is unidentified.

    Detection and Remedies

    SituationWhat to do
    $\text{VIF}_j$ moderately elevated (commonly flagged above $5$ to $10$), but every coefficient still has a sensible sign and magnitudeOften left as is; the model's predictions and joint significance are not compromised, only individual coefficient precision.
    Two predictors are conceptually measuring nearly the same thingDropping one or combining both into a single index avoids an unstable split of one underlying effect, as characterized by the FWL theorem.
    Coefficients need to remain individually interpretable despite correlated predictorsRidge regression trades a small amount of bias for a large reduction in coefficient variance under collinearity, a tradeoff developed later in this track under regularization.
    $X^\top X$ is exactly singular, not just poorly conditioned (perfect collinearity)A structural specification error, most often the dummy-variable trap: including all $k$ indicator columns for a $k$-category variable together with an intercept. One indicator has to be dropped, or the intercept removed, to restore full column rank.

    Centering predictors before forming an interaction or polynomial term reduces the correlation between, say, $x$ and $x^2$ or between $x_1$ and $x_1x_2$, but centering does not fix multicollinearity that exists between genuinely distinct predictors measuring overlapping information; the two situations are frequently conflated, and the model-specification page later in this track separates them explicitly.

    Workflow, counterexample, and edge case. Rank and singular values come first, followed by condition numbers on scaled columns, auxiliary-regression VIFs for coefficient interpretation, coefficient stability under resampling, and prediction checks on the intended support. Dropping a confounder solely to lower VIF is a counterexample to the idea that a smaller VIF always improves a model, since the removal can introduce omitted-variable bias. At exact collinearity, VIF is infinite and the individual coefficient is unidentified. Ridge improves conditioning but changes the estimand through shrinkage. Principal components improve numerical geometry but may weaken original-feature interpretation. More data helps only when it adds independent variation rather than repeating the same narrow predictor relationship.
