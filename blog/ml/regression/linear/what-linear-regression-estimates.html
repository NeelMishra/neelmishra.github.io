    What Linear Regression Estimates

    July 2026 · 13 min read

    Linear regression is often introduced through the formula for $\hat{\beta}$ before the more basic question of what quantity $\hat{\beta}$ is even trying to approximate. That question has a precise answer, and getting it right up front avoids a whole class of misreadings later: overconfident causal claims, surprise at nonlinearity, and confusion about why a coefficient can be statistically significant yet practically small.

    The Population Estimand: A Conditional Mean

    For a predictor $X$ and outcome $Y$, the population quantity linear regression targets is the conditional mean function

    $$\mu(x) = E[Y \mid X = x].$$

    This function is a property of the joint distribution of $(X,Y)$; it exists independently of any dataset and independently of any decision to fit a linear model. A dataset provides finitely many draws from that joint distribution, and every fitted coefficient is a sample-based guess about a fixed population object that is never observed directly.

    The Best Linear Approximation, Not the Truth Itself

    Linear regression restricts its guess to functions of the form $a+bx$. When $\mu(x)$ is itself linear, this restriction costs nothing and OLS targets $\mu(x)$ exactly. When $\mu(x)$ is not linear, OLS does not fail outright; it targets the specific line that minimizes expected squared error among all lines,

    $$(a^*,b^*) = \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{a,b}\; E\big[(\mu(X)-(a+bX))^2\big],$$

    which, for a single predictor, has the closed form $b^* = \text{Cov}(X,\mu(X))/\text{Var}(X)$ and $a^*=E[\mu(X)]-b^*E[X]$. This line is the best linear predictor, and it is what a correctly fit OLS model converges to as the sample grows, regardless of whether $\mu(x)$ is linear. If $\mu(x)$ curves, the best linear predictor systematically misses the curve at some values of $x$ even in the population limit, since it minimizes an average squared gap across the whole range of $x$ rather than fitting any single point.

    ▶ Sample Fits Converging to a Fixed Population Target, Not to the True Curve

    The dashed curve is a nonlinear population mean $\mu(x)=2+3x-0.3x^2$ on $x=1,\ldots,7$. The solid line is the fixed population best linear predictor, $a^*=5.6$, $b^*=0.6$. Stepping through increases the number of noisy observations averaged at each $x$; the fitted OLS line (orange, recomputed from the growing averages) settles onto the fixed best-linear-predictor line, while a visible gap to the true curve remains at the ends.

    The curve-minus-line gap is $-1.5$ at both $x=1$ and $x=7$, $0$ at $x=2$ and $x=6$, and $1.2$ at the midpoint $x=4$. These symmetric gaps follow from projecting a quadratic mean over a predictor distribution that is symmetric around $4$: least squares balances positive interior gaps against negative endpoint gaps instead of eliminating misspecification point by point.

    Reading a Coefficient Correctly

    In the multiple regression $E[Y\mid X]\approx\beta_0+\beta_1X_1+\cdots+\beta_pX_p$, $\beta_j$ is the change in the linear projection associated with a one-unit increase in $X_j$ while the other included coordinates are fixed. Frisch-Waugh-Lovell sharpens “held fixed”: $\hat\beta_j$ is the slope between residualized $Y$ and the part of $X_j$ left after projecting both on the other columns. This is a conditional association, not automatically a feasible intervention or causal effect.

    Under this framing, a fitted slope $\hat{\beta}_1$ estimates the slope of the best linear approximation to $E[Y\mid X]$. Two additional conditions have to hold for that number to carry a stronger meaning:

    • Correct functional form. If the true $\mu(x)$ is linear, or has been made linear through the transformations covered in Model Specification, then $\hat{\beta}_1$ estimates the actual slope of $\mu(x)$, not merely its best linear summary.
    • Exogeneity. $E[\varepsilon \mid X] = 0$ is required for $\hat{\beta}_1$ to be interpreted as the effect of changing $X$ while everything else about the data-generating process is left alone. Association and causation coincide only under this and related identification assumptions, which linear regression itself does not test or guarantee.
    Interview trap. A common question asks whether a positive, statistically significant coefficient on $X$ shows that $X$ causes $Y$ to increase. It does not, on its own. The coefficient is a feature of the conditional mean of $Y$ given $X$ (and any other included predictors) in the observed data. It supports a causal reading only alongside an argument that there are no omitted variables correlated with both $X$ and $Y$, that $X$ was not itself shaped by $Y$ in reverse, and that the functional form is not masking the true relationship. Regression output alone cannot certify any of these conditions.

    Population Function Versus Sample OLS Projection

    The population conditional mean $\mu(x)$ is fixed by the data-generating distribution. The population linear projection $\beta^*=\arg\min_b E[(Y-X^\top b)^2]$ is also fixed, but depends on the distribution of $X$. Sample OLS, $\hat\beta=\arg\min_b\sum_i(y_i-x_i^\top b)^2$, is random because the rows are random. Under representative sampling, finite second moments, full population rank, and suitable regularity, $\hat\beta\to\beta^*$. If the mean function is correctly linear and exogenous, then $\beta^*$ equals the structural $\beta$; otherwise it remains a distribution-weighted approximation.

    Omitted-Variable Bias: Formula and Numbers

    Worked numerical example. A two-predictor population model makes the contamination term visible without sampling noise.

    Suppose the population model is $Y=\beta_0+\beta_1X_1+\beta_2X_2+\varepsilon$ with $E[\varepsilon\mid X_1,X_2]=0$, but $X_2$ is omitted. If the auxiliary projection is $X_2=\delta_0+\delta_1X_1+v$, then

    $$\operatorname{plim}\hat\beta_1^{\text{short}}=\beta_1+\beta_2\delta_1.$$

    For $\beta_1=2$, $\beta_2=3$, and $\delta_1=0.5$, the short-regression slope converges to $2+3(0.5)=3.5$. The extra $1.5$ is not a sampling accident and cannot be removed by a larger sample or sandwich standard errors. A counterexample with $\delta_1=0$ has no slope bias even when $X_2$ strongly affects $Y$, because the omitted contribution is orthogonal to $X_1$.

    Prediction, Interpolation, and Extrapolation

    A point prediction $\hat y_0=x_0^\top\hat\beta$ estimates a conditional mean. A future observation additionally contains fresh noise, so its prediction interval is wider than a mean-response confidence interval. Interpolation places $x_0$ inside the supported joint feature region; extrapolation leaves that region. Coordinatewise ranges alone are insufficient in multiple regression: a combination such as high age with zero experience may be unsupported even when each coordinate separately lies inside its observed range.

    For the population line $5.6+0.6x$, predictions at $x=4$ and $x=7$ are $8.0$ and $9.8$. The former is central interpolation for the displayed support $1$ through $7$; $x=10$ gives $11.6$ by algebra but has no support in the example. Polynomial fits make this edge case especially severe because powers dominate rapidly beyond the fitted range.

    Edge Cases Worth Naming Directly

    SituationWhat actually happens
    $X$ has zero variance in the sample (constant column)$\text{Var}(X)=0$, so the slope formula divides by zero; the design matrix loses full column rank and $\hat{\beta}$ is not identified for that column.
    Prediction at an $x$ far outside the observed rangeThe fitted line is extrapolated with no data supporting it there; the best-linear-predictor guarantee is a statement about the observed distribution of $X$, not about values that were never seen.
    $\mu(x)$ is exactly linear but errors are heteroscedastic$\hat{\beta}$ is still unbiased for the true slope; only the standard errors used for inference are affected, a topic taken up in Gauss-Markov and Inference.
    $X$ is measured with errorThe estimand shifts: $\hat{\beta}$ targets the best linear predictor of $Y$ given the observed, noisy $X$, which is generally attenuated (biased toward zero) relative to the slope with respect to the true, error-free $X$.

    Assumptions, Counterexamples, and Implementation Link

    The projection interpretation needs finite second moments and a nonsingular predictor covariance matrix. Causal interpretation needs a separate identification argument, including consistency, exchangeability or a valid design, positivity, and a defensible treatment definition. Simpson's paradox supplies a counterexample: an aggregate positive slope can become negative within every relevant group when group membership is omitted. Measurement error in $X$, selection on the outcome, and range restriction each change the estimand.
