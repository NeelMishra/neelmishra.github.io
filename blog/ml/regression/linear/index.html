    Linear Regression: Mastery Roadmap

    July 2026 · 12 min read

    Linear regression models a continuous outcome $y$ as a linear function of predictors plus an unobserved error term. For $n$ observations and $p$ predictors, the model is written in matrix form as

    $$y = X\beta + \varepsilon,$$

    where $y$ is an $n\times 1$ vector of outcomes, $X$ is an $n\times(p+1)$ design matrix whose first column is a column of ones for the intercept, $\beta$ is a $(p+1)\times 1$ vector of unknown parameters, and $\varepsilon$ is an $n\times 1$ vector of unobserved errors. This notation is used consistently across every page in this track, so it is worth fixing precisely here before any estimation or inference is discussed.

    SymbolMeaning
    $n$number of observations (rows of $X$)
    $p$number of predictors, excluding the intercept
    $X$$n\times(p+1)$ design matrix, first column all ones
    $\beta$population parameter vector, unknown and fixed
    $\hat{\beta}$sample estimate of $\beta$ computed from data
    $\hat{y} = X\hat{\beta}$fitted values
    $e = y - \hat{y}$residuals, the observable stand-in for the unobservable $\varepsilon$

    The Estimand-to-Diagnostics Lifecycle

    The first decision is the estimand: a population conditional mean, a best linear projection, a causal contrast under an identification design, or a prediction target over a stated deployment distribution. The chosen feature map forms $X$, and the estimate $\hat{\beta}$ is then chosen to minimize the residual sum of squares,

    $$\text{RSS}(\beta) = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \beta)^2 = (y-X\beta)^\top(y-X\beta),$$

    where $x_i^\top$ is row $i$ of $X$. The lifecycle does not end at a coefficient vector. Fitted values and residuals reveal mean-form and variance failures; leverage and influence reveal fragile rows; uncertainty formulas state the inferential target; held-out error checks prediction; and each failure loops back to the estimand, feature map, sampling design, or solver.

    Expanding the objective gives $y^\top y-2\beta^\top X^\top y+\beta^\top X^\top X\beta$. Its gradient is $-2X^\top y+2X^\top X\beta$, so a full-rank optimum satisfies $X^\top X\hat\beta=X^\top y$ and $\hat\beta=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y$. Stable implementations solve the system without forming the inverse.

    Statistical and Solver Layers

    The statistical layer defines $Y$, the rows eligible for analysis, the feature map, assumptions, target population, loss, and uncertainty claim. The solver layer computes the same minimizer using QR, SVD, Cholesky on well-conditioned systems, or iterative methods. A stable solver cannot repair leakage, confounding, extrapolation, or a missing nonlinear term. Conversely, a sound statistical model can still fail in software if direct inversion magnifies roundoff in a poorly conditioned design.

    Assumptions, Counterexamples, and Edge Cases

    • Full rank: unique coefficients require linearly independent columns. Duplicate columns produce many coefficient vectors with the same fitted values.
    • Exogeneity: $E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0$ is required for the stated coefficient target. An omitted confounder biases the point estimate, so robust standard errors do not solve the problem.
    • Variance and dependence: homoscedastic independent errors justify the textbook covariance formula. Sandwich or dependence-aware covariance estimators address some failures without changing OLS coefficients.
    • Support: interpolation stays within observed feature support; extrapolation relies on untested functional form. A high in-sample $R^2$ offers no protection outside that support.

    A Calculated Linear-Fit Lifecycle

    Every fitted linear model can be evaluated by comparing it against the simplest possible baseline: predicting the sample mean $\bar{y}$ for every observation, ignoring $X$ entirely. The total variation in $y$ around that baseline is the total sum of squares,

    $$\text{SST} = \sum_i (y_i - \bar{y})^2.$$

    Fitting $X\beta$ decomposes that total variation into a part explained by the model and a part left over as residual error,

    $$\text{SST} = \text{SSR} + \text{SSE}, \qquad \text{SSR}=\sum_i(\hat{y}_i-\bar{y})^2, \qquad \text{SSE}=\sum_i(y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2,$$

    and the coefficient of determination is the fraction of total variation the model explains, $R^2 = \text{SSR}/\text{SST}$. In simple regression with a single predictor, this decomposition has a compact numerical check: $R^2$ equals the squared sample correlation $r^2$ between $x$ and $y$, since both quantities measure the same linear association through different formulas.

    All displayed values are calculated from $x=(1,\ldots,6)$ and $y=(2.2,3.8,5.1,5.6,7.4,8.3)$. Each step advances one analysis stage and keeps the fitted and residual geometry visible.

    The data behind the animation is the six points $x=(1,\ldots,6)$ and $y=(2.2,3.8,5.1,5.6,7.4,8.3)$, which give $\text{SST}=25.34$, $\text{SSE}=0.379$, $\text{SSR}=24.96$, and $R^2=0.985$. The sample correlation on the same data is $r=0.9925$, and $r^2=0.985$ to three decimal places, matching $R^2$ exactly. This equivalence is specific to simple regression with one predictor; with multiple predictors, $R^2$ still measures explained variance but no longer equals the square of a single pairwise correlation.

    Interview trap. A high $R^2$ is sometimes read as proof that the model is correctly specified. $R^2$ only measures the fraction of variance explained by whatever functional form was fit; a linear model can achieve a high $R^2$ on data with genuine curvature if the curvature is mild relative to the noise, while still producing systematically biased predictions at the ends of the observed range. $R^2$ says nothing about whether the errors are unbiased, homoscedastic, or independent, which is exactly why residual diagnostics exist as a separate check.

    Worked check. Here $\bar x=3.5$, $\bar y=5.4$, $S_{xx}=17.5$, and $S_{xy}=20.9$, so $\hat\beta_1=1.1943$ and $\hat\beta_0=1.2200$. The residual degrees of freedom are $6-2=4$, $\hat\sigma=\sqrt{0.3794/4}=0.3080$, and $\operatorname{se}(\hat\beta_1)=\hat\sigma/\sqrt{S_{xx}}=0.0736$. A zero-variance predictor, a duplicated column, or $n\le p+1$ blocks the usual unique fit or residual variance estimate.
