    Linear Regression: Overview and Roadmap

    July 2026 · 15 min read

    Linear regression estimates a continuous number, such as price, demand, or temperature, from one or more predictors. It gives each predictor a coefficient, adds an intercept, and leaves a row-specific error for variation the model does not explain:

    $$y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1x_{i1}+\cdots+\beta_px_{ip}+\varepsilon_i.$$

    Writing the same equation for all $n$ rows gives the compact matrix form

    $$y = X\beta + \varepsilon,$$

    where $X\beta$ is the part explained by the model and $\varepsilon$ is the unexplained part. The first column of $X$ contains ones, so its coefficient becomes the intercept $\beta_0$. The word linear means linear in the coefficients. The columns of $X$ may still contain squared terms, interactions, or other prepared features.

    SymbolMeaning
    $n$number of data rows
    $p$number of predictors, not counting the intercept
    $X$all model inputs, arranged as $n$ rows and $p+1$ columns; the first column contains ones
    $\beta$unknown true coefficients, including the intercept
    $\hat{\beta}$coefficients estimated from the available sample
    $\varepsilon = y-X\beta$unobservable differences from the true population regression function
    $\hat{y} = X\hat{\beta}$predictions made by the fitted model
    $e = y-X\hat{\beta}$observable differences between the data and the fitted predictions
    Intercept, error, and residual are different quantities. The intercept $\beta_0$ shifts the whole fitted line or plane. The error $\varepsilon_i$ is the unknown gap between row $i$ and the true population relationship. The residual $e_i$ is the known gap between row $i$ and the fitted relationship. The ones column creates the intercept; it does not replace the error term.

    From the Modeling Question to Diagnostics

    Before fitting a model, the target of the analysis must be clear. Statistics calls this target the estimand. It may be the average future outcome, the best linear summary of a relationship, or a causal effect supported by a suitable study design. The remaining stages turn that target into a fitted model and check whether the result is trustworthy.

    1. Choosing the target. A prediction problem usually targets the average future value $E[Y\mid X=x]$. A descriptive analysis may need only the best linear summary of a relationship. A causal coefficient needs additional design and identification assumptions. These goals are different even when they use the same regression equation.
    2. Writing the population model. In $Y=X\beta+\varepsilon$, the term $X\beta$ is the relationship the model tries to capture. The error $\varepsilon$ contains everything left over. The condition $E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0$ means that, after accounting for $X$, the leftover error has no remaining average pattern.
    3. Building $X$. Each observation becomes one row. The first value is $1$ for the intercept, followed by the predictors and any justified transformations or interactions. This choice determines what shapes the model can represent. A solver cannot recover a pattern that was never included in $X$.
    4. Fitting the coefficients. Ordinary least squares chooses the coefficient vector that makes the squared sample errors as small as possible: $$\hat\beta=\arg\min_b\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-x_i^\top b)^2 =\arg\min_b (y-Xb)^\top(y-Xb).$$ Differentiation gives $X^\top X\hat\beta=X^\top y$. Independent columns give a unique solution. QR or SVD is usually safer than explicitly forming an inverse.
    5. Computing predictions and residuals. Predictions are $\hat y=X\hat\beta$. Residuals are $e=y-\hat y$ and can be calculated from the data. The true errors remain unknown because the true coefficients are unknown. With an intercept, OLS residuals sum to zero and satisfy $X^\top e=0$.
    6. Checking prediction on new data. Training error shows how closely the model fits known rows. Held-out RMSE, MAE, and $R^2$ show how well it works on unseen rows. A large gap between training and test performance suggests overfitting, leakage, instability, or distribution shift.
    7. Looking for structural problems. Residual plots can reveal curvature or changing error spread. Leverage finds unusual predictor rows. Cook's distance finds rows that strongly move the fit. Condition numbers and variance inflation factors reveal unstable coefficients. A model can score well overall and still fail these checks.
    8. Reporting only supported conclusions. Standard errors, confidence intervals, and hypothesis tests depend on assumptions about the errors, sampling, and design matrix. Failed checks may require different features, robust standard errors, regularization, a robust model, or a narrower interpretation of the coefficients.

    The process is a loop rather than a pipeline ending at $\hat\beta$: choose the target, build $X$, fit, test, inspect the residuals, verify the assumptions, and revise the model when a check fails.

    The Model Choice and the Numerical Solver

    Two separate questions are involved. The first asks what the model should mean: which rows, target, predictors, assumptions, and population are relevant. The second asks how the coefficients should be computed: QR, SVD, Cholesky for a well-conditioned problem, or an iterative method. A precise solver cannot fix leakage, confounding, missing features, or unsafe extrapolation. A sensible model can still produce bad numbers when an unstable solver is used on a poorly conditioned matrix.

    What Must Be Checked

    • Independent columns: duplicate or perfectly dependent columns prevent unique coefficients.
    • No leftover average error pattern: $E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0$ is needed for the usual coefficient interpretation. Robust standard errors cannot repair an omitted confounder.
    • Error spread and dependence: the textbook standard-error formula assumes constant variance and independent errors. Other covariance estimators are needed when those assumptions fail.
    • Observed range: interpolation stays inside the data range. Extrapolation trusts the fitted shape where no observations support it, and a high training $R^2$ does not make that safe.

    A Small Worked Fit

    The simplest baseline predicts the sample mean $\bar y$ for every row. The total amount by which the observations vary around that baseline is

    $$\text{SST} = \sum_i (y_i - \bar{y})^2.$$

    After fitting a line, this total splits into variation explained by the model and variation left in the residuals:

    $$\text{SST} = \text{SSR} + \text{SSE}, \qquad \text{SSR}=\sum_i(\hat{y}_i-\bar{y})^2, \qquad \text{SSE}=\sum_i(y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2,$$

    Therefore $R^2=\text{SSR}/\text{SST}$ is the fraction explained by the line. With one predictor, $R^2$ also equals the squared sample correlation $r^2$ between $x$ and $y$.

    The data behind the animation is the six points $x=(1,\ldots,6)$ and $y=(2.2,3.8,5.1,5.6,7.4,8.3)$, which give $\text{SST}=25.34$, $\text{SSE}=0.379$, $\text{SSR}=24.96$, and $R^2=0.985$. The sample correlation on the same data is $r=0.9925$, and $r^2=0.985$ to three decimal places, matching $R^2$ exactly. This equivalence is specific to simple regression with one predictor; with multiple predictors, $R^2$ still measures explained variance but no longer equals the square of a single pairwise correlation.

    Interview trap. A high $R^2$ is sometimes read as proof that the model is correctly specified. $R^2$ only measures the fraction of variance explained by whatever functional form was fit; a linear model can achieve a high $R^2$ on data with genuine curvature if the curvature is mild relative to the noise, while still producing systematically biased predictions at the ends of the observed range. $R^2$ says nothing about whether the errors are unbiased, homoscedastic, or independent, which is exactly why residual diagnostics exist as a separate check.

    Worked check. Here $\bar x=3.5$, $\bar y=5.4$, $S_{xx}=17.5$, and $S_{xy}=20.9$, so $\hat\beta_1=1.1943$ and $\hat\beta_0=1.2200$. The residual degrees of freedom are $6-2=4$, $\hat\sigma=\sqrt{0.3794/4}=0.3080$, and $\operatorname{se}(\hat\beta_1)=\hat\sigma/\sqrt{S_{xx}}=0.0736$. A zero-variance predictor, a duplicated column, or $n\le p+1$ blocks the usual unique fit or residual variance estimate.
