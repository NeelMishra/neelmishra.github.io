    Gauss-Markov, Standard Errors, Tests, and Intervals

    July 2026 · 16 min read

    $\hat\beta$ is one number computed from one sample. A different sample from the same population produces a different $\hat\beta$. This part is about that second layer of variation: the classical assumptions under which $\hat\beta$ is the best possible linear unbiased estimator, the formula for how much $\hat\beta$ varies from sample to sample, and how that variation is used to build hypothesis tests and confidence intervals.

    The Classical Linear Model Assumptions

    AssumptionStatement
    Linearity$y = X\beta+\varepsilon$, correct in the parameters $\beta$ (the predictors themselves can be transformed nonlinearly beforehand).
    Full rank$X$ has full column rank, so $X^\top X$ is invertible and $\hat\beta$ is uniquely identified.
    Exogeneity$E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0$: the error has zero mean at every value of $X$, so nothing in $X$ carries information that predicts the sign of the error.
    Homoscedasticity, no autocorrelation$\text{Var}(\varepsilon\mid X)=\sigma^2 I$: constant error variance across observations, and errors uncorrelated with each other.

    Normality of $\varepsilon$ is deliberately absent from this list. It plays a role later in this page, but it is not part of what is required for the next theorem.

    The Gauss-Markov Theorem

    Under the four assumptions above, the OLS estimator is BLUE: the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator. "Best" here means minimum variance among every estimator that is linear in $y$ and unbiased for $\beta$.

    The proof is a short exercise in matrix algebra. Any linear estimator can be written $\tilde\beta = Ay$ for some matrix $A$. Substituting $y=X\beta+\varepsilon$ gives $\tilde\beta = AX\beta + A\varepsilon$, and unbiasedness, $E[\tilde\beta\mid X]=\beta$ for every possible $\beta$, forces $AX=I$. Writing $A = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top + D$ for some matrix $D$, the constraint $AX=I$ becomes $DX=0$, since $(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top X=I$ already. The variance of $\tilde\beta$ is then

    $$\text{Var}(\tilde\beta\mid X) = \sigma^2 AA^\top = \sigma^2\Big[(X^\top X)^{-1} + DD^\top\Big],$$

    because the cross term vanishes: $(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top D^\top = (X^\top X)^{-1}(DX)^\top = 0$, using $DX=0$. Since $DD^\top$ is positive semidefinite for any $D$, every term added by choosing an estimator other than OLS ($D\ne 0$) can only increase variance, never decrease it. Setting $D=0$ recovers OLS itself, and no other choice of $D$ can do better. This is exactly the BLUE property, and it holds without any assumption of normality.

    Variance of $\hat\beta$ and Standard Errors

    Setting $D=0$ in the derivation above gives the variance of the OLS estimator directly,

    $$\text{Var}(\hat\beta\mid X) = \sigma^2 (X^\top X)^{-1}.$$

    $\sigma^2$ itself is unknown and is estimated from the residuals as

    $$\hat\sigma^2 = \frac{\text{RSS}}{n-p-1} = \frac{\sum_i e_i^2}{n-p-1},$$

    where the divisor $n-p-1$ rather than $n$ makes $\hat\sigma^2$ unbiased; the $p+1$ estimated coefficients use up $p+1$ degrees of freedom, since the residual vector is constrained to lie in the $(n-p-1)$-dimensional orthogonal complement of $\text{col}(X)$. The standard error of coefficient $j$ is

    $$\text{se}(\hat\beta_j) = \hat\sigma\sqrt{\big[(X^\top X)^{-1}\big]_{jj}}.$$

    Worked numerical example. The repeated samples below calculate every slope, residual variance, standard error, and interval from five displayed outcomes.

    Homoskedastic Versus Sandwich Covariance

    When $\operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon\mid X)=\Omega$ is not $\sigma^2I$, the exact conditional covariance is

    $$\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta\mid X)=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega X(X^\top X)^{-1}.$$

    The homoskedastic estimator substitutes $\hat\sigma^2I$, producing $\hat\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}$. The HC0 sandwich estimator instead substitutes $\widehat\Omega=\operatorname{diag}(e_1^2,\ldots,e_n^2)$; HC1 multiplies the middle matrix by $n/(n-p-1)$, while HC2 and HC3 use leverage corrections. These estimators change uncertainty, not $\hat\beta$. They are asymptotically consistent under heteroscedasticity, but the usual small-sample $t$ reference is no longer exact.

    ▶ Repeated Samples: Unbiasedness and Confidence Interval Coverage

    A fixed population line $y=1+2x+\varepsilon$ with $x=(1,\ldots,5)$ is sampled six times, each time with a different noise draw. Each sample produces its own $\hat\beta_1$ and its own $95\%$ confidence interval for the true slope, $2.0$. Stepping through reveals the estimates and shows whether each interval happens to contain the true value.

    Every interval in the animation is recomputed from its displayed five response values using $S_{xx}=10$, $\hat\beta_1=S_{xy}/S_{xx}$, $\operatorname{se}(\hat\beta_1)=\sqrt{\text{RSS}/(5-2)}/\sqrt{S_{xx}}$, and $t_{0.975,3}=3.182446$. The six slopes average $2.0433$. Five intervals contain $2$; sample 3 has $\hat\beta_1=2.3800$, $\operatorname{se}=0.0412$, and interval $(2.2488,2.5112)$. Six replicates illustrate rather than establish unbiasedness or nominal coverage.

    Interview trap. "There is a $95\%$ probability that the true $\beta_1$ lies in this interval" is a common but backwards description of a confidence interval. In the classical frequentist framing, $\beta_1$ is a fixed, unknown constant, not a random variable; it is either inside a given, already-computed interval or it is not, with no probability left to talk about once the data are in hand. The $95\%$ figure describes the procedure: across many hypothetical repeated samples, about $95\%$ of the resulting intervals would contain the true value. The animation's sample $3$ is a concrete instance of one interval that missed, which the "$95\%$ probability for this interval" reading has no room for.

    Hypothesis Tests and Confidence Intervals

    To test $H_0:\beta_j=\beta_{j0}$ against a two-sided alternative, the test statistic is

    $$t = \frac{\hat\beta_j-\beta_{j0}}{\text{se}(\hat\beta_j)},$$

    which, under $H_0$ together with normal errors, follows a $t$-distribution with $n-p-1$ degrees of freedom. The most common test sets $\beta_{j0}=0$, checking whether predictor $j$ has any linear association with $y$ once the other predictors are held fixed. A $(1-\alpha)$ confidence interval for $\beta_j$ inverts this test,

    $$\hat\beta_j \pm t_{\alpha/2,\,n-p-1}\cdot\text{se}(\hat\beta_j).$$

    For $q$ joint restrictions $H_0:R\beta=r$, the classical Wald form is

    $$F=\frac{(R\hat\beta-r)^\top[R\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta)R^\top]^{-1}(R\hat\beta-r)}{q},$$

    and under homoskedastic normal errors it follows $F_{q,n-p-1}$ exactly. For nested least-squares models the equivalent form is $F=[(\text{RSS}_R-\text{RSS}_U)/q]/[\text{RSS}_U/(n-p-1)]$. With robust covariance, the Wald statistic is asymptotically $\chi_q^2$ after multiplying by $q$, with finite-sample corrections depending on the implementation.

    Mean Response Versus Prediction Interval

    At feature row $x_0$, a confidence interval for the conditional mean is

    $$x_0^\top\hat\beta\ \pm\ t_{\alpha/2,\nu}\hat\sigma\sqrt{x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0},$$

    whereas a prediction interval for one new response includes fresh outcome noise,

    $$x_0^\top\hat\beta\ \pm\ t_{\alpha/2,\nu}\hat\sigma\sqrt{1+x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0}.$$

    For $x=(1,\ldots,6)$ and $y=(2.2,3.8,5.1,5.6,7.4,8.3)$, the fitted value at $x_0=\bar x=3.5$ is $5.400$. Here $\text{RSS}=0.37943$, $\nu=4$, $\hat\sigma=0.30799$, $x_0^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_0=1/6$, and $t_{0.975,4}=2.77645$. The $95\%$ mean-response interval is $(5.051,5.749)$; the new-observation prediction interval is $(4.476,6.324)$. The second is wider because it includes irreducible individual noise.

    What Normality Actually Buys

    Normality of $\varepsilon$ is not needed for $\hat\beta$ to be unbiased, and it is not needed for the Gauss-Markov theorem, which only used linearity of the estimator, unbiasedness, and the variance formula. Normality is needed for the $t$ and $F$ statistics above to follow their stated distributions exactly in finite samples. Without normality, $\hat\beta$ is still unbiased and still has the variance formula derived above, and for large $n$ the central limit theorem makes $\hat\beta$ approximately normal regardless of the distribution of $\varepsilon$, so $t$-tests and confidence intervals remain approximately valid asymptotically. The practical consequence is that normality matters most for small samples and matters much less as $n$ grows.

    When the Assumptions Break

    • Heteroscedasticity (violating constant $\sigma^2$) leaves $\hat\beta$ unbiased but breaks the BLUE guarantee: a weighted or generalized least squares estimator becomes lower-variance, and the ordinary standard error formula above is simply wrong, understating or overstating uncertainty depending on the pattern of variance. Heteroscedasticity-consistent (robust, "sandwich") standard errors correct the standard error formula without changing $\hat\beta$ itself.
    • Autocorrelated errors (violating the "no autocorrelation" part of the same assumption) have the same effect: $\hat\beta$ stays unbiased, but the usual standard errors are wrong, typically understated when errors are positively autocorrelated, which is common in time-ordered data.
    • Endogeneity (violating $E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0$) is the most serious failure: $\hat\beta$ itself becomes biased and inconsistent, no amount of correcting the standard error formula fixes a biased point estimate, and this typically requires a different identification strategy such as instrumental variables, which lies outside ordinary least squares entirely.
    Interview trap, counterexample, and edge case. Robust standard errors do not make a biased estimator unbiased, do not repair serial dependence unless the covariance estimator models it, and do not turn a confidence interval for a mean response into a prediction interval. Small samples with high leverage can make HC0 unreliable; HC3 or a design-specific method may be preferable. A saturated fit with $n=p+1$ can have zero RSS yet leaves $\hat\sigma^2$ undefined because the residual degrees of freedom are zero.
