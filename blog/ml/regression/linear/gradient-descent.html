    Gradient Descent for Linear Regression

    July 2026 · 12 min read

    QR and SVD are excellent for moderate dense problems. Gradient descent becomes useful when $X$ is very large, sparse, or updated over time. For the normalized least-squares objective,

    $$J(\beta)=\frac{1}{2n}\|X\beta-y\|_2^2,\qquad \nabla J(\beta)=\frac1nX^\top(X\beta-y),\qquad \nabla^2J=\frac1nX^\top X.$$

    Batch gradient descent repeatedly moves opposite the gradient:

    $$\beta_{t+1}=\beta_t-\eta\nabla J(\beta_t).$$

    The objective is a convex quadratic, so there are no bad local minima. Slow convergence comes from poor scaling and uneven curvature.

    Choosing a Stable Learning Rate

    Let $L$ and $\mu$ be the largest and smallest eigenvalues of $X^\top X/n$. A constant learning rate converges when $0<\eta<2/L$. Along each eigenvector, the remaining coefficient error is multiplied by

    $$v_j^\top(\beta_{t+1}-\hat\beta)=(1-\eta\lambda_j)v_j^\top(\beta_t-\hat\beta).$$

    The condition number $\kappa=L/\mu$ controls how uneven the curvature is. A large value creates a long narrow valley, causing zigzagging and slow progress. Standardizing the features often makes the valley rounder and training faster.

    Normalization trap. The objectives $\|e\|^2$, $\|e\|^2/n$, and $\|e\|^2/(2n)$ have gradients with different scales. The same numeric learning rate may converge under one convention and diverge under another.

    Batch, Mini-Batch, SGD, and Online Updates

    VariantGradient estimateBehaviorProduction implication
    BatchAll $n$ rowsSmooth and deterministicEach update scans the full dataset
    Mini-batchA subset of rowsNoisier but efficient on modern hardwareSeed and batch order affect reproducibility
    SGDOne rowVery noisy updatesUseful for streams but sensitive to outliers
    OnlineEach new row onceTracks a changing processNeeds an explicit forgetting and drift policy

    Mini-batches should represent the training objective. Time ordering or clustered batches can make gradient noise much larger than expected.

    Learning-Rate Schedules and Momentum

    A fixed rate is easy to audit. A decaying rate moves quickly at first and takes smaller steps later. Common schedules include step decay, exponential decay, and inverse-time decay. With noisy SGD, a constant rate usually keeps moving around the optimum rather than settling exactly on it.

    Momentum stores part of the previous update:

    $$v_{t+1}=m v_t-\eta\nabla J(\beta_t),\qquad \beta_{t+1}=\beta_t+v_{t+1}.$$

    With $m=0$, this is ordinary gradient descent. Positive momentum builds speed in a consistent direction and reduces some back-and-forth movement. Nesterov momentum evaluates the gradient at a look-ahead point:

    $$v_{t+1}=m v_t-\eta\nabla J(\beta_t+m v_t),\qquad \beta_{t+1}=\beta_t+v_{t+1},$$

    Momentum adds state, so checkpoints must save both $\beta_t$ and $v_t$. Restarting with only $\beta_t$ changes the trajectory.

    Trap. Momentum can amplify an unstable learning rate. Settings tuned on standardized features may diverge on raw features with very different scales.

    AdaGrad, RMSProp, and Adam rescale coordinates using past gradients. They are useful when curvature differs sharply across parameters, but a well-scaled linear-regression objective often needs only a simple, auditable schedule.

    Stopping, Divergence, and Validation

    Stopping should combine loss improvement, gradient norm, coefficient movement, a maximum iteration count, and finite-value checks. Validation loss may stop improving before training loss reaches its minimum.

    A flat loss can still hide large coefficient movement along a weak direction, so both objective and parameter stability matter. Gradient clipping can limit a dangerous step but may also hide poor scaling.

    Important edge cases include constant columns, an incorrectly standardized intercept, stale online scaling, malformed sparse indices, and a final mini-batch normalized with the wrong size. Reproducibility needs the seed, batch size, schedule, stopping rule, feature order, and scaler values.
