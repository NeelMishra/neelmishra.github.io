    Gradient Descent for Linear Regression

    July 2026 · 12 min read

    Direct factorizations solve moderate dense least-squares problems accurately, but iterative optimization becomes attractive when observations arrive continuously, $X$ is too large to factor, or sparse matrix-vector products are cheap. For the normalized objective

    $$J(\beta)=\frac{1}{2n}\|X\beta-y\|_2^2,\qquad \nabla J(\beta)=\frac1nX^\top(X\beta-y),\qquad \nabla^2J=\frac1nX^\top X.$$

    Batch gradient descent applies $\beta_{t+1}=\beta_t-\eta\nabla J(\beta_t)$. This quadratic objective has no local minima distinct from the global minimum. Optimization difficulty comes from curvature, not nonconvexity.

    Learning Rate and Spectral Convergence

    For a full-rank design, $L=\lambda_{\max}(X^\top X/n)$ and $\mu=\lambda_{\min}(X^\top X/n)$ denote the largest and smallest eigenvalues. A constant learning rate converges for $0<\eta<2/L$. Along eigenvector $v_j$, error evolves as

    $$v_j^\top(\beta_{t+1}-\hat\beta)=(1-\eta\lambda_j)v_j^\top(\beta_t-\hat\beta).$$

    The best fixed rate for a strongly convex quadratic is $2/(L+\mu)$, with worst-direction contraction $(\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1)$. Poor feature scaling creates a large $\kappa=L/\mu$, producing a narrow valley and slow zigzagging. Standardization is therefore an optimization transformation as well as a coefficient-unit transformation.

    Normalization trap. Implementations alternate among $\|e\|^2$, $\|e\|^2/n$, and $\|e\|^2/(2n)$. Their gradients differ by factors of $2$ and $n$. A learning rate copied across conventions can turn a convergent update into divergence.

    Batch, Mini-Batch, SGD, and Online Updates

    For unbiased mini-batch gradients, rows must be sampled consistently with the empirical objective. Time-ordered streams violate independent shuffling assumptions, and clustered batches can inflate gradient variance.

    Learning-Rate Schedules and Momentum

    A constant rate below $2/L$ converges. At the best fixed rate, its worst-direction contraction remains $(\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1)$ at every step. Decaying schedules trade early speed for later stability. Step decay halves $\eta$ every fixed number of epochs; exponential decay sets $\eta_t=\eta_0\gamma^t$ for $\gamma<1$; inverse-time decay sets $\eta_t=\eta_0/(1+\kappa_0 t)$. SGD's classical almost-sure convergence condition under sampling noise needs $\sum_t\eta_t=\infty$ and $\sum_t\eta_t^2<\infty$. A $1/t$ schedule satisfies both conditions, while $1/\sqrt t$ fails the second and exponential decay fails the first. A constant SGD rate generally reaches only a noise-floor neighborhood of the optimum rather than $\hat\beta$ itself.

    Momentum reshapes the update into a two-line recurrence rather than a single gradient step:

    $$v_{t+1}=m v_t-\eta\nabla J(\beta_t),\qquad \beta_{t+1}=\beta_t+v_{t+1}.$$

    Setting $m=0$ recovers plain gradient descent. For $m\in(0,1)$, momentum accumulates a running direction, which accelerates along the persistent shallow eigendirection responsible for most of the slow convergence while partly canceling oscillation along steep, rapidly reversing directions. Nesterov's variant evaluates the gradient at the momentum-shifted point instead of the current iterate,

    $$v_{t+1}=m v_t-\eta\nabla J(\beta_t+m v_t),\qquad \beta_{t+1}=\beta_t+v_{t+1},$$

    which corrects the direction using where momentum is about to carry the iterate rather than where it currently sits, typically tightening the worst-case contraction rate for smooth convex quadratics. Momentum and Nesterov both introduce a second recurrence state, so reproducibility and checkpointing must persist $v_t$ alongside $\beta_t$; restarting from $\beta_t$ alone silently resets momentum to zero and changes the trajectory.

    Trap. Momentum amplifies a mis-scaled gradient the same way a larger learning rate would. A rate and momentum pair tuned on standardized features can diverge immediately if applied to raw, differently scaled columns, because $L$ and $\mu$ shift with the feature scale even though the recurrence coefficients did not change.

    Adaptive methods such as AdaGrad, RMSProp, and Adam rescale each coordinate by a running estimate of its gradient magnitude, which alters the implicit geometry of the descent path. For a convex quadratic they need not follow the same trajectory as plain or momentum gradient descent and, without additional decay of the adaptive term, need not converge to the exact finite-sample $\hat\beta$ either; they remain most useful when curvature varies sharply and unpredictably across coordinates rather than for a well-scaled linear-regression objective, where a tuned constant or scheduled rate is usually sufficient and easier to audit.

    For centered columns $x_1=(-2,-1,0,1,2)$ and $x_2=(-1,-1,0,1,1)$, the objective Hessian is $A=X^\top X/n=\begin{bmatrix}2&1.2\\1.2&0.8\end{bmatrix}$, giving $J(\beta)=\tfrac12(\beta-\beta^\star)^\top A(\beta-\beta^\star)$ with $\beta^\star=(2,-1)$. Every ellipse, eigenvector angle, and iterate below is computed from $A$ at runtime, not drawn by hand. The correlated columns make $A$ far from diagonal, so its contours are tilted, elongated ellipses rather than circles, and constant-rate descent zigzags across the narrow axis while creeping along the wide one.

    Stopping, Divergence, and Validation

    A single stopping test is brittle. Reliable training combines relative objective improvement, gradient norm, parameter movement, a maximum iteration count, and finite-value checks. Validation loss can stop earlier than training convergence when prediction rather than empirical optimization is the goal. For exact least squares, a factorization on a manageable audit sample provides a useful optimizer oracle.

    Counterexample. Training loss can decrease while validation loss rises because repeated passes fit sample noise. Conversely, a nearly flat loss can conceal large coefficient motion along a weakly identified direction. Both loss and parameter stability matter. Gradient clipping limits individual updates but can hide a scaling defect or change the optimum when clipping remains active.

    Edge cases include zero-variance columns, an accidentally penalized or standardized intercept, integer accumulation overflow, sparse rows with inconsistent feature indices, stale normalization statistics in online scoring, and a final mini-batch whose smaller size is normalized incorrectly. Reproducibility requires the shuffle seed, batch size, learning-rate schedule, stopping rule, feature order, and fitted scaling constants.
