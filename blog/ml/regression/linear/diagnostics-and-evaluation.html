    Residual Diagnostics, Influence, and Evaluation Metrics

    July 2026 · 13 min read

    A coefficient table and one aggregate score cannot establish model adequacy. Diagnostics ask whether the fitted mean function, variance model, dependence assumptions, and individual observations support the intended interpretation. Evaluation asks a different question: how well the complete training procedure predicts data generated under the deployment regime.

    Residual Structure

    Raw residuals are $e_i=y_i-\hat y_i$. A residual-versus-fitted plot should not be treated as a pass-fail picture. Curvature suggests a misspecified mean; a fan suggests heteroscedasticity; bands suggest omitted groups or rounding; temporal runs suggest dependence. A Q-Q plot assesses tail shape for inference, not whether the linear conditional mean is correct.

    Under homoscedastic OLS, $\operatorname{Var}(e_i\mid X)=\sigma^2(1-h_{ii})$, so raw residuals have unequal variance even when errors do not. Internally studentized residuals divide by $\hat\sigma\sqrt{1-h_{ii}}$. Externally studentized residuals use a variance estimate with observation $i$ deleted, reducing masking by the same point under investigation.

    Leverage Is Not Influence

    Leverage $h_{ii}=x_i^\top(X^\top X)^{-1}x_i$ depends only on predictors. The hat matrix satisfies $\sum_i h_{ii}=p$, where $p$ includes the intercept. A high-leverage point can lie exactly on the fitted surface and have little influence. Influence combines leverage with residual size. Cook's distance is

    $$D_i=\frac{e_i^2}{p\,\hat\sigma^2}\frac{h_{ii}}{(1-h_{ii})^2}.$$

    Rules such as $h_{ii}>2p/n$ or $D_i>4/n$ are screening heuristics, not deletion rules. A flagged point may be the most important valid observation in the dataset. Investigation begins with provenance, domain validity, and sensitivity analysis.

    Masking trap. Several influential observations can support one another, making their individual deletion diagnostics look mild. Group deletion, robust fits, and domain-based slices can reveal effects missed by one-row-at-a-time measures.

    Metrics Encode Different Costs

    MetricDefinitionStrengthFailure mode
    RMSE$\sqrt{n^{-1}\sum e_i^2}$Same units, emphasizes large errorsDominated by heavy tails
    MAE$n^{-1}\sum|e_i|$Robust summary, same unitsHides tail severity
    $R^2$$1-\text{SSE}/\text{SST}$Relative in-sample variance reductionCan rise mechanically with features
    Adjusted $R^2$$1-\frac{\text{SSE}/(n-p)}{\text{SST}/(n-1)}$Penalizes parameter countNot a substitute for validation
    PRESS$\sum(e_i/(1-h_{ii}))^2$Exact OLS leave-one-out residualsUnstable near leverage one

    $R^2$ can be negative out of sample, correctly indicating performance worse than the training-mean baseline. MAPE is undefined at zero targets and heavily weights small denominators. RMSLE requires compatible nonnegative support and changes the error geometry. A metric should reflect the operational cost, with subgroup and tail summaries beside its mean.

    Train versus Test $R^2$, Worked

    Because OLS minimizes training SSE directly, adding any column to the training design cannot increase train $R^2$; it is mechanically non-decreasing and reaches $1$ when a square $n$-parameter training design has full row rank. Test $R^2=1-\text{SSE}_{\text{test}}/\text{SST}_{\text{test}}$ carries no such guarantee and can fall or turn sharply negative as spurious columns are added. This example defines test SST against the fixed training-set mean, matching the baseline available at deployment; the common test-mean convention answers a slightly different, sample-relative question. A simulation with $n=12$ training rows, one true predictor, and up to ten additional pure-noise columns illustrates the split cleanly:

    Parameters (incl. intercept)Train $R^2$Test $R^2$

    Train $R^2$ climbs smoothly toward its ceiling of $1$ as parameters approach the twelve available rows, while test $R^2$ degrades once noise columns start absorbing sampling variation the model mistakes for signal, eventually falling far below zero once the fit interpolates the training rows exactly. The widening train-minus-test gap, not either number alone, is the standard operational signal of overfitting, and it is the quantity validation curves plot against model complexity.

    Validation Design and Statistical Tests

    Random train-test splits estimate interpolation performance only when rows are exchangeable. Time series require chronological splits; repeated entities require group splits; spatial data often require spatial blocks. Every preprocessing operation learned from data belongs inside the resampling loop. Confidence intervals for coefficients answer conditional sampling questions and do not quantify future prediction error under drift.

    Breusch-Pagan and White tests can detect variance structure, while Durbin-Watson and autocorrelation functions inspect serial dependence. Large samples make tiny deviations statistically significant, and small samples leave important deviations undetected. Plots, effect sizes, domain consequences, and robust standard errors remain necessary. Robust standard errors repair estimated uncertainty under heteroscedasticity but do not repair biased coefficients from a wrong mean function.

    Diagnostics under Robust and Regularized Fits

    Leverage $h_{ii}$, Cook's distance, and PRESS are all defined through the OLS hat matrix $H=X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top$, and none transfer unchanged to a different estimator. A Huber or LAD fit reweights or bounds residual contributions, so a weighted hat matrix from the final iteratively reweighted least-squares step provides only a local, algorithm-dependent leverage analogue; a point with high $h_{ii}$ under OLS may already have been downweighted before that diagnostic is computed. Ridge and lasso fits are biased by construction, so the classical inference formulas that assume an unbiased $\hat\beta$, including the standard-error and confidence-interval expressions used elsewhere in this series, do not directly apply; valid uncertainty statements for penalized fits require either a debiasing correction, a bootstrap of the entire fit-and-select procedure, or an explicitly conditional inference framework. Reporting an OLS-style $p$-value next to a lasso coefficient without one of these corrections overstates precision and can suggest significance driven mostly by the arbitrary tie-breaking discussed for correlated features elsewhere in this series.

    Edge Cases and Production Implications

    Edge cases include $n\le p$, leverage equal to one, zero SST for a constant target, duplicated rows, weighted fits whose effective sample size differs from row count, and transformed targets whose back-transformed mean needs a bias correction. Metric code needs explicit behavior for missing values, weights, zero denominators, and infinite predictions.

    Production monitoring should preserve a baseline distribution for residuals once labels arrive, track error by cohort and range, distinguish feature drift from calibration drift, and compare against a simple live baseline. Delayed labels create a blind period, so feature-range, leverage-like distance, missingness, and prediction-distribution monitors complement eventual error metrics.
