    Ridge, Lasso, and Elastic Net

    July 2026 · 15 min read

    Regularization exchanges some training fit for lower variance, improved conditioning, or sparsity. For centered $y$ and standardized predictor columns, a common elastic-net objective is

    $$\min_\beta\left\{\frac{1}{2n}\|y-X\beta\|_2^2+\lambda\left[\alpha\|\beta\|_1+\frac{1-\alpha}{2}\|\beta\|_2^2\right]\right\}.$$

    Ridge has $\alpha=0$, lasso has $\alpha=1$, and intermediate values combine both. The intercept is normally excluded from the penalty. Scaling is part of the definition because a penalty on a coefficient depends on that coefficient's unit.

    Geometric View: Balls versus Penalties

    The Lagrangian objective above has an equivalent constrained form, $\min_\beta\|y-X\beta\|_2^2$ subject to $\|\beta\|_2^2\le t$ for ridge or $\|\beta\|_1\le t$ for lasso, where $t$ shrinks as $\lambda$ grows. Unconstrained OLS contours are ellipses centered at $\hat\beta_{\text{OLS}}$; the regularized solution is the point where the smallest such ellipse first touches the constraint region's boundary. The $L_2$ ball is a smooth circle (or sphere), so a generic tangency point touches it away from any axis and every coordinate is typically nonzero. The $L_1$ ball is a diamond (a cross-polytope) with sharp corners sitting exactly on the coordinate axes. As the ellipse expands from $\hat\beta_{\text{OLS}}$, it tends to first meet the diamond at one of these corners, and a corner has one or more coordinates equal to zero by construction. This geometric picture, not a coincidence of the algebra, is why an $L_1$ penalty routinely zeroes out coefficients while an $L_2$ penalty only shrinks them.

    Ridge as Spectral Shrinkage

    For the convention $\|y-X\beta\|^2+\lambda\|\beta\|^2$, differentiating gives

    $$\hat\beta_{\text{ridge}}=(X^\top X+\lambda I)^{-1}X^\top y.$$

    If $X=U\Sigma V^\top$, then $\hat\beta_{\text{ridge}}=\sum_j \frac{\sigma_j}{\sigma_j^2+\lambda}(u_j^\top y)v_j$. Relative to OLS, each direction receives shrinkage factor $\sigma_j^2/(\sigma_j^2+\lambda)$. Weak directions shrink most, which stabilizes collinear designs. Ridge gives a unique solution for $\lambda>0$ even when $X$ is rank deficient.

    Under Gaussian errors, ridge is the MAP estimate from an isotropic zero-mean Gaussian prior. The prior interpretation depends on standardized coordinates and does not make the estimator unbiased. Frequentist expected bias is $-\lambda(X^\top X+\lambda I)^{-1}\beta$ under the same objective convention.

    Bias-variance consequence, worked. For one orthogonal-basis coordinate with unit signal scale, $\hat\beta_\lambda=\hat\beta_{\text{OLS}}/(1+\lambda)$, so $\operatorname{Bias}(\lambda)=-\lambda\beta/(1+\lambda)$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\lambda)=\sigma^2/(1+\lambda)^2$, giving $\text{MSE}(\lambda)=(\lambda^2\beta^2+\sigma^2)/(1+\lambda)^2$. Differentiating at $\lambda=0$ gives slope $-2\sigma^2<0$ whenever $\sigma^2>0$: some positive ridge penalty always reduces MSE at the margin, for any true $\beta$, purely because variance falls faster than bias grows near $\lambda=0$. The MSE-minimizing penalty is $\lambda^\star=\sigma^2/\beta^2$; for $\beta=0.4$ and $\sigma^2=1$, $\lambda^\star=6.25$.

    MSE falls, bottoms out near $\lambda^\star$, then rises back toward $\beta^2$ as $\lambda\to\infty$ and the estimate collapses to zero. Past $\lambda^\star$, bias dominates and further shrinkage costs more than it saves; the practical difficulty is that $\lambda^\star$ depends on the unknown $\beta$ and $\sigma^2$, which is exactly why $\lambda$ is chosen by cross-validation rather than by this formula directly.

    Lasso, Soft Thresholding, and Sparsity

    The absolute-value penalty is nondifferentiable at zero. Its subgradient permits exact zeros: $0$ is optimal for coordinate $j$ when the partial residual correlation lies inside the interval $[-n\lambda\alpha,n\lambda\alpha]$. For a standardized coordinate, cyclic coordinate descent applies the soft-threshold operator

    $$\beta_j\leftarrow\frac{S(z_j,\lambda\alpha)}{1+\lambda(1-\alpha)},\qquad S(z,\gamma)=\operatorname{sign}(z)\max(|z|-\gamma,0).$$

    KKT derivation. Holding every coordinate but $j$ fixed, let $r_{-j}=y-\sum_{k\ne j}X_k\beta_k$ be the partial residual and $z_j=\tfrac1nX_j^\top r_{-j}$ the partial correlation. The one-dimensional objective in $\beta_j$ is $\tfrac1{2n}\|r_{-j}-X_j\beta_j\|_2^2+\lambda\alpha|\beta_j|+\tfrac{\lambda(1-\alpha)}2\beta_j^2$, with subdifferential stationarity condition

    $$0\in-z_j+\beta_j+\lambda\alpha\,\partial|\beta_j|+\lambda(1-\alpha)\beta_j,$$

    using $\partial|\beta_j|=\{\operatorname{sign}(\beta_j)\}$ for $\beta_j\ne0$ and $\partial|\beta_j|=[-1,1]$ at $\beta_j=0$. For $\beta_j>0$ this gives $\beta_j=(z_j-\lambda\alpha)/(1+\lambda(1-\alpha))$, valid only when $z_j>\lambda\alpha$; the symmetric case gives $\beta_j<0$ when $z_j<-\lambda\alpha$. When $z_j\in[-\lambda\alpha,\lambda\alpha]$, $\beta_j=0$ satisfies the KKT condition because some subgradient value in $[-1,1]$ can absorb $z_j/\lambda\alpha$ exactly. The three cases combine into the single soft-threshold update above, and the same stationarity argument, applied jointly across coordinates, is the KKT system that characterizes any lasso or elastic-net minimizer.

    Lasso selection is unstable among strongly correlated predictors: several nearly equivalent sparse supports can have similar prediction error. Elastic net's quadratic term encourages grouped retention and makes the objective strictly convex under broader conditions.

    Correlated-feature example. Suppose two columns are numerically identical, $X_1=X_2=x$. For any fixed combined coefficient $c=\beta_1+\beta_2=2$, all splits have the same fitted values. Ridge uniquely minimizes its penalty at $(\beta_1,\beta_2)=(1,1)$ because every unequal split increases $\beta_1^2+\beta_2^2$; in the actual penalized fit, ridge also shrinks the optimal combined coefficient below its OLS value and then divides that total evenly. Lasso's penalty is $|\beta_1|+|\beta_2|=2$ for every nonnegative split $(a,2-a)$, so it cannot distinguish points along that equal-fit line segment; its optimal combined coefficient is likewise determined by the loss-penalty tradeoff, but its allocation between the copies is nonunique. An optimizer may therefore report either copy as selected. Elastic net's added $L_2$ term breaks the allocation tie and splits the shrunken total evenly.

    The Regularization Path

    Ridge coefficients $\beta_j(\lambda)=\sigma_j(u_j^\top y)/(\sigma_j^2+\lambda)$ are smooth, infinitely differentiable functions of $\lambda$. Lasso and elastic-net paths with $\alpha>0$ are only piecewise linear in $\lambda$. At $\lambda_{\max}=\max_j|X_j^\top y|/(n\alpha)$, every partial correlation sits inside the inactive interval and all coefficients are exactly zero. As $\lambda$ decreases from $\lambda_{\max}$, each variable enters the active set at its own knot, the $\lambda$ where $|z_j|$ first equals $\lambda\alpha$; between consecutive knots the active coefficients move along a fixed linear direction, and a knot is also crossed whenever an active coefficient's path returns to zero and must leave the active set. This knot structure, shared with the LARS algorithm at $\alpha=1$, is why lasso solution paths are computed at a finite grid of $\lambda$ values with warm starts between them rather than solved in closed form like the ridge path.

    Counterexample. A sparse ground truth does not guarantee lasso support recovery. Selection frequency, domain constraints, and post-selection uncertainty matter when coefficients drive policy.

    Cross-Validation and Production Implications

    Lambda must be selected inside the training process. A validation set repeatedly consulted during tuning is no longer an unbiased final test. Nested cross-validation is appropriate when an unbiased performance estimate is required. The one-standard-error rule selects the most regularized model whose cross-validation error lies within one standard error of the minimum, often improving stability with little predictive cost.

    Leakage trap. Means, scales, missing-value rules, feature screening, and the lambda grid must be fitted within each fold. Global preprocessing allows held-out rows to influence the model. Time-indexed data require forward validation rather than random folds.

    Edge cases include all-zero coefficients at $\lambda_{\max}$, nonunique lasso solutions, penalty factors for protected covariates, sparse categorical levels absent from a fold, coefficient conversion back to raw units, and changing library conventions for the factor $1/2n$. Production artifacts need feature order, scaling constants, intercept policy, $\lambda$, $\alpha$, solver tolerance, convergence state, and the exact objective convention.
