    Model Specification, Transformations, and Interactions

    July 2026 · 16 min read

    "Linear" regression refers to linearity in the parameters $\beta$, not linearity in the original predictors. This single distinction is what allows polynomial terms, logarithms, and interaction products to be fit by exactly the same normal equations developed earlier in this track, and it is also what makes the choice of which columns to place in $X$, the specification, a substantive modeling decision with its own failure modes.

    Linear in the Parameters, Not in the Predictors

    The model $y=\beta_0+\beta_1x+\beta_2x^2+\varepsilon$ is a linear model. Once the derived column $x^2$ is placed into the design matrix, the model has the form $y=X\gamma+\varepsilon$ with $X=[1,x,x^2]$, and the entire OLS machinery, the normal equations, the Gauss-Markov guarantees, the standard errors, applies without modification. What would break linearity is a parameter entering nonlinearly, as in $y=\beta_0 e^{\beta_1 x}+\varepsilon$, which requires nonlinear least squares rather than the closed-form solution used throughout this track.

    Transformations, Logs, Polynomials, and Basis Terms

    SpecificationInterpretation and caveat
    $Y=\beta_0+\beta_1\log X+\varepsilon$A one-percent increase in positive $X$ changes the conditional mean by approximately $0.01\beta_1$. Zero and negative inputs require a substantive alternative, not an arbitrary offset.
    $\log Y=\beta_0+\beta_1X+\varepsilon$A one-unit increase in $X$ changes the conditional median geometrically by $100(e^{\beta_1}-1)\%$. Back-transforming a mean needs a smearing or distributional correction.
    $1,x,x^2,\ldots,x^k$Captures smooth curvature inside the fitted range but can oscillate and explode under extrapolation. Orthogonal polynomials or splines often condition the basis better.
    Spline or other basis $B_1(x),\ldots,B_m(x)$Remains linear in coefficients and permits local flexibility. Knots and complexity belong inside validation rather than being selected from test performance.

    Centering $x_c=x-\bar x$ changes the interpretation of lower-order terms and often reduces nonessential correlation with $x_c^2$ or an interaction. It does not change fitted values when all corresponding terms remain present, and it does not cure collinearity between substantively overlapping variables.

    Omitted-Variable Bias

    The true model is $y=\beta_0+\beta_1x_1+\beta_2x_2+\varepsilon$, but the fitted model omits $x_2$ and regresses $y$ on $x_1$ alone. Writing $\delta_1$ for the coefficient obtained from regressing $x_2$ on $x_1$ (with an intercept), the expected value of the short-model slope is

    $$E[\hat\beta_1^{\text{short}}] = \beta_1 + \beta_2\,\delta_1.$$

    The derivation substitutes the auxiliary projection $x_2=\delta_0+\delta_1x_1+v$ into the true model. The coefficient multiplying $x_1$ becomes $\beta_1+\beta_2\delta_1$, while $\beta_2v+\varepsilon$ becomes the short-model error. The bias vanishes only when $\beta_2=0$ or $\delta_1=0$; otherwise the short slope absorbs part of the omitted variable's influence.

    Functional-Form Misspecification: An Omitted Quadratic Term

    Worked numerical example. A fixed seven-row dataset compares a misspecified line with a quadratic basis.

    A concrete instance of the same bias formula arises when the omitted "variable" is a power of an included one. The population relationship is $y=2+4.5x-0.5x^2+\varepsilon$ on $x=1,\dots,7$, a concave curve, evaluated on the fixed sample below.

    For the curved dataset $y=(6.08,8.92,11.38,12.06,11.68,11.22,9.78)$ at $x=1,\ldots,7$, fitting $y$ on $x$ alone gives $\hat y=7.874+0.571x$, $\text{RSS}=17.72$, and $R^2=0.34$. Adding $x^2$ gives $\hat y=2.429+4.202x-0.454x^2$, $\text{RSS}=0.42$, and $R^2=0.98$. The finite-sample omitted-variable identity is exact: regressing $x^2$ on $x$ gives $\hat\delta=8.00$, so $(-0.454)(8.00)=-3.63$, matching $0.571-4.202=-3.63$ after rounding.

    Interview trap. "Adding $x^2$ makes the model nonlinear, so ordinary least squares no longer applies" is false; the model remains linear in $\beta_0,\beta_1,\beta_2$ and is fit with the same normal equations. A separate and real risk is that in-sample $R^2$ is non-decreasing in the number of columns added to $X$, by the same projection argument from the earlier part on the hat matrix, so an arbitrarily high-degree polynomial can drive in-sample $R^2$ arbitrarily close to $1$ while extrapolating wildly and generalizing poorly outside the fitted range; a low RSS on the fitted data is not by itself evidence of correct functional form.

    Interaction Terms and Marginal Effects

    An interaction term lets the effect of one predictor depend on the level of another. For $y=\beta_0+\beta_1x_1+\beta_2x_2+\beta_3x_1x_2+\varepsilon$, the marginal effect of $x_1$ on $y$ is not the constant $\beta_1$ but

    $$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x_1} = \beta_1 + \beta_3x_2,$$

    a quantity that itself depends on $x_2$. Reading $\beta_1$ alone as "the effect of $x_1$" once $\beta_3\neq 0$ discards exactly the information the interaction term was introduced to capture.

    ▶ Formula-Derived Interaction Surface and Marginal Effect

    The surface is $\hat y=2+1.5x+0.8z+0.6xz$. Each step adds a fixed-$z$ slice calculated from the formula and displays the exact marginal effect $\partial\hat y/\partial x=1.5+0.6z$.

    A separate ten-point example has five rows per binary group $d$, with true slopes $1$ for $d=0$ and $3$ for $d=1$. A model with only $x$ and $d$ forces one shared slope, $\hat\beta_x=1.830$, with $\text{RSS}=12.46$. Adding $x\cdot d$ gives $\hat y=1.683+1.055x+1.701d+1.549(x\cdot d)$, so the slopes become $1.055$ and $2.604$, with $\text{RSS}=0.46$.

    Hierarchy principle. A model containing $x_1x_2$ normally retains both $x_1$ and $x_2$, even if a lower-order term is individually insignificant. Removing a main effect changes the interaction into a constrained model whose surface passes through an artificial equality at a zero point that may not be meaningful. Centering can move that reference point to a substantively useful value.

    Categorical Encoding and the Dummy-Variable Trap

    A categorical predictor with $k$ levels is encoded as $k-1$ indicator columns plus the intercept, with one level chosen as the reference category absorbed into $\beta_0$. Including all $k$ indicator columns together with an intercept makes the indicator columns sum to a constant column of ones, which is already in $X$, so $X$ loses full column rank and $X^\top X$ becomes exactly singular, not merely poorly conditioned. This is the dummy-variable trap named in the previous part on multicollinearity: it is a specification error with a mechanical fix, dropping one indicator or dropping the intercept, rather than a variance problem to be weighed against modeling goals.

    Edge case. Changing the reference category changes coefficient labels but not fitted values. Novel levels at prediction time need an explicit policy, such as an “other” level defined during training; silently mapping an unseen level to the reference category asserts an equality unsupported by data.

    Missingness and Extrapolation

    Complete-case analysis is valid for the intended estimand only under assumptions about why values are missing. Mean imputation understates uncertainty and distorts covariance unless missingness indicators and an appropriate model support the procedure. Imputation must be learned on training folds to avoid leakage. Missing not at random mechanisms require sensitivity analysis or a joint missingness model rather than a generic preprocessing fix.

    Transformed models can extrapolate more violently than a straight line. A polynomial term, an interaction evaluated at an unseen predictor combination, or a log model near zero can dominate predictions. Support diagnostics therefore include ranges, joint density or distance, leverage, and domain constraints. A prediction outside the observed convex hull is a model-based extension, not an observed-data conclusion.

    SymptomLikely specification issue
    Residuals trace a smooth curve against a predictorMissing polynomial or transformed term in that predictor.
    A predictor's apparent effect flips sign depending on which other variables are includedOmitted-variable bias, or a genuine interaction being forced into a single additive slope.
    $X^\top X$ fails to invert during fittingDummy-variable trap, or an exact linear dependency among included columns.
    Residual spread visibly widens or narrows across fitted valuesNot a specification issue in the mean function; a violation of the constant-variance assumption from the earlier part on inference, addressed with robust standard errors rather than added regressors.

    Testing the Functional Form

    A residual-versus-fitted-value plot is the standard first diagnostic: a random-looking scatter around zero is consistent with an adequate mean function, while any visible curvature, fan shape, or grouping signals a specific failure. Ramsey's RESET test formalizes the curvature check by adding powers of the fitted values, $\hat y^2$, $\hat y^3$, back into the regression and testing whether their coefficients are jointly zero with an F-test; a rejection indicates that some nonlinear structure remains unexplained without needing to guess its exact functional form in advance. A compilable implementation of residual diagnostics and fit metrics, including the machinery to run this kind of test, is developed in From Scratch · C++ · Regression Diagnostics and Metrics.

    Interview trap. A model that already includes many predictors and interactions is sometimes assumed to be safely "flexible enough" to avoid specification error. Flexibility in the number of terms does not substitute for checking which terms are the right ones: a model can have a high $R^2$ from irrelevant polynomial terms while still misrepresenting the actual marginal effect of the variable being interpreted, particularly once terms correlate with each other as described in the previous part on multicollinearity.

    Assumptions and counterexamples. Every added term still relies on exogeneity for coefficient interpretation and adequate support for estimation. A log transform does not guarantee normal residuals, centering does not remove confounding, and a RESET rejection does not identify the correct replacement model. Residual patterns, partial residual plots, cross-validated error, influence checks, and subject-matter constraints form a combined workflow.
