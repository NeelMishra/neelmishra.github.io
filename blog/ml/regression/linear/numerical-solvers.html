    Solving Least Squares Reliably: Cholesky, QR, and SVD

    July 2026 · 14 min read

    The formula $\hat\beta=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y$ is an identity, not a numerical algorithm. Explicit inversion forms unnecessary intermediate error, and forming $X^\top X$ squares the spectral condition number. A design with $\kappa_2(X)=10^8$ creates a normal-equation matrix with $\kappa_2(X^\top X)=10^{16}$, approximately the reciprocal of double-precision machine epsilon. The coefficient vector can then lose nearly every meaningful digit even though the residual remains small.

    Conditioning and Forward Error

    For full-rank $X$, singular values satisfy $\kappa_2(X)=\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$. A backward-stable solver returns the exact answer to a nearby problem, but the forward coefficient error can still scale as $\kappa_2(X)$ times the relative data perturbation. Ill-conditioning is therefore a property of the statistical design, while instability is a property of the algorithm. A stable algorithm cannot manufacture information absent from nearly dependent columns.

    Counterexample. The columns $(1,1,1)^\top$ and $(1,1,1+10^{-10})^\top$ are distinguishable in exact arithmetic, yet their separate coefficients are weakly identified. A tiny response perturbation can exchange a large positive coefficient for a large negative one while leaving fitted values nearly unchanged. A tiny residual is not evidence of trustworthy coefficients.

    Four Solver Families

    Cholesky on $X^\top X$$O(np^2+p^3/3)$Fast, but condition number is squared; fails without positive definitenessWell-scaled, confidently full-rank designs
    Householder QR$O(2np^2-2p^3/3)$Backward stable without forming $X^\top X$Default dense full-rank least squares
    Pivoted QRQR plus pivot searchReorders columns to expose numerical rankSuspected dependence and interpretable rank decisions
    SVDHighest constant, typically $O(np^2)$Most transparent rank and minimum-norm solutionRank deficiency, pseudoinverses, audit paths

    Householder reflectors transform $X=QR$ with $Q^\top Q=I$, reducing the problem to $\min_\beta\|Q^\top y-R\beta\|_2$. Cholesky solves $R^\top R\beta=X^\top y$ after factoring $X^\top X=R^\top R$. For $X=U\Sigma V^\top$, the SVD solution is

    $$\hat\beta=X^+y=\sum_{\sigma_j>t}\frac{u_j^\top y}{\sigma_j}v_j,$$

    where $t$ is an explicit numerical-rank threshold. Discarding a direction is a modeling decision with an implicit regularization effect, not a harmless implementation detail.

    Rank, Scaling, and Thresholds

    A common threshold is $t=\epsilon\max(n,p)\sigma_{\max}$, but application noise often warrants a larger cutoff. Column scaling changes singular values and pivot order, so units must be controlled before interpreting rank. The intercept requires separate care: centering predictors improves conditioning while retaining an intercept, but centering sparse matrices may destroy sparsity.

    Trap. A failed Cholesky factorization does not prove exact rank deficiency. Severe roundoff, overflow from unscaled columns, or a matrix that is only theoretically positive semidefinite can also trigger failure. A pivoted QR or SVD provides a more diagnostic fallback.

    Iterative Solvers for Large or Sparse Systems

    Direct factorizations assume a design that fits comfortably in dense memory. When $X$ is sparse, very tall, or available only through matrix-vector products, Krylov-subspace methods solve the same least-squares problem without ever forming $X^\top X$ or a dense $R$.

    Conjugate gradient applied to the normal equations, commonly called CGNR or CGLS, minimizes $\|X\beta-y\|_2^2$ by running CG on $X^\top X\beta=X^\top y$ using only products with $X$ and $X^\top$. Caveat. Never forming $X^\top X$ explicitly does not remove its conditioning. The convergence bound still contains $\kappa_2(X^\top X)=\kappa_2(X)^2$, and a normal-equation-style coefficient error can scale as $\kappa_2(X)^2\epsilon$. CGNR can therefore stagnate well before an accurate coefficient vector is recovered on exactly the ill-conditioned designs that already break Cholesky, even when its data-space residual looks small.

    LSQR and LSMR apply Golub-Kahan bidiagonalization directly to $X$. They generate the same exact-arithmetic iterates as related normal-equation Krylov methods, but avoid explicitly multiplying the matrix condition number through a formed $X^\top X$ and usually behave more stably in finite precision. LSQR reduces $\|X\beta_t-y\|_2$ and is the usual default; LSMR reduces $\|X^\top(X\beta_t-y)\|_2$ and tends to decrease that normal-equation residual more monotonically, which makes early stopping easier to reason about. Both accept an explicit damping term for ridge-style regularization as a native option, with no change to the iteration structure.

    Preconditioning and Stopping Rules

    A preconditioner replaces $X$ with $XM^{-1}$ for some $M$ chosen so the transformed problem has smaller effective condition number, solves for $M\beta$, then unwinds the transform. Column, or Jacobi, scaling by $1/\|X_j\|_2$ already removes gross unit mismatches between columns. A stronger and common choice factors a cheap incomplete or truncated QR, so that $X\approx \tilde Q\tilde R$ only approximately, and uses $\tilde R$ as a right preconditioner for LSQR; well-behaved sparse problems can then converge in a small fraction of the unpreconditioned iteration count.

    A single stopping test is unreliable for iterative least squares, mirroring gradient-based optimization. Production stopping combines a relative residual test such as $\|X^\top(y-X\beta_t)\|_2\le\text{tol}\cdot\|X^\top y\|_2$, a maximum iteration budget, a stagnation check across recent objective values, and a finite-value guard. Reporting the achieved relative residual next to the returned coefficients lets a downstream consumer judge whether the iterative answer meets its tolerance, rather than assuming convergence from iteration count alone.

    Worked conditioning comparison. Consider a consistent, full-rank problem with $\kappa_2(X)=10^6$, double-precision $\epsilon\approx10^{-16}$, and backward perturbations of relative size $\epsilon$. A backward-stable Householder QR solve, and typically a converged LSQR/LSMR solve, has coefficient forward-error scale $\kappa_2(X)\epsilon\approx10^{-10}$, leaving about six trustworthy digits. Cholesky on the normal equations and a CGNR solve limited by normal-equation accuracy can instead reach the scale $\kappa_2(X)^2\epsilon\approx10^{-4}$, leaving only about four trustworthy digits even when $\|X\hat\beta-y\|$ is small. A right preconditioner that reduces the iterative problem's effective condition number to $10^2$ changes the corresponding Krylov error scale to roughly $10^{-14}$. These are conditioning bounds, not exact equalities; inconsistent problems can add residual-dependent amplification. The comparison isolates why squaring $X$'s condition number is costly.

    Production Decision Rules

    Dense, moderate-sized, full-rank systems generally favor QR. Repeated responses with one stable design can amortize a factorization. Sparse or very large systems favor sparse QR or a preconditioned LSQR/LSMR iteration rather than dense normal equations or unpreconditioned CGNR. SVD belongs in fallback and diagnostic paths when rank is uncertain. Production telemetry should record solver, numerical rank, $\sigma_{\min}$ or a condition estimate, residual norm, achieved relative residual for iterative solvers, iteration count against budget, and any discarded singular directions.

    Edge cases include $n<p$, where infinitely many interpolating solutions may exist; exact duplicate or one-hot columns with a full dummy set and intercept; weights equal to zero; NaN or infinite inputs; and mixed-unit columns that overflow dot products. The minimum-norm SVD answer is unique, but it is not automatically the scientifically preferred answer. Ridge can encode a more defensible stability assumption.
