    Linear Regression: Essential Interview Derivations

    July 2026 · 17 min read

    Strong derivations state dimensions, assumptions, and failure conditions rather than ending at a familiar formula. The collection below starts from objectives and conditional moments, keeps intercept and rank details explicit, and separates algebraic facts from statistical claims.

    1. Normal Equations and the Rank Condition

    For $X\in\mathbb R^{n\times p}$ and $\beta\in\mathbb R^p$, expand

    $$L(\beta)=\|y-X\beta\|_2^2=y^\top y-2\beta^\top X^\top y+\beta^\top X^\top X\beta.$$

    Because $X^\top X$ is symmetric, $\nabla_\beta L=-2X^\top y+2X^\top X\beta$. The first-order condition is $X^\top X\hat\beta=X^\top y$. The Hessian $2X^\top X$ is positive semidefinite, so every stationary point is globally minimizing. Full column rank makes it positive definite and yields the unique coefficient vector $(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y$.

    Trap. Rank deficiency destroys coefficient uniqueness, not fitted-value uniqueness. All minimizers project $y$ onto the same column space. A pseudoinverse returns the minimum-Euclidean-norm minimizer.

    2. Projection and Residual Orthogonality

    Substitution gives $\hat y=Hy$ with $H=X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top$. Direct multiplication shows $H^\top=H$ and $H^2=H$, so $H$ is an orthogonal projector. The normal equations also give $X^\top e=0$, placing $e=y-\hat y$ in $\operatorname{col}(X)^\perp$. Therefore

    $$\|y\|^2=\|\hat y\|^2+\|e\|^2.$$

    With an intercept, $\mathbf1^\top e=0$ and the centered decomposition $\text{SST}=\text{SSR}+\text{SSE}$ follows. Without an intercept, centered $R^2$ need not obey this decomposition and can be misleading.

    3. OLS Unbiasedness and Sampling Variance

    Under $y=X\beta+\varepsilon$, fixed $X$, $E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0$, and full rank,

    $$\hat\beta=\beta+(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top\varepsilon,\qquad E[\hat\beta\mid X]=\beta.$$

    For general conditional error covariance $\Omega$,

    $$\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta\mid X)=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega X(X^\top X)^{-1}.$$

    Only under $\Omega=\sigma^2I$ does this reduce to $\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}$. Normal errors are unnecessary for unbiasedness and the variance formula; normality supplies exact finite-sample $t$ and $F$ distributions.

    Counterexample. Heteroscedasticity leaves OLS unbiased under conditional mean zero but invalidates the homoscedastic standard-error formula. Robust covariance estimation changes uncertainty, not coefficients.

    4. Gauss-Markov: OLS Is BLUE

    Consider any other linear estimator $\tilde\beta=Cy$. Unbiasedness for every $\beta$ requires $E[Cy\mid X]=CX\beta=\beta$ for all $\beta$, so $CX=I$. Write $C=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top+D$ for some matrix $D$; substituting gives $CX=I+DX=I$, so unbiasedness is exactly the constraint $DX=0$, which also implies $X^\top D^\top=0$. Under $\operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon\mid X)=\sigma^2I$,

    $$\operatorname{Var}(\tilde\beta\mid X)=\sigma^2CC^\top=\sigma^2\left[(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top X(X^\top X)^{-1}+(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top D^\top+DX(X^\top X)^{-1}+DD^\top\right].$$

    The two cross terms vanish because $DX=0$ and $X^\top D^\top=0$, leaving $\operatorname{Var}(\tilde\beta\mid X)=\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}+\sigma^2DD^\top$. Since $DD^\top$ is positive semidefinite, $\operatorname{Var}(\tilde\beta\mid X)-\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_{\text{OLS}}\mid X)\succeq0$ for every linear unbiased $\tilde\beta$, including for any fixed linear combination $a^\top\tilde\beta$. This is the Gauss-Markov theorem: among linear unbiased estimators under homoscedastic, uncorrelated errors, OLS has the smallest variance in every direction simultaneously, not merely the smallest total or average variance.

    Trap. The proof uses only $E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon\mid X)=\sigma^2I$; it needs no normality assumption. It also only ranks OLS against other linear unbiased estimators. A biased estimator, such as ridge, can have strictly lower mean squared error, and a nonlinear unbiased estimator is outside the comparison entirely.

    5. Prediction Bias-Variance Decomposition

    For a fresh $Y=f(x)+\varepsilon$ with $E[\varepsilon]=0$, $\operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon)=\sigma^2$, and training-dependent predictor $\hat f(x)$, add and subtract $E_D[\hat f(x)]$:

    $$E_{D,\varepsilon}\!\left[(Y-\hat f(x))^2\right]=\sigma^2+\left(E_D[\hat f(x)]-f(x)\right)^2+\operatorname{Var}_D(\hat f(x)).$$

    The cross terms vanish by zero-mean noise and centering around $E_D[\hat f]$. This is a pointwise decomposition over repeated training datasets. A single fitted model does not empirically reveal its own bias and variance without a resampling or generative framework.

    6. Ridge Closed Form and Its Bias

    For $L_\lambda(\beta)=\|y-X\beta\|^2+\lambda\|\beta\|^2$,

    $$\nabla L_\lambda=-2X^\top y+2X^\top X\beta+2\lambda\beta=0,$$ $$\hat\beta_\lambda=(X^\top X+\lambda I)^{-1}X^\top y.$$

    Conditionally, $E[\hat\beta_\lambda\mid X]=(X^\top X+\lambda I)^{-1}X^\top X\beta$, so the bias is $-\lambda(X^\top X+\lambda I)^{-1}\beta$. Adding $\lambda I$ makes the system positive definite for $\lambda>0$, but a standard intercept column is excluded by replacing $I$ with a penalty matrix whose intercept entry is zero.

    7. Frisch-Waugh-Lovell Residualization

    Partition $X=[X_1\ X_2]$ and write the full model $y=X_1\beta_1+X_2\beta_2+\varepsilon$. The second block of the normal equations is $X_2^\top(y-X_1\hat\beta_1-X_2\hat\beta_2)=0$. Solving the first block for $\hat\beta_1$ in terms of $\hat\beta_2$ and substituting back gives

    $$X_2^\top M_1X_2\,\hat\beta_2=X_2^\top M_1y,\qquad M_1=I-X_1(X_1^\top X_1)^{-1}X_1^\top,$$

    which is exactly the normal equations for regressing $M_1y$ on $M_1X_2$. Since $M_1$ is symmetric idempotent, $\hat\beta_2$ from the full regression equals the coefficient from regressing $y$-residuals on $X_1$ against $X_2$-residuals on $X_1$, and the two regressions share identical final residual vectors. The result requires $M_1X_2\ne0$; if $X_2$ lies entirely in the column space of $X_1$, its partial coefficient is unidentified rather than merely small.

    Trick question. "Does adding a control variable change a coefficient because it removes confounding, or because it removes shared variance regardless of any causal story?" FWL shows the second answer is the mechanical one: the coefficient is a partial correlation after removing $X_1$'s linear influence from both $y$ and $X_2$, a purely algebraic operation with no causal content on its own.

    8. Lasso KKT and Soft-Thresholding

    For a single coordinate holding the rest fixed, assume $X_j^\top X_j/n=1$ and define the partial residual correlation $z_j=\tfrac1nX_j^\top r_{-j}$. The subdifferential stationarity condition for $\min_{\beta_j}\tfrac1{2n}\|r_{-j}-X_j\beta_j\|_2^2+\lambda|\beta_j|$ is

    $$0\in-z_j+\beta_j+\lambda\,\partial|\beta_j|,\qquad \partial|\beta_j|=\begin{cases}\{\operatorname{sign}(\beta_j)\},&\beta_j\ne0,\\ [-1,1],&\beta_j=0.\end{cases}$$

    Solving each case gives $\beta_j=z_j-\lambda$ when $z_j>\lambda$, $\beta_j=z_j+\lambda$ when $z_j<-\lambda$, and $\beta_j=0$ whenever $|z_j|\le\lambda$, since some subgradient in $[-1,1]$ then satisfies the condition exactly. These three cases are the soft-threshold operator $\hat\beta_j=S(z_j,\lambda)=\operatorname{sign}(z_j)\max(|z_j|-\lambda,0)$, and the same coordinatewise KKT argument extends to the full lasso or elastic-net system.

    9. Leave-One-Out Residuals, PRESS, and Influence

    Deleting row $i$ is a rank-one downdate of $X^\top X$. Applying Sherman-Morrison to $(X^\top X-x_ix_i^\top)^{-1}$ and simplifying yields

    $$e_{(i)}=y_i-\hat y_{(i)}=\frac{e_i}{1-h_{ii}}.$$

    Thus $\text{PRESS}=\sum_i(e_i/(1-h_{ii}))^2$ computes exact linear OLS leave-one-out error from one fit. The formula assumes the deleted design remains estimable. As $h_{ii}\to1$, the held-out residual becomes unstable, accurately reflecting that the point is nearly responsible for fitting itself.

    The same Sherman-Morrison step gives the exact per-observation influence on the coefficient vector itself, called DFBETA: $\hat\beta-\hat\beta_{(i)}=(X^\top X)^{-1}x_ie_i/(1-h_{ii})$, the deleted-row analogue of the deleted-row residual above. It is large precisely when a point combines a sizeable residual $e_i$ with high leverage $h_{ii}$, the same two ingredients Cook's distance combines into one scalar, $D_i=e_i^2h_{ii}/(p\hat\sigma^2(1-h_{ii})^2)$. A point with either factor near zero cannot be influential regardless of the other, which is why leverage alone and residual size alone each fail to identify influential observations on their own.

Every derivation has an assumption boundary, and naming it is part of the result.
