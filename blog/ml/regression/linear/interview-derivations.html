    Linear Regression: Essential Interview Derivations

    July 2026 · 17 min read

    This page collects the linear-regression derivations most often used in interviews. Each section states the assumptions, shows the key algebra, and names the point where the result can fail.

    1. Normal Equations and the Rank Condition

    Start with the squared-error objective for $X\in\mathbb R^{n\times p}$:

    $$L(\beta)=\|y-X\beta\|_2^2=y^\top y-2\beta^\top X^\top y+\beta^\top X^\top X\beta.$$

    The gradient is $-2X^\top y+2X^\top X\beta$. Setting it to zero gives $X^\top X\hat\beta=X^\top y$. The Hessian is positive semidefinite, so this is a global minimum. Full column rank gives the unique solution $(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y$.

    Trap. Rank deficiency makes coefficients nonunique, but the fitted projection can still be unique. The pseudoinverse chooses the minimum-norm coefficient vector.

    2. Projection and Residual Orthogonality

    Substitution gives $\hat y=Hy$ with $H=X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top$. Since $H^\top=H$ and $H^2=H$, it is an orthogonal projection. The normal equations give $X^\top e=0$, so the residual is perpendicular to every model column. Therefore

    $$\|y\|^2=\|\hat y\|^2+\|e\|^2.$$

    With an intercept, residuals sum to zero and $\text{SST}=\text{SSR}+\text{SSE}$. Without an intercept, the usual centered $R^2$ decomposition may fail.

    3. OLS Unbiasedness and Sampling Variance

    Under $y=X\beta+\varepsilon$, fixed $X$, $E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0$, and full rank,

    $$\hat\beta=\beta+(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top\varepsilon,\qquad E[\hat\beta\mid X]=\beta.$$

    For general conditional error covariance $\Omega$,

    $$\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta\mid X)=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top\Omega X(X^\top X)^{-1}.$$

    Under constant independent error variance, this reduces to $\sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1}$. Normality is not needed for unbiasedness; it is needed for exact small-sample $t$ and $F$ distributions.

    Counterexample. Heteroscedasticity leaves OLS unbiased under conditional mean zero but invalidates the homoscedastic standard-error formula. Robust covariance estimation changes uncertainty, not coefficients.

    4. Gauss-Markov: OLS Is BLUE

    Any linear estimator has the form $\tilde\beta=Cy$. Unbiasedness requires $CX=I$. Write $C$ as the OLS matrix plus another matrix $D$. The unbiasedness condition then forces $DX=0$. Under constant independent error variance,

    $$\operatorname{Var}(\tilde\beta\mid X)=\sigma^2CC^\top=\sigma^2\left[(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top X(X^\top X)^{-1}+(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top D^\top+DX(X^\top X)^{-1}+DD^\top\right].$$

    The cross terms vanish, leaving the OLS variance plus $\sigma^2DD^\top$. This extra term cannot reduce variance. Therefore OLS has the smallest variance among linear unbiased estimators.

    Trap. Gauss-Markov needs no normality assumption and compares OLS only with linear unbiased estimators. Ridge is biased and may still have lower prediction MSE.

    5. Prediction Bias-Variance Decomposition

    For a new outcome $Y=f(x)+\varepsilon$, expected prediction error splits into

    $$E_{D,\varepsilon}\!\left[(Y-\hat f(x))^2\right]=\sigma^2+\left(E_D[\hat f(x)]-f(x)\right)^2+\operatorname{Var}_D(\hat f(x)).$$

    The three parts are irreducible noise, squared bias, and model variance across training samples. One fitted model cannot reveal its own bias and variance without resampling or a data-generating model.

    6. Ridge Closed Form and Its Bias

    For $L_\lambda(\beta)=\|y-X\beta\|^2+\lambda\|\beta\|^2$,

    $$\nabla L_\lambda=-2X^\top y+2X^\top X\beta+2\lambda\beta=0,$$ $$\hat\beta_\lambda=(X^\top X+\lambda I)^{-1}X^\top y.$$

    The conditional bias is $-\lambda(X^\top X+\lambda I)^{-1}\beta$. The added diagonal stabilizes the system. In standard practice, the intercept is excluded from the penalty.

    7. Frisch-Waugh-Lovell Residualization

    Split the predictors into controls $X_1$ and target predictors $X_2$. Eliminating the control coefficients from the normal equations gives

    $$X_2^\top M_1X_2\,\hat\beta_2=X_2^\top M_1y,\qquad M_1=I-X_1(X_1^\top X_1)^{-1}X_1^\top,$$

    This is OLS of the residualized outcome $M_1y$ on the residualized predictor $M_1X_2$. The slope equals the full-model coefficient. If $M_1X_2=0$, no unique variation remains and the coefficient is unidentified.

    Trick question. FWL explains the algebra of holding controls fixed. It does not create a causal interpretation by itself.

    8. Lasso KKT and Soft-Thresholding

    For one standardized coordinate, let $z_j$ be its correlation with the residual left by the other predictors. The Lasso stationarity condition is

    $$0\in-z_j+\beta_j+\lambda\,\partial|\beta_j|,\qquad \partial|\beta_j|=\begin{cases}\{\operatorname{sign}(\beta_j)\},&\beta_j\ne0,\\ [-1,1],&\beta_j=0.\end{cases}$$

    If $|z_j|\le\lambda$, zero is optimal. Otherwise $z_j$ is moved toward zero by $\lambda$. The three cases combine into $\hat\beta_j=\operatorname{sign}(z_j)\max(|z_j|-\lambda,0)$.

    9. Leave-One-Out Residuals, PRESS, and Influence

    Removing one row changes $X^\top X$ by a rank-one update. Sherman-Morrison gives the exact leave-one-out residual without refitting:

    $$e_{(i)}=y_i-\hat y_{(i)}=\frac{e_i}{1-h_{ii}}.$$

    PRESS sums the squared values $e_i/(1-h_{ii})$ and equals exact OLS leave-one-out error. Large leverage makes deletion residuals unstable. Coefficient influence and Cook's distance are also large only when a row combines meaningful leverage with a meaningful residual.

    Important boundaries include $p\ge n$, constant predictors, missing intercepts, zero residual degrees of freedom, correlated errors, post-selection inference, transformed outcomes, and extrapolation. A derivation is incomplete when its assumptions are missing.
