    Maximum Likelihood and the Log-Loss Derivation

    July 2026 · 14 min read

    The log-loss objective is not an arbitrary choice; it is the negative log-likelihood of the Bernoulli model. Deriving it from first principles explains why the loss has the form it does, why it is convex, why its gradient is a residual, and why a numerically careful implementation must avoid materializing the probability before taking a logarithm. This page carries the derivation from the likelihood of a single observation through to the gradient and Hessian used by every solver in the track.

    The Bernoulli Likelihood

    A single labeled observation contributes a Bernoulli probability. With $p_i=\sigma(z_i)$ the modeled probability that $y_i=1$, the probability mass function evaluated at the observed label is written compactly as

    $$\Pr(y_i\mid x_i;\beta)=p_i^{\,y_i}(1-p_i)^{1-y_i},$$

    which equals $p_i$ when $y_i=1$ and $1-p_i$ when $y_i=0$. Assuming the rows are conditionally independent given their features, the likelihood of the whole sample is the product

    $$L(\beta)=\prod_{i=1}^{n}p_i^{\,y_i}(1-p_i)^{1-y_i}.$$

    Maximum likelihood chooses $\beta$ to maximize $L$. Products of many probabilities underflow and are awkward to differentiate, so the logarithm is taken, turning the product into a sum. The log-likelihood is

    $$\ell(\beta)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\Big[y_i\ln p_i+(1-y_i)\ln(1-p_i)\Big].$$

    Maximizing $\ell$ is the same as minimizing $-\ell$. Dividing by $n$ to make the objective a per-row average gives the log-loss, also called binary cross-entropy,

    $$J(\beta)=-\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\Big[y_i\ln p_i+(1-y_i)\ln(1-p_i)\Big].$$

    The cross-entropy name comes from information theory: each term is the cross-entropy between the one-hot label distribution and the predicted distribution $(p_i,1-p_i)$. Minimizing it drives the predicted distribution toward the observed labels.

    The Logit Form and the Stable Softplus

    Written in terms of $p_i$, the per-row loss requires a logarithm of a probability that can round to zero or one in floating point. Rewriting it in terms of the raw logit $z_i$ removes that hazard entirely. Substituting $p_i=\sigma(z_i)$ and using $\ln\sigma(z)=-\operatorname{softplus}(-z)$ and $\ln(1-\sigma(z))=-\operatorname{softplus}(z)$, where the softplus function is

    $$\operatorname{softplus}(z)=\ln(1+e^{z}),$$

    the per-row loss collapses to a single expression valid for both labels,

    $$\ell_i(z_i,y_i)=\operatorname{softplus}(z_i)-y_i z_i=\begin{cases}\operatorname{softplus}(-z_i)=-\ln\sigma(z_i)&y_i=1,\\[2pt]\operatorname{softplus}(z_i)=-\ln\big(1-\sigma(z_i)\big)&y_i=0.\end{cases}$$

    The mean objective in logit form is therefore

    $$J(\beta)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\big[\operatorname{softplus}(z_i)-y_i z_i\big],\qquad z_i=\beta^\top\tilde{x}_i.$$

    Softplus is evaluated with a branch on the sign of its argument, $\operatorname{softplus}(z)=z+\ln(1+e^{-z})$ for $z\ge0$ and $\ln(1+e^{z})$ for $z<0$, so the exponential never receives a positive argument and never overflows. This logit form is the one the solvers differentiate, and it stays finite for every finite logit.     The Gradient and the Hessian

    The derivative of softplus is the sigmoid, $\operatorname{softplus}'(z)=\sigma(z)$, so the derivative of the per-row loss with respect to its own logit is the residual between the predicted probability and the label,

    $$\frac{\partial \ell_i}{\partial z_i}=\sigma(z_i)-y_i=p_i-y_i.$$

    Applying the chain rule through $z_i=\beta^\top\tilde{x}_i$, whose gradient is $\tilde{x}_i$, gives the objective gradient. Stacking the design rows into $X$ with a leading column of ones and collecting residuals into the vector $p-y$,

    $$\nabla J(\beta)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(p_i-y_i)\,\tilde{x}_i=\frac{1}{n}X^\top(p-y).$$

    This is the same residual-times-feature structure as the least-squares gradient, with the residual $\hat y_i-y_i$ replaced by $p_i-y_i$. Setting the gradient to zero gives the score equations $X^\top(p-y)=0$; with an intercept column, the first equation forces the average predicted probability to equal the observed positive rate, so a fitted logistic model with an intercept is automatically calibrated in the mean on its training data.

    Differentiating again uses $\sigma'(z)=\sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z))=p(1-p)$. The second derivative of the per-row loss with respect to the coefficients is the outer product $\tilde{x}_i\tilde{x}_i^\top$ scaled by that variance, so the Hessian of the objective is

    $$\nabla^2 J(\beta)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}p_i(1-p_i)\,\tilde{x}_i\tilde{x}_i^\top=\frac{1}{n}X^\top W X,\qquad W=\operatorname{diag}\big(p_i(1-p_i)\big).$$

    Every weight $p_i(1-p_i)$ is nonnegative and at most $0.25$, so $W\succeq0$. This one fact controls the entire optimization story on the next page.

    Convexity

    For any vector $v$ the quadratic form of the Hessian is

    $$v^\top\nabla^2 J(\beta)\,v=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}p_i(1-p_i)\,(\tilde{x}_i^\top v)^2\ge0,$$

    a sum of nonnegative terms, so the Hessian is positive semidefinite at every $\beta$ and $J$ is convex. Any stationary point is therefore a global minimum, and there are no spurious local minima to trap a solver. The objective is strictly convex, guaranteeing a unique minimizer, exactly when the design matrix has full column rank and no weight collapses to zero. Two situations break strict convexity. Collinear features make some direction $v$ satisfy $\tilde{x}_i^\top v=0$ for all $i$, leaving the objective flat along that direction, the same rank problem as in linear regression. Perfect separation drives the fitted probabilities toward zero and one, sending every $p_i(1-p_i)$ to zero and flattening the objective in the tail so that the minimizer escapes to infinity. The regularization page treats the separation case in full.

    ▶ The Convex Log-Loss Bowl and Newton's Local Quadratic

    A one-parameter model $z=\theta x$ on the non-separable data $x=(-2,-1,-1,1,1,2)$, $y=(0,0,1,0,1,1)$. The curve is the mean log-loss $J(\theta)$; each step fits the local quadratic from $J'$ and $J''$ and jumps to its vertex, the Newton update, converging to $\theta^\star=0.7563$.

    The parameter sequence $0\to0.6667\to0.7530\to0.7563\to0.7563$ shows Newton's quadratic convergence: once close, the number of correct digits roughly doubles per step. The curvature $J''(\theta)$ stays strictly positive along the path, from $0.5000$ at the start to $0.3422$ at the optimum, which is the concrete meaning of convexity for this one-parameter slice.

    Log-Loss Is a Proper Scoring Rule

    Beyond being the negative log-likelihood, log-loss is a strictly proper scoring rule, which is the property that makes it a trustworthy training and evaluation target. A scoring rule is proper when reporting the true probability minimizes the expected score. Writing the expected per-row loss when the true probability is $q$ and the model reports $p$,

    $$\mathbb{E}_{y\sim\text{Bernoulli}(q)}\big[\ell(p,y)\big]=-\big[q\ln p+(1-q)\ln(1-p)\big],$$

    and differentiating with respect to $p$ gives $-\big(q/p-(1-q)/(1-p)\big)$, which vanishes only at $p=q$, where the second derivative is positive. The unique minimizer of the expected loss is honest reporting of the true probability, so a model trained to minimize log-loss is rewarded for producing calibrated probabilities rather than merely correct labels. The squared-error Brier score is another proper rule, but log-loss is the one aligned with the Bernoulli likelihood and is the objective the solvers minimize; the calibration and metrics pages return to properness from the evaluation side.

    Saturation and Numerical Failure

    The reason to keep the objective in logit form is a concrete floating-point failure. Once a logit exceeds roughly thirty-seven in magnitude, the naive probability $1/(1+e^{-z})$ rounds to exactly $0$ or $1$, because the neglected term falls below machine precision relative to one. The probability-first log-loss then evaluates a logarithm of zero. When the surviving term carries a positive weight the result is a positive infinity; when the offending term is the zero-weighted one, floating-point arithmetic returns $0\cdot(-\infty)=\text{NaN}$, and a single NaN silently poisons the batch mean and every gradient sum built from it.

    The animation steps through logit and label pairs and prints the naive combined formula beside the stable softplus form. The two agree while the probability is representable and diverge the moment it saturates, with the naive column producing an infinity and then a NaN while the stable column stays finite.

A training loop that averages the naive values reports infinity or NaN and stalls, while the identical model scored with the stable form trains normally.

    Assumptions, Edge Cases, and the Implementation

    • Independence. The product likelihood assumes conditionally independent rows; clustered data need a different likelihood and typically wider intervals.
    • No closed form. The score equations $X^\top(p-y)=0$ are nonlinear in $\beta$ because $p$ passes through the sigmoid, so a minimizer exists in closed form only in trivial cases and is otherwise found iteratively.
    • Existence. A finite maximum-likelihood estimate exists only when the classes are not perfectly separable; otherwise the objective has an infimum it never attains.
    • Mean calibration. An intercept forces the average predicted probability to equal the training positive rate, a built-in check that a fit is not grossly miscalibrated in aggregate.
