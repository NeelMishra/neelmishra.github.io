    Confusion Matrix, ROC-AUC, PR-AUC, and Log-Loss

    July 2026 · 16 min read

    No single number captures how good a classifier is, because a classifier is evaluated on three different questions that do not reduce to one another. Does it rank positives above negatives? At a chosen cut, how does it trade false alarms against misses? Are its probabilities themselves trustworthy? Ranking metrics, thresholded metrics, and probabilistic metrics answer those three questions, and a model can look excellent on one lens while failing another. This page defines each family, derives the identity that gives the ROC-AUC its probabilistic meaning, explains why the precision-recall curve is the honest choice under heavy imbalance, and places log-loss and the Brier score as the metrics that see calibration. The previous page chose an operating point; this page measures the model around it.

    The Confusion Matrix and Its Rates

    At a fixed threshold every prediction falls into one of four cells: true positive, false positive, false negative, or true negative. The counts $\mathrm{TP},\mathrm{FP},\mathrm{FN},\mathrm{TN}$ form the confusion matrix, and every thresholded metric is a ratio built from them. The two that matter most are conditioned on opposite margins of the table. Precision conditions on the prediction and asks what fraction of positive calls are correct; recall, also called sensitivity or the true-positive rate, conditions on the truth and asks what fraction of actual positives are caught,

    $$\text{precision}=\frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP}},\qquad \text{recall}=\text{TPR}=\frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FN}}.$$

    The complementary rates on the negative class are specificity, the true-negative rate, and its complement the false-positive rate,

    $$\text{TNR}=\frac{\mathrm{TN}}{\mathrm{TN}+\mathrm{FP}},\qquad \text{FPR}=\frac{\mathrm{FP}}{\mathrm{FP}+\mathrm{TN}}=1-\text{TNR}.$$

    Single-number summaries combine these. Accuracy is the fraction correct overall; balanced accuracy averages the two class-conditional recalls and so ignores prevalence; the $F_1$ score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, with the $F_\beta$ family giving recall $\beta^2$ times the weight of precision in the denominator; and the Matthews correlation coefficient is the correlation between predictions and truth across the whole table,

    $$F_1=\frac{2\,\text{PR}}{\text{P}+\text{R}},\qquad F_\beta=(1+\beta^2)\frac{\text{PR}}{\beta^2\text{P}+\text{R}},\qquad \mathrm{MCC}=\frac{\mathrm{TP}\cdot\mathrm{TN}-\mathrm{FP}\cdot\mathrm{FN}}{\sqrt{(\mathrm{TP}{+}\mathrm{FP})(\mathrm{TP}{+}\mathrm{FN})(\mathrm{TN}{+}\mathrm{FP})(\mathrm{TN}{+}\mathrm{FN})}}.$$

    The harmonic mean in $F_1$ punishes imbalance between precision and recall, so a model cannot buy a high $F_1$ by maximizing one at the expense of the other. The Matthews coefficient uses all four cells and is robust under imbalance, although it is mathematically undefined when a constant predictor makes a denominator factor zero and software must report that case explicitly rather than silently inventing a correlation. Every metric in this section changes when the threshold moves, so each describes one operating point, not the model as a whole.

    Why Accuracy Misleads Under Imbalance

    Accuracy weights every example identically, so on skewed data it is dominated by the majority class. If ninety-nine percent of examples are negative, the constant predictor that never predicts the positive class scores ninety-nine percent accuracy while catching zero positives, and it beats many genuinely useful models on that metric alone. Accuracy therefore fails to separate an informative classifier from a trivial one exactly when the positive class is rare and most valuable. The rates conditioned within a class, recall and specificity, and summaries built from them such as balanced accuracy and the Matthews coefficient, do not collapse this way because they are computed inside each class before being combined.

    The ROC Curve and the AUC

    Sweeping the threshold from high to low traces a curve in the plane of false-positive rate against true-positive rate: the receiver-operating-characteristic curve. At the strictest threshold nothing is called positive and the curve sits at the origin; as the threshold falls, each newly admitted example steps the curve up if it is a true positive or right if it is a false positive, until the loosest threshold calls everything positive at the top-right corner. The area under this curve, the AUC, summarizes ranking quality across all thresholds at once, with the diagonal $\text{TPR}=\text{FPR}$ representing random guessing at an area of one half and a perfect ranker reaching one.

    The AUC has an exact probabilistic meaning that explains why it is threshold-free. It equals the probability that a uniformly random positive receives a higher score than a uniformly random negative, which is the Mann-Whitney $U$ statistic normalized by the number of positive-negative pairs,

    $$\mathrm{AUC}=\Pr\big(s_i>s_j\mid y_i=1,\,y_j=0\big)=\frac{1}{PN}\sum_{i:y_i=1}\ \sum_{j:y_j=0}\Big[\mathbf{1}(s_i>s_j)+\tfrac12\mathbf{1}(s_i=s_j)\Big],$$

    where $P$ and $N$ count positives and negatives and ties contribute one half. Because this counts pairwise orderings, the AUC depends only on the ranking of scores, not their values, which has two consequences. It is invariant to any strictly monotone transform of the scores, so it cannot see calibration at all: a model and its arbitrarily rescaled twin share an AUC. And it is computed within each class through the two class-conditional rates, so it is far more stable under a change of class balance than accuracy.     The Precision-Recall Curve and Average Precision

    The ROC curve conditions the false-positive rate on the large negative class, so under heavy imbalance a flood of false positives barely moves the false-positive rate and the curve stays deceptively high. The precision-recall curve replaces the false-positive rate with precision, whose denominator $\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP}$ is dominated by those false positives, so it exposes exactly the failure the ROC curve hides. Plotting precision against recall as the threshold sweeps, and summarizing it by the area or by the average precision, gives the honest picture when positives are rare,

    $$\mathrm{AP}=\sum_{k}\big(R_k-R_{k-1}\big)\,P_k,$$

    a sum of precision values weighted by the increments in recall as each positive is retrieved. The crucial difference from ROC is the baseline. A random classifier traces the horizontal line at precision equal to the prevalence, not one half, so on data with one percent positives a random model has an average precision near $0.01$ and any real comparison must be made against that floor. The curve itself is not monotone: retrieving a false positive drops precision before the next true positive lifts it, producing a sawtooth that is often reported with interpolation. On the ten-example set above, five positives among ten give a prevalence baseline of $0.5$ and an average precision of $0.853$, higher than the AUC because precision stays high through the early, confidently ranked positives.

    Probabilistic Metrics: Log-Loss and the Brier Score

    Ranking metrics ignore the numerical value of the scores, and thresholded metrics collapse them to a single bit, so neither can tell whether a predicted probability of $0.9$ actually corresponds to a ninety-percent event. The probabilistic metrics do. Log-loss, derived as the Bernoulli negative log-likelihood on the maximum-likelihood page, and the Brier score, the mean squared error between probability and label, are both strictly proper scoring rules, minimized in expectation only by honest probabilities,

    $$\text{log-loss}=-\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\big[y_i\ln p_i+(1-y_i)\ln(1-p_i)\big],\qquad \text{Brier}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(p_i-y_i)^2.$$

    Both reward two things at once: sharpness, meaning probabilities pushed toward zero and one, and calibration, meaning those probabilities matching observed frequencies. Log-loss is unbounded and punishes a confident wrong prediction severely, since a probability near zero on a positive label sends its logarithm toward negative infinity, while the Brier score is bounded in the unit interval and penalizes the same mistake only quadratically. Because these metrics read the probability values directly, a model that ranks perfectly but reports systematically inflated probabilities earns a strong AUC and a poor log-loss simultaneously, which is the diagnostic signature that ranking is fine but calibration is broken, the subject of the next page.

    Sampling Uncertainty and Confidence Intervals

    Every reported metric is an estimate from a finite test sample. AUC uncertainty can be estimated by DeLong's method or by a bootstrap that resamples independent observational units; precision, recall, and specificity admit binomial intervals only when the threshold was fixed before the test set was examined. The ordinary Wald interval is unreliable near zero or one and with small denominators, so Wilson, exact, or bootstrap intervals are safer. Paired model comparisons resample rows or groups jointly, since evaluating both models on the same cases makes their metric errors correlated.

    Confidence intervals do not repair selection bias. If the same test predictions choose the threshold, select among models, and report the winning metric, a nominal ninety-five-percent interval omits the search and is too narrow. Nested evaluation or a final untouched test set is required. Dependence also changes the resampling unit: patient-level, account-level, or time-block bootstrap samples preserve the clustering that an independent-row bootstrap would erase. With rare positives, the positive count rather than the total row count controls uncertainty in recall and PR-AUC, so a large dataset can still have a very wide interval.

    Three Lenses and When Each Misleads

    The three families answer different questions and fail in different ways, so a serious evaluation reports at least one from each rather than optimizing a single number.

    LensExamplesSeesBlind to
    RankingROC-AUC, PR-AUC, average precisionOrdering of scores; ROC-AUC is class-balance stable, PR is imbalance sensitiveCalibration and the operating threshold entirely
    ThresholdedPrecision, recall, specificity, $F_1$, MCC, accuracyThe realized trade-off at one chosen cutPerformance at every other threshold; accuracy is also blind to imbalance
    ProbabilisticLog-loss, Brier scoreCalibration and sharpness of the probabilitiesNothing about a chosen decision boundary; dominated by confident errors

    The classic traps follow the blind spots. Reporting accuracy on imbalanced data hides a useless model behind the majority baseline. Reporting only AUC hides miscalibration, because AUC cannot see it. Reporting only a thresholded metric ties the verdict to one arbitrary cut and hides how the model behaves elsewhere on the curve. For multiclass problems each rate generalizes through averaging, with macro-averaging treating every class equally and micro-averaging pooling the counts so that frequent classes dominate; the choice encodes whether rare classes should count as much as common ones, and it must be stated because the two can disagree sharply.

    Edge Cases and the Implementation

    • Ties in scores. Equal scores create diagonal segments in the ROC curve and half-credit in the rank sum, so a tie-aware AUC uses the one-half convention rather than an arbitrary ordering.
    • Degenerate classes. With no positives or no negatives the rates have zero denominators and both curves are undefined, so a single-class validation fold cannot be scored by ranking metrics.
    • PR interpolation. The sawtooth precision-recall curve admits several interpolation conventions, and average precision as a running sum avoids the ambiguity that trapezoidal PR-AUC introduces.
    • Metric-threshold coupling. Thresholded metrics require the operating point from the previous page, so reporting them without stating the threshold is incomplete.
