    Gradient Descent, Newton's Method, and IRLS

    July 2026 · 15 min read

    The log-loss objective is convex with no closed-form minimizer, so fitting logistic regression is an iterative optimization problem. Three methods dominate: gradient descent uses only the slope, Newton's method uses the curvature, and iteratively reweighted least squares reveals that a Newton step is nothing more than a weighted least-squares fit. The three share the gradient and Hessian derived on the maximum-likelihood page,

    $$\nabla J(\beta)=\frac{1}{n}X^\top(p-y),\qquad \nabla^2 J(\beta)=\frac{1}{n}X^\top W X,\qquad W=\operatorname{diag}\big(p_i(1-p_i)\big),$$

    and differ only in how much of that information each step uses. The running example is the eight-row study-hours dataset, whose fitted coefficients are $\hat\beta_0=-5.7703$ and $\hat\beta_1=2.5646$ at a minimum log-loss of $0.3129$.

    Gradient Descent

    Gradient descent takes a fixed-size step downhill along the negative gradient,

    $$\beta_{t+1}=\beta_t-\eta\,\nabla J(\beta_t)=\beta_t-\frac{\eta}{n}X^\top(p_t-y),$$

    where $\eta$ is the learning rate. Each step costs one pass over the data to form the residual vector and the matrix-vector product, so the per-iteration cost is $O(nd)$ and the method never forms or factors a $d\times d$ matrix. That cheapness is why gradient descent and its stochastic and quasi-Newton relatives dominate when the feature dimension is large or the design is sparse.

    The price is the step count. Convergence of gradient descent on a convex objective is governed by the conditioning of the Hessian near the optimum. A constant rate converges when $0<\eta<2/L$, where $L$ is the largest curvature, and the best fixed rate contracts the worst error direction by a factor $(\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1)$ per step, where $\kappa=L/\mu$ is the ratio of the largest to smallest curvature. For the study-hours objective the Hessian at the optimum has $\kappa\approx94$, so the worst-direction contraction is about $0.979$ and progress is slow: the animation below shows gradient descent still at a log-loss near $0.47$ after twelve steps while Newton has reached $0.3129$ in five.

    Scaling trap. The condition number $\kappa$ depends on the feature scale. Raw features on wildly different scales inflate $\kappa$ and force tiny learning rates, so standardizing features before gradient descent is an optimization decision as much as an interpretability one. A learning rate tuned on standardized columns can diverge on raw columns because $L$ moved.

    Newton's Method

    Newton's method multiplies the gradient by the inverse Hessian, taking a step that jumps straight to the minimum of the local quadratic model of the objective,

    $$\beta_{t+1}=\beta_t-\big[\nabla^2 J(\beta_t)\big]^{-1}\nabla J(\beta_t)=\beta_t-\big(X^\top W_t X\big)^{-1}X^\top(p_t-y).$$

    Because the objective is convex, the Hessian is positive semidefinite, and where it is positive definite the step is a genuine descent direction. Newton's method converges quadratically once it is near the optimum, meaning the number of correct digits roughly doubles per step, which is why five or six iterations typically suffice. The cost per step is higher: forming $X^\top W X$ is $O(nd^2)$ and solving the linear system is $O(d^3)$, so Newton is preferred when $d$ is moderate and the data fit in memory, and gradient or quasi-Newton methods take over as $d$ grows.

    IRLS: The Weighted Least-Squares View

    The Newton step has a second identity that connects logistic regression directly to ordinary least squares. Expanding the update and grouping terms,

    $$\beta_{t+1}=\big(X^\top W_t X\big)^{-1}X^\top W_t\,\xi_t,\qquad \xi_{t,i}=z_{t,i}+\frac{y_i-p_{t,i}}{p_{t,i}(1-p_{t,i})},$$

    which is exactly the solution of a weighted least-squares problem: regress the working response $\xi_t$ on the design $X$ with weights $W_t$. This is iteratively reweighted least squares. Each iteration linearizes the model around the current fit, builds a synthetic target $\xi$ that is the current logit plus a variance-scaled residual, and solves an ordinary weighted regression. Every intuition about least squares, conditioning, collinearity, and the design matrix therefore transfers directly to logistic fitting.

    The construction is most transparent at the very first iteration, starting from $\beta_0=0$. Every logit is zero, so every probability is $0.5$, every weight is $p(1-p)=0.25$, and the working response is $\xi_i=0+(y_i-0.5)/0.25$, which equals $-2$ for a negative label and $+2$ for a positive one. The weighted regression is then an ordinary least-squares fit of those transformed labels on the feature, and it returns $\beta_1=(-3,\,1.3333)$, identical to the first Newton step. The next iteration is where the reweighting becomes visible: at $\beta_1$ the weights are no longer uniform but range from $0.0806$ for the most confident rows to $0.2432$ near the boundary, so rows the model is unsure about pull the fit harder than rows it has already explained.

    Conditioning and Standardization

    The weight matrix $W$ makes the effective design $W^{1/2}X$, and its conditioning determines both how fast gradient descent converges and how accurately the Newton linear system can be solved. Two forces degrade it. Features on very different scales produce an ill-conditioned $X^\top W X$ exactly as in linear regression, cured by standardizing the columns before fitting and mapping the coefficients back afterward. Approaching separation is more insidious: as the fitted probabilities push toward zero and one, the weights $p_i(1-p_i)$ collapse toward zero, the Hessian loses rank, and its condition number explodes even on well-scaled features. A rising condition number across iterations is therefore an early warning of separation, not merely of bad scaling.

    Damping and Line Search

    The pure Newton step is only guaranteed to help near the optimum. Far away, or when the Hessian is nearly singular, a full step can overshoot and increase the loss. Reliable solvers damp the step,

    $$\beta_{t+1}=\beta_t-\alpha_t\big[\nabla^2 J(\beta_t)\big]^{-1}\nabla J(\beta_t),$$

    and choose the step fraction $\alpha_t\in(0,1]$ by a line search. A backtracking search starts at $\alpha=1$ and halves it until the Armijo condition holds, requiring the loss to drop by at least a small fraction of the amount the linear model predicts. Because the objective is convex and the Newton direction is a descent direction wherever the Hessian is positive definite, backtracking always finds an acceptable step, and near the optimum it accepts the full step and recovers quadratic convergence. When the Hessian is close to singular, adding a small multiple of the identity, $\nabla^2 J+\lambda I$, keeps the system solvable and interpolates between the Newton direction and the gradient direction, the same regularization a trust-region method applies implicitly. Damping and this small ridge are also the first line of defense against the divergence that separation causes.

    Separation and Divergence

    When the classes are linearly separable, the log-likelihood has no finite maximizer, and the solvers reveal this in characteristic ways. Gradient descent keeps increasing the coefficient norm without ever converging, since a larger norm always sharpens the sigmoid and lowers the loss toward zero. Newton's method drives the fitted probabilities to zero and one, at which point every weight $p_i(1-p_i)$ vanishes, the Hessian becomes singular, and the step $\big(X^\top W X\big)^{-1}X^\top(p-y)$ is numerically undefined. A solver that reports enormous coefficients, weights that have underflowed, or a Hessian that has lost rank is diagnosing separation, not converging. The regularization page shows how a penalty restores a finite, well-posed optimum, and quantifies the coefficient blow-up on a separable dataset.

    Edge Cases and the Implementation

    • Rate too large. Gradient descent diverges for $\eta\ge2/L$; a rate safe on standardized features can be unsafe on raw ones because $L$ scales with the features.
    • Singular Hessian. Collinear features or vanishing weights make $X^\top W X$ singular; a small ridge $\lambda I$ or a pseudoinverse keeps the step defined.
    • Convergence tests. A single criterion is brittle; combining a small gradient norm, a small relative loss change, a small parameter change, and a maximum iteration count is more reliable, and a growing coefficient norm should trip a separation check.
    • Reproducibility. For stochastic variants the shuffle seed, batch size, learning-rate schedule, and stopping rule are part of the fitted model.
