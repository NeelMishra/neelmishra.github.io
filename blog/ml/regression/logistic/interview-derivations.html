    Logistic Regression: Essential Interview Derivations

    July 2026 · 13 min read

    Interviews for applied science and machine-learning roles return to the same short list of logistic regression derivations, because each one tests whether the candidate understands why the model works rather than merely how to call it. This page collects those derivations and works each from first principles, in a self-contained form suitable for reproducing on a whiteboard. The results appear in full across the rest of the track; here they are compressed into the argument that produces each one, in the order an interviewer tends to walk them: the sigmoid identities, the loss from maximum likelihood, the gradient, the Hessian and convexity, Newton as reweighted least squares, the softmax generalization, the effect of an L2 penalty, and the interpretation of the coefficients.

    Derivation 1: The Sigmoid and Its Derivative

    The model maps a linear score $z=\beta^\top\tilde x$ to a probability through the logistic sigmoid, whose defining identities are worth reciting because every later derivation uses them,

    $$\sigma(z)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-z}},\qquad 1-\sigma(z)=\sigma(-z),\qquad \sigma'(z)=\sigma(z)\big(1-\sigma(z)\big).$$

    The derivative follows from the quotient rule: writing $\sigma=(1+e^{-z})^{-1}$, its derivative is $e^{-z}(1+e^{-z})^{-2}$, and splitting that as $\tfrac{1}{1+e^{-z}}\cdot\tfrac{e^{-z}}{1+e^{-z}}=\sigma(z)\sigma(-z)=\sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z))$ gives the product form. The inverse of the sigmoid is the logit, $\sigma^{-1}(p)=\ln\frac{p}{1-p}$, so the linear predictor is exactly the log-odds, the fact the interpretation page builds on. The derivative peaks at $0.25$ when $z=0$ and vanishes as $|z|\to\infty$. Cross-entropy cancels this small link derivative in its logit gradient, leaving $p-y$, so a confidently wrong prediction still has a large gradient; separation instead exploits the vanishing residuals of confidently correct predictions.

    Derivation 2: Log-Loss from the Bernoulli Likelihood

    The loss is not chosen by taste; it is the negative log-likelihood of the Bernoulli model. A single labeled point contributes $\Pr(y_i\mid x_i)=p_i^{\,y_i}(1-p_i)^{1-y_i}$, and independence multiplies these into the sample likelihood. Taking the logarithm turns the product into a sum, and negating and averaging gives the log-loss,

    $$J(\beta)=-\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\big[y_i\ln p_i+(1-y_i)\ln(1-p_i)\big]=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\big[\operatorname{softplus}(z_i)-y_i z_i\big],$$

    where the right-hand form substitutes $p_i=\sigma(z_i)$ and uses $\ln\sigma(z)=-\operatorname{softplus}(-z)$ with $\operatorname{softplus}(z)=\ln(1+e^{z})$ to collapse both label cases into one expression that never evaluates a logarithm of a rounded probability. The maximum-likelihood page carries the numerical-stability argument in full; the interview point is that minimizing cross-entropy and maximizing the Bernoulli likelihood are the same optimization.

    Derivation 3: The Gradient Is a Residual

    Differentiating the per-row loss with respect to its own logit uses $\operatorname{softplus}'(z)=\sigma(z)$, so the derivative is the residual between the predicted probability and the label,

    $$\frac{\partial \ell_i}{\partial z_i}=\sigma(z_i)-y_i=p_i-y_i.$$

    The chain rule through $z_i=\beta^\top\tilde x_i$, whose gradient in $\beta$ is $\tilde x_i$, multiplies this residual by the feature vector, and averaging over rows and stacking into the design matrix $X$ gives the compact objective gradient

    $$\nabla J(\beta)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(p_i-y_i)\,\tilde x_i=\frac{1}{n}X^\top(p-y).$$

    This is the same residual-times-feature structure as the least-squares gradient, with the linear residual $\hat y_i-y_i$ replaced by the probability residual $p_i-y_i$. Setting it to zero gives the score equations $X^\top(p-y)=0$, whose intercept row forces the mean predicted probability to equal the observed positive rate, the mean-calibration property used throughout the evaluation pages. This gradient check is the standard defense against a hand-derived or hand-coded gradient bug, and reproducing it is a common follow-up once the closed form has been stated.

    Derivation 4: The Hessian and Convexity

    Differentiating the gradient again uses $\sigma'(z)=p(1-p)$. Each row contributes the outer product $\tilde x_i\tilde x_i^\top$ scaled by that variance, so the Hessian is a weighted Gram matrix,

    $$\nabla^2 J(\beta)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}p_i(1-p_i)\,\tilde x_i\tilde x_i^\top=\frac{1}{n}X^\top W X,\qquad W=\operatorname{diag}\big(p_i(1-p_i)\big).$$

    Convexity follows from a single quadratic-form argument. For any direction $v$,

    $$v^\top\nabla^2 J(\beta)\,v=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}p_i(1-p_i)\,(\tilde x_i^\top v)^2\ge0,$$

    a sum of nonnegative terms because every weight $p_i(1-p_i)$ lies in $[0,\tfrac14]$, so the Hessian is positive semidefinite everywhere and $J$ is convex with no non-global local minima. The fit is strictly convex, and the minimizer unique, exactly when $X$ has full column rank and no weight collapses, which fails under collinearity or perfect separation. That single sign fact, $W\succeq0$, is the load-bearing result the optimization page and the regularization page both stand on.

    Derivation 5: Newton's Step Is Reweighted Least Squares

    Newton's method updates by the inverse-Hessian-scaled gradient, $\beta^{+}=\beta-\big(\nabla^2 J\big)^{-1}\nabla J$. Substituting the two results above and cancelling the shared $1/n$,

    $$\beta^{+}=\beta-\big(X^\top W X\big)^{-1}X^\top(p-y).$$

    This rearranges into a weighted least-squares solve. Defining the working response $z=X\beta+W^{-1}(y-p)$ and expanding,

    $$\big(X^\top W X\big)^{-1}X^\top W z=\big(X^\top W X\big)^{-1}\big(X^\top W X\beta+X^\top(y-p)\big)=\beta-\big(X^\top W X\big)^{-1}X^\top(p-y)=\beta^{+},$$

    so each Newton step is exactly a weighted least-squares regression of the working response $z$ on $X$ with weights $W$, which is iteratively reweighted least squares. The interview payoff is the recognition that fitting a logistic model is a sequence of linear regressions, each reweighting the rows by the current Bernoulli variance and adjusting the target by the current residual.

    Derivation 6: The Softmax Gradient and Its Jacobian

    The multiclass generalization replaces the sigmoid with the softmax $p_k=e^{z_k}/\sum_m e^{z_m}$. Direct exponentiation can overflow, so subtracting $m=\max_k z_k$ from every logit leaves every probability unchanged and gives the stable log-normalizer

    $$\operatorname{LSE}(z)=m+\ln\sum_k e^{z_k-m},\qquad \log p_k=z_k-\operatorname{LSE}(z).$$

    The shift invariance follows because the common factor $e^{-m}$ cancels between numerator and denominator. The softmax Jacobian is the identity most often asked for by name,

    $$\frac{\partial p_k}{\partial z_j}=p_k\big(\delta_{kj}-p_j\big),$$

    derived by the quotient rule: the diagonal term $j=k$ gives $p_k(1-p_k)$ and an off-diagonal term gives $-p_kp_j$. Feeding this through the categorical cross-entropy $\ell=-\sum_k y_k\ln p_k$ with a one-hot label produces a gradient in the logits that telescopes to the same residual form as the binary case,

    $$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial z_j}=\sum_k y_k\big(p_j-\delta_{kj}\big)=p_j-y_j,$$

    so stacking over the class-specific coefficient blocks gives $\nabla_{\!W}=X^\top(P-Y)$ with $P$ the predicted probabilities and $Y$ the one-hot labels. The binary logistic gradient is the two-class special case, which the softmax page develops with the block Hessian and the reference-class identifiability.

    Derivation 7: L2 Regularization Restores a Finite, Unique Solution

    Under perfect separation the maximum-likelihood estimate diverges, because pushing the coefficients to infinity drives the loss toward zero without ever attaining it. Adding an L2 penalty on the non-intercept coefficients bounds the fit,

    $$J_\lambda(\beta)=J(\beta)+\frac{\lambda}{2}\lVert\beta_{1:}\rVert_2^2,\qquad \nabla J_\lambda=\frac{1}{n}X^\top(p-y)+\lambda\beta_{1:},\qquad \nabla^2 J_\lambda=\frac{1}{n}X^\top W X+\lambda I_{1:}.$$

    The penalized Hessian adds $\lambda$ to the curvature in every slope direction. The unpenalized intercept retains positive likelihood curvature for finite logits whenever data are present, so together the terms make the usual intercept-plus-slopes problem strictly convex even under separation or slope collinearity. The smallest eigenvalue need not equal or exceed $\lambda$ because the intercept is unpenalized. Coercivity in the slopes plus overlap in the intercept direction yields a unique finite minimizer, so the penalty converts an ill-posed fit into a well-posed one, the identifiability argument of the regularization page and the reason production logistic models are almost always penalized.

    Derivation 8: Coefficients as Log-Odds Ratios

    Because the linear predictor is the log-odds, a unit increase in feature $x_j$ adds $\beta_j$ to the log-odds and therefore multiplies the odds by $e^{\beta_j}$, independent of the other features' values,

    $$\frac{\operatorname{odds}(x_j+1)}{\operatorname{odds}(x_j)}=e^{\beta_j}.$$

    So $e^{\beta_j}$ is the odds ratio for a one-unit change, the standard interpretation of a logistic coefficient, and the decision boundary $\{x:\beta^\top\tilde x=0\}$ is a hyperplane because it is the level set of a linear function, which is why logistic regression is a linear classifier despite its nonlinear link. The probability-scale effect of the same change is not constant, which is the non-collapsibility caution the interpretation page develops.

    Derivation 9: The Cost-Sensitive Threshold

    Let a false positive cost $C_{\mathrm{FP}}$ and a false negative cost $C_{\mathrm{FN}}$, with correct actions assigned zero cost. At posterior probability $p$, predicting positive has conditional risk $(1-p)C_{\mathrm{FP}}$ and predicting negative has risk $pC_{\mathrm{FN}}$. The positive action is optimal when

    $$(1-p)C_{\mathrm{FP}}\le pC_{\mathrm{FN}}\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad p\ge\frac{C_{\mathrm{FP}}}{C_{\mathrm{FP}}+C_{\mathrm{FN}}}.$$

    The resulting Bayes threshold is one half only under equal costs. Prevalence is already encoded in the posterior $p$, so it is not multiplied into this expression a second time. If scores were learned under a sampled prior, their intercept is corrected to the deployment prior before this rule is applied, as derived on the thresholds and imbalance page.

    ROC, PR, and Calibration Distinctions

    QuestionReasoned answer
    Can AUC remain unchanged after every probability is squared?Yes. Squaring is strictly increasing on $[0,1]$, so every positive-negative ordering is preserved. Calibration and log-loss can change sharply.
    Why can ROC-AUC look strong when precision is poor?FPR divides false positives by all negatives. With a huge negative class, many false alarms can be a small FPR, while precision exposes them in $\mathrm{TP}/(\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP})$.
    Does PR-AUC have a prevalence-free baseline?No. A random ranking has expected precision equal to prevalence, so PR curves from populations with different base rates are not directly comparable without context.
    Can a constant predictor be perfectly calibrated?Yes. Reporting the prevalence for every case is calibrated in the large and conditionally at its only forecast value, but has no discrimination or resolution.
    Does choosing a new threshold improve calibration?No. A threshold changes hard decisions, not probability values. Recalibration changes the score-to-probability map, while thresholding selects an operating point.
    Does Platt scaling improve ROC-AUC?A strictly increasing fitted map preserves all ranks and therefore preserves ROC-AUC. Isotonic pooling can introduce ties and slightly alter it.
