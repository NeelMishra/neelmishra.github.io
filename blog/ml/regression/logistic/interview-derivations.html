    Logistic Regression: Essential Interview Derivations

    July 2026 · 13 min read

    This chapter collects the short derivations that explain logistic regression from first principles. Each result follows from the one before it: sigmoid, likelihood, gradient, Hessian, Newton and IRLS, softmax, regularization, coefficient interpretation, and cost-sensitive decisions.

    Derivation 1: The Sigmoid and Its Derivative

    The linear score $z=\beta^\top\tilde x$ can be any real number. The sigmoid maps it into $(0,1)$:

    $$\sigma(z)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-z}},\qquad 1-\sigma(z)=\sigma(-z),\qquad \sigma'(z)=\sigma(z)\big(1-\sigma(z)\big).$$

    The derivative follows directly:

    $$\frac{d}{dz}(1+e^{-z})^{-1}=\frac{e^{-z}}{(1+e^{-z})^2} =\frac{1}{1+e^{-z}}\frac{e^{-z}}{1+e^{-z}} =\sigma(z)\big(1-\sigma(z)\big).$$

    The inverse function is the logit, $\sigma^{-1}(p)=\ln\frac{p}{1-p}$. Therefore $z$ is the log-odds. The derivative is largest at $z=0$, where it equals $0.25$, and approaches zero for very large positive or negative scores.

    Derivation 2: Log-Loss from the Bernoulli Likelihood

    For one binary label, the Bernoulli probability can represent both cases in one expression:

    $$\Pr(y_i\mid x_i)=p_i^{\,y_i}(1-p_i)^{1-y_i}.$$

    It equals $p_i$ when $y_i=1$ and $1-p_i$ when $y_i=0$. Assuming independent rows, the sample likelihood multiplies these terms. Taking the logarithm turns the product into a sum. Negating and averaging gives log-loss:

    $$J(\beta)=-\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\big[y_i\ln p_i+(1-y_i)\ln(1-p_i)\big]=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\big[\operatorname{softplus}(z_i)-y_i z_i\big],$$

    The second form uses $\operatorname{softplus}(z)=\ln(1+e^z)$ and is stable because it avoids taking the logarithm of a rounded probability. Minimizing binary cross-entropy is therefore exactly the same problem as maximizing the Bernoulli likelihood.

    Derivation 3: The Gradient Is a Residual

    For one row, $\ell_i=\operatorname{softplus}(z_i)-y_i z_i$. Since $\operatorname{softplus}'(z)=\sigma(z)$,

    $$\frac{\partial \ell_i}{\partial z_i}=\sigma(z_i)-y_i=p_i-y_i.$$

    The chain rule contributes $\partial z_i/\partial\beta=\tilde x_i$. Averaging all rows gives

    $$\nabla J(\beta)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(p_i-y_i)\,\tilde x_i=\frac{1}{n}X^\top(p-y).$$

    The pattern is simple: probability residual times feature. At an unpenalized optimum, $X^\top(p-y)=0$. The intercept column contains only ones, so its equation is $\sum_i(p_i-y_i)=0$. Thus the average training probability equals the training positive rate.

    Derivation 4: The Hessian and Convexity

    Differentiating again uses $\partial p_i/\partial z_i=p_i(1-p_i)$. Each row contributes a feature outer product multiplied by this nonnegative weight:

    $$\nabla^2 J(\beta)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}p_i(1-p_i)\,\tilde x_i\tilde x_i^\top=\frac{1}{n}X^\top W X,\qquad W=\operatorname{diag}\big(p_i(1-p_i)\big).$$

    For any direction $v$,

    $$v^\top\nabla^2 J(\beta)\,v=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}p_i(1-p_i)\,(\tilde x_i^\top v)^2\ge0,$$

    Every term is nonnegative, so the Hessian is positive semidefinite and the loss is convex. At any finite coefficient vector, every probability lies strictly between $0$ and $1$. If $X$ has full column rank, the Hessian is then positive definite and the loss is strictly convex.

    Strict convexity does not guarantee existence. It guarantees at most one finite minimizer. Under complete separation, a full-rank loss can remain strictly convex at every finite point while decreasing toward its unattained limit as the coefficients diverge.

    Derivation 5: Newton's Step Is Reweighted Least Squares

    Newton's update is $\beta^{+}=\beta-\big(\nabla^2 J\big)^{-1}\nabla J$. Substituting the gradient and Hessian cancels the shared $1/n$:

    $$\beta^{+}=\beta-\big(X^\top W X\big)^{-1}X^\top(p-y).$$

    Define the working response $\xi=X\beta+W^{-1}(y-p)$. Its weighted least-squares solution is

    $$\big(X^\top W X\big)^{-1}X^\top W \xi =\beta+\big(X^\top W X\big)^{-1}X^\top(y-p) =\beta^{+}.$$

    One Newton step and one weighted least-squares fit therefore produce the same coefficients. The probabilities change after each step, so $W$ and $\xi$ also change. Repeating this process gives iteratively reweighted least squares (IRLS). Numerical code solves the linear system rather than forming the inverse.

    Derivation 6: The Softmax Gradient and Its Jacobian

    For $K$ classes, softmax gives $p_k=e^{z_k}/\sum_m e^{z_m}$. Subtracting $m=\max_k z_k$ from every score leaves the probabilities unchanged and gives the stable log-normalizer

    $$\operatorname{LSE}(z)=m+\ln\sum_k e^{z_k-m},\qquad \log p_k=z_k-\operatorname{LSE}(z).$$

    The common factor cancels between numerator and denominator. The softmax Jacobian is

    $$\frac{\partial p_k}{\partial z_j}=p_k\big(\delta_{kj}-p_j\big),$$

    The diagonal case $j=k$ gives $p_k(1-p_k)$; an off-diagonal case gives $-p_kp_j$. Combining this Jacobian with one-hot cross-entropy $\ell=-\sum_k y_k\ln p_k$ gives

    $$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial z_j}=\sum_k y_k\big(p_j-\delta_{kj}\big)=p_j-y_j,$$

    the same predicted-minus-observed pattern as the binary model. Across all rows and classes, the gradient is $X^\top(P-Y)$.

    Derivation 7: L2 Regularization Restores a Finite, Unique Solution

    Under complete separation, increasing coefficient magnitude keeps lowering unpenalized loss. An L2 penalty on the slopes grows in the opposite direction:

    $$R=\operatorname{diag}(0,1,\ldots,1),\qquad J_\lambda(\beta)=J(\beta)+\frac{\lambda}{2}\beta^\top R\beta,$$ $$\nabla J_\lambda=\frac{1}{n}X^\top(p-y)+\lambda R\beta,\qquad \nabla^2 J_\lambda=\frac{1}{n}X^\top W X+\lambda R.$$

    The zero in $R$ excludes the intercept. The Hessian gains $\lambda$ curvature in every slope direction, so collinear or separating slopes cannot grow for free. If both outcome classes are present and $\lambda>0$, the usual intercept-plus-slopes problem has a unique finite solution. If every label is the same, the unpenalized intercept can still diverge because slope regularization cannot invent the missing class.

    Derivation 8: Coefficients as Log-Odds Ratios

    Holding the other included features fixed, and assuming no interaction changes the effect of $x_j$, a one-unit increase adds $\beta_j$ to the log-odds:

    $$\log\operatorname{odds}(x_j+1)-\log\operatorname{odds}(x_j)=\beta_j.$$

    Exponentiating gives

    $$\frac{\operatorname{odds}(x_j+1)}{\operatorname{odds}(x_j)}=e^{\beta_j}.$$

    Thus $e^{\beta_j}$ is the conditional odds ratio for a one-unit change. The probability change is not constant because the sigmoid is steepest near probability $0.5$ and flatter near $0$ or $1$. Interactions also make the odds ratio depend on the interacting feature.

    The decision boundary $\beta^\top\tilde x=0$ is a hyperplane. This is why logistic regression is a linear classifier even though the sigmoid is nonlinear.

    Derivation 9: The Cost-Sensitive Threshold

    Let false-positive and false-negative costs be $C_{\mathrm{FP}}$ and $C_{\mathrm{FN}}$, with zero cost for correct actions. At calibrated probability $p$, the positive decision has expected cost $(1-p)C_{\mathrm{FP}}$ and the negative decision has expected cost $pC_{\mathrm{FN}}$. The positive action is cheaper when

    $$(1-p)C_{\mathrm{FP}}\le pC_{\mathrm{FN}}\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad p\ge\frac{C_{\mathrm{FP}}}{C_{\mathrm{FP}}+C_{\mathrm{FN}}}.$$

    The threshold is $0.5$ only when the two mistake costs are equal. Prevalence is already inside a calibrated posterior probability and is not multiplied into the threshold again. Scores fitted under a changed sampling prior need an intercept correction first.

    ROC, PR, and Calibration Distinctions

    QuestionReasoned answer
    Can AUC remain unchanged after every probability is squared?Yes. Squaring is strictly increasing on $[0,1]$, so every positive-negative ordering is preserved. Calibration and log-loss can change sharply.
    Why can ROC-AUC look strong when precision is poor?FPR divides false positives by all negatives. With a huge negative class, many false alarms can be a small FPR, while precision exposes them in $\mathrm{TP}/(\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{FP})$.
    Does PR-AUC have a prevalence-free baseline?No. A random ranking has expected precision equal to prevalence, so PR curves from populations with different base rates are not directly comparable without context.
    Can a constant predictor be perfectly calibrated?Yes. Reporting the prevalence for every case is calibrated in the large and conditionally at its only forecast value, but has no discrimination or resolution.
    Does choosing a new threshold improve calibration?No. A threshold changes hard decisions, not probability values. Recalibration changes the score-to-probability map, while thresholding selects an operating point.
    Does Platt scaling improve ROC-AUC?A strictly increasing fitted map preserves all ranks and therefore preserves ROC-AUC. Isotonic pooling can introduce ties and slightly alter it.

    Quick Reference and the Implementation

    The central results compress into a single reference table.

    QuantityResult
    Sigmoid derivative$\sigma'(z)=\sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z))$
    Objective$J=\tfrac1n\sum[\operatorname{softplus}(z_i)-y_iz_i]$
    Gradient$\nabla J=\tfrac1n X^\top(p-y)$
    Hessian$\nabla^2 J=\tfrac1n X^\top W X,\ W=\operatorname{diag}(p_i(1-p_i))\succeq0$
    Newton step$\beta^{+}=(X^\top W X)^{-1}X^\top W \xi,\ \xi=X\beta+W^{-1}(y-p)$
    Softmax Jacobian$\partial p_k/\partial z_j=p_k(\delta_{kj}-p_j)$
    Stable log-sum-exp$m+\ln\sum_k e^{z_k-m}$ with $m=\max_k z_k$
    L2 Hessian$\tfrac1n X^\top W X+\lambda R,\ R=\operatorname{diag}(0,1,\ldots,1)$
    Cost threshold$t^\star=C_{\mathrm{FP}}/(C_{\mathrm{FP}}+C_{\mathrm{FN}})$
    Coefficient$e^{\beta_j}$ is the odds ratio for a unit change
