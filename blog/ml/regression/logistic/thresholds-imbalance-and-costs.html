    Decision Thresholds, Class Imbalance, and Error Costs

    July 2026 · 18 min read

    A fitted logistic model returns a probability, but a deployed system returns a decision. The bridge between the two is a threshold, and choosing it well is a separate problem from fitting the model. This page treats that bridge as a decision-theoretic question: given the cost of each kind of mistake, what threshold minimizes expected loss, why the reflexive choice of one half is almost never that threshold, what class imbalance genuinely damages as opposed to what it is blamed for, and how class weighting, resampling, and a corrected intercept interact with the probability layer. The first page established that probability estimation and decision-making are distinct layers; this page works entirely inside the decision layer.

    From Probability to Decision

    The trained model supplies a conditional probability $p=\Pr(y=1\mid x)$. A hard classifier applies a threshold $t$ and predicts the positive class when $p\ge t$. The threshold is not estimated from the likelihood; it is a property of how the predictions are used, and moving it trades one error type for the other along a fixed model. Lowering $t$ predicts the positive class more often, catching more true positives at the price of more false positives; raising $t$ does the reverse. Because the ranking of examples by $p$ is untouched by the choice of $t$, threshold selection never changes which examples the model considers more likely to be positive; it only changes where the cut falls. That separation is what makes the operating point tunable after training without any refitting.

    Two quantities therefore need to be kept apart. The probability is a statement about the world that the model estimates and that calibration governs. The threshold is a statement about preferences: how bad a false positive is relative to a false negative, and how common the positive class is. Conflating them leads to the common mistake of trying to fix a business cost asymmetry by retraining the model when the correct fix is to move the cut.

    The Cost-Optimal Bayes Threshold

    Assign a cost to every combination of action and truth through a cost matrix $C(a,y)$, where $a\in\{0,1\}$ is the predicted class and $y\in\{0,1\}$ is the actual class. At a point with positive-class probability $p$, the expected cost of each action is a probability-weighted average over the two possible truths,

    $$\mathbb{E}[\text{cost}\mid a{=}1]=p\,C(1,1)+(1-p)\,C(1,0),\qquad \mathbb{E}[\text{cost}\mid a{=}0]=p\,C(0,1)+(1-p)\,C(0,0).$$

    Predicting the positive class is preferable exactly when its expected cost is no larger. Rearranging that inequality, and writing $a=C(1,0)-C(0,0)$ for the extra cost a false positive incurs over a correct negative and $b=C(0,1)-C(1,1)$ for the extra cost a false negative incurs over a correct positive, the condition becomes $p\,b\ge(1-p)\,a$, which solves to a single threshold

    $$t^\star=\frac{a}{a+b}=\frac{C(1,0)-C(0,0)}{\big(C(1,0)-C(0,0)\big)+\big(C(0,1)-C(1,1)\big)}.$$

    When correct decisions carry no cost, so $C(0,0)=C(1,1)=0$ and only the two error costs remain, this collapses to the memorable form in terms of the false-positive and false-negative costs alone,

    $$t^\star=\frac{C_{\mathrm{FP}}}{C_{\mathrm{FP}}+C_{\mathrm{FN}}}.$$

    Only the ratio of costs matters, not their absolute scale, and the base rate does not appear. If missing a positive is five times as costly as a false alarm, then $C_{\mathrm{FN}}=5\,C_{\mathrm{FP}}$ gives $t^\star=1/6\approx0.167$: the system should act on the positive class whenever the estimated probability clears one sixth, far below one half. This is the Bayes-optimal decision rule, and it is optimal only for a calibrated probability. If $p$ systematically overstates or understates the truth, the threshold is applied to the wrong number and the decision inherits the miscalibration; the calibration page supplies the fix.

    Sweeping the Threshold to Minimize Cost

    When the probabilities are trusted, the empirical operating point is found by sweeping the threshold over a validation set and totaling the realized cost at each cut. The animation below does exactly that on a fixed set of twelve validation predictions with four positives and eight negatives, under the asymmetric costs $C_{\mathrm{FP}}=1$ and $C_{\mathrm{FN}}=5$. Each step advances the threshold, recomputes the confusion counts, and totals the cost; the running minimum is tracked, and the closed-form Bayes threshold $t^\star=1/6$ is drawn for reference.

    ▶ Total Cost as the Decision Threshold Sweeps

    Costs are $C_{\mathrm{FP}}=1$, $C_{\mathrm{FN}}=5$. Each bar is the realized cost $1\cdot\text{FP}+5\cdot\text{FN}$ at that threshold on the fixed validation set. Counts and costs are computed at runtime.

    The realized cost is smallest at a threshold of $0.2$, where the model catches every positive and pays only for six false alarms, for a total of $6$. The default threshold of one half costs $13$ on the same data, more than twice as much, because it lets two of the four positives slip through and each missed positive is charged five. The empirical minimizer of $0.2$ sits just above the theoretical Bayes threshold of $0.167$; the two do not coincide exactly because the validation set is finite and the sweep is over a coarse grid, but they point to the same conclusion: the right cut lies well below one half.

    Why One Half Is Rarely the Right Threshold

    A threshold of one half is the Bayes threshold only in the special case $C_{\mathrm{FP}}=C_{\mathrm{FN}}$, where the cost ratio is one and $t^\star=1/2$. Every departure from equal costs moves the optimal cut, and most real problems have unequal costs: a missed fraud, a missed tumor, and a missed equipment failure are not interchangeable with a false alarm. The threshold that maximizes accuracy is also generally not the threshold that minimizes cost, because accuracy weights both error types equally by construction and ignores the cost matrix entirely. A second, quieter reason to distrust one half is that it is only meaningful for a calibrated model; for a model whose scores are monotone but not probabilities, one half is an arbitrary point on an uncalibrated scale, and the sweep is the only defensible way to locate the operating point.

    What Class Imbalance Actually Breaks

    Class imbalance is routinely blamed for biased probabilities, and the blame is usually misplaced. A correctly specified logistic model fitted with an intercept satisfies the score equation $\sum_i(p_i-y_i)=0$, so its average predicted probability equals the observed positive rate on the training data regardless of how rare the positive class is; imbalance alone does not bias the fitted probabilities in the mean. What imbalance genuinely damages is a different list.

    SymptomMechanism
    Accuracy becomes uninformativeA constant predictor of the majority class scores high accuracy, so the metric no longer discriminates useful models from trivial ones.
    The default cut misclassifies the minorityWith few positives, most points have $p$ below one half, so a one-half threshold predicts almost everything negative even when the ranking is informative.
    High-variance coefficient estimatesThe Fisher information contributed by the minority class is small, so its coefficients have large standard errors and unstable signs on resampling.
    Regularization erases minority signalA penalty tuned on the majority-dominated loss shrinks the minority-driven coefficients hardest, since their contribution to the objective is small.
    The objective is dominated by the majorityThe mean log-loss is an average over rows, so the majority class controls most of the gradient and the optimizer spends its capacity there.

    The practical lesson is to separate two ideas that imbalance tends to fuse. Rarity of the positive class is a statistical fact about the data that inflates variance and makes accuracy useless. Cost asymmetry is a preference about decisions that moves the threshold. A dataset can be balanced yet cost-asymmetric, or imbalanced yet cost-symmetric, and the correct response differs. Treating a cost problem with a data-rebalancing hammer distorts the probability model to solve a problem that a threshold move would have solved cleanly.

    Class Weighting, Resampling, and Prior Correction

    Three interventions change how heavily the minority class counts, and all three ultimately act on the effective base rate that the model sees. Class weighting multiplies each row's loss by a class-dependent weight $w_{y_i}$, giving the weighted objective

    $$J_w(\beta)=\frac{1}{\sum_i w_{y_i}}\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_{y_i}\big[\operatorname{softplus}(z_i)-y_i z_i\big],$$

    which up-weights minority errors so the optimizer stops ignoring them. Weighting a class by an integer factor is equivalent to duplicating its rows that many times, so it is a soft form of oversampling. Resampling changes the data directly, by oversampling the minority class, with plain replication or with synthetic interpolation as in SMOTE, or by undersampling the majority class. Undersampling discards information and raises variance; synthetic oversampling can manufacture points in regions the true minority never occupies and tends to overstate confidence. Both change the base rate the model is trained on, and that is the crucial side effect.

    Changing the training base rate shifts the intercept and leaves the slopes intact, because rebalancing is a pure change of the class prior with the class-conditional feature distributions preserved. Under a change of prior, the population log-odds and the sample log-odds differ only by the log ratio of priors,

    $$\operatorname{logit}\Pr_{\text{pop}}(y{=}1\mid x)=\operatorname{logit}\Pr_{\text{sample}}(y{=}1\mid x)+\ln\frac{\pi_1}{\pi_0}-\ln\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0},$$

    where $\pi$ is the true population prior and $\rho$ is the sample prior after rebalancing. Because $x$ enters both sides identically through the slopes, only the intercept absorbs the difference, giving the prior-correction of the intercept

    $$\beta_0^{\text{pop}}=\hat\beta_0+\ln\frac{\pi_1}{\pi_0}-\ln\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0}.$$

    Skipping this correction is the standard reason a model trained on balanced data reports probabilities that are far too high in deployment: it was fitted as though positives were common and never told they are rare. The same log-odds shift reappears on the diagnostics page as the response to a prevalence change in production, since resampling and prior drift are the same operation from opposite directions.

    Lower-risk alternative. Leaving the data and the prior untouched and moving the threshold changes decisions without distorting the probability model at all. Because threshold tuning preserves the ranking and the calibration of the scores, it addresses cost asymmetry directly, whereas weighting and resampling reach the same decisions only after an intercept correction or a recalibration step. Rebalancing earns its place mainly when the minority class is so rare that the optimizer or the regularizer starves its signal, not merely because the costs are unequal.

    Choosing the Operating Point in Practice

    The threshold is a parameter selected on data held out from training, exactly as any tuning choice is, because a cut optimized on the training predictions inherits their optimism and transfers poorly. Beyond minimizing an explicit cost, two other framings are common. The Neyman-Pearson framing fixes a tolerable false-positive rate and maximizes the true-positive rate subject to it, which suits settings where the false-alarm budget is a hard operational constraint rather than a cost; the symmetric version fixes a minimum precision and maximizes recall. Both correspond to reading a specific point off the receiver-operating-characteristic or precision-recall curve of the next page. A third option abandons a single cut entirely: a two-threshold rule predicts positive above an upper cut, negative below a lower cut, and routes the uncertain middle band to human review or to a fallback, trading coverage for reliability where automatic decisions are risky.

    Whatever the framing, the operating point is chosen on the validation predictions, monitored after deployment, and revisited when the prevalence or the cost structure moves, because a threshold that was optimal at one base rate silently loses its optimality when the base rate drifts.

    Validation Without Test Leakage

    Threshold selection is model selection, even though it happens after coefficient fitting. A clean evaluation therefore has three roles: training data estimate the probability model, validation data calibrate it and select the threshold, and an untouched test set estimates the performance of the complete frozen pipeline. Reusing the test set to try several thresholds converts it into validation data and makes the reported cost, precision, and recall optimistic. When data are scarce, the same separation is obtained with nested cross-validation: each outer training fold contains an inner loop that fits the model, any recalibrator, and the threshold, while the outer holdout scores the resulting pipeline exactly once.

    Imbalanced validation adds two constraints. Splits are stratified so every fold contains enough positives to estimate recall and precision, and uncertainty is reported because a threshold supported by only a handful of minority examples is unstable. Temporal or grouped data override ordinary stratification when random splitting would leak future observations or related entities across folds. The final selection criterion is computed from pooled out-of-fold predictions, not from an average of fold-specific thresholds, since averaging cuts does not generally minimize the pooled cost. If a precision floor is required, sampling variation is handled by selecting the largest-recall threshold whose lower confidence bound on precision clears the floor rather than selecting from the point estimate alone.

    Sampling correction comes before threshold selection. Case-control sampling, oversampling, and class weighting alter the effective prior. The intercept correction or a held-out recalibration restores population probabilities first; only then does the cost matrix produce a meaningful Bayes threshold. Tuning a threshold on uncorrected sample probabilities may reproduce one validation confusion matrix, but it does not transfer when the deployment prevalence returns to its population value.

    Assumptions, Edge Cases, and the Implementation

    • Calibration is assumed. The Bayes threshold acts on a true probability; on miscalibrated scores it cuts at the wrong place, so calibration precedes cost-optimal thresholding.
    • Costs must be stable. The cost ratio is a modeling input; if it shifts with time, seasonality, or segment, the optimal threshold shifts with it and a single fixed cut degrades.
    • Prevalence drift moves the empirical optimum. The Bayes threshold depends only on costs, but the realized confusion counts and the calibration both depend on the base rate, so a drifting prevalence is a production failure mode, not a training concern.
    • Ranking is preserved. Thresholding never reorders examples, so any metric that depends only on the ranking is invariant to the choice of $t$, a fact the metrics page makes precise.
