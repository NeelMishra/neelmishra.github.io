    Probability, Log-Odds, Decision Boundaries, and Interpretation

    July 2026 · 17 min read

    The defining feature of logistic regression is the map from a linear score to a probability. That map is smooth and monotone, so the ranking of examples by score equals their ranking by probability, yet the map is nonlinear, so a fixed change in a feature moves the probability by different amounts depending on where the example sits. Reading a fitted model correctly means separating three quantities that the model relates but that behave differently: the linear log-odds, the multiplicative odds, and the bounded probability.

    The Sigmoid, the Logit, and the Odds

    The model links the probability $p=\Pr(Y=1\mid x)$ to the linear score $z=\beta^\top\tilde{x}$ through the logistic function and its inverse,

    $$p=\sigma(z)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-z}},\qquad z=\operatorname{logit}(p)=\ln\frac{p}{1-p}.$$

    The quantity $p/(1-p)$ is the odds of the positive class, the ratio of the probability of the event to the probability of its complement. Odds of $3$ mean the event is three times as likely as its absence, corresponding to $p=0.75$. The logit is the natural logarithm of the odds, which is why the linear predictor is called the log-odds. Three descriptions of the same example therefore coexist: the log-odds $z$ is linear in the features and unbounded, the odds $e^{z}$ are multiplicative and positive, and the probability $\sigma(z)$ is bounded in $(0,1)$ and S-shaped.

    The sigmoid has two properties used constantly downstream. It is symmetric, $\sigma(-z)=1-\sigma(z)$, so the model treats the two classes evenly and flipping the label negates the score. Its derivative factors through the function itself, $\sigma'(z)=\sigma(z)\big(1-\sigma(z)\big)=p(1-p)$, which is the Bernoulli variance and the source of both the gradient and the marginal-effect formulas below. The derivative is largest at $z=0$, where $p=0.5$ and $\sigma'=0.25$, and vanishes as $|z|$ grows, which is the analytic statement that probabilities move slowly once the model is already confident.

    Coefficients as Odds Ratios, and Why Units Matter

    Because the log-odds are linear, a one-unit increase in $x_j$ (holding the other features fixed) adds $\beta_j$ to the log-odds and therefore multiplies the odds by $e^{\beta_j}$. That multiplier is the odds ratio for $x_j$. Writing the odds at $x_j$ and at $x_j+1$,

    $$\frac{\operatorname{odds}(x_j+1)}{\operatorname{odds}(x_j)}=\frac{e^{\beta_0+\cdots+\beta_j(x_j+1)+\cdots}}{e^{\beta_0+\cdots+\beta_j x_j+\cdots}}=e^{\beta_j}.$$

    The odds ratio is constant in $x_j$, which is exactly why the odds scale, and not the probability scale, is where the coefficient has a single clean interpretation. On the probability scale the same coefficient produces different increments at different starting points.

    A running example fixes the arithmetic. Fitting the study-hours model of the roadmap gives $\hat\beta_0=-5.7703$ and $\hat\beta_1=2.5646$, so the odds ratio for one additional hour is $e^{2.5646}=12.9953$. At $x=2$ hours the log-odds are $-0.6411$, the odds are $0.5267$, and the probability is $0.3450$; at $x=3$ hours the log-odds are $1.9234$, the odds are $6.8445$, and the probability is $0.8725$. The odds ratio between the two, $6.8445/0.5267=12.9953$, reproduces $e^{\hat\beta_1}$ exactly, because the two points are one unit apart.

    Unit trap. An odds ratio is meaningless without the unit of its predictor. The same relationship expressed per ten minutes rather than per hour has coefficient $\hat\beta_1/6=0.4274$ and odds ratio $e^{0.4274}=1.5333$, not $12.9953$. Rescaling a feature by a constant $c$ divides its coefficient by $c$ and raises its odds ratio to the power $1/c$. A reported odds ratio of $1.5$ per ten minutes and $13.0$ per hour describe the identical fit, so comparing raw odds ratios across studies without matching units is a common and serious error.

    For a binary predictor the odds ratio compares the two groups directly, and for a categorical predictor with reference coding each level's coefficient is the log-odds ratio against the omitted reference level. A frequent misreading treats the odds ratio as a risk ratio; the two agree only when the outcome is rare, since then $1-p\approx1$ and the odds approximate the probability. For a common outcome an odds ratio of $2$ can correspond to a risk ratio well below $2$, so the phrase twice the odds must not be paraphrased as twice the risk.

    Marginal Effects on the Probability Scale

    Interpreting a coefficient on the probability scale requires the derivative of the fitted probability with respect to the feature. Differentiating $p=\sigma(z)$ through the chain rule gives the marginal effect

    $$\frac{\partial p}{\partial x_j}=\beta_j\,\sigma(z)\big(1-\sigma(z)\big)=\beta_j\,p(1-p).$$

    Unlike the odds ratio, the marginal effect depends on where the example sits. It is largest at the decision boundary, where $p=0.5$ and the factor $p(1-p)$ reaches its maximum of $0.25$, and it shrinks toward zero in either tail. For the study-hours model the marginal effect at the boundary is $\hat\beta_1\cdot0.25=0.6411$ probability per hour, while the same coefficient produces only $0.0282$ probability per hour out at $x=0.5$ where the model is already confident the student fails.

    Because the marginal effect varies across rows, two summaries are reported in practice. The marginal effect at the mean evaluates the derivative at the average feature vector; here $\bar{x}=2.25$ gives $p=0.5$ and a marginal effect of $0.6411$. The average marginal effect averages the per-row derivatives across the sample and equals $0.2472$ for this model. The two disagree because the derivative is nonlinear: the mean sits at the steepest point, whereas the sample spreads into the flat tails, pulling the average down. The average marginal effect is usually the more honest single number for a heterogeneous population, and quoting the effect at the mean without saying so can overstate the typical sensitivity.

    Decision Boundaries Are Threshold-Specific

    A decision rule predicts the positive class when $p\ge t$ for a threshold $t$. Since $\sigma$ is increasing, that rule is equivalent to a linear condition on the score, $z\ge\operatorname{logit}(t)$, so the decision boundary is the hyperplane

    $$\beta^\top\tilde{x}=\operatorname{logit}(t)=\ln\frac{t}{1-t}.$$

    The boundary is linear in the features for every threshold, and changing the threshold shifts the boundary by the constant $\operatorname{logit}(t)$ without rotating it. The default $t=0.5$ gives $\operatorname{logit}(0.5)=0$ and the boundary $\beta^\top\tilde{x}=0$. For the one-feature study model that boundary sits at $x^\star=-\hat\beta_0/\hat\beta_1=2.2500$ hours. Raising the bar to $t=0.75$ moves the boundary to $x=(\operatorname{logit}(0.75)-\hat\beta_0)/\hat\beta_1=2.6784$ hours, and lowering it to $t=0.25$ moves it to $1.8216$ hours. The fitted probabilities never changed; only the line drawn through them did.

    threshold $t$$\operatorname{logit}(t)$boundary $x$ (hours)
    $0.25$$-1.0986$$1.8216$
    $0.50$$0.0000$$2.2500$
    $0.75$$1.0986$$2.6784$

    With two features the boundary $\beta_0+\beta_1x_1+\beta_2x_2=\operatorname{logit}(t)$ is a straight line whose slope $-\beta_1/\beta_2$ is fixed by the coefficients and whose intercept slides with the threshold. Logistic regression can therefore only draw linear separators in feature space; a problem whose true boundary is curved requires a basis expansion of the features before the linear-in-logit form can fit it.

    Interactions Multiply Odds Ratios

    An interaction term lets the effect of one feature depend on another. With two binary predictors and the model $z=\beta_0+\beta_1x_1+\beta_2x_2+\beta_{12}x_1x_2$, the odds ratio for $x_1$ is no longer a single number. When $x_2=0$ the odds ratio for turning $x_1$ on is $e^{\beta_1}$; when $x_2=1$ it becomes $e^{\beta_1+\beta_{12}}$. The interaction coefficient is the log of the ratio between those two odds ratios, so $e^{\beta_{12}}$ is the factor by which one feature rescales the other's odds ratio.

    Taking $\beta_0=-1$, $\beta_1=0.8$, $\beta_2=0.5$, and $\beta_{12}=0.6$, the odds ratio for $x_1$ is $e^{0.8}=2.2255$ at $x_2=0$ and $e^{1.4}=4.0552$ at $x_2=1$, a rescaling by $e^{0.6}=1.8221$. On the probability scale the four cells read $0.2689$, $0.4502$, $0.3775$, and $0.7109$ for $(x_1,x_2)$ equal to $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, $(0,1)$, and $(1,1)$. The interaction is clean on the odds scale and awkward on the probability scale, which is the general lesson: interactions in logistic regression are statements about how odds ratios multiply, and reading them as additive probability effects will misstate their size in every cell.

    Even without an interaction term, effects are not additive in probability. The linear-in-logit form already makes the probability effect of a feature depend on the values of the other features, because they shift $z$ into a flatter or steeper part of the sigmoid. Adding an explicit interaction changes the log-odds structure itself; omitting one does not restore probability-scale additivity.

    Non-Collapsibility and Causal Cautions

    The odds ratio has a property that surprises practitioners trained on linear models: it is non-collapsible. Averaging stratum-specific odds ratios over a covariate does not in general return the marginal odds ratio, even when that covariate is statistically independent of the exposure and therefore not a confounder. The change of value is a mathematical feature of the odds ratio, not evidence of bias.

    A concrete construction makes this unmistakable. An exposure $X$ and a covariate $Z$ are each binary, with $Z$ independent of $X$ and $\Pr(Z=1)=0.5$. Within the $Z=0$ stratum the risks are $\Pr(Y=1\mid X=0)=0.2$ and $\Pr(Y=1\mid X=1)=0.5$; within the $Z=1$ stratum they are $\Pr(Y=1\mid X=0)=0.5$ and $\Pr(Y=1\mid X=1)=0.8$. Both strata have conditional odds ratio

    $$\text{OR}_{Z=0}=\frac{0.5/0.5}{0.2/0.8}=4,\qquad \text{OR}_{Z=1}=\frac{0.8/0.2}{0.5/0.5}=4.$$

    Collapsing over $Z$ gives marginal risks $\Pr(Y=1\mid X=0)=0.35$ and $\Pr(Y=1\mid X=1)=0.65$, whose marginal odds ratio is $\dfrac{0.65/0.35}{0.35/0.65}=3.4490$, strictly smaller than the common conditional value of $4$. In the same construction the risk difference is $0.30$ in each stratum and $0.30$ marginally, so it collapses cleanly while the odds ratio does not. Adjusting a logistic model for a genuine predictor of the outcome therefore changes the exposure coefficient even when the predictor is uncorrelated with the exposure, and that shift must not be read as confounding control.

    Causal caution. A logistic coefficient is a log-odds contrast between subpopulations that differ in one feature; it is a causal effect only under an explicit identification argument, such as randomization or a defended set of adjustment variables. Non-collapsibility means the numeric value of that coefficient depends on which other predictors are in the model even absent confounding, so a change in an odds ratio after adding a covariate is not by itself proof that the covariate was a confounder. Interpreting coefficients causally without stating the design is the most common overreach in applied logistic regression.

    Interpretation Checklist and Edge Cases

    • State the scale. Log-odds are additive and unbounded, odds are multiplicative, probabilities are bounded. A coefficient is linear only in log-odds.
    • State the unit. An odds ratio is per one unit of its predictor; rescaling the predictor changes the number without changing the model.
    • Do not equate odds ratios with risk ratios unless the outcome is rare in every relevant subgroup.
    • Report marginal effects honestly. The effect at the mean overstates the typical sensitivity when the sample spreads into the tails; the average marginal effect is usually preferable.
    • Separation is invisible here. If a predictor perfectly orders the outcome, its coefficient and odds ratio diverge and any finite reported value is an artifact of the stopping rule, a failure treated on the regularization page.
