    Regularization, Identifiability, and Perfect Separation

    July 2026 · 16 min read

    Maximum likelihood can fail to return a finite answer for logistic regression. When a linear combination of the features separates the classes, the coefficients that maximize the likelihood are infinite, and any finite number a solver reports is an artifact of where it stopped. Regularization is the standard cure: a penalty on the coefficients restores a unique, finite, well-conditioned estimate, and it doubles as a variance-reducing device even when separation is not present. This page explains when the estimate fails to exist, why a penalty fixes it, and how the penalty differs from the class weighting used for imbalance.

    Complete and Quasi-Complete Separation

    Complete separation occurs when some coefficient vector $\beta$ classifies every training row correctly with strict inequality, so $\beta^\top\tilde{x}_i>0$ for every positive row and $<0$ for every negative row. Quasi-complete separation is the boundary case in which the same holds with equality permitted, so one or more rows sit exactly on the separating hyperplane. Both are common in small samples, with many features relative to rows, with a category that perfectly predicts the outcome, or after a feature is accidentally constructed from the label.

    Separation is easy to miss because the fit does not error out; it simply produces very large coefficients, very large standard errors, and fitted probabilities pinned at zero and one. A reliable detector watches for a coefficient norm that grows every iteration, standard errors that balloon, and fitted probabilities that saturate. The distinction from a merely strong but finite effect is whether the estimate stabilizes as iterations continue or keeps drifting outward.

    Why the Maximum-Likelihood Estimate Diverges

    The mechanism is direct. Any $\beta$ that separates the classes can be scaled by $c>1$ to form $c\beta$. Every correctly classified positive row has $\beta^\top\tilde{x}_i>0$, so $c\beta^\top\tilde{x}_i$ is even larger and its probability $\sigma(c\beta^\top\tilde{x}_i)$ moves closer to one; every negative row moves closer to zero. The log-loss of every row therefore decreases monotonically as $c$ grows, so

    $$J(c\beta)\ \text{is strictly decreasing in }c,\qquad \lim_{c\to\infty}J(c\beta)=0,$$

    under complete separation, and the infimum $0$ is never attained at any finite $\beta$. The likelihood has no maximizer; the optimization problem is unbounded below in the separating direction. Under quasi-complete separation the coefficients along the separating direction still diverge, while any remaining directions may be estimated finitely, so part of the coefficient vector is well-defined and part runs off to infinity.

    A concrete separable dataset makes the divergence visible. With one feature, $x=(-3,-2,-1,1,2,3)$ and labels $y=(0,0,0,1,1,1)$, the sign of $x$ perfectly predicts the label. Fitting by Newton's method without a penalty gives slopes $0.857,\,1.475,\,2.196,\,3.050,\,3.992,\,4.972,\dots$, climbing by roughly one per iteration and never settling.     L2 and L1 Penalties, Excluding the Intercept

    A penalty adds a term that grows with the coefficient magnitude, turning the unbounded problem into a bounded one. The two standard choices are the squared L2 norm and the L1 norm, applied to the slope coefficients only,

    $$J_{\text{L2}}(\beta)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\big[\operatorname{softplus}(z_i)-y_i z_i\big]+\frac{\lambda}{2}\sum_{j=1}^{d}\beta_j^2,\qquad J_{\text{L1}}(\beta)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\big[\operatorname{softplus}(z_i)-y_i z_i\big]+\lambda\sum_{j=1}^{d}|\beta_j|.$$

    The L2 penalty is smooth and strictly convex in the penalized slopes, so the penalized objective has a unique finite minimizer under ordinary complete separation and when slope features are collinear, provided both outcome classes are represented and the intercept is identifiable. The all-zero or all-one outcome is a counterexample: an unpenalized intercept can still diverge by itself, so slope-only regularization cannot create information about a missing class. The L1 penalty is convex but not differentiable at zero, and its geometry drives some coefficients exactly to zero, performing feature selection as it regularizes. Both add curvature that keeps separated slope coefficients finite; the L2 penalty adds $\lambda I$ to the Hessian on the slope block, which is exactly the ridge that also cured the singular Newton system on the optimization page.

    Do not penalize the intercept. The sums above start at $j=1$, deliberately excluding $\beta_0$. Penalizing the intercept shrinks the baseline log-odds toward zero, which pulls every predicted probability toward one half and destroys the mean-calibration guarantee that the intercept provides. The baseline positive rate is not an effect to be regularized; it is a property of the population. Standard libraries exclude the intercept from the penalty by default for this reason.

    Because both penalties measure coefficients by magnitude, they are sensitive to feature scale: a feature measured in small units has a large coefficient and is penalized more heavily than the identical feature in large units. Features are therefore standardized before the penalty is applied, so that the penalty treats them comparably, and the coefficients are mapped back to raw units afterward for interpretation.

    The Bayesian MAP View

    Each penalty is the negative log of a prior on the coefficients, so penalized maximum likelihood is maximum a posteriori estimation. The L2 penalty corresponds to an independent Gaussian prior $\beta_j\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\tau^2)$ on each slope, since $-\ln\Pr(\beta)=\frac{1}{2\tau^2}\sum_j\beta_j^2+\text{const}$ matches the L2 term with $\lambda\propto1/\tau^2$. The L1 penalty corresponds to a Laplace prior $\Pr(\beta_j)\propto e^{-|\beta_j|/b}$, whose sharp peak at zero is what produces exact zeros. In both cases the intercept is given a flat, improper prior, which is the Bayesian statement of leaving it unpenalized. This view explains regularization as encoding a prior belief that coefficients are near zero absent strong evidence, and it clarifies that a larger $\lambda$ is a stronger prior, not merely a bigger number.

    Identifiability

    Regularization restores identifiability in two distinct failure modes. Under separation the direction of $\beta$ is identified by the data, but its scale is not, because the likelihood keeps rising as the vector lengthens; the penalty supplies the missing information that pins the scale to a finite value. Under collinearity a whole direction in coefficient space leaves the fitted probabilities unchanged, so the likelihood is flat along it and many coefficient vectors tie; the strictly convex L2 penalty breaks the tie by selecting the minimum-norm solution among them. In both cases the penalized problem has a single well-defined optimum where the unpenalized one had none or infinitely many.

    The Regularization Path and Tuning

    Sweeping the penalty strength traces a regularization path, the fitted coefficients as a function of $\lambda$. At large $\lambda$ the coefficients are shrunk near zero; as $\lambda$ decreases they grow, and on separable data they diverge as $\lambda\to0$, recovering the nonexistent maximum-likelihood estimate in the limit. The second animation traces this path for the separable slope: at $\lambda=5$ the slope is $0.1626$, at $\lambda=0.5$ it is $0.7015$, and at $\lambda=0.02$ it has grown to $2.1815$ and is still climbing as the penalty weakens.

    ▶ The L2 Regularization Path

    Each step lowers the penalty $\lambda$ and refits; the slope climbs and would diverge as $\lambda\to0$ because the data are separable. Every point is computed at runtime.

    The penalty strength is a hyperparameter chosen by cross-validation, not by the training loss, which always prefers the smallest penalty. The standard procedure evaluates a logarithmically spaced grid of $\lambda$ values on held-out folds, scores each by a proper metric such as validation log-loss, and selects the value that minimizes it, often warm-starting each fit from the previous $\lambda$ along the path for speed. Because the penalty is scale-dependent, features are standardized inside each training fold so that the tuning does not leak information about the fold's scale.

    Regularization Is Not Class Weighting

    Regularization and class weighting are often confused because both are passed as arguments to the same fitting routine, but they solve different problems and act on different parts of the model. Class weighting multiplies each row's loss by a weight, typically upweighting the minority class, to counter imbalance or to encode asymmetric misclassification costs. It changes which coefficient vector minimizes the now-weighted risk, and its main effect is on the intercept and the effective decision threshold, shifting predicted probabilities to reflect the reweighted class balance. It does nothing to bound the coefficients: a weighted likelihood on separable data still diverges, because scaling a separating $\beta$ still drives every weighted term toward zero.

    Regularization adds a penalty on coefficient magnitude, shrinking the slopes toward zero and bounding them regardless of class balance. It restores identifiability; class weighting does not. The two are orthogonal and frequently used together: weighting sets what the model should optimize for under imbalance and cost asymmetry, while regularization controls how large the coefficients may grow in service of that objective. Treating a weighting scheme as a fix for separation, or a penalty as a fix for imbalance, misapplies both.

    Edge Cases and the Implementation

    • Silent separation. A category that never appears with one class, or a feature derived from the label, separates the data; the symptom is a coefficient that grows with every iteration.
    • Penalized intercept. Accidentally including the intercept in the penalty biases every predicted probability toward one half and voids mean calibration.
    • Unstandardized penalty. Penalizing raw features shrinks large-unit and small-unit versions of the same variable differently; standardize inside the training fold.
    • Tuning leakage. Selecting $\lambda$ or the scaling statistics on the full dataset before splitting leaks the validation folds into training and understates the error.
