    Probability Calibration and Reliability

    July 2026 · 16 min read

    A ranking metric can be perfect while every predicted probability is wrong. The receiver-operating curve of the previous page depends only on the order of the scores, so a model that reports $0.99$ for events that happen half the time earns a flawless AUC and a dangerously overconfident probability. Whenever a downstream decision multiplies the probability by a cost, feeds it into an expected-value calculation, or shows it to a person as a risk, the number itself must be trustworthy, and that property is calibration. This page defines calibration precisely and separates it from discrimination, reads it off a reliability diagram, measures it with expected calibration error and the Brier decomposition, explains why models drift out of calibration, and repairs it with post-hoc Platt, isotonic, and temperature scaling.

    What Calibration Means

    A probabilistic classifier is calibrated when its stated probability matches the long-run frequency of the event among all cases given that probability. Formally, for every reported value $q$,

    $$\Pr\big(Y=1\mid \hat p=q\big)=q,$$

    so that among all examples assigned probability $0.7$, exactly seventy percent are positive. Calibration is not the same as accuracy and not the same as ranking. It is one of two properties a good probability forecast needs, the other being sharpness, the tendency to commit to probabilities near zero and one rather than hugging the base rate. The distinction is sharpest in a degenerate example: a model that ignores every feature and always reports the overall positive rate is perfectly calibrated, because its single stated probability equals the frequency it is evaluated against, yet it is useless because it never discriminates one case from another. Discrimination without calibration and calibration without discrimination are both incomplete; a trustworthy model needs sharp probabilities that are also true.

    Two grains of calibration are worth naming. Calibration-in-the-large asks only that the average predicted probability equal the overall positive rate, a single scalar check. Conditional calibration asks that the match hold at every probability level, which is far stronger. A model can pass the first and fail the second badly, reporting probabilities that are correct on average but too extreme at the ends and too timid in the middle, and only the conditional view reveals it.

    Calibration Intercept and Slope

    A compact model-based diagnostic regresses the outcomes on the original model's logit without refitting all features,

    $$\operatorname{logit}\Pr(Y=1\mid \hat p)=\alpha+\gamma\,\operatorname{logit}(\hat p).$$

    The ideal calibration intercept is $\alpha=0$ and the ideal slope is $\gamma=1$. With the slope fixed at one, a positive intercept means the event is more common than the predictions imply and a negative intercept means it is less common, so $\alpha$ diagnoses calibration-in-the-large and pure prior shift. The slope measures spread: $\gamma<1$ pulls extreme logits toward zero and identifies overconfident or overfit predictions, while $\gamma>1$ indicates probabilities compressed too close to the center. Estimating both parameters together is useful diagnostically, but their interpretations are clearest in the two staged fits because intercept and slope can partially compensate for each other.

    This regression also reveals the relation to Platt scaling. When the base model supplies the logit score $s=\operatorname{logit}(\hat p)$, Platt scaling estimates precisely an intercept and slope on that score. The difference is purpose: the diagnostic reports departure from $(0,1)$, while the fitted Platt map uses the estimated pair to transform future scores.

    The Reliability Diagram

    Conditional calibration is estimated by grouping. The predictions are sorted into bins by their value, and within each bin the mean predicted probability is compared against the observed fraction of positives. Plotting observed frequency against mean prediction gives the reliability diagram, whose diagonal is perfect calibration. Points below the diagonal mark probabilities that are too high for their bin, points above mark probabilities that are too low, and the shape of the departure names the failure. A curve that is flatter than the diagonal, sitting above it at the low end and below it at the high end, is overconfident: it pushes probabilities toward the extremes faster than the evidence warrants. A curve steeper than the diagonal is underconfident, compressing probabilities toward the middle. Notably the mean prediction is $0.490$ against an observed rate of $0.476$, so calibration-in-the-large is nearly perfect even though the conditional calibration is poor: a single-scalar check would have declared this model fine.

    Measuring Calibration Error

    The reliability diagram is summarized by the size of its gaps. The expected calibration error is the bin-weighted average absolute gap between mean prediction and observed frequency, and the maximum calibration error is the worst single bin,

    $$\mathrm{ECE}=\sum_{b=1}^{B}\frac{n_b}{N}\,\big|\bar p_b-\bar y_b\big|,\qquad \mathrm{MCE}=\max_{b}\,\big|\bar p_b-\bar y_b\big|,$$

    with $n_b$ the count in bin $b$, $\bar p_b$ its mean prediction, and $\bar y_b$ its observed positive frequency. The expected calibration error reports the typical miscalibration a random case experiences, while the maximum calibration error guards a worst-case level, which matters when a single badly miscalibrated region drives a costly decision. Both depend on the binning, and coarse bins hide error while fine bins add noise, so an adaptive scheme with equal-count bins is often preferred to fixed-width bins.

    The Brier score decomposes into calibration and discrimination terms, which is why it appears in both this page and the metrics page. Grouping into bins, the Murphy decomposition writes it as reliability minus resolution plus an irreducible uncertainty,

    $$\text{Brier}=\underbrace{\sum_b\frac{n_b}{N}\big(\bar p_b-\bar y_b\big)^2}_{\text{reliability (lower better)}}-\underbrace{\sum_b\frac{n_b}{N}\big(\bar y_b-\bar y\big)^2}_{\text{resolution (higher better)}}+\underbrace{\bar y\,(1-\bar y)}_{\text{uncertainty}}.$$

    The reliability term is the squared analogue of the calibration error and is driven to zero by perfect calibration; the resolution term rewards bins whose observed rate departs from the base rate, which is sharpness; and the uncertainty term is fixed by the base rate alone and cannot be improved by any model. A one-to-one monotone recalibration preserves the information and ranking in the score, but a finite-bin estimate may move because its bins change. Isotonic regression can also pool adjacent scores into ties and lose some resolution. Recalibration is therefore expected to improve reliability, not guaranteed to leave every empirical decomposition term numerically fixed.

    Why Models Miscalibrate

    Several forces pull probabilities away from the diagonal, and recognizing them points to the right fix.

    CauseEffect on calibration
    Strong regularizationShrinking coefficients pulls logits toward zero, compressing probabilities toward the base rate and producing underconfidence.
    Class weighting or resamplingTraining on an altered base rate inflates probabilities unless the intercept is corrected, the prior-correction shift from the thresholds page.
    Model familyNaive Bayes, boosted trees, support-vector scores, and deep networks are often sharply over- or under-confident, needing recalibration by construction.
    MisspecificationA missing nonlinearity or interaction leaves the fitted logit systematically wrong in some region, bending the reliability curve there.
    Distribution shiftA change in the population or the base rate after training degrades calibration even when the ranking survives, a production failure mode covered next.

    Logistic regression is a partial exception at training time. Its score equation forces the average predicted probability to equal the observed positive rate whenever an intercept is included, so a maximum-likelihood logistic fit is automatically calibrated in the large on its own training data. That guarantee is only in-sample, only in the mean, and only for the unregularized fit; it says nothing about conditional calibration, nothing about a held-out set, and it weakens as soon as a penalty or a rebalancing step is introduced. The comfortable belief that logistic regression is always calibrated rests on overreading this single-scalar property.

    Post-hoc Recalibration

    When a model ranks well but reports untrustworthy probabilities, the ranking is worth keeping and only the mapping from score to probability needs repair. Post-hoc recalibration learns a monotone function from the raw scores to calibrated probabilities on a held-out calibration set, kept separate from both training and test so the correction is not fit to the same noise it is meant to remove. Three methods span the usual choices.

    Platt scaling fits a one-dimensional logistic regression on the scores, learning a slope and intercept so that the calibrated probability is a sigmoid of the score,

    $$\hat p_{\text{cal}}=\sigma(a\,s+b),$$

    with $a$ and $b$ estimated by the same log-loss minimization as the base model. It has only two parameters, so it is stable on small calibration sets, but it assumes the distortion is sigmoidal and cannot fix a reliability curve of a different shape. Isotonic regression drops the parametric assumption and fits the best non-decreasing step function from scores to frequencies by the pool-adjacent-violators algorithm, which repeatedly averages any adjacent bins that violate monotonicity. It can match any monotone distortion and so is more flexible, at the price of higher variance and a tendency to overfit unless the calibration set is large. Temperature scaling is the minimal correction for models whose logits are available: a single positive temperature $T$ rescales the logit before the link,

    $$\hat p_{\text{cal}}=\sigma\!\left(\frac{z}{T}\right),$$

    with $T>1$ softening binary probabilities toward one half and $T<1$ sharpening them. Because it divides the logit by a positive constant it preserves the argmax and the entire ranking exactly, which is why it is the default for recalibrating neural-network classifiers without disturbing their accuracy. The standard calibration maps are constrained to be monotone: positive-slope Platt and temperature scaling preserve the order exactly, while isotonic regression is only weakly increasing and can merge tied scores, the one case where a ranking metric can move at all.

    Calibrate, then threshold. The operating point from the thresholds page and the Bayes decision rule both assume a true probability, so recalibration precedes thresholding rather than following it. Fitting the calibration map on a dedicated split and only then selecting the cost-optimal cut keeps the two corrections from contaminating each other, and it means a single recalibration serves every downstream threshold and cost matrix at once.

    Splits, Cross-Validation, and Leakage

    A calibration curve estimated on the same rows used to fit the calibrator is optimistic. The simplest valid pipeline uses disjoint training, calibration, threshold-selection, and test partitions, although calibration and threshold selection may share a validation partition when the final test set remains untouched and the resulting uncertainty is acknowledged. More efficient cross-validated calibration fits the base model on each fold's complement and obtains an out-of-fold score for every training case; the calibrator is fitted only on those out-of-fold scores, after which the base model is refitted on all development data. This prevents the calibrator from learning the base model's in-sample confidence.

    Hyperparameter selection requires an additional nesting level. In each outer fold, the complete inner procedure selects regularization, generates out-of-fold scores, fits the calibration map, and selects any threshold. The outer holdout evaluates that frozen chain. Fitting one global calibrator before cross-validation leaks outer-fold labels into every model and yields reliability curves that are too favorable. The split is made by the independent unit, such as patient or account, and temporal deployment uses forward splits so future outcomes never calibrate past predictions.

    Shift, Decay, and Recalibration

    Calibration is distribution-specific. Under pure prior shift, where class-conditional feature distributions remain fixed and prevalence changes from $\pi_{\mathrm{old}}$ to $\pi_{\mathrm{new}}$, the required correction is an intercept-only log-odds shift,

    $$\operatorname{logit}(p_{\mathrm{new}})=\operatorname{logit}(p_{\mathrm{old}})+\operatorname{logit}(\pi_{\mathrm{new}})-\operatorname{logit}(\pi_{\mathrm{old}}).$$

    This is cheaper and statistically safer than refitting a flexible calibrator. A changing calibration slope indicates more than a base-rate move, such as altered feature effects, changed measurement, or overfitting to an obsolete population. Platt or isotonic recalibration can repair the score-to-probability map when ranking remains useful, but genuine concept drift eventually requires refitting the underlying model. Production recalibration is triggered from delayed labels on a recent, representative window, validated on a later window, and versioned with the base model and threshold so rollback remains possible.

    Edge Cases and the Implementation

    • Binning artifacts. Expected calibration error depends on the bin scheme, so equal-count adaptive bins are more reliable than equal-width bins, and the reported number should always name its binning.
    • Calibration set leakage. Fitting the recalibration map on the training predictions reproduces their optimism, so a held-out split is required for an honest correction.
    • Small-sample isotonic overfitting. The flexibility of isotonic regression becomes a liability on few hundred points, where Platt or temperature scaling generalizes better.
    • Ranking preserved. Every standard recalibration is monotone, so discrimination is retained and only the probability values move, which is exactly the intended separation of concerns.
