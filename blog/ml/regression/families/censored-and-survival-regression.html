    Censored and Survival Regression

    July 2026 · 18 min read

    Survival regression models time to an event when some event times are only partially observed. Right censoring records that the event time exceeds a censoring time. Treating a censored time as an observed event biases the target because the likelihood contribution is different.

    The survival function is $S(t)=P(T > t)$, the density is $f(t)$, and the hazard is $h(t)=f(t)/S(t)$. The cumulative hazard $H(t)=\int_0^t h(u)du$ satisfies $S(t)=e^{-H(t)}$.

    Exact Censoring and Risk-Set Steps

    Five subjects start at risk. One event occurs at time 2, one censoring occurs at time 3, and one event occurs at time 4. The states derive the risk set, Kaplan-Meier survival, and one exact Cox partial-likelihood contribution.

    Censoring and Likelihood

    For observed time $t_i$ and event indicator $\delta_i$, a right-censored observation contributes

    $$L_i=f(t_i\mid x_i)^{\delta_i}S(t_i\mid x_i)^{1-\delta_i}.$$

    An event contributes density at its time, while a censored row contributes survival beyond its last observed time. Independent censoring conditional on modeled covariates is the core identification assumption. Left truncation is distinct: a subject enters the risk set only after an entry time.

    Tobit Regression for a Censored Continuous Outcome

    A left-censored Tobit model starts with a latent continuous response $y_i^*=x_i^T\beta+\varepsilon_i$, where $\varepsilon_i\sim N(0,\sigma^2)$, and records $y_i=\max(c,y_i^*)$. An uncensored row contributes the Gaussian density $\phi((y_i-x_i^T\beta)/\sigma)/\sigma$, while a row observed at the limit contributes

    $$P(y_i^*\le c\mid x_i)=\Phi\left(\frac{c-x_i^T\beta}{\sigma}\right).$$

    Tobit is appropriate when one latent Gaussian process is genuinely censored at a known measurement limit. It is not a generic model for many zeros, voluntary corner solutions, counts, or censored follow-up times. A two-part model is often more credible when participation and positive magnitude arise from different mechanisms.

    Kaplan-Meier and Risk Sets

    The Kaplan-Meier estimator multiplies conditional survival factors over event times:

    $$\hat S(t)=\prod_{t_j\le t}\left(1-\frac{d_j}{n_j}\right),$$

    where $n_j$ is the risk set just before time $t_j$ and $d_j$ is the number of events. Censoring reduces later risk sets but does not create an immediate survival drop.

    Cox Proportional Hazards Regression

    $$h(t\mid x)=h_0(t)e^{x^T\beta}.$$

    The partial likelihood compares the covariates of each event case with the risk set at that event time, eliminating the unspecified baseline hazard from coefficient estimation. A coefficient exponentiates to a hazard ratio under proportional hazards. A hazard ratio is not a constant ratio of survival probabilities or median times.

    $$L_p(\beta)=\prod_{i:\delta_i=1}\frac{e^{x_i^T\beta}}{\sum_{j\in R(t_i)}e^{x_j^T\beta}}.$$

    For one event with event-case $x=1$, risk-set values $(1,0,-1)$, and $\beta=\log 2$, the partial-likelihood contribution is $2/(2+1+1/2)=4/7$. Proportional hazards means the covariate hazard ratio is constant over time. Schoenfeld residual trends and time interactions diagnose violations. Tied event times require an approximation or exact method such as Breslow or Efron handling. Time-varying covariates use updated values over risk intervals and must not import information recorded after the interval being scored.

    Accelerated Failure Time Models

    An AFT model writes $\log T=x^T\beta+\varepsilon$. Exponentiated coefficients are time ratios. Parametric choices such as Weibull, log-normal, or log-logistic determine the error distribution and tail behavior. Weibull is both proportional hazards and AFT under different parameterizations.

    Interview distinction. Cox coefficients describe relative instantaneous hazards. AFT coefficients describe multiplicative changes in event time. The interpretations are not interchangeable.

    Evaluation and Validation

    • Harrell concordance measures ranking but can be biased under heavy censoring.
    • Time-dependent AUC and Brier scores require censoring-aware weighting.
    • Calibration compares predicted and observed survival at stated horizons.
    • Random row splits can violate temporal deployment structure.
    • Competing risks require cause-specific hazards or cumulative-incidence methods.

    Worked Kaplan-Meier Step

    With five subjects at risk before time 2 and one event at time 2, survival becomes $4/5=0.8$. If one subject is censored at time 3, survival remains $0.8$, but the later risk set loses that subject. With three at risk and one event at time 4, survival becomes $0.8(2/3)=0.5333$.

    Likelihood foundations connect to generalized linear models, while split and metric safeguards connect to diagnostics and evaluation.

    Interview Quick Checks

    PromptComplete answer
    Why not regress observed times directly?Censored rows do not reveal event times, and ordinary regression treats their censoring times as complete outcomes.
    What changes at a censoring time?The estimated survival curve stays level, while the censored subject leaves future risk sets.
    What does a Cox coefficient mean?Its exponential is a conditional hazard ratio under the proportional-hazards assumption and the fitted covariate specification.
    Censoring versus truncation?Censoring gives partial information about an included subject. Truncation determines whether the subject enters the observed sample or risk set.
