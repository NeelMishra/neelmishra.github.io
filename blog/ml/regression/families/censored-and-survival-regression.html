    Censored and Survival Regression

    July 2026 · 18 min read

    Survival analysis models time until an event such as failure, recovery, churn, or death. The event is not observed for every subject during follow-up. If a subject leaves the study event-free at time $c$, the data reveal only that the event time $T$ is greater than $c$. This is right censoring.

    The censoring time is not the event time. Treating it as an observed event makes events appear earlier than they really occurred.

    Four functions describe event time:

    • Survival: $S(t)=P(T>t)$, the probability of remaining event-free beyond $t$.
    • Density: $f(t)$, the distribution of event times.
    • Hazard: $h(t)=f(t)/S(t)$, the instantaneous event rate at $t$ among subjects still event-free just before $t$.
    • Cumulative hazard: $H(t)=\int_0^t h(u)\,du$, with $S(t)=e^{-H(t)}$.

    A hazard is a rate, not the probability that the event has happened by time $t$.

    How Events and Censoring Change a Risk Set

    Five subjects start at risk. One event occurs at time 2, one censoring occurs at time 3, and one event occurs at time 4. The states derive the risk set, Kaplan-Meier survival, and one exact Cox partial-likelihood contribution.

    Censoring and Likelihood

    Let $t_i$ be the observed follow-up time and let $\delta_i=1$ when the event was observed and $0$ when the row was censored. Its likelihood contribution is

    $$L_i=f(t_i\mid x_i)^{\delta_i}S(t_i\mid x_i)^{1-\delta_i}.$$
    • An observed event contributes the density $f(t_i\mid x_i)$ at its event time.
    • A censored row contributes $S(t_i\mid x_i)$ because it is known to survive beyond its last observed time.

    The central assumption is conditionally independent censoring: after the modeled covariates are known, censoring does not reveal extra information about the unseen event time. For example, censoring caused by worsening unrecorded health can violate this assumption.

    Left truncation is different. It means a subject is observed only after surviving to an entry time. That subject enters each risk set only after entry.

    Tobit Regression for a Censored Continuous Outcome

    Tobit regression addresses a different kind of censoring: a continuous measurement clipped at a known limit. A left-censored Tobit model assumes an unseen value

    $$y_i^*=x_i^\top\beta+\varepsilon_i,\qquad \varepsilon_i\sim N(0,\sigma^2),$$

    but records $y_i=\max(c,y_i^*)$. Values below $c$ all appear as $c$.

    An uncensored value contributes a Gaussian density. A value observed at the limit contributes the probability that the latent value was at or below that limit:

    $$P(y_i^*\le c\mid x_i)=\Phi\left(\frac{c-x_i^\top\beta}{\sigma}\right).$$

    Tobit is suitable only when one latent Gaussian process is genuinely clipped. It is not a generic model for counts, ordinary survival follow-up, voluntary zero spending, or any dataset with many zeros. A two-part model is more credible when participation and positive amount come from different mechanisms.

    Kaplan-Meier and Risk Sets

    The risk set at time $t_j$ contains subjects who entered observation and have had neither an event nor censoring before that time. If $n_j$ subjects are at risk just before $t_j$ and $d_j$ events occur, the estimated conditional chance of surviving that time is $1-d_j/n_j$.

    Kaplan-Meier multiplies these factors over event times:

    $$\hat S(t)=\prod_{t_j\le t}\left(1-\frac{d_j}{n_j}\right),$$

    Censoring causes no immediate drop because no event was observed. It only removes that subject from later risk sets.

    Cox Proportional Hazards Regression

    $$h(t\mid x)=h_0(t)e^{x^T\beta}.$$

    The Cox model leaves the baseline hazard $h_0(t)$ unspecified. Covariates multiply that shared baseline. Holding other covariates fixed, a one-unit increase in $x_j$ multiplies the instantaneous hazard by $e^{\beta_j}$.

    For distinct event times, the partial likelihood compares each event subject with everyone in the risk set at that time:

    $$L_p(\beta)=\prod_{i:\delta_i=1}\frac{e^{x_i^T\beta}}{\sum_{j\in R(t_i)}e^{x_j^T\beta}}.$$

    The common $h_0(t)$ cancels, so $\beta$ can be estimated without choosing a baseline-hazard shape. Baseline survival is still needed for absolute survival predictions and is estimated afterward.

    For one event with event-subject value $x=1$, risk-set values $(1,0,-1)$, and $\beta=\log 2$, the contribution is

    $$\frac{e^{\log 2}}{e^{\log 2}+e^0+e^{-\log 2}}=\frac{2}{2+1+1/2}=\frac{4}{7}.$$

    Proportional hazards means each covariate hazard ratio stays constant over time. Schoenfeld residual trends and time interactions can reveal violations. Tied event times use Breslow or Efron approximations, or an exact partial-likelihood method. Time-varying covariates use the value available during each risk interval, never information recorded later.

    Hazard ratio is not a time ratio. A hazard ratio of $2$ means twice the instantaneous event rate under the model. It does not mean half the median survival time or twice the event probability at every horizon.

    Accelerated Failure Time Models

    An accelerated failure time (AFT) model works directly on log event time:

    $$\log T=x^\top\beta+\varepsilon.$$

    Holding other covariates fixed, a one-unit increase in $x_j$ multiplies event time by $e^{\beta_j}$. A value above one stretches time; a value below one shortens it. Weibull, log-normal, and log-logistic choices give different error and tail shapes. The Weibull family can be written in both proportional-hazards and AFT forms under different parameterizations.

    Interview distinction. Cox coefficients describe relative instantaneous hazards. AFT coefficients describe multiplicative changes in event time. The interpretations are not interchangeable.

    Evaluation and Validation

    • Concordance: Harrell's C measures ranking of comparable event times but can become biased under heavy censoring.
    • Time-specific accuracy: time-dependent AUC and Brier scores need inverse-probability-of-censoring weights or another censoring-aware estimator.
    • Calibration: predicted survival is compared with observed survival at stated time horizons.
    • Splitting: deployment through time usually needs forward or time-aware validation rather than random row splits.
    • Competing risks: an event that prevents the event of interest needs cause-specific hazards or cumulative-incidence methods. Treating it as ordinary censoring can overstate event probability.

    Worked Kaplan-Meier Step

    Five subjects are at risk before time $2$, and one event occurs:

    $$\hat S(2)=1\left(1-\frac{1}{5}\right)=\frac45=0.8.$$

    One subject is censored at time $3$. Survival stays at $0.8$, but that subject leaves future risk sets. Three subjects remain at risk just before time $4$, when one event occurs:

    $$\hat S(4)=0.8\left(1-\frac13\right)=0.8\left(\frac23\right)=0.5333.$$

    Likelihood foundations connect to generalized linear models, while split and metric safeguards connect to diagnostics and evaluation.

    Interview Quick Checks

    PromptComplete answer
    Why not regress observed times directly?Censored rows do not reveal event times, and ordinary regression treats their censoring times as complete outcomes.
    What changes at a censoring time?The estimated survival curve stays level, while the censored subject leaves future risk sets.
    What does a Cox coefficient mean?Its exponential is a conditional hazard ratio under the proportional-hazards assumption and the fitted covariate specification.
    Censoring versus truncation?Censoring gives partial information about an included subject. Truncation determines whether the subject enters the observed sample or risk set.
