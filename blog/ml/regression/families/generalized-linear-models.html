    Generalized Linear Models

    July 2026 · 17 min read

    Ordinary linear regression can predict any real number. Its classical Gaussian model also uses constant variance. That combination is a poor match for probabilities, counts, and positive amounts. A generalized linear model (GLM) keeps a linear score but changes how that score connects to the outcome.

    A GLM has three parts:

    1. Response family: the possible outcome values and how variance changes with the mean.
    2. Linear predictor: the score $\eta=X\beta$.
    3. Link: a function connecting the conditional mean $\mu=E[Y\mid X]$ to the score, $g(\mu)=\eta$.

    The inverse link returns the mean from the score:

    • Gaussian with identity link: $\mu=\eta$.
    • Bernoulli with logit link: $\mu=\sigma(\eta)$, which is logistic regression.
    • Poisson with log link: $\mu=e^\eta$, which keeps the expected count positive.

    Poisson Rate, Exposure, and Count

    The stated model has $\eta=\log(2)+0.2x$ and exposure $t=3$. The animation derives rate $e^\eta$, expected count $t e^\eta$, and a deviance contribution for observed count $y=8$ at $x=1$.

    Exponential-Family Form

    Standard GLM response families share the exponential-family form

    $$f(y;\theta,\phi)=\exp\left(\frac{y\theta-b(\theta)}{a(\phi)}+c(y,\phi)\right).$$

    Here, $\theta$ is the natural parameter and $\phi$ controls dispersion. The function $b$ normalizes the distribution and also determines its moments:

    $$E[Y]=b'(\theta),\qquad \operatorname{Var}(Y)=b''(\theta)a(\phi).$$

    A canonical link makes the linear predictor equal the natural parameter, $\eta=\theta$. This simplifies the score equations, but a different link can be chosen when scientific interpretation or known response behavior supports it.

    Likelihood and IRLS

    Maximum likelihood usually has no one-step coefficient formula. Fisher scoring turns each update into iteratively reweighted least squares (IRLS). At the current coefficients, it creates a working response and weight:

    $$z_i=\eta_i+(y_i-\mu_i)g'(\mu_i),\qquad w_i=\frac{1}{\operatorname{Var}(Y_i)[g'(\mu_i)]^2}.$$
    1. The current score $\eta_i$ is mapped to a mean $\mu_i$.
    2. The working response $z_i$ adjusts that score using the response error $y_i-\mu_i$.
    3. The weight $w_i$ measures local precision on the score scale.
    4. Weighted least squares of $z$ on $X$ produces the next coefficients.

    The response, weights, and means are recomputed after every update. Stable implementations also protect against exponential overflow, singular weighted designs, boundary probabilities, and nonfinite likelihoods.

    Deviance, Dispersion, and Diagnostics

    Deviance is twice the log-likelihood gap between the fitted model and a saturated model that fits every row. Deviance residuals divide this gap into signed row contributions. Pearson residuals divide $y_i-\mu_i$ by the modeled standard deviation.

    For Poisson regression, conditional variance should equal the conditional mean. Variance much larger than the mean is overdispersion. It can come from missing predictors, correlated rows, unobserved groups, or genuine extra count variation. Quasi-Poisson, negative binomial, random effects, or a better mean model address different causes, so the cause matters.

    An offset is a known term added to $\eta$ with coefficient fixed at one. For count exposure $t_i$,

    $$\log\mu_i=\log t_i+x_i^\top\beta.$$

    The offset $\log t_i$ lets the model estimate a rate while predicting a count. It is not an ordinary feature and its coefficient is not fitted.

    Count, Positive, and Zero-Heavy Responses

    Outcome patternStarting familyMain point
    Counts with variance near the meanPoissonZero is valid; $\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid X)=\mu$
    Overdispersed countsNegative binomialZero is valid; a common form is $\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid X)=\mu+\alpha\mu^2$
    Strictly positive continuous valuesGammaExact zero is not valid; variance is proportional to $\mu^2$
    Exact zeros plus positive continuous valuesTweedie with $1<p<2$Compound Poisson-Gamma behavior uses $\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid X)=\phi\mu^p$

    Ordered categories need an ordinal model, often cumulative logits with a proportional-odds assumption. This is related to link-based regression but is not an ordinary single-mean GLM.

    A zero-inflated model mixes a structural-zero process with a count process that may also produce zeros. A hurdle model first models zero versus positive, then models positive counts with a zero-truncated distribution. Many observed zeros do not prove that either two-process model is needed; an ordinary Poisson or negative binomial fit may already predict many zeros.

    Exact Poisson Exposure Example

    For $\log(\text{rate})=\log 2+0.2x$, exposure $t=3$, and $x=1$,

    $$\text{rate}=e^{\log 2+0.2}=2e^{0.2}=2.4428,$$ $$\mu=t\cdot\text{rate}=3(2.4428)=7.3284.$$

    If the observed count is $y=8$, its Poisson deviance contribution is

    $$2\left[y\log\left(\frac{y}{\mu}\right)-(y-\mu)\right]=0.0597.$$

    A one-unit increase in $x$ multiplies the rate by $e^{0.2}=1.2214$, or about $22.1\%$. It does not add a constant number of events because expected count also depends on the starting rate and exposure.

    Interview Failure Modes

    • A link constrains the mean, while a distribution specifies the likelihood and variance.
    • A canonical link is convenient rather than universally mandatory.
    • Quasi-likelihood estimates a mean-variance relationship without a full response likelihood.
    • Complete separation can make unregularized logistic estimates diverge.
    • Zero inflation is not established merely by observing many zeros; the fitted count model determines the expected zero rate.

    Detailed Bernoulli likelihood and optimization derivations appear in MLE and log-loss and Newton and IRLS.

    Interview Quick Checks

    PromptComplete answer
    What makes a model a GLM?An exponential-family response, a linear predictor, and a link connecting the conditional mean to that predictor form the standard GLM contract.
    Link versus inverse link?The link maps the mean to the linear predictor. The inverse link maps the linear predictor back to the response-mean scale.
    Why does IRLS work?A second-order likelihood approximation creates a working response and local precision weights, producing a weighted least-squares update.
    What does deviance measure?Deviance is twice the log-likelihood gap between a saturated model and the fitted model, scaled according to the family.
