    Generalized Linear Models

    July 2026 · 17 min read

    A generalized linear model combines a response distribution, a linear predictor $\eta=X\beta$, and a link function $g$ satisfying $g(\mu)=\eta$ for $\mu=E[Y\mid X]$. This separation preserves a linear coefficient structure while respecting binary, count, positive, and other constrained responses.

    Linear and logistic regression are GLMs. Gaussian regression with identity link returns $\mu=\eta$. Bernoulli regression with logit link returns $\mu=1/(1+e^{-\eta})$. Poisson regression with log link returns $\mu=e^\eta$.

    Poisson Rate, Exposure, and Count

    The stated model has $\eta=\log(2)+0.2x$ and exposure $t=3$. The animation derives rate $e^\eta$, expected count $t e^\eta$, and a deviance contribution for observed count $y=8$ at $x=1$.

    Exponential-Family Form

    $$f(y;\theta,\phi)=\exp\left(\frac{y\theta-b(\theta)}{a(\phi)}+c(y,\phi)\right).$$

    The log-partition function gives $E[Y]=b'(\theta)$ and $\operatorname{Var}(Y)=b''(\theta)a(\phi)$. A canonical link sets $\eta=\theta$. Canonical links simplify score equations but are not always the best scientific choice.

    Likelihood and IRLS

    Maximum likelihood produces score equations that Newton scoring solves through iteratively reweighted least squares. At the current mean,

    $$z_i=\eta_i+(y_i-\mu_i)g'(\mu_i),\qquad w_i=\frac{1}{\operatorname{Var}(Y_i)[g'(\mu_i)]^2}.$$

    Each iteration solves weighted least squares of $z$ on $X$. Stable implementations guard against overflow, singular weighted designs, vanishing variance near boundaries, and nonfinite likelihood values.

    Deviance, Dispersion, and Diagnostics

    Deviance compares the fitted log-likelihood with a saturated model. Deviance residuals allocate signed contributions to that gap. Pearson residuals standardize by the modeled variance. Count data with variance far above the mean violate Poisson equidispersion and may require quasi-Poisson, negative binomial, random effects, or an omitted-structure investigation.

    Offsets enter $\eta$ with a fixed coefficient of one. A log exposure offset in Poisson regression converts expected counts into rates. An offset is not an ordinary feature and is not estimated.

    Count, Positive, and Zero-Heavy Responses

    Poisson regression assumes $\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid X)=\mu$. A negative binomial model adds a dispersion parameter, commonly $\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid X)=\mu+\alpha\mu^2$, and is appropriate when unexplained count heterogeneity produces overdispersion. Gamma regression targets positive continuous responses with variance proportional to $\mu^2$; a log link gives multiplicative mean effects. Neither family accepts structural negative values or exact zeros under its ordinary support.

    Tweedie models with power variance $\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid X)=\phi\mu^p$ include compound Poisson-Gamma behavior for $1<p<2$, useful for a point mass at zero plus positive continuous values. Ordinal regression instead models ordered category probabilities, often through cumulative logits and a proportional-odds assumption; it is related to the link-function framework but is not an ordinary single-mean GLM.

    A zero-inflated model combines a structural-zero process with a count process. A hurdle model separately models zero versus positive outcomes and a zero-truncated positive count. Many zeros alone are not evidence for either construction because an ordinary Poisson or negative binomial model can imply a high zero probability. Sampling design and a defensible two-process mechanism are required.

    Exact Poisson Exposure Example

    Let $\log(\text{rate})=\log 2+0.2x$ and exposure $t=3$. At $x=1$, the rate is $2e^{0.2}=2.4428$ events per exposure unit and the expected count is $\mu=3(2e^{0.2})=7.3284$. If $y=8$, the Poisson deviance contribution is $2[y\log(y/\mu)-(y-\mu)]=0.0597$. The coefficient gives a rate ratio $e^{0.2}=1.2214$, not a constant count increase.

    Interview Failure Modes

    • A link constrains the mean, while a distribution specifies the likelihood and variance.
    • A canonical link is convenient rather than universally mandatory.
    • Quasi-likelihood estimates a mean-variance relationship without a full response likelihood.
    • Complete separation can make unregularized logistic estimates diverge.
    • Zero inflation is not established merely by observing many zeros; the fitted count model determines the expected zero rate.

    Detailed Bernoulli likelihood and optimization derivations appear in MLE and log-loss and Newton and IRLS.

    Interview Quick Checks

    PromptComplete answer
    What makes a model a GLM?An exponential-family response, a linear predictor, and a link connecting the conditional mean to that predictor form the standard GLM contract.
    Link versus inverse link?The link maps the mean to the linear predictor. The inverse link maps the linear predictor back to the response-mean scale.
    Why does IRLS work?A second-order likelihood approximation creates a working response and local precision weights, producing a weighted least-squares update.
    What does deviance measure?Deviance is twice the log-likelihood gap between a saturated model and the fitted model, scaled according to the family.
