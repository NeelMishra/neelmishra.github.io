    Bayesian Regression

    July 2026 · 17 min read

    Bayesian regression describes uncertainty about coefficients with probability distributions. It combines three pieces:

    • Prior: plausible coefficient values before the current outcomes are used.
    • Likelihood: how well each coefficient value explains the observed outcomes.
    • Posterior: the updated distribution after prior and likelihood are combined.
    $$p(\beta,\sigma^2\mid y,X)\propto p(y\mid X,\beta,\sigma^2)p(\beta,\sigma^2).$$

    The symbol $\propto$ means proportional to. The omitted normalizing constant makes the posterior integrate to one. Unlike a single point estimate, the posterior keeps uncertainty and dependence among parameters. Prediction then averages over that uncertainty.

    How Prior and Data Precision Combine

    The three states combine prior $\beta\sim N(0,4)$ with likelihood summary $\hat\beta\mid\beta\sim N(\beta,1)$ at $\hat\beta=2$, yielding posterior $N(1.6,0.8)$.

    Gaussian Conjugacy

    A prior is conjugate when the posterior stays in the same distribution family. With

    $$y\mid\beta\sim N(X\beta,\sigma^2I),\qquad \beta\sim N(m_0,V_0),$$

    and known $\sigma^2$, the posterior is $N(m_n,V_n)$:

    $$V_n^{-1}=V_0^{-1}+\frac{X^\top X}{\sigma^2},\qquad m_n=V_n\left(V_0^{-1}m_0+\frac{X^\top y}{\sigma^2}\right).$$

    Precision is inverse variance, or inverse covariance in several dimensions. The first equation says

    $$\text{posterior precision}=\text{prior precision}+\text{data precision}.$$

    The posterior mean balances the prior mean with the information in the data. More precise information receives more weight.

    A zero-centered spherical Gaussian prior has negative log density proportional to $\lVert\beta\rVert_2^2$, so the posterior mode matches ridge regression. A Laplace prior has an absolute-value penalty, so its posterior mode resembles lasso. The full posterior contains uncertainty and dependence that a mode alone discards.

    Posterior Prediction and Intervals

    For a new feature row $x_*$, the posterior mean response is $x_*^\top m_n$. Uncertainty about that mean is

    $$\operatorname{Var}(x_*^\top\beta\mid y)=x_*^\top V_nx_*.$$

    A future observation also contains fresh noise:

    $$\operatorname{Var}(Y_*\mid y,x_*)=x_*^\top V_nx_*+\sigma^2.$$

    The posterior predictive interval is therefore wider than an interval for the conditional mean.

    A Bayesian $95\%$ credible interval contains $95\%$ of the posterior probability under the stated model and prior. A frequentist $95\%$ confidence procedure targets $95\%$ coverage over repeated samples. The numerical intervals can look similar in simple models, but their interpretations differ.

    Hierarchical Regression and Partial Pooling

    Several stores, hospitals, or regions may need separate regression effects.

    ApproachWhat it does
    Complete poolingUses one shared coefficient and ignores group differences
    No poolingFits each group independently and shares no information
    Partial poolingLets group coefficients differ while learning a shared population distribution

    Partial pooling pulls noisy, data-poor groups more strongly toward the common mean. Data-rich groups move less because their own likelihood is more precise.

    This relies on exchangeability: after modeled differences are considered, group labels do not imply a known ordering or systematic difference before their outcomes are seen. The group-level distribution is a real modeling assumption, not a free source of information.

    • A weakly informative prior rules out implausible scales while leaving a broad plausible range.
    • An informative prior represents defensible outside evidence.
    • A hierarchical prior learns shared location and spread from related coefficients or groups.

    Prior predictive simulation shows what outcomes these assumptions imply before the observed outcomes influence the fit.

    Computation and Diagnostics

    Simple conjugate models have exact formulas. More complex posteriors need approximation or sampling:

    • Laplace approximation replaces the posterior near its mode with a Gaussian.
    • Variational inference finds a simpler distribution close to the posterior, often quickly but sometimes with understated uncertainty.
    • Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) draws dependent samples whose long-run distribution is the posterior.

    MCMC needs several diagnostics. Independent chains should mix into the same region. Rank-based $\hat R$ should be near one, effective sample size should be large enough, autocorrelation should decay, and Hamiltonian samplers should not report divergent transitions.

    Posterior predictive checks generate replicated outcomes from the fitted model and compare them with features of the observed data. They can expose wrong spread, tails, zero counts, group patterns, or other missing structure.

    Interview trap. A prior does not make a misspecified likelihood harmless. Posterior concentration can become confidently wrong when dependence, support, censoring, or the mean structure is incorrect.

    Bayesian logistic regression with a Gaussian coefficient prior is not conjugate. Laplace approximation, variational inference, MCMC, or specialized augmentation is needed. A proper prior keeps separated coefficients from drifting to infinite scales, but computation and predictive calibration still need checking.

    Worked Scalar Update

    The scalar example uses prior $\beta\sim N(0,4)$ and data summary $\hat\beta\mid\beta\sim N(\beta,1)$ with $\hat\beta=2$.

    Prior precision is $1/4=0.25$ and data precision is $1/1=1$. Therefore

    $$V_n=\frac{1}{0.25+1}=0.8,$$ $$m_n=\frac{0.25(0)+1(2)}{0.25+1}=1.6.$$

    The data-only estimate is $2$, but the posterior mean is pulled toward the prior mean $0$. The amount of shrinkage follows the relative precisions rather than an arbitrary average.

    Modeling Safeguards

    • Prior predictive checks reveal impossible scales before outcomes are fitted.
    • Sensitivity analysis shows whether conclusions change under other plausible priors.
    • Weakly informative priors stabilize separation and weak identification.
    • Cross-validation measures held-out prediction, while posterior predictive checks inspect the kinds of data the model can generate.
    • Data-dependent priors need an explicit empirical-Bayes or hierarchical account to avoid using the same evidence without acknowledgment.

    Frequentist regularization connections appear in ridge, lasso, and elastic net. Logistic separation and prior stabilization appear in regularization and separation.

    Interview Quick Checks

    PromptComplete answer
    Posterior mode versus full posterior?The mode is one high-density parameter value. The full posterior represents uncertainty, dependence, asymmetry, and predictive integration.
    Why does a Gaussian prior resemble ridge?The negative log prior is quadratic, so maximizing the posterior adds an L2 penalty to the negative log likelihood.
    What validates a Bayesian model?Prior predictive checks, posterior predictive checks, sensitivity analysis, and computational diagnostics address distinct failure modes.
    What is partial pooling?Group estimates share population-level information, with shrinkage determined by within-group information and estimated between-group variation.
