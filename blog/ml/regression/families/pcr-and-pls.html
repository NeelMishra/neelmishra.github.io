    PCR and PLS

    July 2026 · 15 min read

    Principal component regression and partial least squares replace correlated predictors with a small number of latent scores. PCR constructs scores using predictor variance alone. PLS constructs scores using predictor-response association. Both methods require standardization decisions and validation of the component count.

    These approaches are valuable when $p$ is large, predictors are collinear, and a low-dimensional signal is plausible. They do not automatically provide sparse or directly interpretable original-feature effects.

    Stated-Data Projection Counterexample

    The centered rows are $(-3,-1),(-1,1),(1,1),(3,-1)$ with $y=x_2$. The states derive predictor sums of squares, PCR projections, and the response-aware PLS direction.

    Principal Component Regression

    For centered $X=UDV^T$, PCR retains the first $k$ right singular vectors $V_k$ and regresses $y$ on scores $Z_k=XV_k=U_kD_k$. The coefficient in original coordinates is $V_k\hat\gamma$. Truncation removes low-variance directions and stabilizes inversion.

    PCR can discard a low-variance direction that strongly predicts $y$ because component construction never examines the response. Large predictor variance is not equivalent to predictive relevance.

    Partial Least Squares

    PLS sequentially constructs scores with high covariance between projected predictors and the response, then deflates the predictor matrix. Different PLS algorithms vary in normalization and deflation details, so the stated variant matters. PLS often reaches useful prediction with fewer components than PCR when response signal lies outside the leading variance directions.

    Standardization and Component Tuning

    Without scaling, high-variance units dominate both PCA and PLS. Centering and scaling parameters must be estimated on training folds only. The component count is a hyperparameter selected within cross-validation. Refitting PCA or PLS on the complete dataset before validation leaks information from held-out rows.

    Keeping fewer components introduces bias by forcing coefficients to zero in discarded directions, while reducing variance from unstable low-singular-value directions. Keeping more components reduces truncation bias but can restore multicollinearity and estimation noise. Cross-validation estimates this bias-variance tradeoff for the prediction target.

    Interview distinction. PCR is unsupervised dimension reduction followed by regression. PLS is supervised dimension reduction because $y$ influences the score directions.

    Two-Direction Counterexample

    Take centered rows $X=\{(-3,-1),(-1,1),(1,1),(3,-1)\}$ and response $y=x_2=(-1,1,1,-1)$. The columns are orthogonal because $x_1^Tx_2=0$, with sums of squares $20$ and $4$. One-component PCR selects $x_1$, which explains $20/(20+4)=83.33$ percent of predictor variation, but its response coefficient is $x_1^Ty/20=0$. The response covariance vector is $X^Ty=(0,4)^T$, so one-component PLS selects the $x_2$ direction and predicts $y$ exactly in this noiseless example.

    Comparison with Ridge and Lasso

    PCR applies hard spectral truncation: discarded singular directions receive coefficient zero. Ridge applies continuous shrinkage $d_j/(d_j^2+\lambda)$ across all directions. Lasso works in the original feature coordinates and can produce sparsity, but correlated predictors can make selection unstable. Prediction, stability, and interpretability determine the appropriate comparison.

    Diagnostics and Edge Cases

    • Cross-validated prediction error selects components; cumulative predictor variance alone does not.
    • Loadings can change sign without changing the represented component.
    • Repeated singular values make individual component directions nonunique.
    • Missing values require a fold-safe imputation strategy.
    • PLS can overfit through many response-aware components.

    SVD and conditioning details appear in reliable least-squares solvers. Continuous spectral shrinkage appears in regularization.

    Interview Quick Checks

    PromptComplete answer
    PCR versus PCA?PCA produces predictor scores. PCR adds a regression of the response on a selected subset of those scores.
    PCR versus PLS?PCR ranks directions by predictor variance, while PLS constructs directions using association with the response.
    Why can PCR fail?A predictive direction can have low predictor variance and therefore appear late in the principal-component ordering.
    Where does leakage occur?Scaling, component construction, and component-count selection all leak information when fitted outside each training fold.
