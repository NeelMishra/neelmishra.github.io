    Polynomial and Basis Regression

    July 2026 · 15 min read

    Polynomial regression is ordinary linear regression on extra columns made from powers of an input. These constructed columns are called basis features. For degree $d$,

    $$E[Y\mid x]=\beta_0+\beta_1x+\beta_2x^2+\cdots+\beta_dx^d.$$

    The graph can curve with $x$, but every unknown coefficient still enters as a simple multiplier. The model is therefore linear in its parameters, so ordinary least-squares geometry and inference still apply when their usual assumptions hold.

    Powers are only one basis choice. Transformed columns can also contain interactions, logarithms, indicators, Fourier waves, or domain-specific functions.

    Simple, Multiple, and Polynomial Regression

    • Simple linear regression: one input and columns $\phi(x)=(1,x)$.
    • Multiple linear regression: several inputs and columns $\phi(x)=(1,x_1,\ldots,x_p)$.
    • Polynomial regression: added power columns, such as $\phi_2(x)=(1,x,x^2)$ or $\phi_3(x)=(1,x,x^2,x^3)$.

    With two inputs, a complete quadratic basis contains $1,x_1,x_2,x_1^2,x_1x_2,x_2^2$. The product $x_1x_2$ is an interaction: it allows the effect of $x_1$ to change with $x_2$.

    A term such as $\beta_2x^2$ remains linear in the unknown $\beta_2$. A model such as $e^{\beta x}$ places the unknown inside an exponential and is nonlinear in its parameter, so ordinary linear projection does not apply directly.

    Design Matrix and Hierarchy

    Applying the basis to every row creates the design matrix $\Phi$. For one input, row $i$ is

    $$\phi(x_i)=(1,x_i,x_i^2,\ldots,x_i^d).$$

    Strong hierarchy keeps $x_1$ and $x_2$ whenever $x_1x_2$ is included. Weak hierarchy keeps at least one of them. Strong hierarchy makes the interaction easier to interpret because the model still contains each feature's baseline effect. It also avoids a fragile search over isolated high-order terms.

    $$\hat\beta=\arg\min_\beta\lVert y-\Phi\beta\rVert_2^2.$$

    The normal equations are $\Phi^\top\Phi\hat\beta=\Phi^\top y$, but QR or SVD is numerically safer than forming an inverse. Raw power columns can become almost copies of one another, especially at high degree or over a large input range.

    Centering and Orthogonal Bases

    Poor conditioning means that a small numerical or data change can create a large coefficient change. Centering and scaling $x$ before forming powers reduces this problem. Orthogonal polynomial bases go further by constructing sample columns whose dot products are zero.

    Under unpenalized OLS, two full-rank bases produce the same fitted values when they span the same column space. Their coefficients differ because the columns have different meanings. Raw coefficients multiply $x,x^2,\ldots$ directly; orthogonal coefficients multiply constructed directions.

    Penalty caveat. Ridge and lasso penalize coefficient size in the chosen basis. A basis change can therefore change a penalized fit even when the unpenalized OLS fitted values would be identical.
    Interview trap. A small coefficient on $x^3$ does not imply that the cubic contribution is small. Its scale depends on the units of $x$, and correlated basis columns make isolated coefficient magnitudes unreliable.

    Degree Selection and Regularization

    A degree-$d+1$ polynomial contains every column from degree $d$ plus one more, so training error cannot increase with degree. Training error alone will therefore keep favoring more flexibility.

    Degree is chosen with cross-validation, an information criterion, or a degree fixed by domain knowledge. All centering, scaling, and basis construction used for tuning belong inside each training fold.

    Ridge can stabilize correlated power columns:

    $$\lVert y-\Phi\beta\rVert_2^2+\lambda\lVert\beta_{1:}\rVert_2^2,$$

    where $\beta_{1:}$ excludes the intercept. Lasso can remove terms entirely, but an unrestricted lasso may keep an interaction while dropping its main effects. Hierarchical penalties prevent that structure from breaking.

    Degree alone does not determine effective complexity. A strongly penalized degree-five curve can be smoother than a sparse, unpenalized degree-three fit. High-degree global polynomials can also oscillate near interval boundaries, known as Runge's phenomenon.

    Extrapolation remains separate. Cross-validation inside the observed range does not prove that a polynomial behaves sensibly outside that range. High powers can grow or reverse direction very quickly.

    Worked Calculation

    The observations $(x,y)=(0,1),(1,2.5),(2,3)$ produce the following equations after substitution into $y=\beta_0+\beta_1x+\beta_2x^2$:

    $$\beta_0=1,\qquad \beta_1+\beta_2=1.5,\qquad 2\beta_1+4\beta_2=2.$$

    Solving gives $\beta_1=2$ and $\beta_2=-0.5$. The three distinct input values make the $3\times3$ design full rank, so this exact fit is unique:

    $$\hat y=1+2x-0.5x^2.$$

    At $x=2$, the intercept, linear, and quadratic contributions are $1$, $4$, and $-2$, giving $\hat y=3$. The local slope is

    $$\frac{d\hat y}{dx}=2-x.$$

    It equals $2$ at $x=0$ and $0$ at $x=2$. The coefficient $\beta_1=2$ is therefore not a global slope after a quadratic term is included.

    Three parameters fit these three points exactly. That exact training fit does not show that degree two will predict new points well.

    Diagnostics and Edge Cases

    • Residual curvature indicates an inadequate basis or a missing interaction.
    • Large condition numbers indicate unstable raw powers or sparse support.
    • Boundary oscillation and extreme predictions indicate extrapolation risk.
    • Duplicate transformed columns create rank deficiency and nonunique coefficients.
    • Categorical variables should not be assigned polynomial powers merely because their labels are numeric.

    Splines often replace high-degree global polynomials because local basis functions confine the effect of a knot-region change. The splines and GAMs page develops that alternative. Core projection and regularization results appear in OLS and projection and ridge, lasso, and elastic net.

    Interview Quick Checks

    PromptComplete answer
    Why is polynomial regression linear?The prediction is linear in unknown coefficients after the basis map, so least-squares projection and linear-model inference still apply.
    Do orthogonal polynomials change predictions?No, provided the orthogonal and raw bases span the same full-rank column space. They change conditioning and coefficient interpretation.
    How is degree selected?Degree is tuned using held-out performance or a justified information criterion, with all preprocessing repeated inside each training fold.
    What is the dominant failure?Global extrapolation can grow rapidly and reverse shape outside observed support, even when in-range residual diagnostics look strong.
