    Polynomial and Basis Regression

    July 2026 · 15 min read

    Polynomial regression is linear regression after a nonlinear feature map. For one predictor, a degree-$d$ model has $E[Y\mid x]=\beta_0+\beta_1x+\cdots+\beta_dx^d$. The curve is nonlinear in $x$ but linear in the coefficients, so ordinary least squares geometry, inference, and regularization still apply to the expanded design matrix.

    This distinction is a frequent interview checkpoint. A model is linear when it is linear in unknown parameters, not when its graph must be a straight line. The feature map can contain powers, interactions, logarithms, indicators, Fourier terms, or domain-specific transformations.

    Simple, Multiple, and Polynomial Regression

    Simple linear regression uses one predictor, $\phi(x)=(1,x)$. Multiple regression uses several predictors, $\phi(x)=(1,x_1,\ldots,x_p)$. Polynomial regression changes the feature map rather than the fitting principle. Quadratic and cubic maps for one input are $\phi_2(x)=(1,x,x^2)$ and $\phi_3(x)=(1,x,x^2,x^3)$. For two inputs, a complete quadratic map includes $1,x_1,x_2,x_1^2,x_1x_2,x_2^2$.

    Linearity in parameters permits terms such as $\beta_2x^2$. A model such as $e^{\beta x}$ is nonlinear in the unknown parameter and requires nonlinear optimization. The distinction determines whether ordinary linear-model projection theory applies directly.

    Design Matrix and Hierarchy

    With observations $x_i$, the raw polynomial design has row $\phi(x_i)=(1,x_i,x_i^2,\ldots,x_i^d)$. In several variables, interactions such as $x_1x_2$ represent effect modification. Strong hierarchy retains both main effects whenever an interaction is included. Weak hierarchy retains at least one main effect. Hierarchy protects interpretation and reduces fragile searches over arbitrary terms.

    $$\hat\beta=\arg\min_\beta\lVert y-\Phi\beta\rVert_2^2.$$

    The normal equations remain $\Phi^T\Phi\hat\beta=\Phi^Ty$, but QR or SVD is preferable to explicitly forming an inverse. Raw powers can be nearly collinear, especially when $x$ has a large range or the degree is high.

    Centering and Orthogonal Bases

    Centering and scaling $x$ before forming powers reduces numerical imbalance. Orthogonal polynomial bases go further by constructing columns that are orthogonal over the observed sample. Fitted values remain the same when two full-rank bases span the same column space, although coefficient meanings differ. Raw-basis coefficients describe algebraic powers; orthogonal-basis coefficients describe directions created by the basis construction.

    Interview trap. A small coefficient on $x^3$ does not imply that the cubic contribution is small. Its scale depends on the units of $x$, and correlated basis columns make isolated coefficient magnitudes unreliable.

    Degree Selection and Regularization

    Training error cannot select degree because nested polynomial spaces make it nonincreasing. Cross-validation, an information criterion, or a prespecified domain degree provides a defensible selection rule. Ridge regression stabilizes correlated powers through $\lVert y-\Phi\beta\rVert^2+\lambda\lVert\beta\rVert^2$. Lasso can remove terms, but unrestricted sparsity may violate hierarchy unless the penalty encodes hierarchical constraints.

    Effective complexity depends on degree, penalty, sample size, and feature distribution. A degree-five model can behave smoothly under strong ridge shrinkage, while a degree-three unpenalized fit can oscillate when points are sparse.

    Runge's phenomenon is the oscillation that can appear near interval boundaries when high-degree global polynomials interpolate evenly spaced points. Cross-validated degree selection reduces overfitting risk but does not make unsupported extrapolation trustworthy. Prediction outside the observed $x$ range should therefore be reported as a separate risk.

    Worked Calculation

    Suppose the three observations are $(x,y)=(0,1),(1,2.5),(2,3)$. Substitution into $y=\beta_0+\beta_1x+\beta_2x^2$ gives $\beta_0=1$, $\beta_1+\beta_2=1.5$, and $2\beta_1+4\beta_2=2$. Hence $\beta_2=-0.5$ and $\beta_1=2$. The square $3\times3$ design has distinct $x$ values and full rank, so the exact OLS fit is unique:

    $$\hat y=1+2x-0.5x^2.$$

    At $x=2$, the contributions are $1$, $4$, and $-2$, giving $\hat y=3$. The marginal slope is $2-x$, which equals zero at $x=2$. A coefficient therefore does not equal a global slope once nonlinear terms are present.

    Diagnostics and Edge Cases

    • Residual curvature indicates an inadequate basis or a missing interaction.
    • Large condition numbers indicate unstable raw powers or sparse support.
    • Boundary oscillation and extreme predictions indicate extrapolation risk.
    • Duplicate transformed columns create rank deficiency and nonunique coefficients.
    • Categorical variables should not be assigned polynomial powers merely because their labels are numeric.

    Splines often replace high-degree global polynomials because local basis functions confine the effect of a knot-region change. The splines and GAMs page develops that alternative. Core projection and regularization results appear in OLS and projection and ridge, lasso, and elastic net.

    Interview Quick Checks

    PromptComplete answer
    Why is polynomial regression linear?The prediction is linear in unknown coefficients after the basis map, so least-squares projection and linear-model inference still apply.
    Do orthogonal polynomials change predictions?No, provided the orthogonal and raw bases span the same full-rank column space. They change conditioning and coefficient interpretation.
    How is degree selected?Degree is tuned using held-out performance or a justified information criterion, with all preprocessing repeated inside each training fold.
    What is the dominant failure?Global extrapolation can grow rapidly and reverse shape outside observed support, even when in-range residual diagnostics look strong.
