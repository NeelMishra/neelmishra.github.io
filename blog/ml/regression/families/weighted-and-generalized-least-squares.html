    Weighted and Generalized Least Squares

    July 2026 · 16 min read

    Ordinary least squares (OLS) gives every squared residual the same importance. That is sensible when errors have similar spread and do not move together. Two extensions handle failures of those assumptions:

    • Weighted least squares (WLS) gives more influence to precise rows and less to noisy rows.
    • Generalized least squares (GLS) also accounts for errors that move together across rows.
    $$E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon\mid X)=\Sigma.$$

    The first condition is exogeneity: errors still average to zero at every predictor value. The matrix $\Sigma$ describes error spread and dependence. Different diagonal values mean unequal variances, called heteroskedasticity. Nonzero off-diagonal values mean correlated errors.

    Core distinction. Unequal variance alone does not bias OLS coefficients when $E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0$. It makes OLS less efficient and makes the constant-variance standard-error formula wrong.

    Why the More Precise Measurement Gets More Weight

    The states add $y_1=10$ with variance $1$ and $y_2=14$ with variance $4$, then compare the unweighted mean $12$ with the precision-weighted mean $10.8$.

    Weighted Least Squares

    Suppose row $i$ has error variance $\sigma_i^2$ and errors are otherwise uncorrelated. WLS minimizes

    $$\sum_i\frac{(y_i-x_i^\top\beta)^2}{\sigma_i^2}=\sum_i w_i(y_i-x_i^\top\beta)^2,\qquad w_i=\frac{1}{\sigma_i^2}.$$

    A row with four times the variance receives one quarter of the weight. With $W=\operatorname{diag}(w_1,\ldots,w_n)$,

    $$\hat\beta_{\mathrm{WLS}}=(X^\top W X)^{-1}X^\top W y.$$

    The inverse is mathematical notation; numerical code solves the weighted linear system. Multiplying every weight by the same positive constant leaves $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{WLS}}$ unchanged.

    The word weight has several meanings:

    • Precision weights represent inverse error variance.
    • Frequency weights represent repeated identical rows.
    • Sampling weights represent how many population units a sampled row stands for.

    These weights answer different statistical questions and are not interchangeable.

    GLS and Whitening

    WLS handles unequal diagonal variances. GLS allows the full covariance matrix $\Sigma$ to contain both unequal variances and correlations:

    $$\hat\beta_{\mathrm{GLS}}=(X^\top\Sigma^{-1}X)^{-1}X^\top\Sigma^{-1}y.$$

    The objective $(y-X\beta)^\top\Sigma^{-1}(y-X\beta)$ measures residual size after accounting for their joint covariance.

    Whitening makes this connection concrete. If $C^\top C=\Sigma^{-1}$, multiplying by $C$ gives

    $$Cy=CX\beta+C\varepsilon,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(C\varepsilon\mid X)=I.$$

    The transformed errors have equal variance and no correlation, so GLS is OLS on the whitened response $Cy$ and design $CX$.

    If $\Sigma$ is the complete covariance matrix,

    $$\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\mathrm{GLS}}\mid X)=(X^\top\Sigma^{-1}X)^{-1}.$$

    If $\Sigma=\sigma^2\Omega$ is known only up to $\sigma^2$, the covariance is $\sigma^2(X^\top\Omega^{-1}X)^{-1}$ and the scale must be estimated. Common structures include time-series correlation, shared correlation within groups, and spatial correlation.

    Feasible GLS and Robust Alternatives

    The true covariance is rarely known. Feasible GLS first fits a mean model, estimates a variance or correlation pattern from residuals, builds $\hat\Sigma$, and refits with GLS. A good covariance model can improve efficiency. A bad one can lose efficiency and produce wrong model-based standard errors.

    A sandwich covariance takes a safer route when the mean model is trusted but the variance pattern is uncertain. It keeps the OLS coefficients and changes only their estimated covariance. Heteroskedasticity-consistent versions allow unequal variance. Cluster-robust versions allow dependence within clusters while treating clusters as independent.

    Interview distinction. WLS changes coefficients because the objective changes. Robust standard errors do not change coefficients because only the covariance estimate changes.

    Worked Precision Example

    Two independent measurements of one mean are $y_1=10$ with variance $1$ and $y_2=14$ with variance $4$. Their precision weights are $1$ and $0.25$. The first measurement is four times as precise, so the result stays closer to $10$:

    $$\hat\mu_{\mathrm{WLS}}=\frac{1(10)+0.25(14)}{1+0.25}=10.8.$$

    The unweighted mean is $12$. The weighted estimate has variance

    $$\operatorname{Var}(\hat\mu_{\mathrm{WLS}})=\frac{1}{1+0.25}=0.8.$$

    Assumptions and Diagnostics

    • Mean model: $E[\varepsilon\mid X]=0$ is still required for the intended coefficient target.
    • Positive precision: precision weights must be positive.
    • Variance evidence: residual plots can reveal changing spread but do not identify one unique variance formula.
    • Dependence: time and cluster residuals need checks that preserve their ordering or grouping.
    • Data-dependent weights: weights related to the same outcome noise can change the target or introduce bias unless their construction is justified.

    Interview Quick Checks

    PromptComplete answer
    Is OLS biased under heteroskedasticity?Not from heteroskedasticity alone. Conditional mean exogeneity preserves unbiasedness, while efficiency and the homoskedastic covariance formula fail.
    When does WLS equal GLS?WLS is GLS when the covariance matrix is diagonal, so dependence is absent and only observation-specific variances differ.
    Why inverse-variance weights?Higher-variance observations carry less precision. Inverse-variance weighting minimizes variance among linear unbiased estimators when the covariance model is correct.
    When are robust errors preferable?Sandwich inference is attractive when the mean model is trusted but a full variance model lacks scientific or empirical support.
