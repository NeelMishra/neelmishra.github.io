    Splines and GAMs

    July 2026 · 16 min read

    A spline divides an input range into intervals and fits a low-degree polynomial in each interval. The joining locations are knots. Smoothness conditions make the pieces meet without visible jumps or sharp corners.

    This local construction avoids asking one high-degree polynomial to control the entire range. B-spline bases add a further benefit: each basis column is nonzero over only part of the range, so a local change does not disturb the whole curve.

    Generalized additive models (GAMs) combine several smooth effects:

    $$g\big(E[Y\mid X]\big)=\beta_0+f_1(x_1)+\cdots+f_p(x_p).$$

    The main tradeoff is fit against smoothness. Knots define which curves are available; a penalty determines how much curvature the fitted curve actually uses.

    How Truncated Basis Terms Turn On at Knots

    For $x=(0,1,2,3,4)$ and knots $\kappa=(1,3)$, the animation derives $(x-1)_+^3$ and $(x-3)_+^3$, where $(u)_+=\max(u,0)$.

    Spline Construction

    A cubic spline uses cubic pieces. At each interior knot, the value, first derivative, and second derivative agree on both sides. The curve is therefore smooth through the join.

    One representation is the truncated power basis

    $$1,\ x,\ x^2,\ x^3,\ (x-\kappa_1)_+^3,\ldots,(x-\kappa_K)_+^3.$$

    A term $(x-\kappa_j)_+^3$ is zero before its knot and changes the cubic shape after it. This basis is easy to understand but can be poorly conditioned. B-splines span the same spline space with more stable columns that have local support.

    Knots can follow predictor quantiles, a regular grid, or meaningful boundaries. Modern smoothers often use enough knots to represent the shape and let a penalty suppress unnecessary wiggles. Any tuned knot count or placement belongs inside cross-validation.

    A natural cubic spline forces zero curvature beyond the boundary knots, so both tails become straight lines. This is usually safer than unrestricted cubic tails, but it still imposes an assumption outside the observed range.

    Penalized Smoothing

    $$\hat f=\arg\min_f\left\{\sum_i(y_i-f(x_i))^2+\lambda\int[f''(t)]^2dt\right\}.$$

    The first term rewards closeness to the data. The integral measures total squared curvature, so the second term penalizes wiggles.

    • At $\lambda=0$, the available basis can be used freely.
    • As $\lambda$ grows, curvature becomes more expensive.
    • As $\lambda\to\infty$, a second-derivative penalty leaves a straight line.

    Generalized cross-validation, restricted maximum likelihood (REML), or nested validation can select $\lambda$.

    For a Gaussian smoother, fitted values can be written as $\hat y=S_\lambda y$. The effective degrees of freedom are

    $$\mathrm{EDF}=\operatorname{tr}(S_\lambda).$$

    EDF measures flexibility actually used after shrinkage. It can be fractional and is often much smaller than the number of basis columns.

    Generalized Additive Models

    A GAM combines smooth terms with a GLM response family. Each smooth $f_j$ is usually centered to have sample mean zero. Without centering, a constant could move freely between the smooth and the intercept, so their separate values would not be identifiable.

    Gaussian GAMs use penalized least squares. Non-Gaussian GAMs use penalized IRLS. A purely additive model assumes that effects combine through a sum. It cannot represent an effect of $x_1$ that changes with $x_2$. Tensor-product smooths or varying-coefficient terms add that interaction structure.

    Interview distinction. Additivity is an assumption about how predictors combine. Smoothness is an assumption about each effect. A smooth additive model can still be badly misspecified when interactions dominate.

    Worked Degrees-of-Freedom Interpretation

    A smoother with $20$ basis columns but EDF $4.7$ offers many possible shapes, while its penalty suppresses most high-curvature directions. The fitted curve behaves more like a model using roughly $4.7$ freely estimated parameters than an unpenalized $20$-parameter curve.

    Confidence bands must include uncertainty from smoothing. A pointwise $95\%$ band targets repeated-sample coverage at one fixed input. Simultaneous coverage over the whole curve is a stronger claim and needs a separate construction.

    Diagnostics and Edge Cases

    • Remaining pattern. Curved residual structure can indicate too small a basis or a missing interaction.
    • Sparse regions. Few input values near some knots make the local shape uncertain.
    • Tuning leakage. Basis and smoothing choices are made inside each training fold.
    • Causal overreach. A partial-effect curve is a conditional model summary, not automatically a causal effect.
    • Concurvity. One predictor's smooth can be approximated by other smooths, creating the nonlinear analogue of multicollinearity.

    Basis geometry starts in polynomial and basis regression. The underlying penalized least-squares connection appears in regularization.

    Interview Quick Checks

    PromptComplete answer
    Knots versus smoothing?Knots define the available basis space. The smoothing penalty determines how much curvature the fitted function actually uses.
    Why are B-splines stable?Their basis functions have local support and avoid the extreme scaling and correlation associated with high raw polynomial powers.
    What makes a natural spline natural?Linear tail constraints beyond boundary knots remove unstable cubic curvature where observations provide little information.
    How is a GAM interpreted?Each centered smooth is a conditional partial effect on the link scale, holding the other modeled terms fixed.
