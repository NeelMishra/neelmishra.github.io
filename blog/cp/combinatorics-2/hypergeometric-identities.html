Hypergeometric Identities — WZ Method & Vandermonde Convolution

January 2026 · 28 min read

Binomial Sum Identities

Many competitive-programming problems reduce to evaluating a closed-form for a sum involving binomial coefficients. Instead of brute-forcing Σ C(n,k)·(…), knowing the right identity lets you compute the answer in O(1) or O(n) and shave an entire dimension off your solution.

Vandermonde’s Identity

The most fundamental convolution identity for binomial coefficients is Vandermonde’s identity:

Σk=0r C(m, k) · C(n, r−k) = C(m+n, r)

Combinatorial proof (committee selection). We want to choose r people from a group of m men and n women. The right side counts this directly. The left side splits the selection: pick k men (C(m,k) ways) and r−k women (C(n,r−k) ways), then sum over all valid k.

Proof via Generating Functions

Consider the coefficient of xr on both sides of (1+x)m · (1+x)n = (1+x)m+n. On the left, [xr] equals Σk C(m,k)·C(n,r−k) by the Cauchy product. On the right it equals C(m+n,r). □

Chu–Vandermonde Generalization

Vandermonde’s identity generalizes when we allow the upper index to be a real (or complex) number. Using the generalized binomial coefficient C(α, k) = α(α−1)…(α−k+1) / k!, the identity becomes:

Σk=0r C(α, k) · C(β, r−k) = C(α+β, r)

This is exactly the Chu–Vandermonde identity. In hypergeometric notation it reads 2F1(−r, −α; β−r+1; 1) = C(α+β, r)/C(β, r). Setting α = m, β = n recovers the standard version.

An important special case sets β = −n with n a positive integer:

Σk=0r C(α, k) · (−1)r−k C(n−1, r−k) = C(α−n, r)

This “negative upper-index” form is used frequently in inclusion-exclusion arguments in competitive programming.

More Key Identities

Beyond Vandermonde, several identities appear so often in CP that they deserve a permanent place in your toolbox.

Upper Summation

Σk=0n C(k, r) = C(n+1, r+1)

Proof sketch. Induction on n, using the Pascal recurrence C(n+1, r+1) = C(n, r+1) + C(n, r). Alternatively, a committee argument: from {0,…,n} choose r+1 elements and let k be the largest — the remaining r come from {0,…,k−1}, giving C(k,r) choices.

Parallel Summation

Σk=0r C(n+k, k) = C(n+r+1, r)

This counts lattice paths: the number of monotone paths from (0,0) to (n,r) that first reach column k at row n equals C(n+k, k), and the total is C(n+r+1, r) by the hockey-stick identity.

Dixon’s Identity

Σk=−aa (−1)k C(2a, a+k)3 = (3a)! / (a!)3

More precisely, for non-negative integers a, b, c:

Σk (−1)k C(a+b, a+k) · C(b+c, b+k) · C(c+a, c+k) = (a+b+c)! / (a! · b! · c!)

Dixon’s identity can be proved by the WZ method (see below). In contests it often appears disguised as a triple-sum convolution that simplifies to a multinomial coefficient.

Pfaff–Saalschütz Identity

3F2(−n, a, b; c, 1+a+b−c−n; 1) = C(c−a, n) · C(c−b, n) / (C(c, n) · C(c−a−b, n))

This is a balanced identity (the sum of numerator parameters plus 1 equals the sum of denominator parameters). It subsumes Vandermonde as a special case (set a=0). The combinatorial interpretation involves counting lattice paths with weighted steps.

Zeilberger’s algorithm can automatically prove any identity of this type. Given the summand F(n,k) it finds a recurrence Σj=0J aj(n)·S(n+j) = 0 satisfied by S(n) = Σk F(n,k). If S(n) also satisfies that recurrence, the identity is verified.

Hypergeometric Series

A hypergeometric series is a power series whose consecutive term ratio is a rational function of the index. The generalized hypergeometric function is:

pFq(a1,…,ap; b1,…,bq; z) = Σk=0∞ [(a1)k … (ap)k / ((b1)k … (bq)k)] · zk / k!

where (a)k = a(a+1)…(a+k−1) is the Pochhammer symbol (rising factorial). The most common case in combinatorics is the Gauss hypergeometric function 2F1(a,b;c;z).

Connection to Binomial Sums

When one of the upper parameters is a negative integer −n, the series terminates after n+1 terms:

2F1(−n, b; c; 1) = Σk=0n (−n)k (b)k / ((c)k k!)

Since (−n)k = (−1)k n!/(n−k)! = (−1)k C(n,k) · k!, each term becomes a product of binomial coefficients. This is why almost every binomial sum identity can be restated as a hypergeometric evaluation:

Binomial Form

Σk C(m,k) C(n,r−k) = C(m+n,r)

(Vandermonde)

Hypergeometric Form

2F1(−r, −m; n−r+1; 1) = C(m+n,r)/C(n,r)

(Chu–Vandermonde)

Similarly, Dixon’s identity is a special evaluation of 3F2 at z=1, and the Pfaff–Saalschütz identity is a balanced 3F2 evaluation. The hypergeometric viewpoint unifies dozens of seemingly unrelated identities into a single framework.

The Term-Ratio Test

Given a sum S = Σk tk, compute the ratio tk+1/tk. If it is a rational function of k, the sum is hypergeometric. Factor the numerator and denominator to read off the parameters. For example, if tk+1/tk = (k−n)(k+b) / ((k+c)(k+1)), then S is proportional to 2F1(−n, b; c; z) evaluated at some z.

The WZ Method

The Wilf–Zeilberger (WZ) method is a mechanical procedure that proves hypergeometric identities. It was developed by Herbert Wilf and Doron Zeilberger in the early 1990s and earned Zeilberger the Steele Prize.

Core Idea

Suppose we want to prove Σk F(n,k) = 1 for all n ≥ 0 (after normalizing). The WZ method finds a certificate function R(n,k) such that with G(n,k) = R(n,k) · F(n,k):

F(n+1, k) − F(n, k) = G(n, k+1) − G(n, k)

This is called the WZ equation. Summing both sides over all k (from −∞ to +∞):

Left side: S(n+1) − S(n), where S(n) = Σk F(n,k).
Right side: telescopes to 0 (assuming G vanishes at ±∞).

Therefore S(n+1) = S(n) for all n, so S is constant. Checking S(0) = 1 completes the proof.

The power of WZ: Once you exhibit R(n,k), verification is a finite computation — just check the WZ equation holds as a polynomial identity. The human ingenuity is replaced by an algorithm.

Example: Proving Σk C(n,k)2 = C(2n, n)

Set F(n,k) = C(n,k)2 / C(2n,n). We want Σk F(n,k) = 1. The WZ certificate for this identity is:

R(n,k) = −k2 / (2(n+1)(2n+1))

(found by Zeilberger’s algorithm). Let G(n,k) = R(n,k) · F(n,k). One can verify algebraically that F(n+1,k) − F(n,k) = G(n,k+1) − G(n,k). Summing over k, the right side telescopes; the left gives S(n+1) − S(n) = 0. Since S(0) = F(0,0) = 1, we conclude S(n) = 1 for all n. □

Finding the Certificate Algorithmically

Zeilberger’s creative telescoping algorithm works as follows:

  1. Write the ratio F(n+1,k)/F(n,k) as a rational function of n and k.
  2. Seek R(n,k) = P(n,k)/Q(n,k) (a rational function) satisfying the WZ equation.
  3. Clear denominators and match polynomial coefficients — this yields a linear system that can be solved mechanically.
  4. If a solution exists of bounded degree, the algorithm finds it. By a theorem of Wilf and Zeilberger, a certificate always exists for proper hypergeometric terms.

In practice, one uses a computer algebra system (Maple, Mathematica, or the EKHAD package by Zeilberger) rather than solving by hand. For competitive programming, the identities derived by the WZ method are what matter — not running the algorithm during a contest.

Snake Oil Method

The snake oil method, popularized by Herbert Wilf in his book generatingfunctionology, is a systematic technique for evaluating combinatorial sums using generating functions.

Recipe

  1. Identify the free variable. In a sum like Σk f(n,k), the free variable is n (or whichever index is not summed over).
  2. Introduce a GF variable. Multiply by xn and sum over n to form F(x) = Σn S(n) xn.
  3. Interchange summations. Switch the order of the k-sum and n-sum.
  4. Evaluate the inner sum using a known generating function.
  5. Extract [xn] F(x) to get S(n) in closed form.

Example 1: Σk C(k, n−k)

Let S(n) = Σk C(k, n−k). We want a closed form.

Step 1. Multiply by xn and sum: F(x) = Σn≥0 S(n) xn = Σn Σk C(k, n−k) xn.

Step 2. Substitute j = n − k (so n = j + k): F(x) = Σk≥0 Σj≥0 C(k,j) xj+k = Σk≥0 xk Σj≥0 C(k,j) xj = Σk≥0 xk (1+x)k = Σk≥0 (x(1+x))k = 1 / (1 − x − x²).

Step 3. The generating function 1/(1 − x − x²) generates the Fibonacci numbers! So S(n) = Fn+1 (with F1=F2=1).

Snake oil is mechanical. The method works whenever the summand is a product of binomial coefficients in k and n. The “trick” is always the same — introduce xn, swap sums, use a known GF.

Example 2: Σk C(n,k) · C(n,k+1) / C(2n, n+1)

Let S(n) = Σk≥0 C(n,k) · C(n, k+1). By Vandermonde, this equals C(2n, n+1) — just set r = n+1 in Σ C(n,k) C(n, r−k) = C(2n, r). So S(n) = C(2n, n+1) = Cn · (n+1), where Cn is the n-th Catalan number.

Example 3: Double Sum via Snake Oil

Evaluate Σk C(n+k, 2k) · 2k. Introduce F(x) = Σn S(n) xn, interchange, and use the identity Σn≥0 C(n+k, 2k) xn = xk/(1−x)2k+1. After simplification, F(x) = 1/(1 − x − 2x/(1−x)²), which can be expanded to find S(n). The details are a good exercise.

C++ Toolkit

Below are core routines that appear in virtually every CP problem involving binomial identities.

Precomputing Binomial Coefficients mod p

// Precompute factorials and inverse factorials mod p
// Supports C(n, k) queries in O(1) after O(N) precomputation
const int MOD = 998244353;
const int MAXN = 2000001;

long long fac[MAXN], inv_fac[MAXN];

long long power(long long a, long long b, long long mod) {
    long long res = 1;
    a %= mod;
    while (b > 0) {
        if (b & 1) res = res * a % mod;
        a = a * a % mod;
        b >>= 1;
    }
    return res;
}

void precompute() {
    fac[0] = 1;
    for (int i = 1; i < MAXN; i++)
        fac[i] = fac[i - 1] * i % MOD;
    inv_fac[MAXN - 1] = power(fac[MAXN - 1], MOD - 2, MOD);
    for (int i = MAXN - 2; i >= 0; i--)
        inv_fac[i] = inv_fac[i + 1] * (i + 1) % MOD;
}

long long C(int n, int k) {
    if (k < 0 || k > n) return 0;
    return fac[n] % MOD * inv_fac[k] % MOD * inv_fac[n - k] % MOD;
}
Modular pitfall: The formula C(n,k) = n! / (k! (n−k)!) only works modulo a prime p when n < p. For n ≥ p, you must use Lucas’ theorem or factor out p-powers with Legendre’s formula.

Lucas’ Theorem for Large n

// Lucas' theorem: C(n, k) mod p for prime p and arbitrary n
// Works by decomposing n and k in base p
long long lucas(long long n, long long k, long long p) {
    if (k == 0) return 1;
    long long ni = n % p, ki = k % p;
    if (ki > ni) return 0;
    // Small C(ni, ki) mod p — precomputed or direct
    // Here we compute directly for small p
    long long num = 1, den = 1;
    for (long long i = 0; i < ki; i++) {
        num = num * ((ni - i) % p + p) % p;
        den = den * ((i + 1) % p) % p;
    }
    long long small_c = num % p * power(den, p - 2, p) % p;
    return small_c % p * lucas(n / p, k / p, p) % p;
}

Evaluating Vandermonde Convolutions Efficiently

When a problem requires computing Σk=0r C(m,k) · C(n, r−k) and you know the answer is C(m+n, r), just compute a single binomial coefficient. But what if the identity is disguised?

Pattern recognition: Given a sum Σk f(k) · g(r−k), check if f and g are columns of Pascal’s triangle. If f(k) = C(A, k) and g(j) = C(B, j), the sum is C(A+B, r) by Vandermonde.

NTT-Based Polynomial Convolution

// When identities don't simplify, compute the convolution directly
// using NTT (Number Theoretic Transform) in O(n log n)
#include <vector>
using namespace std;

const int MOD = 998244353, g = 3;

void ntt(vector<long long>& a, bool inv) {
    int n = a.size();
    for (int i = 1, j = 0; i < n; i++) {
        int bit = n >> 1;
        for (; j & bit; bit >>= 1) j ^= bit;
        j ^= bit;
        if (i < j) swap(a[i], a[j]);
    }
    for (int len = 2; len <= n; len <<= 1) {
        long long w = inv ? power(g, MOD - 1 - (MOD - 1) / len, MOD)
                         : power(g, (MOD - 1) / len, MOD);
        for (int i = 0; i < n; i += len) {
            long long wn = 1;
            for (int j = 0; j < len / 2; j++) {
                long long u = a[i + j];
                long long v = a[i + j + len / 2] * wn % MOD;
                a[i + j] = (u + v) % MOD;
                a[i + j + len / 2] = (u - v + MOD) % MOD;
                wn = wn * w % MOD;
            }
        }
    }
    if (inv) {
        long long n_inv = power(n, MOD - 2, MOD);
        for (auto& x : a) x = x * n_inv % MOD;
    }
}

vector<long long> multiply(vector<long long> a, vector<long long> b) {
    int result_size = a.size() + b.size() - 1;
    int n = 1;
    while (n < result_size) n <<= 1;
    a.resize(n); b.resize(n);
    ntt(a, false); ntt(b, false);
    for (int i = 0; i < n; i++) a[i] = a[i] * b[i] % MOD;
    ntt(a, true);
    a.resize(result_size);
    return a;
}

// Usage: convolve the k-th column of Pascal's triangle
// a[k] = C(m, k), b[k] = C(n, k) => result[r] = C(m+n, r)
vector<long long> vandermonde_via_ntt(int m, int n) {
    precompute();
    int R = m + n;
    vector<long long> a(m + 1), b(n + 1);
    for (int k = 0; k <= m; k++) a[k] = C(m, k);
    for (int k = 0; k <= n; k++) b[k] = C(n, k);
    return multiply(a, b); // result[r] == C(m+n, r) for each r
}
NTT modulus requirement: The standard NTT requires MOD = 998244353 = 119 · 223 + 1 (a prime with a large power-of-2 factor). If the problem uses a different modulus, you need either CRT-based NTT or an arbitrary-mod NTT wrapper.

CP Applications

Recognizing Vandermonde in Disguise

Many contest problems give a double sum or a recurrence whose closed form is a Vandermonde convolution. The key signal: a sum of products of two binomial coefficients where the indices add up to a constant.

Example (Codeforces style): Count the number of ways to distribute r identical balls into two groups of urns (m urns of type A, n urns of type B), where each urn holds at most 1 ball.

If k balls go into type-A urns: C(m,k) choices. Then r−k go into type-B: C(n, r−k) choices. Total = Σk C(m,k) C(n, r−k) = C(m+n, r). The problem reduces to a single binomial coefficient!

Multi-Convolution Problems

When you have three or more groups, Vandermonde generalizes naturally:

Σk1+k2+k3=r C(a, k1) · C(b, k2) · C(c, k3) = C(a+b+c, r)

This follows by applying Vandermonde twice: first convolve a and b to get C(a+b, ·), then convolve that with c. In code, if the groups are too large for direct computation, use NTT convolution.

Contest Walkthrough: Lattice Path Counting

Problem: Count lattice paths from (0,0) to (n,m) that pass through at least one of the points (a,b) or (c,d), modulo 109+7.

By inclusion-exclusion: paths through (a,b) + paths through (c,d) − paths through both. Paths from (0,0) to (a,b) to (n,m) = C(a+b, a) · C((n−a)+(m−b), n−a). This is a product of two binomial coefficients — each computed in O(1) with precomputed factorials.

Contest Walkthrough: Alternating Sum

Problem: Compute Σk=0n (−1)k C(n,k) · C(2n−2k, n−k) modulo a prime p.

Rewrite C(2n−2k, n−k) = C(2(n−k), n−k). Let j = n−k: the sum becomes Σj=0n (−1)n−j C(n, n−j) C(2j, j) = (−1)n Σj (−1)j C(n,j) C(2j,j).

Now recognize this as the coefficient extraction from the generating function (1−4x)−1/2 composed with sign changes. Using the identity Σj C(n,j) (−1)j C(2j,j) xj evaluated at x=1, and the Chu–Vandermonde identity, the sum evaluates to C(2n,n) · (−1)n / 2n (with appropriate modular inversion).

Strategy for contest binomial sums: (1) Check if Vandermonde applies directly. (2) Try substitution j = n−k or j = r−k to rearrange. (3) Check the term ratio — if rational in k, it’s hypergeometric and a known identity likely applies. (4) If stuck, compute the first few values with brute force and look up the sequence in OEIS.

When No Closed Form Exists

Not every binomial sum has a nice closed form. If Zeilberger’s algorithm produces a recurrence of order > 1, there may be no “simple” expression. In that case:

  • Matrix exponentiation: If the recurrence has constant coefficients and bounded order d, compute S(n) in O(d³ log n).
  • NTT convolution: If the sum is a literal convolution of two sequences, compute it in O(n log n).
  • Prefix sums: For cumulative binomial sums Σk=0m C(n,k), use the recurrence C(n, k+1) = C(n,k) · (n−k)/(k+1) to accumulate in O(m).

Summary

Hypergeometric identities transform intimidating binomial sums into simple closed forms. Here is the hierarchy of techniques, from easiest to most powerful:

  1. Direct identity: Vandermonde, upper summation, parallel summation. Recognize the pattern and write C(m+n, r) directly.
  2. Snake oil: When the identity isn’t obvious, introduce a generating function variable, swap sums, and extract coefficients.
  3. Hypergeometric classification: Compute the term ratio, identify the pFq type, and look up known evaluations (Dixon, Pfaff–Saalschütz, etc.).
  4. WZ method: For proving identities or finding recurrences. Best used offline with a CAS; in contests, use the results it produces.
  5. NTT / brute force: When no closed form exists, fall back to algorithmic evaluation.
Key identities to memorize:
• Vandermonde: Σ C(m,k) C(n,r−k) = C(m+n,r)
• Upper summation: Σk=0..n C(k,r) = C(n+1,r+1)
• Parallel summation: Σk=0..r C(n+k,k) = C(n+r+1,r)
• Dixon: Σ (−1)k C(a+b,a+k) C(b+c,b+k) C(c+a,c+k) = (a+b+c)!/(a!b!c!)
• Σ C(n,k)² = C(2n,n)
